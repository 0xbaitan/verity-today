 A Beginners Guide to Money Laundering


Dhruv Chandra

Dec 1, 2020   |   Biz & Eco

Welcome to Money Laundering 101. Before I start, a disclaimer: this article is not meant for you. Unless you have about a million USD or two with questionable origins lying around that you wouldn’t want your friends, family and national tax authorities to know about. Then feel free to take notes.


What is money laundering?

Simply put, money laundering is the (highly illegal) process of making illegitimate money look legitimate. The illegitimate money in question, more often than not, has criminal origins and is hence considered dirty. The process of making this money legitimate or clean is laundering, allowing it to be used in the legal economy. There are a lot of ways to launder money and the methods you employ should be according to the level at which you are functioning: are you a small-time petty street-criminal, cartel running drug lord, terrorist organisation, white-collar financial criminal or a corrupt Government official? (Fun-fact: Pablo Escobar was all of the above!) Based on your answer to those questions, you could either mix in your dirty money with some clean money at the casinos, or choose to form a shell company in the Cayman Islands. Despite your personal choice in methods of committing white collar crimes, all forms of money laundering have 3 basic steps-

Placement: Here, funds are moved away from direct association with the crime, into the traditional financial institutions, to be infused with the legitimate banking system. This step liquidates the funds because cash in a bank account can be transferred and manipulated more easily. When criminals possess large amounts of physical cash, you’re they’re most vulnerable, as it can directly link them to the crime they’re committing. Therefore, they need to place the cash into the system through these bank accounts under anonymous corporations or professional middle-men, in order to commence the laundering process. This is the first stage in the washing cycle.

Layering: This integral step in the process serves the purpose of using multiple transactions to further distance the funds from their origins. Once the cash has been successfully placed into the banking system, launderers carry out an infinite number of complicated transfers disguising the money/audit trail and providing anonymity to foil any authorities who might be onto something. If it isn’t obvious, one of the primary objectives of this stage is to confuse any criminal investigation and place as much distance as possible between the money source and their present status and appearance. This is the second stage in the washing cycle.

Integration:This last step allows the now-clean clean money to re-enter the mainstream economy for the original criminal to use. By now, the money either has a seemingly legitimate source on paper or simply cannot be traced back to its origin due to the extensive layering that has taken place. It is now difficult to distinguish legal wealth from illegal wealth and the money has been successfully laundered. Unless the authorities catch you, of course. I wouldn’t exactly call it a success then.

Fun-fact: American organised-crime boss Al “Scarface” Capone, invested (integrated) his shadily acquired money in “Cash-only Laundromats” all around the US, as a front for his money laundering scheme that annually turned $1.4 Billion. The ultimate ownership of these laundries couldn’t be proven, and hence couldn’t be tied to him. This is also one of the reasons, this act is called money ‘laundering’.


So, there you have it! Money Laundering 101 for newbies: the 3 basic steps of how it works.
