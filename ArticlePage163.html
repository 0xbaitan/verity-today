 Big Data? Big Brother.


Karnav Popat

Dec 1, 2020   |   Biz & Eco

Billy Joel’s “We Didn’t Start the Fire”, when it came out in 1989, was widely applauded as a celebration of pop culture from the past forty years. In the form of a list song, Joel goes through more than a hundred headline events, highlighting the rises and falls of trends and popular conscience in our society. One does, of course, wonder what the song would say had it been written about the 80s through 20s instead. “We Didn’t Start the Fire”? More like “We Didn’t Start the Exponential Growth of Data Collection and Profiling by Monopolist Corporations with Restrictive, Unfair Terms of Use”.
There is a good reason the 21st century has become widely known as the Information Age. Since the glory days of IRC and dial-up. The data industry has become essentially the most important industry on the planet over the past twenty years, with a handful of data-driven mega-corporations dominating not just the industry or the stock markets, but the entire world.
In 1993, the internet was in fact the Usenet. Usenetters were restricted to academics and college students, and every autumn there would be a new wave of college freshers who would walk into the digital promised land of fractal chat-boards and the underground culture that connected the world, quickly learning netiquette and the Rules of the Internet. In September that year though, all that stopped. It all changed when the AOL Nation attacked. With an intensive mailing campaign and a massive effort to reduce the barrier of entry to the Usenet, that September the wave of new users was significantly larger than ever before and simply did not stop, going on and on for years as AOL’s campaign and dominance grew. Today we know that point in time as the Eternal September, the September that never ended but instead ushered in a new, less-golden era of Internet history.
In 1994, a user on alt.forklore.computers commented on a thread complaining about the new, often netiquette-violating users, that “It's moot now. September 1993 will go down in net history as the September that never ended.” What the user meant to say was that the sanctity of the internet had forever been broken, and that the stream of new users had essentially wiped out the culture that had existed before them, replacing it with what we would today call “normies”.
A large part of that culture was anonymity, though naturally this was one of those aspects that would not die out for atleast another decade. In the IRC chats before the eternal September, everyone knew not to use their real name on the internet. The web was a mysterious and somewhat sus place, filled with equally mysterious strangers and potential thieves and h4xors. Nobody would dream of identifying themselves beyond a username, usually in obscure 1337speek, and everybody but fresh-faced fourteen-year-old phishing victims knew better than to give away identifying information.
What happened to that internet? Where did the mature community of privacy-conscious academics go? How did we reach the dire straits of today, when everything we do on the web is tracked, profiled, fingerprinted and meticulously stored away never to be erased from stack upon stack of servers? Today, any given user on the web will have a Google account, and a dozen accounts built on top of it, and a massive ecosystem of applications making sure everything they do is tracked. They will have a Facebook account, and tracking pixels that log everything they do on any website even if they are not logged in. They’ll have a Windows machine, and a smartphone with a slew of bloatware, and they’ll have their PII stored on Amazon, and Twitter, and Zoom and a dozen other accounts that nobody remembers signing up for or logging into. They’ll train Google’s AI for free disguised as Captchas and they’ll accept cookies because it’s unfeasible to scroll through a thousand checkboxes. They will have, in short, a massive web of personal information to replace the simple anonymous username. How did this happen?
The commercialization of personal data has been a long way in the making. What started with simple surveys and metadata has today become far more intrusive and malicious. With thousands of free services everywhere on the internet, the modern saying “If it’s free, the product is you” is ever relevant. Today, every node of data collected from a person’s reading preferences on Instagram and Facebook, the contents of their emails on Gmail, and the products they buy on Amazon are sold and resold and bundled together to create massive psychological profiles in the hands of shady advertisers that seek to manipulate the entire world for their profit. The value of the personal data industry in 2020 stands at not less than a trillion dollars, a considerable amount that is barely representative of how important it has been in shaping our society. Nevertheless, there is hope in the future: American presidential candidate Andrew Yang’s calls for personal data to be made personal property is just one of many movements to bring privacy and security back to the web. Whether or not these will be success, will have to be seen.
