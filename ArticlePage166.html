 Trading vs Investment


Rashi Pareek ;
Rishita Chatterjee

Dec 1, 2020   |   Biz & Eco

Investing and trading both pursue the goal of seeking profit in the stock markets. In general, investors seek better returns by buying and holding stocks for the long-term. Traders, by contrast, take advantage of both rising and falling markets, over a short timeframe, taking smaller but more frequent profits. In layman’s terms, trading involves identifying market trends and then quickly buying or selling stocks to book profits. investing, on the other hand, is based on buying stocks of a company after carefully analyzing the business of a company.
The hidden reason for the investor to begin is that the market is wrong in the short term and in the long term it will show its actual worth. Notwithstanding, the trader begins with the differentiating suspicion that the market is directly for the short term, and subsequently bringing in cash simply follows it.
Both trading and investing rely heavily on the movements of the market and therefore come with their own share of market-linked risk.
The trading vs. investing debate is often likened to a ‘skill vs. art’ comparison. With day trading, an individual needs to be able to make quick decisions, know how to time the market and carefully select every single stock. It is built on years and years of experience.
The main distinction between the two methodologies is unmistakably that of the time span. With day exchanging, the individual holds the portions of an organization for a brief timeframe i.e., for an exchanging day. Informal investors capitalize on the day by day patterns and purchase and sell based on minor value vacillations. Then again, financial specialists in the securities exchange purchase impart to the expectation of clutching them however long they might be beneficial. The venture is constantly made in view of a more extended time skyline, for example, a long time or many years, and is unbothered by short term price fluctuations.
Trading and Investment both have the same goal, profit. However, the difference here lies in how the profit is pursued. Traders jump in and out of stocks within weeks, days and in certain cases even minutes. Therefore the aim here is to consider small fluctuations in price and leveraging it over large investments. Let’s break the math down - when the value of a security fluctuates from $100 to $101, this temporary fluctuation does not affect a long term investor to the least, however, in the case of a day trader who purchases $50000 worth of the $100 security and sells them at $101 later that day, a subsequent profit $500 is earned in a single day. A daily earning of $500 does seem incredibly lucrative, but when it comes to trading everything is not Jordan Belfort and roses, in most cases. Professional traders invest extensive programmes in order to attain the desired profit and yet the risk remains as high. Professional day traders invest in algorithm detecting software, professional reports from news agencies and other aids to make this a secure and consistent pursuit.
After evaluating both trading and investment the starkest difference between traders and investors is in the timing, however, delving deeper into this evaluation, their respective study of the company’s potential is a crucial factor as well. On one hand, where investors focus on a company’s long term growths and advancements. Traders focus on small mispricing in the market, one of the recent examples being the temporary fluctuation in share prices amidst the ongoing U.S elections.
