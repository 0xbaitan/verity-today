 The Secret to Enjoying things more: Learning'


Arunav Ghosh

Dec 1, 2020   |   Education

Paul Bloom, a Canadian-American psychologist is the Brooks and Suzanne Ragen Professor of psychology and cognitive science at Yale University. His research has explored how children and adults understand the physical and social world, often through the lens of Essentialism. According to Bloom, Humans are natural-born essentialists, he also believes that if we want to find greater pleasure in things, we must learn more about them.
A 2008 study led by Hilke Plassmann used fMRI (functional magnetic resonance imaging) scans to show how the stated price of various wines affected brain activity at the moment of consumption. The price tags presented were deliberately falsified—a $5 wine, for example, was identified as costing $45—yet the drinkers’ brains couldn’t tell the difference. The price cue, not the objective quality of the product, predicted people’s level of enjoyment. Researchers call this the marketing placebo effect (MPE)—a behavioural and/or physical change produced by marketing alone, totally separate from the product itself, this study was in opposition to the basic assumption in economics is that the experienced pleasantness (EP) from consuming a good depends only on its intrinsic properties and on the state of the individual, It showed that changing properties of commodities, such as prices, that are unrelated to their intrinsic qualities or to the consumer’s state affected reported EP and, more importantly, also modulated the blood-oxygen-level-dependent (BOLD) signal in mOFC. The main hypothesis of this study was that an increase in the perceived price of a wine should, through an increase in taste expectations, increase activity in the mOFC. The results described above provide evidence consistent with the hypothesis.
This means that to a great extent, How you feel about something, or how much pleasure you receive from it, depends on the context of its origin or existence, I.e. Who created it?, Who touched it?, Where and what happened to it?. This can be seen in the case of Art, where near-identical forgeries are not worth nothing, while original works of art that look identical could be sold for millions, meaning "When you like art, you just don't like the physical thing, you like the story behind it".
From this phenomenon, we can derive a rather pleasant conclusion, if you want to enjoy something more, if you want to find greater pleasure in it, you just need to find out more about it. I encourage you to try and learn more about the things you enjoy, if you enjoy eating bread, just by reading up about the history, manufacturing process, and other information about the bread you eat, you can significantly improve its taste (probably) and enjoy it more or by reading books and articles about the kind of music you like you might find it even more pleasurable, this is how you can choose to enjoy those things more.
I guess maybe that is why I say "Being a Nerd is Fun!" so confidently.
