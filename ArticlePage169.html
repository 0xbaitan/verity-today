 THE ZETTELKASTEN METHOD, OR WHY YOU NEED A SECOND BRAIN, AND HOW YOU CAN MAKE ONE.


Arunav Ghosh

Dec 1, 2020   |   Education



What is a "Zettelkasten"?

Niklas Luhmann, a German academic popularized a system of organizing and managing your knowledge that lives on today, digitally. The Zettelkasten which Luhmann had described as his conversational partner, and at other times as a "second memory, cybernetic system, a ruminant, or septic tank."

Niklas Luhmann wrote over 70 books and more than 400 scholarly articles, he was a researcher and teacher at the University of Bielefeld from 1968 to 1993 and has been described as being, "apart from Max Weber, the most famous and influential German sociologist of the twentieth century". His work was highly influential, to the extent that there this a research project dedicated to his literary estate and also, his Zettelkasten at the University of Bielefeld, Faculty of Sociology. However, The impact Luhmann's work and approach had on the field of sociology and the world is the subject of a completely different article. When asked how he had managed to publish such an extensive body of literature, he would say "Much of it happens in my Zettelkasten", rendering his system of "Personal Knowledge Management System" a legend. A Zettelkasten can be simply described as a "Slip-Box", referring to the "Zettels" which means "Notes or slips of paper", and "Kasten" which means box. It consists of many unique and individual with information or thoughts that are noted in real-time, as they are acquired or occur. The notes have a hierarchy and contain metadata so that notes can be connected and associated, i.e. referenced using the metadata assigned to each atomic "Zettel" creating an efficient system of internal linking. The Zettelkasten has been used by intellectuals in research, and by organizations to manage information, for instance in the form of a Library catalogue. Luhmann built a Zettelkasten in the analogue method, it comprised of six stacks of four wooden drawers stacked on top of each other, filled with paper notes made with index cards, 90,000 index cards specifically. These index cards are of such great value today in the fields of sociology and systems theory that they were digitized and made online in 2019. Essentially, Luhmann created a second brain for himself, capable of networked thought that lives on after his demise. How useful is "building a second brain" for you? Tiago Forte, thought-leader and the founder of Forte Labs puts it in his aptly named, highly sought-after online course "Building a Second Brain"(BaSB), if you consume a lot of interesting information but it doesn't lead to meaningful results in your life, a "Personal Knowledge Management System" or a Second Brain can dramatically expand your creative output, learn better, and think more critically, What's the difference between a PKM (Personal Knowledge Management System) and taking notes in a notebook? The answer is design-thinking and networked thought. It is enshrined in the book, "Getting Things Done" by David Allen, that the brain is a place for creating ideas, not storing them, the brain has an extraordinary capacity for storage, retrieving information is not always perfect and information retention is not reliable. While technologies like notebooks, filing cabinets, or even modern world digital note-taking software like Evernote, all these methods have the same approach towards organization, they are hierarchal, to retrieve the information you must remember a pathway, trace it and then use it. For example, you will find an idea you had noted on a specific page of a notebook, in a specific folder, nested with several other individual notes in a hierarchy, while this works perfectly fine with a casual approach to note-taking, it is a primitive system compared to the human brain. Such a method arguably lacks interconnectivity or internal linking. Knowledge is rid of context, and there is a lack of independence when it comes to defining relationships between unique ideas and information. Which is where Knowledge Graph Softwares like Roam Research, Obsidian, etc. take centre stage. Where can you start? You can build a Zettelkasten virtually by using various existing software, or venture into networked thought and conquer the frontiers of knowledge. The ZKN3 built by Daniel LÃ¼decke is a free, cross-platform and open-source software that is built explicitly to emulate the Zettelkasten system, Or you can use the Zettler or The Archive, Even classic note-taking apps like Bear notes or Evernotes can be used as Zettelkastens Conclusion? Today, we consume a critical mass of media and content, but if we were to apply ourselves and build upon this overload of information and ideas, We could achieve as a society, as the popular meme summarizes well, "Galaxy Brain". Sir Isaac Newton said in 1675: "If I have seen further it is by standing on the shoulders of Giants." Today in the age of Artificial Intelligence and Automation, To improve the human condition, we must see further, hence, we must stand "upon the shoulder of giants" and cling hard onto those shoulders to expand our worldview, and make our brains useful for the times ahead of us. This is how we can create "Good Change" that lasts beyond our lifetime.
