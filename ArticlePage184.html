 Impact Of Biden's Win On India


Gaurav

Dec 1, 2020   |   Global Affairs

With Joe Biden being elected the 46th President of the United States of America, the American voters have most likely changed the course of the global economy. The change of governmental setup from the extreme rightist that was Trump, to the Centre-Leftist Biden will certainly have tangible changes the course of the global geopolitics. The impact of Biden’s election and the health and policy choices of the Government shall certainly be felt by the Indian economy. First, we need to understand how the United States and India are dependent on each other. Over the past 20 years, India has enjoyed a trade surplus with the US. Trade surplus had peaked at $21.2 billion in FY18 (2017-18) and has moderated to some extent. Apart from trade in goods, India accounts for nearly 5 per cent of USA’s services imports. Beyond trade, over the past two decades, the US has been the fifth-largest source of Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) into India. Of the total $476 billion FDI that has come in since April 2000, the US accounted for $30.4 billion — roughly 6.5 per cent — directly. Only Mauritius, Singapore, Netherlands, and Japan have had more FDI in India since 2000. In the Trump worldview, trade was a zero-sum game. In other words, a country had to lose for another to gain. Of course, that is not the case most of the time. More often than not, trade is mutually beneficial — while it may be true that it may not be “equally” beneficial to all countries. Under a Biden administration, India’s trade with the US could recover from the dip since 2017-18. A Biden presidency may also see a renewed push towards a rules-based trading system across the world — instead of outright ad-hocism as was the case under Trump — as well as a move away from the protectionist approach that has been getting strong across the world.

With the Biden Administration soon to be in power, the control of COVID infections and economic recovery should be foremost in their checklist of actions to recover the devastation caused by Trump. This will help the US to provide a growth impulse to the global economy that countries such as India need to boost their exports and grow.

Apart from these very direct ways in which the US is important to India, there are massive policy concerns as well. Areas of contention between India and US — such as the tricky issue of data localisation or capping prices of medicines and medical devices — have a better chance of getting towards a resolution as we move away from the radical approach of President Trump (and his America First approach) to the pragmatism of a Biden presidency.

Further, under the Trump administration, the US sanctions on Iran severely limited India’s sourcing of cheap crude oil. For an economy such as India, which needs a regular supply of cheap oil to grow fast, a normalisation of US-Iran relationship (and lifting of sanctions) would be more than useful.

On China, too, while the US apprehensions are unlikely to be eliminated even under a Biden administration, it is more likely that a Biden administration will help India against China, instead of clubbing the two together.

Last but not the least, Biden has promised to rejoin the Paris Climate Accord, and this may help countries such as India in dealing with the massive challenges — both technical and financial — on this front.

Thus, Joe Biden’s election as the 46th President of the United States is going to be having strong implications on India. Only time will tell how beneficial a Biden presidency really is for India.
