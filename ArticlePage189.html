 Mental Health In India


Narayani Talukdar

Dec 1, 2020   |   Mental Health

In olden days, an individual who was mentally ill was believed to be possessed by a demon or influenced by an ancestral spirit as a result of violating a taboo or neglecting a ritual. As a result, they were beaten and tortured to make the body unsuitable for demonic possession. The Indian Society of Psychiatric Nurses was started in 1991 under the guidance of Dr. Reddemma. Its purpose was to enhance the advanced knowledge and skills in the field of psychiatric nursing, to provide a platform for discussion and deliberation on evidence-based practice and to create awareness.

The recent prevalence rate of psychiatric disorders in the country is 18 to 207 cases per 1000 population, and the world scenario is also almost the same. Most of these patients live in the rural areas, and the person visiting a general clinic is not diagnosed for their mental problems but for other ailments, which causes financial burdens on the patients.

In rural areas, the stigma around mental health is much higher and knowledge about it much lower in comparison to the urban areas. Many of the people living there believe that mental illness can be cured by a faith healer or an ‘ojha’. In urban areas, people are scared to talk about their mental health issues due to the fear of being ostracized from society and being labelled “weak” or “crazy”.

The people of India are far from realizing the importance of mental health and how it can affect our daily lives along with that of the people around them. The topic of mental health is such a huge taboo in our country, that the media would rather concoct a whole story about drugs abuse, rather than openly talk about the suicide of a famous actor. The media, blatantly disregarding the fact that the actor was going through genuine issues and had committed suicide even with ample evidence provided to them, shows how people refuse to talk about such a huge issue in our country and the taboo around it. Many people believe that the issues regarding mental health are a myth and people need to “toughen up”.

In India, modern psychiatric facilities are available only in big cities. Although mental hospitals are being modernized, mental health is still a low priority. Studies have shown that 80% of the Indian population depends on indigenous treatments such as Ayurvedic and Unani systems of medication, and religious treatments consisting of prayers and fasting.

In April 2011, a policy group was constituted to form the National Mental Health Policy of 2014. Based on the recommendations of the experts, the policy was considered in accordance with the World Health Assembly resolution. It incorporates a participatory-right, integrated and evidence-based approach. It aims at promoting awareness regarding mental health, preventing mental illness and enabling the recovery from mental illness. It also promotes destigmatisation and desegregation, and provides accessible, affordable and quality mental health and social care to all persons through their life spans within the rights-based framework. Its long-term goals aim at strengthening the leadership in the mental health sector at the district, state and national levels. It also aims at reducing distress, disability, exclusion morbidity and premature mortality associated with mental health problems, along with enhancing the understanding of mental heath in the country.

Its short-term goals include providing universal access to mental health care in the country: to increase the access of mental health care services to vulnerable sections of society such as the homeless, people living in remote areas and those from the deprived sections of society. It also aims at reducing suicide and its attempts. Other objectives include respecting the rights of the mentally ill people and protecting them from harm, along with equitable distribution of skilled human resources, and enhancing financial allocation and utilization in the sector.

It is clear that India has to go a long way to make mental health an open and comfortable topic to discuss. However, with the help of skilled doctors, proper media representation, the government, and local communities, we can make India a more hospitable place for people suffering from mental health issues.
