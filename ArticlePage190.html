 Sharing Your Story


Anoushka Dasgupta

Dec 1, 2020   |   Global Affairs

When it comes to sharing personal experiences on mental disorders, being a well-known personality on the internet can invoke a lot of responsibility. Having a large audience, especially if they are young and impressionable, can mean that they are easily swayed by your opinions, regardless of whether it holds any actual value. It’s easy to fall down an endless rabbit hole lined with misconceptions and lies without realising that the life they are so desperately romanticising is actually toxic and harmful.

One in four people in the world are diagnosed with some sort of mental illness. It’s important to understand that sharing your own personal stories and experiences does not invalidate someone else’s. A large step has been taken towards understanding various symptoms of common mental disorders and how it affects different people. Even so, the stigma and disdain which surrounds this topic still persists.

Anyone who’s even glanced at Indian news channels in the past couple months knows that Kangana Ranaut is a big topic right now. Whether it’s turning a blind eye to rampant casteism, or debating about drug abuse in the Bollywood industry, she seems to grab attention no matter what she does.

Recently, she seemed to reclaim the spotlight when she engaged in a twitter feud with 22-year-old artist Priyanka Paul. It all started when Paul re-tweeted a post which mentioned several symptoms of a depressive breakdown and joked about experiencing them all in one day. Ranaut, already established as a no-holds-barred, painfully upfront persona, proceeded to slam Paul, claiming that admitting such breakdowns wasn’t something that deserved any praise.

The thing is, like Paul, many people share their experiences with a touch of humour. It has become a very common coping mechanism and is one of the ways in which people bring attention to their own mental health. It has become harder to distinguish whether a post is merely a joke or a carefully fabricated cry for help.

Twitter feuds like this are just the tip of the iceberg. When it comes to personal experiences with mental illnesses, the real toxicity lies in the several micro-aggressions, the passive aggressiveness and the downright rejection of the idea that mental disorders even exist. Frankly, suffering from a mental illness has become a double-edged sword: you don’t only have to deal with the symptoms, but the stigma too. Whether we are talking about Piers Morgan discrediting celebrities’ stories about mental illness by saying they are making it ‘fashionable’, or talking about Ranaut’s statements on ‘depressed and suicidal snowflake feminists’, the age-old conversation about mental health keeps persisting.

However, for every ignorant bigot, we have stars who genuinely speak up about their trauma and talk about their mistakes and regrets. Talking about these experiences not only opens up more conversations, but connects us to a person we hardly know through a common thread of humanity. Reading about the struggles of other people can often help you connect the dots or reconsider taking up that appointment with your therapist that you’ve been avoiding. Opening up more avenues and changing the angles can provide an entirely different perspective to the mental disorder you thought you knew everything about.

Like many other things, mental health is also a spectrum. The only way we can educate ourselves and move forward in this field is to sit back and listen. Sometimes, all it takes is an attentive ear and a willingness to learn, so that we can be more accommodating for all kinds of people.
