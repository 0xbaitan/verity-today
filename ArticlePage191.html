 Social Media Advocacy


Mohan Rajagopal

Dec 1, 2020   |   Mental Health

The age of digitisation and the prevalence of the internet has brought about a paradigm shift in socio-cultural affairs across the world. No longer are protests marched only in the streets or news disseminated through newspapers and media channels. Especially in the hands of Generations Y and Z, social media has evolved from a mere tool to stay in touch with one’s friends and family to an instrument that can be weaponised to bring attention to any affair, no matter how small or big, in any corner of the globe. The extent to which this evolution has taken place has enabled people to keep in check their privilege, voice their opinions about important matters, and make sure that people in positions of power are held accountable for their crimes against humanity. However, putting such worldly responsibilities on the shoulders of the common people with no external support systems and no end to the troubles that they face can only deteriorate their mental well being after a certain point.

Bringing attention to important causes that need global awareness is, in itself, a wonderful phenomenon. The problem begins when this awareness takes the form of a competitive race used by people for clout and to seem ‘woke’. On Twitter, frequent captions to posts about some catastrophe of the other include “I don’t know why no one is talking about this” or “I’ve barely seen this issue covered on my timeline”. Whether intentional or not, the only effect such captions have is to shame those people who may not have heard of said events and place on these individuals the responsibilities of being up to date with every issue in the world and vocally fighting against them every waking second. Blaming ordinary individuals for the government’s failure in transparency and awareness can only worsen the toxic environment that festers on platforms such as Twitter and Instagram.

The more obvious side-effect of news saturation must also be made note of. Scrolling through one’s feed, viewing one disaster after another can only serve to instill a sense of despondency, despair, and frustration in the hearts of internet consumers. While social media advocacy is a brilliant tool if used correctly and sparingly, care must be taken to not go in too deep. Although the ideas of “social media detox” may seem frivolous and attention-seeking, it’s the best way people can distance themselves from overexposure to politics and society online. Yes, it is our responsibility as citizens to keep an eye out for our fellow human beings and attempt to dismantle systems that oppress and subjugate others, but not at the cost of our own sanity, pride, and vulnerability.
