 The Misconceptions Surrounding Therapists and their Portrayal in the Media


Shagun Kothari

Dec 1, 2020   |   Mental Health

Therapy and counselling have often been portrayed as unhelpful and been vilified in media to the extent that a large number of people believe they don’t need “shrinks” to solve their problems, and that going to therapy doesn’t help. The stigma against receiving help for mental health problems already persists in many societies and this portrayal of counselling does not help matters.

Mental health professionals such as psychologists, psychiatrists, therapists and counsellors usually possess the proper credentials, skills, as well as experience for their job, yet are often shown in movies as uninterested, unprofessional and most importantly, unhelpful. We have often seen movies and TV shows which have the protagonist despising therapy and going for help to their peers who often magically ‘solve’ all their problems. While peers do play a role in the development of a person’s mental state for better or worse, they are largely unqualified, and the media propels the idea that a professional is worse at their job than a layman when in reality, such an approach may prove to be harmful. Leaning on those around us, often of our own age, to be the solution to our problems is unrealistic and toxic. Therapy doesn’t work in that way: it doesn’t fix our problems, but only guides us to find our own solutions. People with no knowledge in this field can prove to be unhelpful in the long run without meaning to.

Therapists and counsellors in media are often shown to be uncaring, even selfish and money minded, as well as crossing boundaries and prodding into their client’s life in a completely unprofessional manner. They can often be portrayed as toxic and unhealthy which is NOT the case for most of them. Even psychiatric hospitals are often shown as oppressive and stifling with uncaring medical professionals. They are likened to prisons which one has to escape from.

In reality, mental health workers are qualified health care providers. They have the experience and skill set required to mentally and emotionally help their patients, guide them to process and address difficult emotions or feelings as well as treat and explain any potential mental health conditions. They do care about their patients, but are painted in a sinister way by the media. Mental health hospitals aim to create a healthy environment and to nurture and help people admitted there.

Another issue is that therapists are often shown to be people whom you cannot trust with secrets or to respect your privacy. Therapists are ethically bound to maintain confidentiality, the only exception being if someone is in danger.

While the portrayal of mental health professionals is improving in recent media, the negative connotations persisting are still too large to be ignored and any media depicting therapists for the worse is detrimental. It may be true that not all mental health workers fit the ideal, but propagating the idea that seeking professional help is a waste of time is harmful to today’s minds. It is extremely important to create positive imagery of therapists and reduce the problematic portrayal of those suffering mentally.

We wouldn’t let a friend perform a surgery on us instead of a doctor. Neither would mass media portray doctors as people incompetent enough to not be able to treat us. Why then, is this the case for mental health?
