 Diplomacy of Deception – The Chinese Debt Trap


Namit Mahajan

Dec 1, 2020   |   Biz & Eco

If you've been following the news about China, the other thing that makes headlines except the current COVID-19 pandemic is the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) or the "One Belt, One Road" initiative by China. Considered as a Trillion dollar project of economic expansion and development project by China, it has received global backlash for giving loans to countries, who cannot repay them, thus sending them into a debt trap and then seizing their assets. This specific policy of China is what experts call "Debt trap Diplomacy."
China, being the manufacturing center of the globe, is utilizing its logistics, energy, communications, and international trade in an integrated manner. However, the way it is panning it out has raised suspicions. Though Chinese funds are indeed helping to build vital transport links and in increasing industrialization in low-income countries, the debts they are incurring in the process are looking increasingly unsustainable.
In December 2017, Sri Lanka gave away the control of Hambantota Port at a 70 percent stake to China on a ninety-nine-year lease after it failed to clear its debts, of around $8 billion, with interest rates as high as 7 percent, with which they built the port. The port, which is in the Indian Ocean, becomes an area of concern as it may become a naval base in the future, which could pose a threat to the security of India. The China Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) is one of the most extensive infrastructure projects by China, amounting to over $60 billion. However, it comes with its debts of over $6 billion, leading to Pakistan leasing the deepwater port of Gwadar to China. It also recently renegotiated the deal for constructing the Diamer Bhasha Dam, worth $2.6 billion. It all falls under "the string of pearls" that China is trying to create. (read: String of Pearls theory).
Africa, the largest trading partner, is itself stuck in a myriad of issues. China takes cheap raw materials from Africa, processes it in China, and later sells it in African markets profitably. Kenya has recently taken a loan of $2.3 billion for a port, which serves as collateral and thus may go to China if Kenya cannot pay the loan back. China has financial relations with around 49 nations in Africa. In addition to these cases, other debt-ridden countries include Djibouti, Maldives, and Montenegro.
Montenegro and China struck a deal of $1.3 billion, to create their first-ever highway, spanning over hundreds of miles. However, Montenegro cannot afford it. Their debt-to-GDP ratio grew by over 78 percent in 2019. (the debt-to-GDP r. is the ratio between a country's government debt and its gross domestic product.)
Maldives has also landed itself in literal "deep waters" by undertaking the bridge project with China, which has increased its debt-to-GDP ratio being almost 100 percent! Djibouti is at the crossroads of Africa and the Middle East, on a narrow strait that controls access to the Red Sea and the Suez Canal. It is a very strategic location, which has garnered its huge infrastructural investments by China, including the construction of the Doraleh Multipurpose Port and a $3.5 billion free-trade zone with China, with many assets that can be lost. Estimates say that 88 percent of the Gross Domestic Product (GDP) of Djibouti, which corresponds to $1.72 billion, is owned by China, all from investments based on development and infrastructural loans.
All these nations need to find alternatives and find them fast because, with the pace that China is moving, they might have to give up their significant assets over to China, and it may use them for their prime benefit, which may lead to consequences unfavorable. Currently, we're still ambiguous about where China's "giving" to these developing countries stand - on the side of good, or bad.
