 Binge Watch on Women’s Liberation Movement


Dipali Banka

August 31, 2021   |   Creativity


“Each time a woman stands up for herself, she stands up for all women” – anonymous.

The women’s liberation movement was a heterogeneous social movement which worked towards the accomplishment of gender equality. It started in the late 1960s and continued till the 1980s. This movement shook the strong pillars of validated patriarchy and social hierarchies deeply rooted in the grounds of the society confining the physical independence and individuality of women.

After an intense fight for women’s right to vote in the 19th century, feminists all over the world presented a strong front against discrimination; at the same time women activists were hosting campaigns and leading movements to inform the women about their responsibility and importance as new voters. Organizations such as the League of Women Voters and the Women’s Institute worked towards this cause.

With women being treated as second-class citizens, their participation and contribution in historic events were completely excluded from the academic syllabus in schools. Therefore, to understand the bigger picture of how women have been no less courageous than men, the WLM groups launched study programs which included feminist history, sociology and psychology as well as adult education to counter the gender biases in reading these texts.

The WLM did not only stand against sexism but also fought racism, homophobia and stood in solidarity with any individual or group affected by other forms of oppression.



The WLM was successful in many ways such as in criminalizing violence in a marriage which was legal in UK till 1991, the Abortion Art 1967 was a landmark for the women’s reproductive rights which is even now a constant threat in many countries. The small milestones of the group were to help girls be confident in their own skin and not pay heed to societal standards of beauty. In the late 1980s, during the HIV/AIDS crisis which primarily focused on the LGBTQ community, they made sure that the impact on women were not ignored.

Having been said this, let’s talk about how Hollywood has done justice to the portrayal of some prominent feminists, and creating an image of the historic events that took place in the fight to end discrimination.

1. ON THE BASIS OF SEX (2018)

On the Basis of Sex, a 2018 biographical legal drama directed by Mimi Leder, is a film based on the Late Supreme Court Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg’s life and her path to become the leading gender rights lawyer of her time. The film follows her early life of studying in Harvard and during the unfortunate disease of her husband: how she took care of him, and, at the same time, attended his classes, returning home and nurturing their infant daughter Jane, her transfer to Columbia and graduating at the top of her class. Due to difficulties in landing a job as a lawyer in any firm because of her gender, she takes up a job at the Rutgers Law College teaching ‘sex discrimination and the law’.



Alongside her husband Marty, Ruth takes up the Moritz v. Commissioner – the first federal case to declare discrimination on the grounds of sex unconstitutional. Felicity Jones delivers a strong and exceptional performance as RBG. The cinematic scenes of Ruth’s research for the case shows her passion and love for the law. It is indeed an important story to be shared, for it speaks about a woman who changed how law was practiced and wasn’t willing to give up without a proper fight.

2. SUFFRAGETTE (2015)

Suffragette is a 2015 British historical drama film Directed by Sarah Gavron is about a group of working women who joined hands and fought for the women’s suffrage movement in the United Kingdom: they were sent to prison and beaten up, fired and died for it. Maud Watts’s life changes after she is caught in a suffragette protest - she was not allowed to see her son and was thrown out by her husband which only prompted her towards reformism in the favor of women’s rights. The movie ends with clips showing various countries giving women the right to vote. It shows the means by which women have organized and gained attention for their rights, from campaigning from hunger strikes in prison to bombing pillar boxes. The film represents real life activists, with several characters based on them.



3. HIDDEN FIGURES (2016)

Hidden Figures is a 2016 American biographical drama directed by Theodore Melfi, which is based on Margot Lee Shetterly's book of the same name. The movie revolves around the African American mathematicians working at NASA during the Space Race in the '60s. Katherine Johnson, the brains behind the success of the Mercury -Atlas 6 orbital mission, which was a turning point in the race between the Soviet Union and United States, Mary Jackson - the first female black aeronautical engineer, and Dorothy Vaughan - the first black supervisor of NASA; these three women achieved their positions and status in their professional fields by fighting the long built walls of racism and sexism surrounding their workplace and in the society outside.



It received overwhelming praise for the outstanding performance of Henson, Monáe, and Spencer who played the three pioneering black women and also for its writing, emotional tone and historical accuracy. The movie highlights the contributions of these women whose contributions to NASA and Space Race couldn't be left unacknowledged, and it leaves a strong social message at its core.

4. THE GLORIAS (2020)

The Glorias is a 2020 American biographical drama directed by Julie Taymor. It’s a movie on the multiple life stages of Gloria Steinem, who has lived such a significant and honorable life that four actresses were casted to play her, among whom were two Oscar winners. Gloria Steinem is a fine woman who is a feminist icon to most people and someone who chose not to get involved with a man and settle down in a time where women were always expected to get married and have children.



The movie is not the same as the traditional biopics; it doesn't begin with Steinem's life's chronological events but keeps bouncing backward and forward to different parts of her life. The film traces her extraordinary life as she actively participates in the women's liberation movement and her role as a leading figure in the '60s, '70s and the '80s; it also depicts some of the most important historic events such as the 1977 National Women's Conference and the launch of Ms., America's first feminist magazine. Women around the world have acknowledged this movie as a source of learning more about Gloria's activism and are very much grateful for her contributions in making a better place for them to live in.

5. NORTH COUNTRY (2005)

North Country is a 2005 American drama directed by Niki Caro. It is a movie based on the true story of a mine labourer, Lois Jenson. Here, she flees from her abusive husband and returns to her hometown where she lands a job at an iron mine. She finds that the women in her workplace were constant targets of sexual harassment by most male co-workers who believed that women were not fit for these jobs. After being assaulted, she, with the support of a friend, files a suit against the company and the union after they did not provide any solutions for her situation of unchecked humiliation and intimidation, while the other women refused to stand with her since they felt their jobs were at threat. Jenson was the first woman who won a sex discrimination case in the United States, setting a powerful precedent for the future. The movie brings us realistic depictions of the discomfort women have to face at a workplace that is dominated by men and at the same time have to tolerate constant nagging and mockery.



The Women’s Liberation Movement was recognized as a branch of the second-wave of feminism which touched on every area of a women’s affair, starting from work to sexuality, while the first-wave had only focused on basic rights to vote.
