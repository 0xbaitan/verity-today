 How Thomas Paine’s ‘Common Sense’ Changed America


Sreemita Mukherjee

August 31, 2021   |   Creativity


‘These are the times that try men’s souls’ begins Thomas Paine in his pamphlet, ‘Common Sense’ released in early 1776, in which he details all the reasons as to why the 13 British colonies deserve the right to self-govern and by extent, gain independence from the overbearing British monarchy. The success of the American revolution inspired countries around the globe to fight monarchy and tyranny and was the first time the British monarchy had been challenged and subsequently defeated. The American Revolution was hardly only an American effort - the battle of Yorktown, which was the last land war of the Revolution, was effectively won by the Americans because of the French intervention, led by Lafayette. This is an isolated example, instances of such interventions exist in plenty. That is not to say that loyalists to the British Crown did not exist - about 20% of the white population belonged to the category. Much of the enslaved population and the Indigenous population too supported the British cause and understandably so. On the other hand, the majority of the white population were patriots and ‘Common Sense’ was a major reason why.



‘Common Sense’ was released in January 1776, amid the American Revolution, merely a few months before America gained its independence. The catalyst to its huge success could be attributed to the fact that the pamphlet was extremely accessible. It was about 47 pages long and was written in a language that could connect to people of all social statuses. ‘Common Sense’ did not present philosophical abstractions and metaphors, instead, it harped on personal experiences and emotions. Thomas Paine channelled his own experiences, as he came from a humble background. This did a marvellous job in helping common people recognize themselves in the argument and, therefore, helping them adopt the ideals of the patriots - the need for a free America. A free America, and emphasis on this one, concerning white people.

‘Common Sense’ sold about 500,000 copies during the Revolutionary War. It was read out loud at almost every meeting organized by the patriots; pirated copies of the pamphlet were in circulation throughout the colonies. In a letter, George Washington once wrote to a friend, applauding the impact ‘Common Sense’ had on the common American and how it inspired people who already felt resistance to act on it. Thomas Paine provided the common people with the confidence they needed. For the first time, somebody was openly criticizing the monarchy and was confident in the fact that the Continental Army would be able to defeat the British army, which was a global superpower. ‘Common Sense’ presents the revolution as what it was - achievable and largely inevitable.

The pamphlet heavily criticizes the English constitution, especially the role of the monarch. He calls the English constitution contradictory and against the will of God. He criticizes the role of the monarch, the sheer existence of one, and concludes that the so-called representative of God on earth and their role itself defied the Scriptures. Paine writes of how their connections to Great Britain will only hold them back, how, despite reasoning that the British Colonies would be safer under the rule of the Crown, when questioned by the patriots, they still didn’t put the wellbeing of the people of the Colonies over their profits.

‘Common Sense’ is written in an inspiring and confident tone. In this case, it targeted the freedom of a place that was already on the brink of revolution. However, at the end of the day, most people of the British Colonies still were not convinced. After all, how could a ragtag army of volunteers defeat a global superpower? Paine harped on the fact that their victory was not only possible but also inevitable. French and Spanish reinforcements were already on their way, and together with this, the usual casualness and utter confidence the entire pamphlet had was able to convince almost everyone to have faith in the revolution. As a piece of propaganda, ‘Common Sense’ worked marvellously well.

The pamphlet also spoke of the kind of government America would be governed under, after they achieved their long-due independence. Paine stressed the importance of American freedom and most importantly, their ability to be able to self-govern. Paine pushed on the existence of a strong central government that would protect the rights, freedoms, and beliefs of the people. ‘Common Sense’ painted the picture of a free America, at a time, most were scared to even imagine it.

America went on to achieve its independence and managed to abolish the same crimes, they didn’t even consider crimes at the time they fought for their independence. ‘Common Sense’, after all these centuries, still stands tall and true. Its impact on the events of history, and its genius are still worthy of being praised.

In ‘’Common Sense’ Thomas Paine writes, ‘We have it in power to begin the world over again’ and between all the bleeding and fighting, the reading and writing, ‘Common Sense’ achieved that in its entirety.
