 The Cabo Delgado Insurgency


Ananya Singh

August 31, 2021   |   Global Affairs & Politics


ISIS strongholds are slowly resurrecting in Mozambique with brutal massacres already at play; how do you prevent the havoc ISIS will unleash if resuscitated in East Africa in its full infamy?



Cabo Delgado was once the centre of liberation from the colonial occupation, and now it faces another crucial conflict. Since 2017, groups of fighters, often carrying ISIS flags and denouncing the state and the Frelimo ruling party, have grown from small units targeting remote security posts into heavily equipped militants that threaten national stability and international peace. The ISIL-linked fighters in Mozambique have racked up immense chaos with mass abductions, beheadings and destruction of infrastructure in towns, and are even expanding their operations into Tanzania. They’ve also taken control over a key port town in Mozambique. This has displaced some 700,000 people and killed more than 2,500. The most horrifying incident was of 50 people being beheaded in a sports field over the course of a weekend. Security sources report that these militants have developed cells not just among the civilian population but also within armed force units.

Factors that made the region a centre for conflict include poverty, destruction of people’s livelihoods (mainly mining of ruby deposits), the rise of local criminals enforcing Islamist ideologies, and colonial-era tensions between the Mwani and Makua peoples. The socio-economic inequalities faced by Mwani against the dominance of the Makonde elites after independence cause them to remain sympathetic to the opposition- RENAMO. All of this internal strife and unrest was never conclusively handled, and it has since snowballed and turned into the crisis we know now.

Officials in the Mozambican government initially dismissed the matter and referred to the group as local criminals; this has since changed to labelling them as terrorists, and in so doing, they have admitted the problem is greater than initially thought. However, this classification implies that terrorist groups are the only ones behind the threat. Islamist militants from Tanzania are among the militants’ leaders, but the majority of the group’s members are Mozambicans - disenfranchised, unemployed and frustrated youth. Their motivations for joining the group are less shaped by ideology than by frustration against the government. They are slowly turning into committed jihadists from frustrated revolters as their interaction with terrorists continues.

Along with this, Amnesty International accused both the fighters and Mozambique’s security forces, as well as a South African private military firm hired by the government, of war crimes against civilians, including extrajudicial executions and acts of torture. This makes all parties in this conflict guilty and blurs the lines between the perpetrators and victims.

Mozambique’s army is currently incapable of handling this threat as their forces are already stretched out thin handling the security of the country and the surrender of a residual armed faction of the Renamo opposition group. The navy, meanwhile, is barely functioning. The elite paramilitary was able to stop the militants’ advance and destroyed some of their camps but was unable to neutralise them. Mozambique’s government needs foreign partners to provide the resources, including lethal assistance. Mozambique’s Western partners are concerned about the reports of abuses committed against the population by security forces and potential leaks of government weapons into militants’ hands. Hence, private military contractors have secretly been operating- Russian mercenaries from the Wagner group and the South Africa-based Dyck Advisory Group (DAG) were believed to have been invited. The Mozambican government has invited US military advisers to train local forces fighting the militants. However, it seems the US is framing the insurgency in a very oversimplified manner by referring to the militants as an extension of the Islamic State solely. The Southern African Development Community (SADC) is also seeking to build international support to deploy its own troops into Mozambique. But, Filipe Nyusi - the president - has been nervous about that happening. His critics say he wants to keep prying eyes out of the province, a zone for illicit activity including heroin trafficking. His supporters emphasise rather that he is just being careful about what kind of intervention he allows, wary that a heavy presence of foreign troops could become difficult to control.

Any external intervention should be measured so that it can both respond to the security threat posed by the militants and also protect civilians on their return. Mozambique should direct international assistance to support and train its own special forces and conduct restricted military operations against al-Shabab. Patrolling territorial waters could also deny militants opportunities to move fighters and supplies. Mozambique’s forces should focus on providing security around the areas that natives were forced to flee from and aid their return. Nyusi should start dialogues with civilians to soothe local tensions and rebuild trust with communities that feel let down by the state. Such dialogue might also help authorities open lines of communication with the militants.

The modus operandi of ISIS is well recorded - preying on the existing conflict areas or weakened states by recruiting disillusioned and disenfranchised members that are on the precipice of revolting violently against the government. These people readily accept extremist help in order to call upon the government to accede to their demands and gain local control, later falling prey and getting recruited. That's exactly what is occurring in Mozambique as well. One can try to defeat ISIS by calling upon all kinds of unsanctioned foreign military help but it will never be enough without pacifying the existing internal unrest that fuels their growth. Good governance is the unparalleled answer to such conflicts.
