 The confines of Parenting


Pragya Roy Barman

August 31, 2021   |   Social Change


Brought up in one of the top metropolitan cities in India, my parents raised me to be a strong woman. They let me study what I want, play with any toy that caught my interest and never censored the books I read. My parents raised me to be a strong woman, and now nineteen years later when I have come to crave independence as my fundamental right, they second guess the strength they let me wield. Does that sound familiar?



My mother has been born and raised in centre Kolkata, and, as most of my peers tell, is one of the “coolest” parents to have. And I agree. She lets me choose my career, does not intervene or obstruct my social life or encroach on my privacy. But, I am not satisfied. What we deem as privilege is the basic respect that humans ought to give other humans. Would you run up to your doctor cousin and ask them to choose acting because it brings in more money, or will you randomly snatch a friend’s phone and critique on who they are texting and what they are texting?

We have grown up on a strict diet of authoritarianism so much so that we censor ourselves in questioning things that make us uncomfortable because we are taught to “Respect elders”. Conditions are introduced in the name of unconditional love, and children are disciplined with threats of being disowned. What we consider culture, is actually systemised enforcement of principles that fail to stand the test of time.

Every daughter has been the butt of a marriage joke - “You speak like that and see how your in-laws tolerate you?” and “She is going to keep her husband on her toes with that attitude” - as if, the only thing that impacts how we live in matrimony. Keeping aside the blatant heteronormativity in this, why is it impossible for families to bring up girls without imagining a marriage in the distant future?

Every son has had their machoism drilled into the back of their brain like a staple diet. Their personality has been carefully moulded, colour-coded and labelled to be less “girly”. Their actions, especially the violent ones, have been justified with the presence of testosterone in their body. And when they dare grow into an individuality that trespasses into unfamiliar territory, they are ridiculed into compliance.

As children are made to surrender their freedom of choices in daily life, their personal growth gets compromised. Their beliefs and ideologies start to mimic that of their elders without question, they lose the power of rationalising orders, and blindly obey, not realising who they are hurting, even if it is themselves.

And all of this is carefully wrapped as idealistic traits of strength. We normalise tolerating, accepting and sacrificing to the point where raising voice against discomfort and breaking down in the face of hurt is considered a weakness. When these children grow up into adults, they continue to cater to others’ expectations and demands at the cost of themselves. Thus, a toxic cycle of hurt and hurting is born.
