 The Hundred


Achintya Newatia, Gaurav Rampuria

August 31, 2021   |   Sports


The England and Wales Cricket Board (ECB) recently launched “The Hundred”, a 100-ball tournament in England, which had been in the ECB’s plans for quite some time. In order to make the Hundred unique, the ECB has introduced a variety of new rules such as removing the concept of overs, with bowlers being allowed to bowl 5 or 10 deliveries consecutively and 20 at a maximum throughout the innings. While their attempt at innovation is commendable, the question remains: what do such unorthodox forms of the game hold for the cricket world in general. We have already seen a T10 league being played where matches were played with 10 overs per inning. These different forms of cricket calls into question whether it is appropriate to continue experimenting with different kinds of cricket, other than the three established formats - Test, ODI and T20I.



We concede that ODI and T20I were innovations in the game in their own right, but the underlying fact here is that we are altering the essence of the game by introducing such different rules and formats just for the sake of commercialisation. It is important to remember at the same time that ODIs were introduced because test cricket was dropping in relevance and similarly, T20Is were introduced because ODIs and Tests were starting to get stale for the new audience and cricket needed fresh energy. But, as of now, the T20 format hasn’t become boring at all. It’s still the most popular format and is currently in its peak. Thus, the introduction of the Hundred might be sabotaging the current formats of the game. However, we believe that it is unlikely that other countries establish a similar or another innovative form of cricket since every major cricket playing country has an established T20 League, e.g., IPL, Big Bash League, CPL, etc.

The ECB might be prioritizing commercialization and economic benefits too much over the sport itself, which is disheartening. The Hundred has been launched with the aim to attract new audiences, targeting those people who are not really fans of cricket and are unaware of the more complex rules of the game. Let’s see if it lives upto that expectation. As cricket enthusiasts, we should perhaps give the Hundred a fair try. Who knows, it might just be another form of entertaining cricket for die-hard cricket lovers, or the spark which cricket needs to burst into the global scene on a larger scale? Just like criticizing a film before watching it in a theatre is irrelevant, it is wrong to judge the tournament before watching it.
