 COVID-19 and the Indian MSMEs


Namit Mahajan

July 18, 2021   |   Business & Economics


The COVID-19 pandemic has taken a toll on all aspects of human life. There is loss all around. One of the many sectors of the economy devastated by the pandemic is the MSME, or the ‘Micro, Small, and Medium Enterprises’ sector.



63 million of these in India have around 110 million people employed, most of whom have had to bear the brunt of this pandemic, fuelled by the imposition of lockdowns, which were essential to ensure safety. The economic impact of this situation is vast, considering this sector holds 30 per cent in the total GDP of this country. It not only disrupts the supply of goods in the market but also affects the employment rates; with shops closed down and no business, many of them have had to permanently close down their businesses and shops. During the lockdown, sights of “CLOSED” and “TO-LET” signs on these enterprises increased. 83 per cent of MSMEs reported reduced revenue, 70 per cent reported reduced investment, and 48 per cent reported bringing changes to their work ways to fit into the new normal. (Source: GoDaddy)

The GDP fell by a negative of approximately 24 per cent in the second quarter of 2020. The government gave out a relief package of INR 3.7 lakh crores targeted towards the MSMEs. While this step is appreciated, it is of no avail to them, for these entrepreneurs will not be able to use this money for further investments, but will instead use it to salvage the mess that the pandemic created. They will use it for paying off the previous debts, and more essentially, to feed their families. The issues they faced, and still do- in terms of availability of employees, breaking of supply chains, and obtaining raw materials- ultimately led to liquidation for a significant number of them.

Yet, there is always hope. Karyana stores and corner shops have blossomed during this time, with major improvements to the way they work - adding services like online payment and home delivery, for only they were open during those tumultuous times, under essential services. There was also an increase in businesses dealing with textiles and chemicals, due to the boom in demand for PPEs, masks, and sanitisers.

Studies project that while it is a long road, the MSME sector is expected to get back on its feet and rise again. The growth is expected to be slow, considering the fluctuations in the market. It will take time to rebuild the supply chains, and an increase in investments, along with better policies by the government would help to rebuild the MSME sector, along with a myriad of other economic and sociopolitical variables.
