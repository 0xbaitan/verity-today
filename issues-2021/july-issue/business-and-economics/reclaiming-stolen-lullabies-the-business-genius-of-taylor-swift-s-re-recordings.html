 Reclaiming Stolen Lullabies: The Business Genius of Taylor Swift’s Re-recordings


Mohan Rajagopal

July 18, 2021   |   Business & Economics




In June 2019, the whole world woke up to the shocking news that Taylor Swift no longer owned her own music. In a surprise business move, her former record label, Big Machine Records, had sold off her entire past discography to private equity group Ithaca Holdings, founded by talent manager Scooter Braun. Over the following two years, the controversy has only gotten denser and more convoluted, requiring an in-depth analysis of the business contracts that led to the unethical sale, as well as the reaction by Swift that seems to ensure that her legacy may end up in the right hands after all.

To begin, we must go all the way back to 2005: a 15-year-old country singer from Nashville by the name of Taylor Swift has just signed a record deal with a relatively new label, Big Machine Records. Having found some success in performing at cafes and small venues across the city, the young musician is barely in a position to negotiate a proper deal with her label, as is the case with most up-and-coming artists. The resulting contract is, hence, somewhat exploitative, but nothing new in the music industry: the masters for Swift’s first six albums would belong not to her, but to BMR instead. A master is essentially the original audio recording of a particular song, from which copies are made for sale and distribution, as well as usage in other media, such as films or advertisements. Ownership of a master affords an individual the right to that specific recording of a song, implying that Swift’s albums available for streaming and purchase would belong not to her, but to BMR, owned by Scott Borchetts, whom she viewed as a father figure.

Fast forwards 13 years later, to 2018. Swift is no longer a small-town country singer, but a full-blown popstar with global fame, having won ten Grammy awards and performed sold-out stadium tours all across the planet. With the expiry of her contract with BMR, she chooses to sign a new deal with Republic Records, under Universal Music Group. While her masters and recordings may not belong to her, the actual songs (the lyrics and melody) themselves still did; the original contract ensured that Swift would still maintain writer’s rights over all her music, and since the songwriter has been adamant in writing every single song in her discography, 50% of all profits continued to go to her.

The new contract with UMG was a step-up not only for Swift herself, but for all other artists signed under the label. Now capable of leveraging her international status and fame, Swift made certain that all her future albums would belong to her absolutely. At the same time, the contract also ensured that any sale of UMG’s shares in Spotify, the largest streaming service, would result in profits (in the form of equity) for all Universal artists.

Behind the scenes, Borchetta has been gearing up for the sale of Big Machine to another company, which everyone expected to be Universal Music Group. It was thus that the acquisition of BMR by Ithaca Holdings came as a great shock to most people in the industry, especially considering Braun’s role in the company. As the manager to Kanye West and Justin Bieber, Braun has been vocal about his distaste for Swift, owing to the conflict between the artists that has lasted nearly a decade. Although all these events were perfectly legal from a business perspective, the ethical standpoint became murky when Swift claimed that she herself got to know about Braun’s purchase of her masters that morning, along with the rest of the world. In a 300 million USD deal, Braun had purchased BMR, 80% of whose profit and revenue came from Swift’s discography. The controversy was exacerbated when Bieber posted a picture with Braun later that day, captioned “my friend just bought Taylor Swift”.

In a statement on Tumblr, Swift made clear that although she was aware of BMR’s potential sale and had “made peace with it”, never in her “worst nightmares” did she believe that the buyer would be Braun, whom she accused of “incessant, manipulative bullying”. The statement also revealed that she had attempted to buy back her masters from BMR, but this option was refused to her. Instead, Borchetta offered a new 10-year contract, as per which Swift would “earn back” each of her past albums with every new one she released. However, as she no longer wished to engage in business relations with BMR, this contract was turned down. Swift’s masters being taken from her is a brilliant case study on the complicated relationship between business and morality: just because something is legally viable for a corporation, does not mean that it is the right thing to do. Her Tumblr post made it clear that parting with her past work was a source of great heartbreak:

“I had to make the excruciating choice to leave behind my past. Music I wrote on my bedroom floor and videos I dreamed up and paid for from the money I earned playing in bars, then clubs, then arenas, then stadiums. Now Scooter has stripped me of my life’s work, that I wasn’t given an opportunity to buy. Essentially, my musical legacy is about to lie in the hands of someone who tried to dismantle it. This is my worst case scenario. This is what happens when you sign a deal at fifteen to someone for whom the term ‘loyalty’ is clearly just a contractual concept.”

Nevertheless, Taylor Swift is now a household name for a reason- not only is she a talented musician, but also a clever businesswoman. Although she may have lost the rights to her masters, her retention of the writer’s rights meant that she still owned the music and lyrics of all the songs she wrote. It was only the original recording of the same that belonged to Braun; hence, a new recording would belong to her absolutely. In August 2020, she made it clear that she planned on gaining control over her old music by re-recording her past discography, creating a new set of masters intended to completely replace the ones owned by Braun. While fans were elated at the news, critics and professionals were skeptical. Losing control of one’s masters is a common tale in the music industry, with artists like Kanye West speaking out about their unsavoury contractual obligations, but few had gone to the painstaking hassle of re-recording their music. Those that had done so, had been unsuccessful in their endeavour, with the new music being ignored. What these skeptics failed to account for, however, is Swift’s huge fanbase and her hold over the general public.

In February 2021, the re-recordings era officially began with the announcement of ‘Fearless (Taylor’s Version)’, the new rendition of her 2008 breakthrough hit album ‘Fearless’. The rollout of the new music is a masterful business move which few contemporary musicians would have been able to execute properly. In order to devalue the masters owned by Braun, the new versions were kept as similar to the original as possible, in terms of lyrics, vocals, sound, and production. However, as an incentive to encourage audiences to purchase and consume the new album over the old one, she unveiled vault tracks: never-before-released songs that she had written for the albums, but had been left out for various creative or logistical reasons. In doing so, Swift ensured that her old masters would be deemed practically worthless, with no profit going to Braun, as most of the world would be streaming and purchasing the new albums to enjoy the vault tracks as well.

Needless to say, the strategy paid off. ‘Fearless (Taylor’s Version)’ topped the Billboard 200 charts, selling nearly 300k units in just one week. Meanwhile, the original album fell off the chart entirely, and its sales and streaming figures have been gradually declining. With one re-release down, there are five still waiting to be released, but it is evident that they too are gearing up to enjoy the same success. In a matter of less than two years, Swift has managed to pull off a business move that no other artist in the music industry has been capable of. Perhaps, it is now that the world begins to look up to her not just as a musician, but as a shrewd and ruthless business mogul.
