 Amrita Sher-Gil: An Artist Beyond Time


Priyanka Banerjee

July 18, 2021   |   Creativity




“Europe belongs to Picasso, Matisse, Braque and many others. India belongs only to me.” - Amrita Sher-Gil

An artist with astounding talent, sensibility and boldness, Amrita Sher-Gil is remembered for having played a pivotal role in subverting dominant ideas in twentieth-century India. Her work, which explored the then taboo topics of gender, identity and sexuality, paved the way for feminism in modern India and has empowered women across the world for generations.



Amrita Sher-Gil was born in Budapest, Hungary, on 30th January, 1913 to a unique set of artistic parents. Her father was a Sikh aristocrat, well-versed in Persian and Sanskrit literature, who met her mother, a Hungarian opera singer, just a year prior to Sher-Gil’s birth when she was visiting Lahore.



From a very young age, Sher-Gil developed a reputation for boldness. She was infamously expelled from a convent school in Shimla for proclaiming that she was an atheist, which is where her formal schooling came to an end. It is said that she was only seven years old when she first began illustrating the stories she read. In her words, Sher-Gil wrote: “Rather independent at that age, it will be of psychological interest to note that I detested the process of ‘colouring in’ the drawings of picture books. I always drew and painted everything myself and resented correction or interference with my work.”



In 1929, Sher-Gil went to one of the finest art schools of the time in Paris to develop her skill as a painter. She found interest in model painting and her most famous works boldly depict the human form. She was heavily influenced by modern aesthetics and a bohemian sub-culture while living in Paris. She became a popular artist in Europe and her works were showcased in big exhibitions in the early 1930s.

In 1935, Sher-Gil returned to India where, in some ways, she rediscovered her art all over again. This was the beginning of a certain reclamation of what it meant for her to be Indian. Of her return to India, Sher-Gil writes that she felt “haunted by an intense longing to return to India, feeling in some strange inexplicable way that there lay my destiny as a painter.” What affected art most significantly, however, was her visit to the Ajanta and Ellora caves, where the Ajanta Caves wall paintings and the sculptures of Ellora “led to a series of visual and contextual reorientation in her artistic quest.”

Sher-Gil’s work includes a number of self-portraits in various different styles of clothing, moods and expressions — both Indian and European. A recurrent theme in her art is that of the female body, which she explored in a series of famous self-portraits at a time when it was not acceptable for women to own their sexuality. By tweaking artistic expression in terms of position, placement, colours and shape, Sher-Gil used the female form in her paintings to make significant statements about herself and the choices she was making not only as an artist, but also as a woman.

The ‘Self Portrait as a Tahitian’, 1934 (pictured above), is one of Sher-Gil’s most famous and captivating self-portraits which shows her naked from the waist up. Influenced by the paintings of post-Impressionist artist Paul Gauguin, Sher-Gil uses expressive brown-shades of colour to purposefully depict her not-white body as the ‘exotic other’, thus making a powerful comment about feminism and about the Western colonial lens through which Indians were exoticised by their white rulers.

She further liberated the image of the Indian woman by depicting both the plain reality of her existence in popular works such as ‘Three Girls’, 1935, ‘Red Verandah’, 1938 and ‘Hill Women’, 1935, as well as her unique position in society as a woman of sexual confidence and individual identity in her portraits like ‘Nude’, 1931 and ‘Two Girls’, 1935. Women, for Sher-Gil, were not one-dimensional beings that could fit into neat binaries of victim or winner. She painted most of her work in bold strokes and earthly tones which was a sombre portrayal of the reality of women who, for her, are a lot like her own dynamic and complicated identity.

Sher-Gil’s Indian identity was important to her. In an article named ‘Modern Indian Art’ published in The Hindu (November 1936), Sher-Gil wrote, “The Indian art committed the mistake of feeding almost exclusively on the tradition of mythology and romance. I am an individualist evolving a new technique that though not necessarily Indian in the traditional sense of the word, will yet be fundamentally Indian in spirit.”

Sher-Gil was a loud and active advocate for the end of colonialism in India. The exploitation of Indians at the hands of the British Empire and the harsh realities of the everyday lives of rural Indians are captured in Sher-Gil’s works, where her subjects always maintain the dignity of their position as human beings. Her ‘South Indian Trilogy’ is regarded as one of her finest achievements. The three portraits of the ‘Trilogy’ titled ‘Bride’s Toilet’, ‘Brahmchari’ and ‘South Indian Villager Going to Market’ are remarkable depictions of the lives of simple, ordinary Indians living under the oppressive forces of colonialism and patriarchy.

Amrita Sher-Gil died on 5th December, 1941, at the age of 28. But in her terribly short life, Sher-Gil changed the narrative of Indian women forever with her art, her passion and her unapologetic fierceness. She can perhaps be considered one of India’s first truly sexually liberated feminists, who paved the way for a modernism in Indian art and life that unapologetically broke conventions, both in art and in life.
