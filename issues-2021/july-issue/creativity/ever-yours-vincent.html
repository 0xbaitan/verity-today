 Ever yours, Vincent


Sreemita Mukherjee

July 18, 2021   |   Creativity





Vincent presents a type of escape within his sunflowers, and almond blossoms, his field with irises and night cafés— one that is sad, beautiful, and lonely. His art presents a sense of comfort, belonging, and a type of relatability that remains intact after an entire century—the experience of loving Vincent's art is entirely personal. Vincent van Gogh's art doesn't need the intense gaze of an art historian to dissect his message, it is entirely universal.

The Vincent of the paintings is often presented as the mad, obsessed genius, his struggles and illness are often romanticized by the media made in honor of him. To say that Vincent was not any of those things would be wrong, but to say that those were his only flares would be an understatement.

Vincent was a brilliant artist but his literary prowess is seldom discussed. There remains a collection of 903 letters written by Vincent, sent to various receivers, most were sent to Theo, Vincent's brother.

The letters give us a glimpse into who Vincent was, the man behind the art. The letters are a portrait of a time long left behind, the only holding memory of a place, the testament of a profession. The letters are a journal, portraying the turbulence of his life and the building of his aesthetic.

The evidence of Vincent being tremendously passionate is in his art, the evidence of Vincent being unable to take an ounce of criticism is in his letters. Vincent was a couple of things, being understanding was not one of them. For instance, Vincent's relationship with his family had always been faulty, it is through the letters that we see that the corresponding family drama had reached its height when Van Gogh was in his late twenties. What stands out though, is the fact that throughout it all, the letters also present to us the example of a family trying their best to stay together despite their divide. Van Gogh's aforementioned inability to take criticism stems from his familial issues and is the primary reason why he would never last under any artist for too long. It is fair to say, that as loveable as his rebelliousness is on paper, the reality of his situation was quite exhausting to live with.

The letters further legitimize the fact that Van Gogh was severely inconsistent. His thoughts, ideas, and opinions changed and developed rather rapidly. For the sake of comedic purpose, Van Gogh once wrote to Theo about his love for the author, Jules Michelet. Jules Michelet was famous for having written a book on the French revolution, Vincent though was professing his love for the books Michelet wrote on how to pursue women and love, at times even comparing these books to the Bible. The Bible. He says the Bible is 'eternal' but Michelet's books give him clear instructions on how to navigate modern times.

A year later, Vincent wrote to Theo, 'I am going to destroy all my books by Michelet and I wish you would do the same.'

Vincent van Gogh's passion for art is the one thing highlighted the most within his letters to Theo. Vincent's progress in art, and his journey to finding the style he is synonymous with today, is well documented within his letters. Van Gogh had trouble being under anyone's instruction for too long, part of this could be attributed to the fact that Van Gogh was a late starter. Van Gogh still needed to learn drawing. His solution was to follow Charles Bargue's Drawing Course religiously. The letters have captured every frustration and anger he had felt whilst still learning to develop his skills. After the initial outburst, came Van Gogh's most introspective feelings about his failures. He compares his process of learning to the birth of a child—pain and then, joy.

Van Gogh had the innate ability to see the inherent beauty of things so mundane and normal. He once told his brother that life was seen through a lens of art by him. He was capable of feeling great appreciation and joy for the most mundane of things. In his letters, he's written plenty about how the beauty of nature overwhelms him. His metaphors are primarily natural processes, the hurried sketches he scribbled on the letters, the ones that reflect his art the most are mostly things that belong entirely to nature. He draws of growth and decay and seasons, of fields filled with irises and vineyards splashed with red at noon, of starry nights and olive trees, of hospitals and houses and café's he's seen—he draws of things he wants to remember. He draws of things never standing and entirely fleeting. His letters contain endless scribbles of paintings, which would later become iconic. Entire letters were simply dedicated to orders of colors and canvases, Van Gogh was ever eager to describe anything to do with art.

Van Gogh's life work was a product of a mere decade's work. His paintings were spontaneous and impulsive, and he wrote plenty about how he could paint as 'quick as lightning'. At the near end of his life, he was painting two or three canvases a day. Van Gogh's letters also show that he understood quite a lot about his illness. In a time wherein much was not known about mental illnesses, Van Gogh recognized that he needed help. That his illness revealed itself in episodes, that he needed to be in isolation when these episodes occurred and that his well being could be assured if he kept himself engrossed within work.

Van Gogh's last letter to Theo talks of the ability of painters to solely communicate through the art they produce. Van Gogh praises Theo for being one of the only art dealers he knows who works with humanity, who understands the essence of art. He signs the letter off the same way he had for all the proceeding ones, only this time, it would prove to be his last. He bid his brother goodbye with a simple, known — Ever Yours, Vincent.
