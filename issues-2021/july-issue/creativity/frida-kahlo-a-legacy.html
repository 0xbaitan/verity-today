 Frida Kahlo: A Legacy


Ishani Datta and Arshia Goyal

July 18, 2021   |   Creativity





Mexican artist Frida Kahlo is remembered for her self-portraits, pain and passion, and bold, vibrant colors. She is celebrated in Mexico for her attention to Mexican and indigenous culture and by feminists for her depiction of the female experience and form.

Kahlo, who suffered from polio as a child, nearly died in a bus accident as a teenager. She suffered multiple fractures of her spine, collarbone and ribs, a shattered pelvis, broken foot and a dislocated shoulder. She began to focus heavily on painting while recovering in a body cast. In her lifetime, she had 30 operations.



Frida Kahlo’s Art Background

Life experience is a common theme in Kahlo's approximately 200 paintings, sketches and drawings. Her physical and emotional pain are depicted starkly on canvases, as is her turbulent relationship with her husband, fellow artist Diego Rivera, who she married twice. Of her 143 paintings, 55 are self-portraits.



Kahlo's first self-portrait was Self-Portrait in a Velvet Dress in 1926. It was painted in the style of 19th Century Mexican portrait painters who themselves were greatly influenced by the European Renaissance masters. She also sometimes drew from the Mexican painters in her use of a background of tied-back drapes. Self-Portrait - Time Flies (1929), Portrait of a Woman in White (1930) and Self-Portrait Dedicated to Leon Trotsky (1937) all bear this background.

In her second-self portrait, "Time Flies," Kahlo uses a folk style and vibrant colors. She wears peasant clothing, and the red, white and green in the painting are the colors of the Mexican flag. During her life, self portrait is a subject that Frida Kahlo always returns to, as artists have always returned to their beloved themes - Vincent Van Gogh, his Sunflowers, Rembrandt, his Self Portrait, and Claude Monet, his Water Lilies.



The 1937 painting Memory, the Heart, shows Kahlo's pain over her husband's affair with her younger sister Christina. A large broken heart at her feet shows the intensity of Kahlo's anguish. Frida Kahlo and Diego Rivera divorced in 1939, but reunited a year later and remarried.



The Two Fridas (1939) depicts Kahlo twice, shortly after the divorce. One Frida wears a costume from the Tehuana region of Mexico, representing the Frida that Diego loved. The other Frida wears a European dress as the woman who Diego betrayed and rejected. Later, she is back in Tehuana dress in Self-Portrait as a Tehuana (1943) and Self Portrait (1948).

Frida Kahlo and her impact on fashion:

There's no doubt that Kahlo expressed herself through her art but her avant-garde style also represented her bold personality. From extravagant floral headdresses and accessories to sharp, constructed suits, her style was boundless. She would beautifully ecorporate both traditional Mexican and European elements of clothing. Tehuana dresses, huipil tops (each having its own intricate design), long embroidered skirts and robezo scarves were a recurring part of her wardrobe. Her Mexican culture played an important role in her style, the tehuana dresses being worn by the indeginous women of Oaxaca state, Mexico and the huipil tops, common in Mayan groups.The robezo scarves were used by female revolutionaries during the Mexican revolution to smuggle guns in order to end the decades long dictatorship of Porfirio Diaz and became symbolic of the revolution.



In a letter to her mother, Frida says, “The gringas really like me a lot and pay close attention to all the dresses and rebozos that I brought with me, their jaws drop at the sight of my jade necklaces.”



Kahlo had a way of turning tragedy into something beautiful through her art, in the same way, her style that was adapted to cover her disabilities has been beautifully adapted into many runway lines in the modern day.

Designers are not unknown to channeling an artist’s creativity, their passion and their art, give it their own form and expression and make it a driving source of their inspiration. Frida Kahlo’s style has inspired a lot of designers, some of them include Alexander McQueen, Jean Paul Gaultier and Caroline Herrera.



Dolce and Gabbana’s 2018 Alta Moda collection elegantly depicts Frida Kahlo’s style, featuring flower headdresses to the bright pink and green robezos worn by the models as they walked down the runway accompanied by the music of mariachi classics.This collection had everything you could ask for, from fringed jackets to sequined dresses to absolutely regal gold embroidered garments.



Riccardo Tisci showcased Frida’s struggles in his Givenchy Fall 2010 Couture line. He highlighted the bones of the spinal cord and the rib cage by using beaded embroidery, the zippers were designed like little bones and even the belt was shaped like the spinal cord.

Naeem Khan’s Spring/Summer 2014 Ready-to-wear collection had a Latin/Spanish backdrop. His inspiration for this collection included everything from flamenco dancers to Frida Kahlo. The models wore braided hair and the creative vibrant patterns were tokens of Frida's influence.



In Rouland Mouret’s Spring/Summer 2018 show, he celebrated Frida Kahlo for her raw expression of women’s lives and Georgia O’Keeffe as she rejected the idea of fear even though she herself was not fearless and her paintings contained an element of femininity and seductiveness.

Frida Kahlo’s impact was international and strong traces of her work and style can be seen clearly today. A true visionary in her own right, she paved the way for artists of the generations that came after her. Art is boundless, and so is the inspiration of their creators, and it is something that should always be celebrated and respected.
