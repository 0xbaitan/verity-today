 Blue Velvet - Lynch’s Convoluted Masterpiece


Pradyumn Nahata

July 18, 2021   |   Entertainment




A few days ago, I decided to start watching David Lynch’s movies, considering the amount I’d heard of the singularity of his filmography and the exhilarating noir surrealism that was ubiquitous in his work. I went with Blue Velvet.

Blue Velvet is a downright strange movie, and its characters don’t fail to admit that they live in a strange story world. Blue Velvet is possibly Lynch’s most famous work, which gave him a boost in confidence and vision that inspired him to design weirder, more fascinating films in his career. The film starts with a rendition of Blue Velvet by Bobby Rinton (the song which inspired the title and plays in such a mystical flair throughout the 2 hour feature’s sequences) with curtains of blue velvet at the backdrop.

The sequence proceeds to a North Carolina town called Lumberton, which serves as a satirical display of suburban safety through the viewing. As the enigmatic music unfolds in its gradual romance, we see a middle-aged man watering his garden suffer a near-fatal fit which results in unforeseen hospitalization. Lynch begins his meandering horror from this very scene, establishing the rules of the game: anything can happen. We find out that this man is Raymond Beaumont, and his son, Jeffrey Beaumont, has come back from school to secure his relatives and his father’s hardware store in the absence of Raymond. However, both of these entities rarely need him, and Jeffrey has most time to himself.

On the way back from a hospital visit; as Jeffrey wanders about in his roused manner, he sees something very eerie locked from sight by surrounding blades of green grass. He bends to examine the Macguffin. It's a human ear. Thus begins the director’s Oedipean tale of sex, drugs and depravity, as Jeffrey unrestrictedly goes on a mission to find out what mystery the detached ear is embedded in. This he does through the help of his blossoming next-door neighbour Sandy, who is the teenage daughter of a local detective. They soon find out a link in the labyrinth. She has a name, and it’s Dorothy Vallens, an opera singer who works nights at a club literally called “The Slow Club”.

The plot picks up from here, as Jeffrey gets entangled in an unhinged world through the unhinged Dorothy Vallens, a woman who is pure emotion, raw sex and the depraved portal of the movie’s deliberate peculiarities. In a very controversial scene, as Jeffrey watches Dorothy and an abusive man (Dorothy’s torturer, the antagonist Frank Booth) play out incestous roleplay of masochistic sadism from the gaps in the closet door, the boundaries of the narrative are blurred.

The movie is a juxtaposition in many ways. Lynch juxtaposes the exuberant innocence of Sandy’s utopia and the degenerate vice of Dorothy’s world in a subtle design, and inserts our protagonist in both, as we figure out with him the inner fabrics of both worlds, one more elusive and enticing than the other. This in a way is what the movie is about: the young hero’s expedition into the depths of irrationality to resolve the questions of morality and purpose. In the movie’s most thematically expository line, when Sandy asks Jeffrey why he’s getting himself mixed up in all this, Jeffrey replies, “I'm seeing something that was always hidden. I'm in the middle of a mystery and it's all secret.” This echoes the Oedepian nature of the narrative. If you don’t know who Oedipus was, check Oedipus Rex out by Sophocles. It’s an ancient Greek tragedy, where a youth called Oedipus fated to kill his father and marry his mother, in the process of running from the destinal curse, unknowingly ends up fulfilling his fate. It’s weird, it really is, but it’s also intriguing and thematic. Much like David Lynch’s works.

The critical opinions on Blue Velvet are also very polarized, with some critics hailing it as a film ahead of its age, masterfully crafted and unbelievably raw, while others labelling it pretentious and senseless. For instance, Roger Ebert gave it 1 out of 4 stars, perplexed as to what the movie even means, and why it’s so amoral. It’s tough to pick a side. Blue Velvet is a film too engrossed in itself, spending half of its runtime on exposition and the other half on climactic revelations and resolutions. The ending, too, is disappointing and anti-climactic. However, all those rational criticisms aside, Blue Velvet is a genius product of intricate filmmaking- imaginative and essential as a deviant approach to what a Hollywood film really entails. It creates suspense and keeps the viewer hooked, as though asking us to make sense of what’s happening. While it never delivers the answer to the question, it makes the question so urgent and allusive that the viewer can’t help but be impressed by how absurd and alluring the film is. And in my opinion, that’s all that matters.
