 Olivia Rodrigo’s SOUR: A Sweet Debut Into Mainstream Pop


Mohan Rajagopal

July 18, 2021   |   Entertainment




Unless you’ve been living under a rock for the past six months, you probably already know who Olivia Rodrigo is. The Disney actress starring in ‘High School Musical: The Musical: The Series’ exploded into fame in January 2021 with the release of her debut single ‘driver’s license’, which peaked the Billboard Hot 100 chart and went viral across the entire planet. Her two follow up singles also landed in the top 10 of the chart, and her music industry takeover has only been exacerbated by the release of her first studio album, titled ‘SOUR’.

In past interviews, Rodrigo had spoken about how the release was initially meant to be a much shorter EP, which she extended to a full-length album following the success of ‘driver’s license’. Initially, I found this concerning, as it seemed like she would be adding songs to the project at the last-minute which didn’t deserve to be on the tracklist. However, with the very first listen to the album, it’s clear that there isn’t a single song that feels like a filler track. With ‘SOUR’, Rodrigo has crafted the perfect ode to teenagedom in all its angst, heartbreak, mood swings and messiness. The very first track, ‘brutal’, is well placed at the start of the album, as it sets the bitter and furious tone for the rest for the release. “I want it to be, like, messy”, she quips, before accelerating into rock-inspired heavily distorted guitar riffs, loud drums, and screaming vocals. “I’m so sick of seventeen / where the hell’s my teenage dream?”, she sings in light of the disillusionment of being an adolescent in the modern world. The alternative rock and punk sounds drive home the general theme of dissatisfaction and rage experienced by most teens.

The primary plot throughout the album, however, explores Rodrigo’s relationship to an unnamed boy, who soon left her to date another girl. Although the paparazzi and internet at large have made several assumptions about who these individuals are, it is not necessary to discuss the relationship drama while examining the quality of the songs themselves. ‘Traitor’, the second song on the album, begins the relationship narrative. Lush with ghostly vocals and simple instrumentals, the singer-songwriter details the betrayal she felt when she was left behind by her ex, even though he had not explicitly engaged in an illicit affair; “guess you didn’t cheat / but you’re still a traitor.” Undoubtedly, the climax of the song appears in the bridge, where the full emotional scope of Rodrigo’s pain comes through as she spits out every word in bitter rage and hurt. The emotion is clearly audible in her voice- a talent which even seasoned artists find hard to channel into their recordings- and transforms ‘traitor’ from just another breakup song to a standout track.

The track ‘good 4 u’ is another song with a similar style to ‘brutal’, laced with sarcasm and passive aggression as she clinically deconstructs her ex’s indifference to their breakup: “you look happy and healthy, not me // I’ve lost my mind, I’ve spent the night crying on the floor of my bathroom”. As the third single in the album release cycle, it was a perfect choice, as it cemented Rodrigo’s status as not a mere one-hit wonder, but a developing artist capable of putting out hit after hit of varying sounds, styles, and diversities. ‘Happier’ brims with all the pettiness that can be expected in the aftermath of a relationship, as she sings about her past lover’s new girlfriend. While she wishes for him to find joy in the future, she selfishly hopes that it would never match up her own experiences with him: “So find someone great but don't find no one better/ I hope you're happy, but don't be happier”. Once again, she displays a tenacity for switching from one musical genre to another, as ‘happier’ is almost waltz-like, with a swinging beat and a piano sequence that sounds like it’s straight out of a 1950’s ballroom.

As would be expected, Rodrigo has come under fire for writing “too much” about her breakup- a sexist criticism which seems to be a rite of passage for all women in the music industry. ‘Jealousy, jealousy’, however, is the epitome of the inaccuracy of this baseless accusation, perfectly summarising adolescence in the age of social media. The song explores the envy and inadequacy felt when comparing oneself to the “girls too good to be true/ With paper-white teeth and perfect bodies”. Personally, it’s my favourite track on the album, and also the one that is the most relatable. Sleek with a sultry bass guitar riff and vocals that are nearly shouting in the bridge, the track presents an anthem of rebellion against the facetuned and photoshopped illusions of curated fantasies that pollute and infest Instagram and Snapchat alike.

Perhaps the only miss on the album (for me) is the final song, ‘hope ur ok’. Closing tracks hold an important place in an artist’s discography: it’s the final statement that they put out within the current body of work, until a few years later when the next release comes out. As such, I prefer these tracks to be personal and intimate. In the case of ‘SOUR’, however, ‘hope ur ok’ is the only track that doesn’t deal with Rodrigo herself, instead delving into homophobia and domestic violence. A heartwarming track of solidarity and allyship, it would have been perfect anywhere else in the tracklist, but falls short as the final track of an album which has so closely followed the inside of the mind of a teenage girl for ten tracks, only to jump out and extrospect.

Overall, ‘SOUR’ is a brilliant debut album, especially for a seventeen-year-old. In just a week of its release, it has broken several records, topping the Billboard 200 albums chart, and logging the highest streaming week on Global Spotify. The chaotic ricochets from loud, rock-inspired electric guitars to tear-jerking piano ballads serve as a perfect metaphor for the instability and intricacy of teenage life, a phase that is often dismissed by adults by being immature and trivial. Olivia centres the trivial in a blend of melodrama and emotion, crafting a superb package of fluorescent adolescence.

“Who am I if not exploited?” she screams out in the opening track. Sure, there’s moments of joy and satisfaction in juvenile life, but modernity has failed the youth; the overwhelming experience is one that is bittersweet, and rather sour.
