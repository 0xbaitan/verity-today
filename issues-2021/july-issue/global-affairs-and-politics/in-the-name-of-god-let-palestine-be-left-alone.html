  “In The Name Of God, Let Palestine Be Left Alone”


Priyanka Banerjee

July 18, 2021   |   Global Affairs & Politics




The International Convention on the Suppression and Punishment of the Crime of Apartheid defines apartheid as a series of inhuman acts that are “committed for the purpose of establishing and maintaining domination by one racial group of persons over any other racial group of persons and systematically oppressing them.” Moreover, the State of Israel ticks nearly every box in that definition.

Israel was established on 14th May, 1948, to create a national homeland for the Jews fleeing persecution in Europe. This, however, forced the native Arab population of Palestine to escape their country, leaving behind their homes, their land and all their property. By 1949, almost 80% of the Arab population of Israel had fled the country and over 720,000 Palestinians had become stateless refugees. Nonetheless, a considerable part of the Arab population stayed on, interrupting the fundamental structure of the new Jewish state and, effectively becoming Israel’s chief crisis. As a result, the leaders of the state were forced to present a racist settler-colonial apartheid regime as a liberal democratic nation that could protect the rights of all people and be, as they claimed, “both Jewish and democratic.”

Today, Palestinians have no right to return to the homeland they were forcibly removed from (although any Jew from any part of the world has the right to obtain citizenship in Israel), no right to reclaim the property they had to leave behind while fleeing their country, no right to self-determination, and they are also legally prohibited, as an ethnic minority in Israel, from observing any cultural traditions to commemorate the loss of their homeland. Israel does not merely work towards making the colonised Palestinians socially inferior and irrelevant, its racism is written into the policies of the nation. The discrimination is sanctioned by the the State’s most fundamental legal documents. To establish that Israel indeed is an apartheid state, a comparison can be drawn with South Africa, where the term originates.

Apartheid is an Afrikaans word meaning separateness. Across the three centuries over which European colonialism laid down the structure of apartheid in South Africa, racial discrimination and exclusion took from the indigenous people their land, their rights, and their freedom. They were forced to work as labourers under inhumane conditions, they were beaten or killed if they so much as spoke up and they were treated no better than slaves by settler-colonial foreigners in their homeland.

In 1948, apartheid was introduced as a policy to systemize this racism by the National Party. Under this policy, the indigenous people were politically suppressed, systemically incarcerated and territorially segregated, forced to live in ‘bantustans’ which were residential areas set aside for the natives. This area included only thirteen percent of the total land area of South Africa even though it was meant to be occupied by the indigenous people who formed the majority of the population and for whom this land was their home.

In South Africa, reserved land belonged exclusively to the natives. In Israel, while Palestinian citizens can only inhabit parts of the land and the rest belongs entirely to the Jews, there is no law that forbids the settlers from also occupying the land that has been set aside for Palestinians. Therefore, Israel’s land division and distribution, by people who have no real claim over it, is even more discriminatory and burdens the Palestinians more than South African laws did the natives of the country.

Israel’s citizenship laws can very easily be compared to South African inter-racial marriage laws and to policies that racially segregated the people, While Israel does not prohibit inter-ethnic marriages, its laws make family life very difficult by assigning different areas of residency to the Jews and non-Jews of the nation. These laws are enacted in an attempt to discourage interbreeding of the colonisers and the indigenous people. In Israel, like South Africa, social and cultural practices are certainly factors enabling apartheid. But it is the institutionalisation of this racial discrimination and segregation by a state-sponsored and state-sanctioned method of oppression that makes Israel an apartheid state in 2021, decades after the world collectively demanded the end of apartheid rule in South Africa. Desmond Tutu, a South African anti-apartheid and human rights activist, drew a comparison between the two countries that tells us how similar both the apartheid regimes are:

“I have witnessed the racially segregated roads and housing in the Holy Land that reminded me so much of the conditions we experienced in South Africa under apartheid… The systemic humiliation of Palestinian men, women, and children...is familiar to all black South Africans who were corralled, harassed, insulted, and assaulted by the security forces of the apartheid government.”

In the history of colonialism, whenever settler-colonial states were unable to wipe out the native population, the colonial regime worked to make them politically irrelevant. Israel, as a settler-colonial and apartheid regime, has worked no differently since its very establishment as a nation and continues to treat its Palestinian citizens as second-class citizens on their native land. Israel’s laws continue to create a divide between its Jewish and non-Jewish citizens, often erasing Palestinians as a people altogether even though they form a significant section of the country’s population. It is essential to oppose and stand up against an oppressive racist regime and its war crimes and to speak out against the discrimination and suppression of an entire people, which has been perpetuated for nearly a century.
