  Cats Are Liquid: A Gameplay Exploration of Grief and Depression


Mohan Rajagopal

July 18, 2021   |   Mental Health


If you know anything about me, it's that I adore music, so much so that you can constantly find me wearing a pair of headphones, humming along to some song. Usually, though, I’m too restless to sit still while listening to music, so I try to find some simple games on the app store that I can mindlessly tap and play through to only give my fingers an avenue to fidget. One such game I downloaded a few months ago was the ‘Cats Are Liquid’ series, which seemed like a simple platformer game on the surface. But the moment I began playing it, it became clear that there was a much darker and significant storyline lurking beneath.

Unfortunately, I have been unable to find much discourse on this game online. For this reason, I had to piece together the plot points of the game. The series comprised two games: ‘A Light In The Shadows’, and ‘A Better Place’. Although ALITS was the first game to be released, it is agreed amongst the fanbase that ABP is a prequel to the original game, despite being released afterwards.



Both games feature simple gameplay whereby the protagonist, a cat called Lumi, needs to manoeuvre her way through various obstacles. In ABP, Lumi is joined by three other cat friends. Multiple dialogue boxes scattered across the levels subtly reveal that the cats had an owner who had died. Thus, leaving them behind in total shock. In the wake of grief, the four friends created the world of the ABP. It was done to help each other through their grief and eventually move on out into the real world.

As the levels progress, each of the friends can recover and leave the makeshift until Lumi is the only one left behind. Unfortunately, she is unable to get over the loss of her owner. At the end of the game, she decides to lock herself into a new world of her creation.

ALITS begins in this new world, although Lumi seems to have no recollection of the events in the previous game. She claims that she has been locked in the world by the owner. The game features 90 levels spread across nine worlds. Each of them ends with its own distinct “neon level”, where we can see the cat conversing with another unnamed and unspecified group, assumed to be her friends from the first game. Through the course of the neon levels, her friends plead with Lumi to return to the real world:

“You just have to try to move on. We know that it’ll be difficult, but you have to try. It’s the only way out. We know that dealing with a close friend's death can be difficult… We were there with you. You have to try to move on. Staying in a world where death has no consequences doesn’t help.”

With the neon levels, Lumi’s intentions become clear: the game breaks the fourth wall here. Acknowledging that “death has no consequences” as you can respawn at the start of the level and try again. Lumi, too, is aware of this and has chosen to trap herself in a deathless world in an attempt to distance herself from the grief at her owner’s death. As she progresses through ALITS, she repeatedly comments on how the rooms seem to be forming endless loops, having forgotten that it was her own doing. Although she claims she wants to make it out of the “claustrophobic world”, the final level sees her suddenly change her mind, no doubt an effect of the vow she took at the end of ABP to stay locked inside the world, and never leave: “Everything is so much better in here. Everything can be fixed. Nobody will die. All that happens is that the room will be reset. Everything can always be fixed. I want to start over. Do this all again. Over and over again. So that I never have to leave.”

It is at this point that the main game ends. With this, a new world is unlocked, called “The Reset”. Here, the levels are backwards numbered, starting from 10 and counting down to 1, implying that Lumi is making her way back to the start of the world to restart the game in a loop. Within The Reset, Lumi finally talks to her dead owner, who, like her friends, encourages Lumi to let go of her hurt and move on: “You can’t blame me for my own death. It was clearly an accident. Nothing could’ve been done to prevent it.”

When I first saw this game on the Play Store, it seemed utterly whimsical and ridiculous, although it soon turned out to be nothing of the sort, and I am glad it did not. It is rare for any mainstream media, especially video games, to tackle such heavy topics. Such overarching representation can only go far in the public approach to mental health. The entire game stands as a metaphor for the process of working through grief and the importance of having a proper support system in place to help you through it, as represented by Lumi’s friends.

However, the key message of the game is that the final decision to move on can't be influenced by anyone else. It must be taken by oneself. Moreover, this decision comes at a different pace for everyone: some, like the friends, can accept the circumstances quicker, while others like Lumi find it harder to do so.

No one can be blamed for the death and the emotions that follow. The game serves to be a cautionary tale to its players. It is important to take your time to heal completely and accept help when it is offered, no matter how difficult and improper it may seem. While it may seem ideal to wallow in sorrow for the rest of one’s life, the best step that can be taken, for both the deceased and the bereaved, is to move on into a better place.
