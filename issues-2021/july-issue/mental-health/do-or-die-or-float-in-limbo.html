 Do Or Die… Or Float In Limbo


Niharika Rajsaha

June 1, 2021   |   Mental Health


For a long time, society had drawn a clear line between what professions were careers and what were mere hobbies. On the one hand, those with intensive foundations rooted in studies, employment stability and lucrative salaries were considered careers. On the other hand, those that require a studio apartment to live in till thirty without workplace savings plans (401Ks) were considered stepping stones. More intuitively, a ‘year off’ before they start a ‘proper career’.



After years of advocating, researchers have found substantial evidence that music and art are just as important to this world as finance and computers. However, despite the changes that have come about, inherent and orthodox beliefs about different aspects of life still surface, albeit in a more hidden and implicit way. These days, rather than antagonising the unconventional career itself, people urge students to push themselves into the more mainstream ones out of fear. It is the fear of failure, instability, and the inability to live life on their own.

Our journey through self-discovery starts at school. A large part of our personalities is shaped in these years, regardless of whether we enjoyed our experiences. Those who enjoyed the company of their peers know a little about the general kind of people they are comfortable around than those who weren't aware whether they felt comfort or discomfort around other people.

Our life goals come within the purview of our interests in a particular subject during these years. However, school and education curricula, due to the need for uniformity, are rigid. Examinations and syllabi are set, in a way, to primarily influence decisions as to what students may like to pursue in their higher education. However, due to the unscalable depth of information and peer influence from others who stick with mainstream professions, our education has often edged along a specific curve.

There is a school of thought that considers certain subjects to be of common interest. Hence, institutions deem them as necessary while labelling others as extra-curricular. Even in the subjects themselves, there are well-defined but restrictive domains of study material that only caters to few topics. For example, our history textbooks teach us about how the reign of Louis XIV was prominent as France reached heights in terms of economic prosperity and flourished in art forms such as music and art. However, there is a bare minimum mention of the Baroque period which took place around the same time. The mention of the rise of legends like Bach and Vivaldi who introduced the concepts of sonata and concerto which are still very prevalent today remain absent in our textbooks.

The inadvertent prioritisation of subjects fuels the idea that some careers or academic subjects require more nurturing and effort than others. It inevitably raises self-doubt and anxiety among those who wish to pursue careers with little to no representation at career counselling. It is common to feel like such careers are insignificant and fruitless when there is no growth or popularity. Combined with the constant rise in unemployment and poverty, fear is only natural among an already distressed youth. This ‘do or die’ complex has driven away so many from their dreams of pursuing something that they loved with their heart and soul.

A survey conducted on over 10,000 students (between the ages of 14 and 21) in India showed that over 70% of those students were only aware of seven career options. The options included medicine, computer and IT technology, law, finance, engineering, design and management. India has over 250 career options, which are inclusive of 5000 job types. It shows that children are not just scared to pursue their calling. It also highlights their breadth of knowledge about different options is only limited to safe and conventional ones.

What rests in the unknown is a commonplace of fear among teenagers and adults alike. We, as a species, like to have things figured out. It is the reason why conversations regarding ‘your plan after school’ are exasperating. Most teenagers take a long time, ridden with anxiety over the wrong choice and fear of regret, to figure out what they want to do in life even with all the information laid out in front of them. When crucial information is held back, especially regarding a potential career choice that may be appealing but assures a lot less certainty when it comes to success, it drives anxiety amongst people who do not see themselves fitting into any of the options laid before them. When this happens, people start settling for what they may have been taught to be the safest option. Eventually, the crippling fear of failure subsides into a dull ache that lingers between confusion and regret.

There is a growing acceptance of humanity’s need for millions of different professions. Professions that were once hobbies can be pursued and still be considered significant. It does not eliminate the need for doctors or engineers. It simply integrates the importance of every career such that there is a deserved equity of respect and importance of every profession.
