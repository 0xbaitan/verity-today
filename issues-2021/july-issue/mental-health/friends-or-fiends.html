 Friends or Fiends?


Vaishali Sarah Mundle

July 18, 2021   |   Mental Health


Friends. Our chosen family. They are there for fun and laughter, as well as being there through difficult times. We share things with friends which we are not comfortable sharing with our family. It is no surprise to us that friends are, in fact, a vital part of our lives. Be it just an acquaintance or our closest friend, they all affect us, but in different ways and magnitudes.



As human beings, it is natural to have conflicts with people you love. Of course, all of us occasionally get into a quarrel or two with the best of our friends. However, the real question is, when does it start to get toxic? When and how are you supposed to identify that this “friendship” is doing no good to any of you?

There is a simple answer to that. When you start feeling that you are being held back by your friend, that is the moment when you must realise that something needs to be done urgently. It is very emotionally draining to have someone weigh you down. In most cases, you probably will not even realise that it is the person that you believe to be your close friend who is weighing you down. It is precisely where the complications arise. We are so blindsided by the fact that our friend could never possibly do any harm to us, that we lose ourselves in the process. Toxic friendships are more common than we think. We just fail to identify it.

In a utopian setting, any relationship, be it romantic or platonic, must always have an equal balance. It means that there should be initiation and effort from both individuals. They should be enough to keep the relationship going without mentally draining one person out. Unfortunately, the world is not perfect, neither are we. Therefore, we cannot always expect that kind of balance at all times. What can we expect then? Well, ask yourself, what do you expect from your friends? Your company is supposed to make you feel good. They are supposed to tell you if they sense you are going wrong somewhere and respect your decisions. They are supposed to make you feel motivated, not drained out and frustrated. It works both ways; you give, you receive. That is how simple it is. Friendship is no rocket science after all!

Now coming to toxic friends, we’ve heard this term several times in our life. So, who is a toxic friend, and how harmful can they be?

A “friend” who does not make you feel loved or supported. One who makes you feel insecure and even questions your identity. They disregard all your problems and expect you to understand theirs. They will use you in their times of need and forget about you when you need them the most. It is the suffocating toxicity that some “friends” have to offer that these are only a few of the things that such people generally do. They proceed to call you their “bestie” to your face while they snicker behind your back or not even try to hide the fact that they are toxic.

What can a toxic company possibly do to our mental health? For starters, it feels like all the energy has been leeched from us; however, we cannot tell how. Even if we can, we do not have the courage or energy to stand up against our wrongdoers. Talking to that individual feels like a mammoth task. At the same time, we feel guilty for not interacting enough with them. We lose our self-esteem, confidence and blame ourselves for what is happening. Its impact is more significant than what we may believe.

One may suffer from anxiety or depression, owing to a toxic friend solely. In severe cases of gaslighting and mental abuse, one may even reach a point of suffering from post-traumatic stress disorder. As for cases involving body shaming, victims get pushed into eating disorders. It not only aggravates their mental health but also their physical health.

Now that we're aware of the severity of having a toxic company, how do we identify and act on it?

First, we should gather our thoughts and reflect upon what WE feel, not the other person. For once, prioritise ourselves over others. Identify the issue and then come to the main question.

To stay or to leave?

Ask yourself, is all this mental abuse worth this friendship? One way of figuring it out is clear communication and working things out like mature individuals rather than internalising and making things worse. Even after repeated second chances, if you still do not feel secure or supported, then there is, sadly, only one way out. Distance yourself, for your good. It is not necessary to end on bad terms and completely cut each other off. The distance with the space you require to breathe is what you need. The change will be gradual and liberating. Coming from a person who recently got out of a stiflingly toxic and problematic friendship, I can positively claim that it is one of the best feelings one can feel.

Yes, you will inevitably miss the good moments, but sometimes, it is just better to let go of your well-being.

On this note, I present to you a typical Pinterest quote: “Don’t let getting lonely make you reconnect with toxic people. You shouldn’t drink poison just because you’re thirsty”.

As much of an angsty teenager quote, it sounds, it is true. As I said earlier, friends are like your chosen family, your escape from many of your problems, so choose the ones who will stay throughout as your hype-people, not as energy-draining leeches.
