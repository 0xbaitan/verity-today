 It’s All About You: Eating Disorders and the Self


Aarna Garg, Anayaa Agarwal and Tritee Basu

July 18, 2021   |   Mental Health


An underlying feeling that overwhelmingly shapes our self-image and influences our behaviour could be called our insecurity. Do you ever find yourself filled with self-doubt, lack of confidence and uncertainty?



Insecurity is a feeling which you have that is often accompanied by a critical inner voice and manifests in the form of our anxiety or uncertainty as self-deprecating words. We fear that others will judge us.

Hence, before they do, we start looking for flaws in ourselves. At times we may doubt our appearance, style, personality, the way we talk, and sometimes we question our very existence.

Some of us worry that we are not where we would like to be in our personal or professional lives. Others feel that they are too incompetent to pursue a demanding career or worry that their appearance lacks a certain quality they might find in others.

People are critical of themselves for not meeting a certain standard or level of achievement set by others. Nowadays, most individuals on social media only display what they want others to see — a pretence that masquerades the reality beneath it. With vague ideas or guesses, we compare ourselves to them and question if we are good enough to reach where they are in life.

We are either being compared to someone or put in a position to compare ourselves to one another. Judgment perpetually follows this and often causes us to dwell in our insecurities.

Insecurities cause mental health issues, depression, low self-esteem, and anxiety. Being insecure is not simply self-assessment. While there is always room for improvement, self-criticism and self-doubt in all matters is not the right way.

At some point in life, almost everyone has insecurities. Although, there is a fine line between being insecure and self-assessment. What being insecure means is that your insecurities control your life, and they take over your personality.

All you see are your insecurities. Without even making an effort to change yourself, you decide you are not good enough. Meanwhile, having self-doubts is just a part of you and neither is it your identity.

When you tend to improve yourself and work on it, you remind yourself of your strengths, skills, and talents. Being insecure turns into a self-sabotaging mechanism.

Self-assessment allows us to grow because we notice our flaws and tend to work on them.

On the other hand, by being insecure, we are consumed by our flaws which do not allow us to reach our full potential as our inner critical voice says it is not good enough.

An eating disorder is not simply just not eating. People often comment how “it's no big deal just to eat”, but it is hard for them to comprehend how damaging this phrase can be. They say that “it is a choice, not an actual illness”. It is when a person starts developing unhealthy habits like obsession with food, body weight or shape. We have to watch out for controlling calories or comparing our bodies to standards set by society.

It is crucial to identify symptoms of an eating disorder or even the start of it. If failed to stop, the person might live an extremely unhealthy life that could affect their social and professional lives. Unfortunately, it is also associated with an extremely high mortality rate.

There are three main types of eating disorders:

  • Anorexia nervosa: a result of extreme restriction of calories.

  • Bulimia nervosa: binge eating, eating very little or even adequate amounts followed by purging.

  • Binge eating disorder: regular consumption of large portions of food even without having an appetite.

In these times, we see millions of people on social media with millions of likes on their pictures or having a large following. Inadvertently, we notice the body type they possess and believe that this is how it should be, but accepting this only makes you unhappy.

Society makes us believe that a thinner body is the ideal body, but that is not it. The biggest lie that we have been told on multiple occasions is that a thinner you is superior. But in reality, it is just simply a different version of you, not a happier one. People might judge you on your weight or how you look, but in all honesty, it does not matter.

As long as you believe in yourself, are satisfied with what you eat, like how you look, other people’s opinions should not matter. The person commenting about you is just simply saying it, but that does not mean that it is how you look, because in the end, what matters is what rests inside you; how you see yourself.

Do not let someone else's judgment take away your happy years. It is not worth it. At first glance, we might notice someone’s body and immediately realise what is inside them. It is their personality that catches our attention. Because in the end, people do not remember your weight but what you were like as a person. The weight scale or how people see you do not affect your beauty, strength, purpose or possibilities.

Our society has set a very high standard on how one's body should look. This high standard is hard to achieve and may sometimes be unachievable too. The media and social media constantly swamp us with images of ‘idealised bodies’. For women, the emphasis is on slimness, youthfulness and flawless skin. For men, the ideal body is characterised by a muscular, V-shaped body and a flat stomach. And these utopic standards of beauty have become the norm to which many of us compare ourselves.

Many studies have shown that in this modern age of social media, the vast majority of people on these platforms are unhappy or dissatisfied with their body image. Recently, the focus has moved from a general and temporary dissatisfaction to a more serious aspect of how one’s body image can influence our mental health.

Although body image concerns are indirectly linked to mental health concerns, they can cause mental health problems and issues over time and unhealthy behaviours that include eating disorders. Issues that can emerge as a result of poor body image include depression, anxiety, lower self-esteem, social isolation.

However, we have to remember that society came up with the idea of the ‘ideal body type', referring to what is appealing to their eyes. Maintaining a healthy body is necessary. However, prioritising your mental health is also of utmost importance. Fortunately, many psychologists, medics and other professionals worldwide work to support people with poor body image. But until the causes are eliminated or at least reduced, the problem remains and is becoming increasingly urgent.

“I had a moment the other day that really changed my perspective on how I view my physical appearance. I released that no one has ever impacted my being beautiful or having the perfect body. It might get my initial attention or be inspiring but it's never the reason I remember them. I'm impacted by the people for their kindness, their loyalty, their patience, generosity, or vulnerability. I’ve never said that she changed my life because she is pretty or skinny. We spend so much time worrying about the thing that means the least to a lot of people.” - Becca Tilley from the bachelor.
