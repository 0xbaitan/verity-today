 National Minority Mental Health Awareness Month


Nysa Malhotra

July 18, 2021   |   Mental Health


A lot of individuals are aware of World Mental Health Day. However, not many of us, including those who spread awareness about mental health issues or claim to be mental health activists, know about National Minority Mental Health Awareness Month. It is also sometimes referred to as minority mental health month.



Minority Mental Health Month is a nationwide effort in America that had begun to shed light on the mental health experiences within the communities of ‘Black, Indigenous, People of Colour’, abbreviated as BIPOC communities.

These communities have historically faced unequal treatment due to systemic and cultural barriers. Moreover, around this time in 2008, the entire month of July was declared National Minority Mental Health Awareness Month.



Since then, July has been a time to acknowledge and explore issues concerned with mental health illness, depression, mental disorders, etc., that the communities had been facing.

This month signals the period where we should: destigmatize mental illnesses, make people aware, and amplify the voices of affected minority groups.

In the U.S. alone, confirmed sources suggest that racial and sexual minorities show higher rates of suicidal tendencies, post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), among other mental health disorders. It stems from deep-rooted societal prejudice towards stigmatized minority groups wherein they experience rejection, estrangement, and harassment.

It is about time we not only educate ourselves about this issue but also the people around us. It is more than just a necessity that we eradicate feelings of hatred towards such minority groups from the minds of people who project hatred and subjugate these communities.



Some lesser-known facts:

  • Recent studies suggest 14 percent of Native American and Alaskan Native adults have mental & substance abuse disorders.

  • More than 16 percent of them who identify as Black or African American have been diagnosed with a mental health condition in the past year. Estimates show that only one in three of these respective individuals will probably receive mental health care.

  •More than 60 million Americans suffer from a mental health condition per year. A significant majority of these individuals identify as African American, Hispanic, Asian American or a part of another minority population.

  •Yet studies suggest that African Americans and Hispanic Americans seek treatment at only half the rate of whites, while Asian Americans only do so at one-third the rate. Some of these differences are due to inaccessibility to health care; others due to cultural reasons.

Indeed, America’s entire mental health system needs improvement, especially when it comes to serving marginalized communities.



How can we contribute?

Together, we can help spread this shared vision of a nation where anyone affected by mental illness, irrespective of their: background, culture, ethnicity or identity, can get appropriate support and quality of care to live healthy and fulfilling lives where no one feels alone in their struggle.
