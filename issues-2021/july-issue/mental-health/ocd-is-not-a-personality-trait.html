 OCD Is Not A Personality Trait


Sayani Kar

July 18, 2021   |   Mental Health


Raise your hand if you have ever heard someone or said it yourself that you have OCD without really giving a second thought to it. Well, news flash! OCD is nothing less than a legitimate anxiety disorder or mental illness.



Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is a disorder where people experience recurring, unwanted thoughts, ideas or sensations that compel them to do something repetitively. Repetitive behaviours, for instance, excessive hand washing, checking on things or cleaning, can cause significant disturbance in day-to-day activities and social interactions.

In recent times, people have been carelessly associating one or a few traits with OCD. As a result, the term has been extensively misappropriated by many and used casually in conversations. Consequently, the delegitimization of the word has been detrimental to patients with OCD. The problem lies in the fact that people belittle OCD patients of their experiences and reduce their illness to a mere personality trait.

If OCD brings only the simple desire for everything to be organised, clean, repeated, and rearranged, then it would not be an illness. The misuse of the term indirectly or directly causes people to believe that the disorder is not as debilitating as it truly is.

As a result of this, people conceive the notion that OCD has no crucial implications. They believe it is more akin to an everyday worry. OCD may appear harmless at first glance but has lethal effects on people suffering from this disorder. From having grotesque, disturbing and sexual thoughts leading to ludicrous actions, it is nothing short of agony.

OCD is not just about effectuating perfection or being germaphobic. According to NOCD, a telehealth provider that caters to people with OCD:

“Some people with OCD are preoccupied with excessive thoughts about germs or contamination, some with rigid morality, some with a need for symmetry, balance, or numerical patterns. One common form of OCD involves intrusive thoughts of terrible things happening.”

Let us take an example. Assume that someone preoccupied with excessive thoughts of cleanliness might keep washing their hands, legs and other body parts until these thoughts fade away. Sometimes this activity can even lead to bleeding. Yes, it is that severe an issue.

OCD sufferers generally have impertinent or neurotic thoughts related to an incident in their lives. While there are no official classifications of OCD, research suggests people experience OCD symptoms in four main categories: cleaning and contamination, symmetry and order, forbidden, harmful, or taboo thoughts and hoarding.

According to PSYCH GUIDES:

“Sufferers who have a fear of contamination may take baths and wash their hands many times throughout the course of one day, or they may be obsessed with cleaning every inch of their home to ensure it is spotless and free of germs. OCD sufferers who hoard items are obsessed with making sure they keep everything they have ever owned, even if the item is worn out or useless. A person who hoards becomes emotionally attached to items, and the very thought of throwing things away can cause great distress.”

OCD sufferers generally feel anxious over things that might seem trivial to non-sufferers but affects them severely. Apart from the evident mental trauma, there are no physical signs of OCD. Only in rare cases, when sufferers become intractably obsessed with the fear of contamination, it usually leads to bleeding, rashes, etc.

There are short-term as well as long term implications. A sufferer might face a problem while nurturing an intimate relationship, friendship or even maintaining their desired schedule. Long term implications include depression, increased rate of irritability and anxiety (leading to panic attacks). Adults with OCD have a greater than 75% chance of having another anxiety disorder or panic attack in their lifetime.

In this age of social media, people can foolishly do anything to sound fancy. Especially claim to have an ailment that appears sophisticated to them. Obsessive-compulsive disorder makes it to their list.

Keeping one's workplace immaculate and tidy does not give one the audacity or right to claim to have OCD. Another crucial thing to look at, whenever an individual claims to have OCD, it is usually followed by a superior giggle as if they are proud to have an ailment with a voguish name. That is preposterous and messed up on so many levels.

The internet is doing more harm than good. Websites all over the internet upload videos, publish articles and blogs on OCD and make it seem less detrimental than it is. It only makes it seem more enigmatical for the actual sufferers of OCD.

The misuse of the term OCD has made it more arduous for genuine sufferers to seek professional health. The internet and society have made a mockery out of this serious ailment.

Having OCD is just way more than being a neat freak or pernickety. Actual sufferers will only feel lackadaisical and anguish if we fail to spread awareness of this illness and make a mockery of their suffering.

How can we help the sufferers of OCD?

 1. Encourage the sufferers to express their emotions openly. However, do not compel them to share their ordeal. Be patient and affable towards them.

 2. Ask and encourage them to seek professional help like therapists. Assure them that OCD is not something to be embarrassing or unsettling.

 3. Educate yourself on OCD from professionals rather than the internet. Try to clear out various myths and misconceptions of OCD that you may have considered.

 4. Make them feel comfortable and provide them with emotional support. Remember that sufferers often tend to feel anxious or even depressed.

 5. Some parts of OCD treatment can be afflicting and demanding. Ask them what you can do to make them feel a little less antagonised and flustered.

 6. Instead of silencing the sufferers, support their journey. If they are faltering at a job, try to be accommodating and understanding towards them.

 7. Do not exert pressure on them. Instead of delineating people with OCD as pushovers, we should earnestly task them with appropriate jobs.
