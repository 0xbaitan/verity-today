 The Stigmatisation of Therapy


Tirna Ray

July 18, 2021   |   Mental Health


Therapy. Merriam-Webster’s dictionary describes it as the medical treatment of any impairment, injury, disease, or disorder. More commonly known as a practice that many condemn for no valid reason.



Individuals tend to believe that those who attend therapy are wasting money, weak, incapable of dealing with their emotions, and called insane, to name a few. It is an incredibly harmful stereotype that still sees the light of day in 2021.

To many, therapy is an intimidating concept. People treat it as a last resort for patients who need to seek professional help. It is because a diagnosis is usually required to avail it.

About 48% of the population seek professional counselling. However, it is often quite a difficult decision to make. It is due to the negative stigmas and stereotypes surrounding the concept of therapy.

In reality, most people who seek professional help are simply in need of a safe space to speak to and receive advice from someone experienced and unbiased.

Such negative connotations of therapy can be traced back to its portrayal in the media. Whether it is the individual seeking therapy in the sitcom, or the punchline of the show, or showing exaggerated and crude demonstrations about how certain mental illnesses look like, for example, schizophrenia. The media almost always shows therapy in a negative light.

Schizophrenia seems to be the epitome of ridicule in modern society. When individuals think of schizophrenia, they do not think of the negative thoughts that patients must be hearing in their head constantly. Instead, these individuals continuously demean their self-image and gnaw at their self-esteem.

They do not acknowledge the strain such mental illnesses can inflict on relationships that the patient may have with their family and friends.

In TV or movies, one would notice people with schizophrenia are often the ones with tangled hair and large dilated eyes, proposing theories and concepts of human life that many would not consider normal. Producers depict their rooms as messy; peculiar objects and furniture placed in odd positions in their rooms are a common trope. They often become the punching bag of the show.

I cannot help but think that their ideology has devolved into the: “Are we out of original content? No worries, let us make fun of the mentally ill individual for simply existing LOL.”

Yes, I can make the argument that all stereotypes derive from some vague truth. I agree that society has pushed many mentally ill individuals beyond the point of ‘normality’ where they cannot act per the norms of modern society. When they disobey these stringent norms, they are most likely to be ridiculed by the people they considered to be their closest allies.

However, the gross exaggeration that people that suffer from intense or extreme versions of their mental illness are all ‘insane’ or ‘crazy’ is incredibly harmful. Going as far as generalising those with mental illness (milder versions included) to be weak or incapable to any degree is disdainful. It leads to the further devolvement of society.

Self-stigma;

A type of stigma that an individual imposes upon oneself. They doubt that their mental illness is deserving of help from a therapist. They often buy into the idea that others have it worse. That is not even remotely true.

Every individual in society is deserving of rehabilitation, recovery and support. No one can feign superiority when it comes to mental health. Some choose to believe they do not have an issue or struggle, to begin with, to avoid being called an attention-seeker.

Note that if an individual is genuinely misleading others by faking their mental illness, then they would not have to worry or question themselves if they are faking it since they would have already known about it. Even if an individual is indeed faking a mental illness, it would be a different and separate mental health issue altogether.

Therapy, in its true essence, is just counselling another individual from their medical impairment. The role of a therapist is to ensure their patients are at their best state to function as contributing members of society. The simple action of ridiculing an individual brings them to a state where they keep their suffering to themselves.

A solution to this particular situation would be to reduce the stigma which is emanating from the public. Corrigan and Penn (1999) stated their three ways and approaches to reducing overall public stigma. Those cited were protest, education, and contact. They suggested that these steps help in reducing the exaggeration and misinformation made by television shows and movies (media in general).

Protests. By protesting against said misinformation and signalling how the reality of mental illness is more than just a punchline, we can initiate destigmatisation. Psychotherapists and other professionals should be encouraged to speak out against all misleading information that reaches the masses. Moreover, they bear the moral responsibility to do so since they possess the most expertise in their respective fields.

Education. We should prioritise educating the public on why the insensitive and inaccurate portrayal of characters with mental illnesses is damaging. Putting factful and palatable information on the mode of entertainment can help enhance one’s knowledge of the mental illness at hand.

Fair representation of characters with an actor who has maybe gone through such an event in their life can make a difference. Besides, simply just helping people understand that maybe laughing at the crazy one in that show may be humorous to someone but incredibly triggering for someone else. Failure to do so may cause individuals to feel invalidated and drive them away from help.

Contact. It would be the act of having personal contact with individuals that have sought help from therapists before. It could also mean having contact with those who have been through the mental illness and may show symptoms. Having this contact would blur the lines between people who seek help and people who do not feel the need. Therefore, creating a more fluid, inclusive, and educated society.

To reduce self-stigma: one of many studies (Rosen, 2003) has shown that many people would feel less stigmatised and ridiculed if society normalises their actions, behaviour and symptoms. They would be prone to feel less ashamed and less guilty for seeking help. Discussing how normal it is to struggle with varieties of mental illnesses and how common diagnosis is may also decrease the stigmatisation and help people get rid of the ideology that seeking help is a bad thing.

In all honesty, seeking help is one of the toughest and intimidating decisions to make. Living through it and seeking help for something you are internally struggling with is incredibly brave. Hence, we should all admire those who took the courage to better themselves.

The more we normalise therapy, the less of an issue it becomes. Many therapists can assist individuals through the burden and stigma that they feel. We could consider this as a step taken in the right direction towards normalising therapy.

Everyone deserves recovery. Everyone deserves support. You are not better off because you have a milder version of someone else’s struggle. You are just as important. You are not any less of a person for having someone help you get through life. Life is by no means simple. It is understandable if some people have someone helping them make the journey a little smoother.
