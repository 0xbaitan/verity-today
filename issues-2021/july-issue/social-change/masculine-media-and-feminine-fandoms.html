 ‘Masculine’ Media And ‘Feminine’ Fandoms


Priyanka Banerjee

July 18, 2021   |   Social Change


In recent times, the world has become aware of sexism and misogyny in the most unexpected place — pop culture. There is a special contempt reserved for young people who choose to analyze the media they love or publicise their love for it on the internet, and when this media solely makes spaces for young female individuals, it’s hard to avoid the conclusion that this contempt is gendered. Why are One Direction fans met with a brand of judgement or disgust that cricket fans are never met with? And do age-old gender stereotypes play a role in categorising and hierarchizing media as ‘masculine and ‘feminine?



Let us take the most common comparison — pop music vs sports. There is a preconceived notion that pop music fandoms are mainly female or queer-dominated ones and sports fans exhibit a certain brand of masculinity that is associated with the cisgender heterosexual male. This gendering of forms of entertainment is inaccurate, but let us put that aside for a moment. Modern-day pop music fans are dismissed for having silly interests and, for most fans, this results in a lot of shame and a need to justify their involvement with pop fandoms. Sports, on the other hand, has always been considered to be a legitimate and sensible interest, if not inspiring and educational for those who closely follow it. The frenzied loyalty of sports fans to specific teams is never mocked or dismissed. Such interests are considered normal and appropriate by society because these fans adhere to the prescribed standards of ‘masculinity’. If we compare a Taylor Swift concert to a Manchester United vs. Arsenal football match, we don’t see a real difference in the volume of yelling and cheering, the number of painted faces or the kind of enthusiasm expressed. It's the pitch of the shrieks that makes all the difference in how society perceives these two groups of very excited, very loyal fans.

Not only does this result in the dismissal of one group’s interests and the pedestalizing of another, but it also reveals how we perceive media through a gendered lens and create hierarchies in forms of entertainment based on the gender identities of its supporters. The media that tries to create a space for female individuals to feel included and seen doesn’t necessarily market to women, but it is automatically labelled ‘feminine’ media and, by extension of society’s definition of femininity, it is automatically considered ‘frivolous’. For instance, Little Mix songs are never considered to be meaningful or sophisticated and mainstream media has, for far too long, portrayed Taylor Swift as a vengeful woman writing shallow songs about break-ups. So whenever artists create spaces for women or make them feel heard, their art becomes ‘shallow’ and their fans ‘foolish’.

When it comes to female-dominated fandoms and the backlash they face, the term ‘fangirl’ and the negative connotation attached to it becomes an important point to address. Why has a word which should mean a fan who identifies as female turned into such a dirty term? It has become a pejorative term used to dismiss and belittle an entire section of all fandoms — a primarily young, female section. Not only is it hard for women to own spaces that have been created for them without facing backlash for it, it also becomes harder for them to admit to themselves that they are, in fact, fangirls (the actual definition really does refer to a female fan) because of the negativity that is associated with it. Similarly, the term ‘fanboy’ should technically refer to a male fan. But today, the term is used as a derogatory one for those men who have typically ‘feminine’ interests. A 17-year-old football fanatic or a 45-year-old diehard cricket lover will never be called a ‘fanboy’. But male fans of Ariana Grande will. As the term was invented much after the term ‘fangirl’, it is commonly used to refer to a man who has fangirl-like interests and is, therefore, not ‘manly’ enough. Thus, the term ‘fanboy’, in the way in which it is used today, becomes inherently sexist because it not only humiliates men who have so-called ‘feminine’ interests, but it also used to insult the male version of a fangirl, furthering the idea that being a fangirl is something silly and shameful.

Another layer of gendering reveals itself when a woman claims to be a dedicated fan of supposedly ‘masculine’ interests. For instance, female football lovers are often dismissed by male fans — or quizzed by men to test whether their knowledge and devotion to the sport is at par with that of men. Such sports are traditionally considered too ‘manly’ for women and, therefore, beyond their understanding. And pre-existing gender stereotypes play an important part in why this occurs.

The patriarchal idea of an ‘ideal’ woman is that of a quiet, composed woman who is supposed to have no opinion, no voice of her own and is simply an extension of a man. Men, typically, were associated with brute strength and aggressiveness. This, even in 2021, plays a part in how women are received in typically ‘masculine’ circles. Much of society finds it hard to associate women with a sport that is characteristically very aggressive. The thought that a woman cannot really be interested in a particular sport because it’s too ‘manly’ and, therefore, beyond her comprehension, is essentially discriminatory. But besides that, this thought also comes from a very archaic idea of how men and women should be but be that, in the twenty-first century, should be behind all of us. It’s high time that people of any and all genders are allowed to decide for themselves what they are interested in instead of society defining where their interests should lie. It’s high time we stop belittling pop culture fans as having frivolous and non-serious interests or for publicly expressing those interests. And it’s high time that we stop letting regressive and primitive gender stereotypes hold us back from loving what we truly love.
