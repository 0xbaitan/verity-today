 Reasonable Restrictions or Violation of Privacy: IT Rules 2021


Karen Kaur

July 18, 2021   |   Social Change


When concerns were raised over the new IT laws implemented by the government IT minister Ravi Shankar Prasad came out with an explanation saying that "as per all established judicial dictum, no fundamental right, including the Right to Privacy, is absolute and it is subject to reasonable restrictions. The requirements in the intermediary guidelines pertaining to the first originator of information are an example of such a reasonable restriction." Making clear that according to the government the IT Rules, 2021 which include “traceability” among other things, falls under “reasonable restrictions”, and the question that now arises is what draws the thin line between imposing reasonable restrictions and violating human rights, that the government fails to address.



Several experts of technology and privacy, say that imposing IT Rules 2021, would violate the privacy of every person who communicates digitally, they argue that even though regulation of digital exchange is necessary, it cannot be done at the expense of putting innocent people at risk and violating human rights. The IT laws have also been criticized by the Report of the Justice Srikrishna Committee on Data Protection for mandating low encryption standards in license agreements with telecom service providers because “this poses a threat to safety and security of the personal data of data principals.

While it is important to stop hateful and instigating messages from spreading we also need to understand if “traceability” will be of the use, that the government claims it would if multinational companies collected data as to which message was sent by whom and further shared by whom, for every text that is sent in the day it will open up an avenue to spoofing, and it might be used to incriminate innocent people, as majority commoners send texts copy-pasted from a website, a social media page, or any source, without understanding the weight the message holds or being fully educated about the issue, even though it is problematic to do so, people cannot be criminalized for being uneducated, or should I say “woke” enough?

Moreover, it would make it difficult for the authorities to understand the original context of the message, be it sarcastic, ironic or literal, there is no way which an algorithm can identify the original intent or context that the sender had, therefore creating more scope for people being framed for things they did not say. Moreover, this kind of excessive data will make the companies less secure and open to hacks, when government websites that do not have one-quarter of the amount of data, are not immune to hacks, is it responsible for the government to put at stake daily conversations of billions of digital users?

The imposition of “traceability” also gives enormous power in the hands of the central government and goes against the basic spirit of our federal democracy. This provision to trace people’s messages also gives the government the right to remove messages that it deems to be hateful. In 2021, when hashtag resign Modi was trending on Twitter, the government asked Twitter to remove various tweets, this was before the imposition of the new IT Rules, if the governments’ reaction to criticism then was to censor it, it is very likely that the government will use these rules to curb dissent. Given these drawbacks and the government’s claims to the IT Rules, the Delhi HC shall rule on the validity of these laws after being challenged by the Social Media giant Whatsapp. The fate of our daily conversations lies in the hands of the judiciary.
