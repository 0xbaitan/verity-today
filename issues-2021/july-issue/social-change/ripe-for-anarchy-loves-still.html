 Ripe for anarchy, loves still


Niharika Rajsaha

July 18, 2021   |   Social Change


Feminism has had its roots actively intertwined with queer liberation since the 1960s, influencing each other as two movements in portraying solidarity in struggle. They have both fought for the basic right to equality between people regardless of their gender or sexuality, trying to curb society’s inherent prejudice against them and fighting discrimination based on the same. Shared struggle and trauma has formed a bond of understanding between the two movements and they have had an impact on each other to achieve, what is, at its core, the same goal. The two movements have, far too often, been studied as separate ‘objects’. However, looking through the past and the present, it is evident that no two movements that have the values and impact of this magnanimity can ever be completely separate. They all focus on people. And anything to do with people, especially those who have been oppressed and discriminated against for centuries, can never be as simple as one problem to be addressed.



Feminism is chronologically older than the LGBTQ+ movement, with its first wave of advocacy for gender equality hitting in the nineteenth and early twentieth century. First-wave feminism advocated equal property rights for women, discarding the idea of the subordinate position of married women to their husbands. By the late 19th century, feminist activism was primarily focused on women’s suffrage. The second wave of feminism brought out the concerns of lesbians’ fight for specific rights against sexism. The National Organisation for Women, a feminist organization that aimed to end sex-classified employment notices, for greater representation of women in government, for child-care centres for working mothers, and for legalized abortion. This period converged with the beginning of the long silent LGBTQ+ movement in the 1950-60s when in 1955, Del Martin and Phyllis Lyon formed the Daughters of Bilitis (DOB), the first national organization for lesbians. It aimed to end the feelings of isolation and seclusion felt in the community, to educate them about their legal rights to integrate social acceptance and fight for the social and political equality of lesbians. Therefore, when the second wave of the feminist movement struck, lesbians played an important role in advocating civil rights equality in the workplace, home and public without discrimination based on gender or sexuality.

This was when the term ‘Lavender Menace’ was attached to the lesbian community. Many in the movement were against the involvement of lesbians in the fight for their own rights, insinuating that they would corrupt the integrity of the movement by driving away women out of fear of association and drawing too much attention to themselves. Lesbians were also facing the marginalization of their ideologies by their male counterparts in the mixed-sex gay rights organizations. Although this led to the establishment of a movement to support pride in the lesbian community, the alienation of women in the feminist movement is an almost cruel irony. It disregarded an entire community of women, telling them to not fight for their rights. Sexism victimizes every woman, regardless of their sexuality. The inequality in salary is something that every woman faces in the workplace, whether or not they belong to the LGBTQ+ community. It is equivalent to telling them that they have to make a choice between the two; that being a woman in the LGBTQ+ community is not an option, that they are pariahs. To tell any woman that they should not fight for their basic right to equality in society, for the simple idea that they are their own independent people who do not need to measure their value in accordance to their affiliation with a man not only belittles the character but portrays feminism as selective, which is aimed to be anything but.

Feminism refers to the non-variable equality among people and prohibition of discrimination on grounds of gender. It isn’t technically possible to integrate equal social and political rights for people in the LGBTQ+ community and counter societal shame with pride without the prerequisite of gender equality. Women in the community are a huge section of the people that are affected by sexism. Without feminism liberating every single person from the constraints of gender inequality, the liberation of every person in the LGBTQ+ community cannot be achieved. The success and impact of feminism is a major stepping stone for the queer community to be truly liberated.

The queer liberation movement has been to inculcate societal acceptance of people belonging to the community and to rid the world of discrimination based on one’s sexual orientation. The main objective of the feminist movement has been to tear down the belief that our roles in the world and the opportunities we get should be influenced by gender; to fight society’s inherent gender-based discrimination. The goals of both these movements are to fight the constructed ideas of society and the places of those who belong to it. The similar values they employ- of respect for every identity and objectivity in general decisions, such that their identities do not become the basis for their place in the world, professional and otherwise show the interconnectedness of their struggles. While the two movements fight against different strains of marginalisation, they have converged, to fight a fight that is not necessarily polar.
