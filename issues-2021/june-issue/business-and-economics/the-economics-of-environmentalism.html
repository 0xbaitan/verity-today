 The Economics of Environmentalism


Karnav Popat

June 1, 2021   |   Business & Economics


Ever since the Industrial Revolution, it has been accepted that economic growth and environmental preservation are natural opposites. Profiting under the capitalist system depends heavily on maximising the utility that can be extracted from resources, but unfortunately, this doesn’t sit so well with Mother Nature. Since the turn of the century, there has been increasing pressure to limit this rampant over-exploitation of natural resources that are either difficult or impossible to replenish.



The problem is largely driven by the developing regions of the world, which have neither the political will nor the economic advantages to slow down their economic growth and take better care of the environment. For example, India is the 5th largest economy in the world and the fastest growing trillion-dollar economy, but ranked a dismal 177th on the 2018 Environmental Performance Index [2]. In fact, the vast majority of developing nations rank in the bottom half of the EPI, even including stronger economies like Nigeria (100th) and China (120th). However, most nations, including the developed ones, are doing far less than they promised: according to the 2019 UNEP Emissions Gap Report, G20 nations (which are responsible for more than three quarters of the world’s emissions) would have to reduce a massive 2.5 gigatons more, just to meet their minimum contributions per the Paris Climate Agreement; horrifying given that the agreement itself had been criticized as not being enough. In the same period, they’ve seen steady economic growth at a moderate average of 2-3 percent annually, while the less-concerned developing nations have done even better. Clearly, nations aren’t going to stop destroying the environment until it stops being profitable.

Of course, that’s exactly the point. The evidence clearly shows that if the world has to choose between the environment and the economy, the economy will be prioritized regardless of whatever agreements they voluntarily sign. The solution, then, is to eliminate this binary. If the choice itself is eliminated by bringing the good of the environment in line with the good of the economy, it seems intuitive that nature will benefit, even if the world doesn’t actually care about preserving it. While most efforts seem to focus on shifting an imaginary slider between the Industrial Revolution on the one side and an idyllic nature-centric society, the rapid developments in environment-friendly technology sparks hope that profit, the only proven motivator for our economic leaders, can be utilized to the earth’s advantage.

Take the energy sector, for example, which is one of the primary sources of pollution and environmental degradation. Solar power generation is already cheaper than new coal plants in the entirety of the world. Carbon Track, a trailblazing environmental data analytics firm, released in its report that not only is investing in renewables now the better option, renewable energy will be cheaper as a whole than coal, the worst offender in terms of pollution and environmental destruction. Additionally, there has been an extremely steep downward trend in the price of solar power generation, with prices having more than halved in the last decade alone. As breakthroughs in research keep happening and our technology becomes more and more efficient, renewable energy is becoming the best choice under all considerations.

As it stands, the only thing standing in the way of environmentally-positive economic development is the lobbying money of the fossil fuel industry. However, it seems almost inevitable that at some point in the (hopefully near) future, the benefits and savings from environmentally friendly options will outweigh the subsidies and incentives of the fossil fuel industry. The way forward is built on integrating our concern for the environment into the economy itself, which will be a natural consequence of using new methods and developments to replace old, inefficient and polluting ways.

The overarching answer to the question, “How do we balance economic growth and environmental preservation?” seems to simply change the dynamic. While in the past using nature’s resources has been to its detriment, the smarter methods that we now have at our disposal have given us the option of keeping the economy chugging without harming the environment. Instead of trying to manipulate the equation ‘Economy + Ecology = 1’, perhaps we should work with the far more positive ‘Economy = Ecology’.
