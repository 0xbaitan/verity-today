 The Venezuelan Economy: Rise and Fall


Namit Mahajan

June 1, 2021   |   Business & Economics


“In Venezuela, we either accept domination, total oppression and torture... from Maduro's regime, or we choose freedom, democracy, and prosperity for our people.”
- Juan Guido



According to analysts, the collapse of the Venezuelan economy is the single biggest economic collapse outside of the war in at least 45 years.

However, armed strife has not devastated Venezuela, which was once the wealthiest country of Latin America. Instead, experts believe that the weak administration, corruption, and foolish policies of President Nicolás Maduro and his predecessor, Hugo Chávez, have fueled rampant inflation, shattered industries, and brought the country to its knees.

The rapid plunge into the economic and political upheaval is a cautionary tale about the potentially disastrous impact of resource wealth on emerging countries. It serves as a classic case of the perils of a petroleum rich statehood. The Venezuelan economy and politics have deteriorated under President Nicolas Maduro, as once-significant oil exports have dwindled to a trickle. Analysts believe that the prospects of the country are bleak in the absence of a power shift. Decades of maladministration have led to the economic and political devastation of what was once one of the wealthiest countries of Latin America.

According to the United Nations Refugee Agency, the crisis is taking shape in the form of corruption, hyperinflation, one of the worst homicide rates worldwide, food and medication shortages, and the most comprehensive flight "in the recent history of Latin America."

Since 2014, the South American country began to see a dramatic decline. With the GDP falling even faster than the United States during the Great Depression, many of the approximately 32 million citizens could not purchase food, and resource-strapped hospitals lacked soap and antibiotics. Meanwhile, the political system of Venezuela collapsed. How did Venezuela sink so low in such a short time? The most pressing issue in the country is hyperinflation, one of the many reasons for the sinking of the nation’s economy.

Trish Regan says “Think about it: Venezuela, Cuba, Nicaragua, Vietnam, North Korea, the former Soviet Union - they all started with the intention of leveling the playing field - or making things better for the little guy - and instead, they created misery, poverty, destruction and a permanent ruling class of bureaucrats.” Scholars who have examined the country, attribute several causes to its growth and demise. According to Jo-Marie Burt, “The decline of oil prices, the massive social spending of the Chavez and Maduro governments, the US sanctions, and a combination of economic mismanagement and corruption at the top have contributed to the economic collapse.”

Venezuela has the most prominent proven oil reserves in the world. However, according to figures from the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries, its oil output, which was once the highest in Latin America, has declined quicker in the last year than Iraq's following the American invasion in 2003. Sanctions imposed by the United States on Venezuela's oil industry have exacerbated the country's situation.

To put even more pressure on Maduro's administration, it sanctioned the Venezuelan gold mining industry in March and the Central Bank of Venezuela in April, cutting off that institution's access to US dollars and restricting its capacity to undertake international transactions.

As the country's economy crumbled, violent gangs seized control of entire communities, governmental services disintegrated, and most Venezuelans' purchasing power reduced to a few kilos of wheat per month. Shortages have pushed a large portion of the population into a humanitarian disaster. Many people believe that each month brings new lows.

“Venezuela needs to develop; economic growth is essential for the country after so many years of lagging behind,” says Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva. The situation in Venezuela continues to deteriorate. Strong fiscal measures, foreign intervention, and policy changes could make things better. In the meanwhile, all one can hold on to is hope.
