 Boston Marriage, Or Rich Women Confusing Historians


Sreemita Mukherjee

June 1, 2021   |   Creativity




‘The Bostonians’ by Henry James included a kind of relationship between its two main female protagonists—Olive Chancellor and Verena Tarrant—that was, at the time, relatively unknown. ‘The Bostonians’ used the term Boston Marriage to better establish and explain the two ladies’ relationship, seemingly inventing the term. However, James’ sister, Alice was also involved in a Boston Marriage with Katherine Loring, a history professor, thus proving that the phenomenon existed long before James explored it.

By definition, the term refers to the monogamous cohabitation of two, upper or middle-class women independent of financial support from a man and was an occurrence usually in the late 19th and early 20th-century in parts of the UK and US. Whether or not these partnerships were romantic and/or sexual, remains a topic of heavy debate. However, back in the late nineteenth century, wherein women were encouraged to not interact with men, the phenomenon of a Boston Marriage was oftentimes common, and surprisingly, socially acceptable. The earliest recorded occurrence of such a marriage was amongst two Anglo-Irish upper-class women--Eleanor Butler and Sarah Ponsonby. The two were settled in a town in Wales, where they were lovingly called the title of ‘Ladies of Llangollen’.

It is necessary to understand that although these marriages can be accepted as examples of early female-presenting same-sex relationships, it cannot be dismissed that there exists an alternative, more practical reason that can be attributed to the popularity of this phenomenon in certain social groups. It has to be reiterated and highlighted that most women that were in Boston marriages came from financially stable families and were, therefore, able to sustain a career for themselves because they were educated or make a living out of their inherited wealth. Women, especially those part of the suffragette movement at the time, were highly critical of the institution of marriage as it fundamentally restricted their freedom. In retaliation, they created a phenomenon that supported them both in public and private lives.



The phenomenon was also immensely prevalent in women’s colleges and boarding schools, wherein having a crush on another woman was considered part of school life. Nancy Sahli in 1979 wrote, that such relationships within colleges were accepted by almost everyone as it was viewed as women simply having a more emotional nature than men. Testament to the fact, the term ‘Wellesley Marriage’ became a term because ‘Boston Marriages’ were immensely popular amongst professors of the Wellesley College. Towards the start of the twentieth century, there was a rise of middle-class women who also started participating in Boston Marriages. Because Boston marriages were not officially legal, most of these women remained seemingly single whilst being in committed, lifelong relationships. This was fairly reflected when in the period between 1890 and 1920, the percentage of never-married women was the highest than any other era, in the US.

After the 1920s, there was a rapid decline in the social acceptance of any kind of relationship that challenged the heterosexual model of ‘family’, it became common to label any such relationship ‘abnormal’. It has to be understood that before this, there existed hardly any study on women’s sexualities and desires, leading people to believe that women simply did not have those qualities. Boston marriages, in particular, were viewed as strictly asexual relationships. However, Havelock Ellis’s influential book ‘Sexual Inversion’, identified these relationships as being ‘unhealthy’ and possibly sexual, thereby effectively starting the cause of the phenomenon’s decline. In the 1920s and 1930s, there no longer existed the previous acceptance of such relationships, and girls who partook in such relationships in school were often sent back home to be ‘healed back to heterosexuality’. For the rest of the century, there existed almost no such accepted phenomenon that opposed the societal norms of heterosexual relationships.

That is until the Stonewall riots took place in 1969 leading up to the Gay Liberation movement. The term, ‘Boston Marriage’ was reintroduced by Rothblum and Brehony (1993) to refer to a lesbian relationship that was romantic but was asexual. This could be supported by the fact that women who were in Boston Marriages, had in the past, spoken about why the term marriage was included in this context. For most, it could be explained by the fact that their relationships were nothing short of a kind of union, and for them to ignore that love and devotion would be an insult to their relationships. If we were to put their personalities and relationships in today’s context, it would be fair to call these relationships ‘domestic partnerships’.

However, there is no true way of knowing if all of these relationships were truly asexual. It also cannot be estimated that all of these women were necessarily lesbian, as there exist other possibilities. Some of them could’ve been placed on the asexual spectrum, some were bisexual and some could’ve easily eliminated gender from their attraction, choosing to love their partner simply because they want to. To term Boston Marriages as examples of lesbian relationships, would be erasure in a lot of different areas and as stated earlier, there is no true way of knowing. Today, the term is used exclusively to signify these historic relationships, to celebrate the union of these women who lived in times that effectively worked against them.

This pride month, as we celebrate our own identities, to remember and honour these women and the many other people who have made it possible for us to come this far, is exactly what we should be doing. To keep their stories and memories alive, to keep fighting for equal footing, to be able to express ourselves fully and completely, because, in our path of equality, we still have a long way to go.
