 BTS: An Everlasting Tale of Exclusion


Sreemita Mukherjee

June 1, 2021   |   Creativity


To say that BTS is anything less than legendary, would be to deny the sheer power of their popularity and their impact on the music industry. This year alone, BTS has procured partnerships with McDonald's, Coway, and Kloud. They’re the latest ambassador of Louis Vuitton and have been confirmed to appear on the FRIENDS reunion episode. Along with these feats, BTS is also working on their next album and are preparing to drop their 2nd English single, ‘Butter’. Despite the many accolades that can be attached to the bands’ name, there exists an overwhelming plethora of backlash and criticism, that isn’t even the result of their immense popularity. There has always existed a kind of repulsion since before BTS’ mainstream success that has alienated them in their home industry, and it continues today, now that they have achieved global success.



BTS was originally planned to be a rap-focused hip-hop group that strayed away from the usual structure of a ‘K-pop group’. BTS modified their image to fit the more conventional K-pop group type with the release of the HYYH album series in 2015, but they refused to leave behind their hip-hop roots and introspective concepts. The crux of the matter is that BTS tried to incorporate the lyric-heavy style of rap into the idol group structure, wherein dancing and visuals were given greater importance. This concept was met with criticism from the K-pop industry dominated by the big three firms (JYP, YG, and SM) as well as the Korean rap community.

RM, Suga, and J-Hope—the rappers of BTS were the members that upheld this hip-hop image the most. RM was a kind of celebrity in the Korean rap scene, his talent so immense that it prompted Bang Shi-Hyuk (Ceo of BigHit Entertainment) to create an entire group with RM’s image in its midst. When RM left the rap scene, his fellow artists were immensely disappointed at his giving in to the strict ideals of idol life. This backlash from the hip-hop community continued long after BTS’ debut. For example in 2013, mere months after its debut, rapper B-Free slammed BTS for ‘selling out and becoming K-pop artists’. The band was made the subject of mockery in 2014 because of their very name, which translates to ‘Bulletproof Boy Scouts’. Instances of such criticism and mockery in the early years of the band are abundant, however, the rising fame and unstoppability of BTS gradually left the petty voices complaining about trivial things behind.

This brings us to our next topic of scrutiny—the K-pop Industry.

BTS’ debut stage was filmed as a substitute in the absence of another act. The K-Pop industry refused to treat them as anything of value, giving them the bare minimum, which is sadly, still faced by many groups who aren’t commercially successful. In BTS’ case, however, their success was hardly acknowledged until it was impossible to ignore. After two years of a gradual and organic rise in album sales, BTS dethroned Big Bang in the Hanteo Charts, becoming one of the best-selling artists of 2015. This achievement was immediately heavy speculation that BTS had manipulated charts and of course, they hadn’t. In 2019, there was an influx of artists that had been accused of chart manipulation but once officials stepped in, it was made clear that BTS was indeed one of the only artists who hadn’t resorted to any such means in their entire career.

From black eyeliner and golden chains, to leather jackets and a more aggressive approach to their topics, BTS evolved to a softer, brighter concept. HYYH was the pinnacle of that and that continued with their ‘Love Yourself' series and ‘Map of the Soul:7’. The kind of masculinity BTS presents is different and honestly, welcoming. The members have shown keen interest in things considered ‘feminine’ and have said that the idea of ‘masculinity’ doesn’t mean much to them. Jimin, in particular, has been the most open about his stylistic choices and presents himself to be extremely ambiguous in stage performances. This reflection and evolution is never acknowledged outside their fan circles.

BTS faced the overwhelming wrath of xenophobia when they finally started entering the American music industry. These aren't fan observations though, it’s something the members have admitted to while condemning the violence Asian-Americans are facing currently. During an interview, member RM said that the group most often felt like ‘aliens’ in the American music industry, and although their success is immense, they’re hardly respected for their artistry.

This year alone, BTS and their culture were mocked on Chilean TV amidst the growing calls to address and stop Asian hate. A German show host compared the band to ‘some crappy virus that will get eradicated soon’, while Topps released stickers showcasing the band members beaten up, all of this in five months. This isn’t new, this mockery was happening last year and the year before, and the year before, and soon enough, you’ll realize that BTS have been facing some or the other kind of exclusion and backlash from some sphere of the music industry.

Today, BTS stands at a position that is not reached by many. They’re the most respected artists in their home country and although the K-pop industry still likes to hoard on their success after excluding them time and time again, BTS members themselves remain unbothered. In the western music industry, however, BTS stands at a place of immense doubt. On one hand, they are far more successful than most artists but on the other hand, they are seldom respected as true artists. Their songwriting or incorporation of the Jungian psychology into their 2020 release ‘Map of the Soul:7’ isn’t widely known, their ‘Love Yourself’ series and the variety of ideas and interpretations of ‘love’ wasn’t acknowledged. It’s the fact that I found more threads on Twitter and Reddit written by fans that give more insight into their creative process and genius than most high-end entertainment articles do. BTS is plagued by the ‘Too mainstream, won’t listen to’ phenomenon, or on the flip side, ‘K-pop is too obscure, ew’ phenomena.

The tale of exclusion on grounds of deconstructed masculinity and Asian origins will go on, unless there is a sudden and drastic change, and when BTS’ turn is over, it’ll be someone else because that’s what this largely bigoted industry does best.
