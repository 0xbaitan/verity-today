 Not Another Gay Sidekick


Priyanka Banerjee

June 1, 2021   |   Creativity




Generally considered a mere casual pastime amongst teenagers who live in fantasy worlds more than in reality, fanfiction can be defined as any form of writing that continues, interrupts, or just riffs on stories and characters other people have already written about. Contrary to popular belief, fanfiction has existed for as long as fictional stories. For instance, John Milton’s ‘Paradise Lost’ and Dante’s ‘Inferno’ are both classics that are based on the Old Testament and almost all versions of the Arthurian legends are adaptations of a literary work that has existed for several centuries. Modern fanfiction, such as the kind we read online today, is considered to be a separate genre of literature with unique individual storylines that are connected to the original story by way of plot, character, or timeline. However, modern fanfiction is often treated as a dirty word or a shameful child of original popular media. In the year 2019, a popular online fanfiction database, Archive of Our Own (AO3), won a Hugo Award for Best Related Work, an award typically reserved for notable literary or dramatic works of print media and never before given to a website or unpublished work. While all this highlights the relevance and popularity of fanfiction amongst young people today, it is of special significance for the LGBTQ+ community. As a genre, fanfiction provides that rare, safe space where LGBTQ+ individuals can explore and own their identity.

While fanfiction may have existed in some form for centuries, the concept of modern fanfiction was popularised by fans of the American TV series ‘Star Trek’, a majority of whom “shipped” the characters Kirk and Spock romantically. While it is likely that this is where fanfiction’s association with homoeroticism began, it is also where fanfiction first started to be negatively associated with lewdness or distastefulness. And it is most definitely not a coincidence that fan-made work became a shallow and frivolous concept the moment a large group of fans started popularising a gay romance, one that would never have canonically been shown on-screen. This pairing, however, popularised fanfiction enough to make it the beginning of the queer community’s aim to make themselves a part of popular culture.

LGBTQ+ representation, in the modern age, means the portrayal of all non-heterosexual sexual orientations and non-cisgender gender identities in literature and media. But any consumer of entertainment belonging to the LGBTQ+ community knows that, while there has been a definite improvement in the last decade or so, there still isn’t nearly enough representation. Fanfiction questions the narrative of dominant mainstream pop culture as a whole as well as the portrayal of heteronormativity and cisnormativity in popular culture by providing a platform for people to publish character-driven stories that may put together any number of characters in various kinds of relationships, irrespective of society’s ‘norms.

The LGBTQ+ representation that we do have in mainstream media is more often than not a heterosexual cisgender person’s attempt at making their work more diverse and progressive. This often leads to representation that is unrealistic and flawed and which inaccurately stereotypes non-heterosexual relationships and non-cisgender people. The gay sidekick stereotype that even more progressive movies, such as ‘To All The Boys I’ve Loved Before’ and ‘Crazy Rich Asians’, adopt often makes people believe that the purpose of knowing a non-heterosexual person is to have a ‘gay best friend’ who can either help boys win over girls or help girls pick out shoes and dresses. A major percentage of fanfiction writers, however, actually belong to the LGBTQ+ community and write stories from their own experiences, making it more relatable for all readers belonging to the LGBTQ+ community. Here, queer and trans characters are often the main protagonists going through real-life experiences and living their lives, contrary to popular belief, as ordinarily as any heterosexual cisgender person.

Fanfiction not only represents queerness and queer characters more accurately than most movies and TV shows, but it also brings together a large number of people who have similar interests and love similar characters, thereby forming a community. And due to the anonymity that is provided to most fanfiction writers and readers as a result of it being online, these communities, more often than not, become a safe place for queer individuals to explore their identity. In fact, for those queer people for whom it is hard to come out to family and friends, these online communities become spaces where they can be themselves and receive a lot of support from people who have gone through similar issues.

The world of queer fanfiction, however, is not free of the prejudices that are at play in all other forms of mass media. People of colour, non-cisgender people, people with disabilities, people with mental health issues, and, most importantly, women are largely underrepresented, even in the more progressive world of fanfiction. For instance, records from Archive of Our Own (AO3) show that, as of 2019, 41.9% of total fanfictions are about male homosexual couples, 17.6% about heterosexual couples and only 5.8% are about queer females. In the table given below, the first seven pairings, along with many others on the list, are of good-looking white men, making them the symbols of queer fanfiction, as though that is all that queer fanfiction has to offer. This needs to change because we’re no longer living in a time where fanfiction is an unknown, unexplored genre and where characters like Kirk and Spock were first written out of the heteropatriarchy and into a gay space relationship. If white cisgender homosexual men become the symbol of queerness in fanfiction then there really won’t be much progress happening at all. Since a lot of fanfiction is written to include marginalized communities and out of a deep sense of discontent for the exclusive and oppressive media that is more popular, prejudice defeats the purpose of fanfiction which is to challenge authorial intent and take a swipe at the assumption of authority and dogma.

The most empowering thing about fanfiction, however, is that it does not limit its writers. Unlike movies and TV shows that represent the queer community as queer or, fanfiction provides a world where characters can be queer and. They can be queer and happy, queer and carefree, queer and robot-dragon underwater spies, queer and normal. Fanfiction has often been accused of glossing over the realities of homophobia and transphobia. But while it is important to address the oppression and the discrimination faced by queer people, it is also important to normalise queerness. And fanfiction does that by allowing queer individuals to believe that being queer does not necessitate a life of struggle or sorrow, that queer people can have their regular sweet love stories and their happily ever afters as well.
