 A Classic Case of Over-Compensation at the 2021 Grammy’s


Mohan Rajagopal

May 1, 2021   |   Entertainment


Since the announcement of the nominations in November 2020, the 63rd Annual Grammy Awards got off to a rocky start, with allegations of racism and seemingly bizarre selections of artists leaving industry professionals and fans alike, enraged. Matters came to a head with the blatant snub of The Weeknd (Abel Tesfaye). His 2020 album ‘After Hours’ was critically acclaimed on all fronts, spouting the song that undoubtedly dominated the charts for the entire year, ‘Blinding Lights’. However, when the nominations were announced on November 23rd, Tesfaye came out with not a single nomination. Fans and professionals in the industry were shocked, with the artist tweeting the next morning:

“The Grammys remain corrupt. You owe me, my fans and the industry transparency…”



While ‘Blinding Lights’ was left in the dark, Justin Bieber’s ‘Yummy’, one of the most hated songs of the year by listeners and critics alike, managed to receive a surprising nomination for ‘Best Pop Solo Performance, with its parent album ‘Changes’ being nominated for Best Pop Vocal Album. On a more positive note, Beyonce received the most nominations at a total of 9, with Taylor Swift and Dua Lipa right behind her with 6 nominations. However, all eyes were on the ceremony on 14th March 2021, to see how the Recording Academy would respond to the criticism it had come under.

Admittedly, the show itself was remarkably well done, showcasing dazzling and immersive performances by a host of different nominees and artists, such as Harry Styles, Billie Eilish, Taylor Swift, Dua Lipa, and Meghan Thee Stallion. It was much of the same artists that walked away with a golden trophy that night. Styles won his first Grammy for ‘Watermelon Sugar’, in the “Best Pop Vocal Performance” category, while Lipa won “Best Pop Vocal Album” with ‘Future Nostalgia’. In the premiere ceremony, Meghan Thee Stallion and Beyonce secured a victory for ‘Savage’ in the “Best Rap Song” category.

With the completion of the genre-specific awards, the Ceremony moved onto the Big Four General Categories. “Best New Artist” went to Meghan Thee Stallion, marking her fourth award of the night, while “Song of the Year” was won by H.E.R for ‘I Can’t Breathe’, a chilling political commentary on the Black Lives Matter movement that broke out in the summer of 2020. To everyone’s great surprise, ‘everything I wanted’ by Eilish won “Record of the Year”, although the Grammy was highly expected to go to Meghan for ‘Savage’. Eilish herself reflected the audience’s shock in her acceptance speech, devoting most of it to praise of Meghan and her embarrassment at having won instead.

The 2021 Grammy’s saw two major records being made. First, Taylor Swift won “Album of the Year” for her critically acclaimed ‘folklore’, a victory that was a surprise to none. Considered to be the most prestigious award presented by the Recording Academy, Swift was now the first woman in history to have won it three times, and only the fourth artist ever, with the likes of musical legends Frank Sinatra, Stevie Wonder and Paul Simon. On the other hand, with her 28th total win, Beyonce became the woman with the most Grammy awards in all of history.



As far as possible, the distribution of awards was surprisingly equitable, with each of the Big Four categories being won by a different artist. While Lipa and Swift were expected to sweep with multiple victories, each of them was able to walk away with only a single trophy. It must be noted that only thrice in the past decade has the Big Four gone to four different artists, and each time, it was when Swift won ‘Album of the Year’. This could either be a coincidence, or a reflection of the Academy’s unwillingness to award Swift on account of her controversial status within the music industry. However, the decision of the Academy to give away the awards equally has largely been seen as an attempt to not offend any particular artist or fandom, especially in light of The Weeknd’s public denunciation of the ceremony. Whatever the case may be, it seems to have worked well, as each artist (more or less) received their dues. Undoubtedly, the highlight of the evening was the fact that ‘Yummy’, to the pleasure of millions across the world, did not receive a Grammy after all. Perhaps, there is still some hope for the Recording Academy.
