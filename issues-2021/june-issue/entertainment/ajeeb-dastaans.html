 Ajeeb Dastaans: Movie Review


Anwesh Banerjee

May 1, 2021   |   Entertainment


What is the special attraction of the four-film anthology? There is the trilogy of Bombay Talkies, Lust Stories and Ghost Stories. And then, in the last year, Netflix and Prime have produced at least four more. I think it’s the idea that the films will have variety without being so varied as to be collectively forgettable. There were eighteen films in Paris, je t’aime, for instance, and I remember none well except faint flashes of the one starring Natalie Portman.  The latest in this parade is Dharma Productions, awkwardly titled Ajeeb Daastaans. A word about that title. How exactly do we pronounce it? Is the n nasal or not? I guess it doesn’t matter because the English plural grafted onto the Hindi noun just never escapes its awkwardness. There are four films here, directed by Shashank Khaitan, Raj Mehta, Neeraj Ghaywan and Kayoze Irani. The films have little in common, apart from the fact that they all end in unexpected places – two are outright ‘twists’, two are simply unanticipated closings. A closer look at the title might force us into a queer reading of the same for two of the four films deal with queer themes while another prominently has its narrative around the issue of speech disability. The queer angle is of particular interest to me because if my memory serves me right, the Karan Johar short in Bombay Talkies too was titled “Ajeeb dastan hai yeh” and dealt with a closeted and married gay man in Bombay.



Khaitan’s short is called Majnu and is the most theatrical, albeit ridiculous, of the lot. Jaideep Ahlawat, deeply deeply miscast, stars as Babloo, the scion of a landed family somewhere in North India who, on his wedding night, informs his young bride Lipakshi (a positively now being typecast Fatima Sana Shaikh) that he will not be able to commit to their marriage fully. He has married because he was forced to, but he is still in love with someone else. And he wants to punish his father by not producing an heir, so he will not consummate the marriage either.

The Indian Constitution guarantees the universal adult franchise as a fundamental right to every Indian citizen above the age of 18. Yet, five people were killed at a polling booth in Sitalkuchi on 10th April, 2021. Reports of repeated bombings in front of people’s houses in villages, death threats to voters and confiscation of Voter ID cards are all examples of how political parties are trying to discourage civilians from voting by inducing violence and fear in an attempt to prevent the balance from tipping in favour of the opposition.

What follows is a naughty little story that, upon reflection, is actually pretty tragic, but because this is a short film, it’s hideously rushed, making it less upsetting than it might have been if the relationships had been allowed to blossom fully in feature length. There’s a lot of plot here: a young man Lipakshi sets her eyes on (Armaan Ralhan), a deeply buried secret, revenge for long-ago wrongs, duplicity, and, of course, love and lust. It’s perhaps the most Bollywood of the films, the most grandiose. Khaitan’s heartland stories have become twistier and tougher to deal with since his instantly likeable debut, Humpty Sharma ki Dulhania. Majnu packs in so much – there are actually two twists – that it can feel kind of contrived and pulpy in terms of aesthetics at the best. I definitely saw the first twist coming. Is it gimmicky? Yes. Can it be said for the closing two minutes of the film too? Hell yes! However, despite its operatic grandiose, it’s a well-made film, and it’s entertainingly told. And it’s also clever: the twist made me revisit some of the earlier dialogue in a new light. For instance, Babloo tells Lipakshi on their wedding night that if he had had his way, ‘aapki jagah koi aur hota.’ Later, this particular choice of words began to make a different kind of sense. The next film is Raj Mehta’s Khilauna. This film is structured as a whodunnit, but an interesting one: the actual crime is just as much a mystery as who committed it. There is a back-and-forth structure that contrasts the police investigation in the present with the sequence of events in the past. Nushrratt Bharuccha (once again, I have not misspelt this) is a housemaid named Meenal who works hard to put her much-younger sister (the very talented Inayat Verma) through school. Meenal also has a maybe-maybe-not sexual relationship with the local istrivaala Sushil (Abhishek Banerjee). The three of them go up against their employers, who live in a high-rise.

The title is a metaphor: in the capitalist dog-eat-dog world, everybody is a khilauna (toy) for someone else. The rich want to use the poor, the poor want to use the rich. Even familial relationships are not free of personal-gain machinations, this film wants to tell us. This last comes through in the closing twist – but while the motivation is clear, the twist itself is totally unconvincing. It’s a case of the writer (Sumit Saxena, who has writing credits on all but one of the films) trying to be too clever- trying insanely hard to pull off a Roald Dahl- and unfortunately it feels so fake and forced. Also, the reveal comes from one of Meenal’s employers and...how did she know? I don’t know. It’s the makers sacrificing coherence for shock value, which on the other hand lands positively because I doubt any viewer will easily forget the image of a pressure cooker spewing blood - yes you heard me right. The third film is Neeraj Ghaywan’s Geeli Pucchi, which means ‘wet kiss’. This is a most unusual film from a most unusual filmmaker. The protagonist is Bharti Mandal (Konkona Sensharma in a role that finally pushes her acting chops thoroughly after so long), a queer Dalit woman – which means three separate strains of marginalisation. Sensharma loses herself in this character, getting the walk and mannerisms just right, and letting Bharti’s hurt cross her face in just the right amounts. Bharti works in a factory and hopes for the job of a data collector, but this goes to Priya Sharma (Aditi Rao Hydari, who is both beautiful and terrific). What are Priya’s advantages? Well, she is Brahmin.

The film is not subtle about its characters’ castes. One of Bharti’s co-workers, Dashrath, tells her that her surname is Mandal, not Banerjee, and that that should make it clear to her that she’ll never have Priya’s job. Later, the film is not subtle about Bharti’s queerness either: she watches a video of her old girlfriend, in which she kisses her. These are interesting decisions because the film’s climax (the ‘twist’, if you will, which is more an I-didn’t-see-this-coming) is very subtle. I thought about it a lot: but why? But why would she…? But how could she…? And I realise that I have an urban, caste-blind view myself: I don’t see the centuries of continued oppression that Bharti does, I don’t feel the sting of losing a job simply because of my surname, and my self-worth doesn’t therefore depend on my career security. When films place the marginalised in the centre, there is often a desire to portray them as good and valorous, or as suffering victims. Hollywood films are rife with the ‘bury your gays’ trope: films about queer lives often end with them dying and eliciting our sympathy. But in Geeli Pucchi, Ghaywan is not trying to pump sympathy out of us. In fact, he doesn’t want us to project emotions onto the screen, but rather to evaluate our own in-built caste-related biases, and our understanding of the intersection of identities.

A sweet and tentative romance develops between Priya and Bharti (and a lovely song plays over their time together), but when Priya’s mother-in-law tells her to be careful about her friendships because her father-in-law is going to be a mahant soon, we know this relationship is doomed. What, Ghaywan and Saxena ask, is important to someone who faces the levels of discrimination that Bharti does? Every day, she goes up against Brahmanical patriarchy. Every day, her ‘manly’ clothes and rough exterior render her – tragically – vulnerable to her co-workers’ taunts. Every day, she is reminded that even if she has accepted her own queerness, the world around her is so innately homophobic that any romantic attachment she forms comes with more than a tinge of uncertainty. In such a world, in such a life, is it surprising that Bharti resorts to any means to hold on to the one thing that gives her stability: her career? This definitely will be one of the most talked about films of the year but having seen Juice I only wish Ghaywan had been tighter in his editing. That is not saying that I did not like this film. For me it will always be a close second to Juice and Masaan (I am sorry I cannot choose between those two masterpieces and you shall rot in hell if you make me choose too).

The last film of the anthology is Ankahi, directed by debutant Kayoze Irani. I weirdly enough loved this film. It’s probably because it’s the easiest of the four to like and Irani constructs it beautifully. It tells the story of Natasha (Shefali Shah is, of course, devastatingly good), the mother of a teenager (Sara Arjun) who is going deaf. Natasha has learnt sign to speak to her daughter, but her husband Rohan ( an as usual stiff Tota Roy Choudhury) is too busy trying to get the girl a cochlear implant to learn sign language.

And then Natasha forms a tender relationship with a photographer (Manav Kaul finally as a swoon-worthy romantic lead), who is also deaf. Kaul and Shah are simply two of the best actors in Hindi movies and it is a wonder to me that nobody has cast them together before. In the space of this small film, and with no spoken dialogue, they create a tangible chemistry with their hands and their eyes.

Ankahi is about fleeting moments, things recognised but unsaid, and how important truth and communication are to relationships. But it doesn’t beat you on the head with these musings: they are merely hinted at before the film comes to a close with a scene where Kaul and Shah tell each other things they never say.It is likely Ankahi will leave some viewers unsatisfied – where is the closure? What is the future of these connections and communications? True to the film’s title, Irani leaves these questions unanswered. But this is what the short film format allows – an exploration of a situation or a relationship, without the burden of character arcs or conflict and resolution or beginning, middle and end. Ankahi simply is.

Ajeeb Daastaans is long at two hours and twenty-two minutes. But the films themselves rarely feel stretched – and, of course, you can go away in between the shorts and come back. Like all anthologies, some of the films will hold up better on close examination and others are enjoyable only when viewed from a distance (the less we try to unpack Karan Johar’s horror short in Ghost Stories, for instance, the better although I will guiltily admit to loving its absurd sense of humour) But I definitely some of these, even if they feed less into each other than I would have liked. I will be thinking about them, especially Geeli Puchhi and Ankahi.
