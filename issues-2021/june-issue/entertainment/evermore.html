 Taylor Swift’s ‘Evermore’: The Winter Album Of Your Wildest Dreams


Mohan Rajagopal

May 1, 2021   |   Entertainment


The internet surely has not forgotten the pop culture phenomenon that was the surprise announcement and release of Taylor Swift’s ‘folklore’, her eighth studio album that charted alternative folk music completely unexpected from the pop star. Mere months after ‘folklore’ (an Album of the Year nominee at the 2021 Grammy’s), Swift leisurely dropped another bomb, this time to do with her ninth album, ‘evermore’. In her own words, she found herself “standing on the edge of the folklorian woods with a choice: to turn and go back or to travel further into the forest of this music”. Fortunately for the rest of us, she chose to wander deeper in.

While ‘folklore’ represented the muted beauty of spring and summer, ‘evermore’ completes the seasonal anthology with its depiction of wild autumn and winter. The songs on the album have a certain liberation to them, and indeed the metaphor used by Swift herself about the forest of music is the best way to describe its quality: ‘evermore’ exhibits similarities in style and content to its sister ‘folklore’, but this time it is untamed and illimited in its tone. With energetic drum beats and syncopating piano melodies joining the calmer symphonies, the resulting album is one that personally appeals more than ‘folklore’ to my pop sensibilities.



Undoubtedly, Swift has stepped up her game in terms of her lyrics as well as the stories she manages to convey. Like its predecessor, ‘evermore’ intertwines tales of fiction with the musician’s own life, although the stories are now more mature and melancholic. Long gone are the days of teenage love triangles and dancing at the disco, with Swift exploring broken marriages, winter depression, and even murder.

Yes, you read that right: ‘no body, no crime’, a collaboration with HAIM, features a twisted tale of an affair gone wrong, culminating in the narrator murdering the adulterer in order to avenge her fallen friend. With twists and turns fit to rival any full-length murder mystery novel, I had my jaw on the floor as Swift unravelled her account in a country-pop ballad, accompanied by police sirens and dialogue to propel the plot-line. Meanwhile, ‘cowboy like me’ chronicles a romance of two swindlers who trick elderly rich folks into relationships, only to steal their wealth and run off together. Akin to the ‘august’- ‘cardigan’- ‘betty’ trilogy of ‘folklore’, ‘dorothea’ and ‘tis the damn season’ narrate Dorothea’s return home for the holidays, after her attempts at making it big as an actress in Los Angeles. At home, she rekindles a love affair with her high school boyfriend who has been living in their small town all these years.

The lead single, ‘willow’, presents a plucky exploration of the attempts at first trying to enter a relationship with someone. The mind-games and “bait and switch” used in such flirtatious advances are seen by Swift as a witchy ritual to enchant your crush into falling in love with you. At the other end of the spectrum, ‘happiness’ offers a glimpse into the end of a relationship. While it is a breakup song, it lacks the quintessential bitterness that Swift allegedly is famous for. Instead, ‘happiness’ is a mature track that refuses a black and white view, recognising that in spite of the pain and misery of a broken heart, there is still joy and comfort in looking back on a failed relationship. Dichotomies of this sort are found several times in ‘evermore’, especially with ‘marjorie’, a song addressed to Swift’s late grandmother. The song sees the singer regret her inability to be with Marjorie anymore, who used to be an opera singer. In fact, her vocals have even been sampled in the final chorus, when her operatic soprano accompanies Swift opining that she could hear her grandmother singing to her after all these days.

The more upbeat brilliance of Jack Antonoff’s production shines through in ‘gold rush’, my personal favourite track. It sees the speaker dive into a glittery quickfire daydream about a possible life with her potential love interest, only to decide at the end to snap out of it and return to her senses. Swift expertly uses the California Gold Rush as a metaphor for her complex romance, when individuals from all across America moved to the state in order to get their hands on the gold. Here, the significant other is compared to the gold who is wanted and desired by everyone around him, while the narrator is forced to look on in jealousy. The track abounds in cleverly crafted lyrics and angelic choirs that mimic the sparkle and shine of real gold.

Such themes of bitter love affairs echo throughout the album, culminating in ‘tolerate it’, inspired by the novel ‘Rebecca’ by Daphne du Maurier. The primary conflict occurs between the narrator and her husband who refuses to “celebrate” her love, and only “tolerates” it. It is a song that is sure to hit hard for anyone who has ever felt patronised and belittled by others, describing the speaker waiting by the door like a “little kid” for her partner who is “so much older and wiser”. By far, though, the climax of moroseness comes about only in the second track, ‘champagne problems’. Co-written with her longtime boyfriend Joe Alwyn, Swift croons a piano ballad about a rejected marriage proposal by a woman suffering from mental health issues. Unable to come to terms with the end of the relationship, the proposer’s friends and family brush it off as being caused by the singer’s alcoholism (hence, champagne problems) and her failing mental health. With deceptively simple production featuring a straightforward four-chord piano instrumental, it transports the listener to the imagery within the very song, especially with an impassioned bridge that has Swift rattle off the entire story in the space of a single minute. In my opinion, ‘gold rush’ and ‘champagne problems’ mark the very apotheosis of Swift’s songwriting abilities in ‘evermore’, with creative concepts, catchy melodies, and production that shines on its own without detracting from the lyrics and melodies.

In my previous analysis of ‘folklore’, I had remarked that the final track ‘hoax’ was strange as it ended on an uncharacteristically miserable note. Perhaps it was an intentional nod to the album being only the first of a duology, as the eponymous closing track of ‘evermore’ provides the much-needed closure. Unlike most of the other songs, ‘evermore’ is one narrated by Swift herself, set within a period of depression and blues propelled by the current lockdown as well as the 2020 presidential elections. Initially, it starts off despondent, claiming that her “pain would be forevermore”. Another melancholic piano ballad, the slow chords suddenly give way to a more upbeat riff, penned by none other than Alwyn himself, and Swift’s vocals are now replaced by Justin Vernon of Bon Iver. The sudden increase in pace is seen to symbolise Swift’s change of mood from her earlier dejection, until the piano slows down to its original tempo once again, albeit with a crucial change: she ends the album with the hopeful closing line, “I knew this pain wouldn’t be forevermore.” While the album as a whole may have been even gloomier than ‘folklore’, its ending betrays its true nature as an album of finding light amidst all-encompassing darkness.

On ‘evermore’, Swift makes a subtle reference to her sister album in the lyric, “My mind turns your life into folklore”, which perfectly sums up the pursuit of art and creative expression, and hence the purpose of the two albums themselves. In all her songwriting glory, Swift has the power to transmit the lives of those around her into a long-lasting legacy of musicality, but the true beauty of the lyric lies in its more mundane applications. We need not be the artist of the decade to possess such abilities, but the mere remembrances in our very mind are enough to create fables and tales to be passed down from generation to generation. Through her album duology, Swift drives home the power of storytelling as a vessel to immortalise our loved ones in the form of folklore, to flourish and thrive within memories and art for evermore.
