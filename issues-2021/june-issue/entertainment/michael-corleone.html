 Michael Corleone: The Will to Change (or Not)


Pradyumn Nahata

June 1, 2021   |   Entertainment


When Al Pacino, in the Godfather III (a very decent movie, despite the constant repugnance on the matter), said “Just when I thought I was out, they pull me back in,” he didn’t only realize what the major theme of the trilogy (and quite majorly, his adult life) was, he subconsciously hinted at a major philosophical debate which has persevered through the ages.



For those even amateurly familiarized with philosophy, this may allude to the free will debate. Are we really capable of change? I mean, sure we are. It’s how the world works. Just because someone chokes on McDonald’s 4 times a week doesn’t mean they aren’t eventually going to shift to gluten-free burgers (what a genius idea!)

All jokes aside, I want to clarify that this article dwells on the issue of moral responsibility for change, and the capability to take control of it: through the beautifully written fall arc of The Godfather’s Michael Corleone. It is a trivial observation of philosophical conflict in a movie, the name of which is exhausted in conversations and commentaries. Let us begin.

It is accurate, as the existentialist philosophers say, that Existence precedes Essence: the widely revered theory that your interests and actions mould who you are, making identity a by-product which flows alongside your life (and not a component present from before your birth) Does that mean you’re in control of your essence? According to the Existentialists, yes. According to ancient and modern determinist theories? No.

Not surprisingly, who you are is, in fact, controlled to a large extent by the way your brain works and interprets things, involving the impulses it sends across to your consciousness. Now, these impulses, in turn, are carefully designed by the consciousness through the perception of past experiences. These experiences range from hostile encounters to value education, and majorly determine who you are and how you view the world. However, most of these experiences are… designs of external agencies, many of them being physical in the sense that they can be scientifically predicted. In contrast, Epicurus, an ancient Greek philosopher said-

“Some things happen of necessity, others by chance, others through our own agency. Necessity destroys responsibility and chance is inconstant; whereas our own actions are autonomous, and it is to them that praise and blame naturally attach.”

On one side, you have a school of thought proposing free will, stimulating us to examine whether we are in control of our actions, to which many of us respond “Aye.” But on the other and more convincing hand, proving to us that our identity is shaped by past experiences with foreign stimuli or even other people, who in turn were stimulated by predictable physical changes to act in the way they did. Even the brain, which controls our impulses and actions, is biological- making it a physical agent. This strikes an existentialist dilemma- are we in control of who we are? Don’t we feel free? Don’t we make our own decisions- significant or insignificant, consciously or less consciously- and in the process, undergo experience and change?

It’s complicated. Let’s take a look at Michael Corleone’s character arc.

Initially, Michael is an outlier as compared to his family, all the patriarchs of which (his brothers and father) run an empire of crime. He estimates authority, but also values objective morality. He understands and explicitly states that he is not like his family, which is quite believable considering his participation as a navy-man in the Second World War. Michael is free from the natures of his family, and wills to be so. However, when Don Corleone, his father is shot several times and hereby weakened by antagonizing forces in the city, Michael formulates a responsibility that he feels he is obliged to take. He starts to nudge his brothers to involve him in the family business, ultimately killing those who ordered the attempted ambush on his father, and becoming a true Corleone. All this while he has started neglecting his old habits, duty to his country, and ethical, moral and romantic attachments to Kay Adams, his honest and charming partner, who kept Michael on the path of goodness.



Besides, Michael is now a murderer, and scarcely regards it as traumatic or a hindrance to his values. Thus begins one of the most Satanic falls into depravity and turpitude in narrative history- a picture of how environment degrades man and man, his environment. Jump to Godfather III, and you see that Michael knows of his fall from grace: he is conscious and deeply regretful. He is partly in shock- he wonders when he was transformed into his father, nay, a man even more barbaric- devoid of honour and moral composure; a man capable of killing his own blood.

From this description and my deduction, it follows that many people naturally lean towards allotting Michael (or individual agents in civilization) responsibility for his (their) actions. If Michael was revered for being independent through choice in the beginning of the franchise, it is only reasonable that he be solely burdened with culpability for his fall. Even Michael, in fact, says in the screenplay a line which was cut out of the final product-

“A man has to choose what he will be. I believe that.”

He says this in conversation with his father, Don Corleone. The Don then goes onto say-

“Believe in a family. Can you believe in your country? Those Pezzonovante of the State who decide what we shall do with our lives? Who declare wars they wish us to fight in to protect what they own. Do you put your fate in the hands of men whose only talent is that they tricked a bloc of people to vote for them? Michael, in five years the Corleone family can be completely legitimate. Very difficult things have to happen to make that possible. I can't do them anymore, but you can, if you choose to.”

The next line in the screenplay states-

Michael listens.

If we look (or read) carefully enough, this is the conversation where Michael is almost wholly converted to Evil. Mind you, he doesn’t choose to be. But he does sign off on it. He similarly signed off on joining the army because he respected his father but not his business (read the screenplay for more insights.) He steps up to kill for the similar reason: family takes precedence. His environment constantly pushes him towards spiritual devolution, whether it be through Don Corleone, his brothers’ hotheadedness and subsequent incompetence, or the antagonists throughout the three films. His first wife was bombed and killed in front of him because of prejudice against himself. Environment degrades man. Nonetheless, Michael changes again- and this time for the better, though it is too late. He changes primarily because of Kay’s participation (and lack thereof) in his life, in addition to his witness of the murder and decay he has caused around him.
