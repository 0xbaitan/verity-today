 Satyajit Ray's Portrayal of Women in Film


Anoushka Dasgupta

May 1, 2021   |   Entertainment




Satyajit Ray is a man who needs no introduction. He is a master craftsman, who has won international accolades for his films. A pinnacle of Bengali cinema, his versatility is unmatched, not only with filmography but with story writing, music, and other forms of artistry. He can weave a story that enthralls an audience of all ages.

Ray’s depictions of female characters stray far from a subservient housewife whose persona has been completely crushed under the weight of societal pressure. They are typically soft-spoken but witty, intelligent individuals who have a strong moral compass and an abundance of honesty. His characters are also intersectional- they exist from various strata of society, which helps preserve the relatability of his films.



When you think of some of Ray’s iconic female protagonists, one film comes to mind straightaway- Charulata (1964), an iconic film based on a story written by Rabindranath Tagore, whose exemplary portrayal of ‘Charu’ in Nastanirh (The Broken Nest) was initially received with mixed feelings from Bengali aficionados. Ray later brought her alive on screen with a stunning portrayal by Madhabi Mukherjee. It is the story of a woman grappling with the burden of loneliness and repressed passion. We see her as a poised and distinguished woman with a restless longing stirring inside her and her bookshelves stocked with novels. She spreads her wings from being a fervent reader to an adept writer when her work is published in a magazine despite the mixed feelings from her brother-in-law Amal (Soumitra Chatterjee). Charu is a character who undergoes an emotional, sexual, and intellectual awakening within the four walls of her domesticity- an inspiration for many women in the 60s.

Another depiction by Madabhi Mukherjee shows the evolution of Arati, who goes from elegant housewife to working woman in Mahanagar (1963). This endeavour to support her family is met with the stone-cold disapproval of her father-in-law and fosters a green-eyed monster of jealousy in her husband Subrata (Anil Chatterjee). For Arati though, this is her chance to explore new horizons and a world that is rapidly developing before her very eyes. Her fresh persona is depicted by a single tube of lipstick, which she wears at work but wipes off before coming home. This subtle use of symbolism is used by Ray in several other instances like the single hairpin in his debut film Pather Panchali (1955) and needless to say, it is a stroke of genius.



Speaking of Pather Panchali, this is another of Ray’s films which is revisited again and again. A stunning debut that overcame several obstacles and took over three years to film, Pather Panchali also features strong women roles. The first film in the Apu trilogy, Ray explores characters like Apu’s resilient sister Durga (Uma Dasgupta) and his stoic, yet silently emotional mother, Sarbojaya (Karuna Banerjee). The later films feature his colourful and candid wife Aparna (played by Sharmila Tagore) who’s like a breath of fresh air despite her premature demise in Aparajito (1956).



It would feel incomplete to talk about Ray without mentioning some of his smaller women roles- those who effortlessly stole the show despite being a side character. Like Aparna (Sharmila Tagore) in Aranyer Din Ratri (1970) who quietly let the male protagonist Ashim (Soumitra Chattopadhyay) win at a memory game to avoid shattering his frail male ego; it’s these characters that add a certain charm to his films.



Ray’s portrayal of women gives them an underlying, yet superior sense of morality and judgement- a stark contrast with the often oblivious or impulsive male heroes. That being said, both his male and female counterparts usually mesh with the film effortlessly, and exist parallelly to one another. His characters are expressed not by diminishing or undermining his male characters, but by creating a harmonious coalescence between them and the film. There’s a reason why his films are still celebrated today, and it is because of his ability to show these relatable characters through engaging tales.
