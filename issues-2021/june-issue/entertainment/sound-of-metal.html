 Sound of Metal


Pradyumn Nahata

May 1, 2021   |   Entertainment


You wake up one fine day, turning through another awaited page of a truly fulfilling life- you’re free to look at how the pale sun polishes the surface of your surroundings symbolizing your blossoming joy of being thankful for life and sip your hot latte with a hint of cream which serves as a near-perfect supplementary device to flourish in the light cold weather. All your senses awaken and jubilate as you put on your clothes for work. But there’s something different- what is it? You queerly cannot hear the gay chirping of the woodpigeons nor the busy horns on the streets which characterize the stimulating mornings in your town. You get a call from your mother- but it’s strange- she’s inaudible. You try to tell her to unmute her cell’s microphone- but the words don’t come out. Suddenly, it all dawns on you and the world flips upside down.

Okay- now, Sound Of Metal, what a movie. This movie, and a few other brilliant ones which came out in a truly tragic year for the industry- might just make you believe that the Academy Awards in April won’t be all that numb.



Riz Ahmed (you may know him from The Night Of, Nightcrawler) plays Ruben Stone, a drummer by profession- who performs for a very, very angry heavy-metal duo Blackgammon, with the other member being his significant other - Lou, played by Olivia Cooke (Ready Player One). The film starts, and you do not know what to expect. While the beginning is vague, it gives a thrilling radiance as dim and variant lights flash in a frenzy with the cameras moving around, and fixating themselves on one of Blackgammon’s tour performances with Olivia Cooke singing lines ranging from “There’s something I want to eat.” to “I’m sorry you’re cheap and violent.” (Art.) However, there’s something very significant about this opening sequence- Lou’s singing does not have perfect clarity- it’s somewhat indistinct and muffled up. Subtitles say the same, and it serves as a forerunner to one of the very important decisions the director (Darius Marder, Sound of Metal is the second film directed by him) makes in the film. We’re led into Ruben Stone’s repetitive but peaceful world of metal music and angry drumming, with his girlfriend and him on an amateur tour whilst living in his somewhat wholesome 70s trailer.

One fine morning, Ruben wakes up in jubilation, amazed by his life like our anonymous character at the beginning of this article. And very much like that character, Ruben faces a most daunting fate when he consults a doctor in desperation and finds out that he’s almost completely lost his hearing- and narcotics in addition to (probable) involvement of the constant exposure to the music which he plays, have been factors leading up to it. From hereon, the main focus of the movie becomes character development as Ruben kicks off on his journey of being deaf. As expected, he doesn’t take it well. He’s aggressive, hostile and in denial of the situation which has knocked on his door. I wouldn’t know about how this captures the crisis one goes through after one loses one of their biological senses, but it’s definitely not a touchy-feely reaction without chaotic outburst. And the movie realizes that. It was originally supposed to be more of a documentary to be released a few years earlier by another director with Darius Marder on their team, but I’m pretty sure it does much better as a drama feature, because the writing is absolutely immaculate and the actors execute it well.

Capturing one as he goes through losing his hearing is not an easy task. At all. But the directing team makes a very, very strong attempt. As the film progresses, we see Lou frantically try to help Ruben, and eventually going reluctantly to Belgium after the host of a deaf community they decide upon tells her that Ruben needs to be free from distraction- in order to cope with the loss that has just crossed him. Here, the theme of the conflicts between consensual love and self-detachment is explored- a theme forming one of the bases of many insightful spiritual texts. Ruben involves himself in the deaf community and gradually learns a little to live with himself.

Marder uses scenes of Ruben in the same environment in a “before/after” sort of fashion to contrast our lead’s progress and maximize the evidence behind it. However, Ruben has still failed to reject the assumption that being deaf is a handicap. After doing what he needs to to get a highly costly cochlear aid against the advice of the community leader, he goes to Lou’s father’s house in Belgium- and has a heart-to-heart with him. All this while, his cochlear implants have been giving him a most severe irritation, with feedback buzzing itself in his eyes constantly.

The implants are a disappointment, and actually outweigh what Ruben went through in terms of difficulty hearing. When Lou comes home, Ruben and her are meeting after a long, long time. But it’s not at all how you would expect two long-lost lovers with their love deterred by “handicaps” to meet. It’s slightly awkward, and very disappointing to the immediate audience- like life. After a party at Lou’s place and spending the night with her- a night where they both decided that parting was the best option for them- one of the most heart touching scenes of the movie takes precedence (“You changed my life, Lou.”), Ruben leaves, the following morning. Here comes the most important part of the movie. Ruben sits on a bench in the city, as life revolves around him- people having a bike ride in the morning, pedestrians indulging their mildly entertaining self-absorbed routine- as Ruben’s cochlear implant keeps making his ears buzz with feedback. The elemental beauty of nature and life around him is ruined by the implants. And Ruben simply takes his sound processors off. All the chatter of the city and his processor is gone. He looks around with a new perspective on life and experiences the true beauty of detachment and observation- no matter audible or inaudible. Life is tough, and ugly. But he’s made a decision, and is determined to live with it.

I love this movie for everything it is- a heavily accurate representation of a pertinent issue, a cinematic mosaic of talented actors and sound producers, a 2-hour watch of excellent cinematography, but all of these are toppled by the writing and the themes that the writing explores.

The themes met in spirituality of self-detachment and letting go take precedence in this motion picture, and they resonate with everyone in their audience- peculiarly having a universal effect through a movie specific to a community of people. Ruben represents the modern man attached to life and all it’s virtues, in love with what he does, and empty when made devoid of it. His implants symbolize the attachments to his old life he still bears after months of self-discipline, and him taking them off represents self-detachment and inner peace. When the camera zooms in on the local church at the end, it represents Ruben having resonated with himself and a superior being, if there is one. This is all obviously a very spiritual overview, but it’s interpretation of the aspect of the universality cannot be compromised because of the very impactful detailing and construction of Ruben and his challenges.

A true masterpiece, Sound of Metal is sure to be one of the Nominees For Best Picture in the Academy Awards. Riz Ahmed with his stellar performance, is more or less bound to receive a few awards for his spectacular portrayal of Ruben, with a very serious and prospective crack at Best Actor.
