 Women in the Pop Music Industry


Mohan Rajagopal

June 1, 2021   |   Entertainment


Over the past few years and decades, it has undoubtedly always been women that have dominated the music industry, especially within the pop sphere. At both the 2020 and 2021 Grammy Awards, for instance, all of the Big Four categories were swept by women, In spite of this indisputable monopoly, both the industry itself and the general public have paid these female artists dust in a culture that is rife with sexism and misogyny. The men and women of the music business seem to be operating on two different planes of existence, and while one is allowed to get by on the bare minimum of effort, the other is forced to constantly evolve in a circus act of pleasing the crowds while staying true to one’s brand. Double standards continue to be imposed on these artists which police every aspect of their lives, be it professional or personal.



A cursory glance at the stage tours and album eras of male versus female artists will expose a glaring disparity in the amount of work and effort expected to be put in by either gender. Most male artists are allowed to continue within the same style of music and similar aesthetics for each album release cycle; although this isn’t necessarily a bad thing, it becomes destructive when juxtaposed against the unrealistic expectations placed on the women. Artists like Taylor Swift, Ariana Grande and Katy Perry are seen to be constantly evolving their genres and styles because stagnating in a similar avenue as their previous era would label them a lightning rod for criticism by the media and public alike. Ed Sheeran and Shawn Mendes are notoriously celebrated for the uniformity in their music with a cult following across the globe, and it is especially unfair that the women are forced to put in a greater amount of work for an equal (and often even lower) level of recognition.

Not only does the sexism extend to the type of music created, but also the lyrical content. More often than not, women writing about heartbreak and romance leads to their manifestation as a lightning rod for slutshaming, something that has dominated the careers of artists such as Swift and Grande. On a radio show, Swift spoke out about the issue:

“You’re going to have people who are going to say, “Oh, you know, like, she just writes songs about her ex-boyfriends.” And I think frankly that’s a very sexist angle to take. No one says that about Ed Sheeran. No one says that about Bruno Mars. They’re all writing songs about their exes, their current girlfriends, their love life, and no one raises the red flag there.”

The same unfounded criticism has also found its way to Olivia Rodrigo, Swift’s protegee who found new success with her breakout hit single, ‘driver’s license’. Having released only two songs so far, Rodrigo has already attracted the same hate for music about teenage love and breakups, to which she recently responded in an interview with The Guardian:

“[There has been a] sexist criticism of songwriters like me being told that they only write songs about boys. I’m a teenage girl, I write about stuff that I feel really intensely – and I feel heartbreak and longing really intensely – and I think that’s authentic and natural. I don’t really understand what people want me to write about; do you want me to write a song about income taxes? How am I going to write an emotional song about that?”

If a barely eighteen year old girl cannot help but attract misogynistic commentary on her music, it isn’t too difficult to understand what the more seasoned women in the industry often have to go through on a daily basis. Be it awards, commercial success, or simply the quality of the music they put out: women have been running the music industry for the past decade. When will the patriarchy come to terms with it?
