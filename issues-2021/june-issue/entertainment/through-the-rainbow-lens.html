 Through The Rainbow Lens: LGBT+ Representation In The Film Industry


Mohan Rajagopal

June 1, 2021   |   Entertainment


In a bid to better understand the changing dynamics of the queer community, Procter and Gamble, in partnership with the Gay and Lesbian Alliance Against Defamation (GLAAD), undertook a survey entitled “LGBT+ Inclusion in Advertising and Media”. The reports declared that non-LGBT individuals were more likely to be accepting of the community and its activism if they had recently been exposed to LGBT+ representation in the media (80% of such individuals supported the community), as opposed to those who had not (70% of such individuals were in support). These findings can be epitomized in the gay rights movement sparked in Kenya, where the depiction of a lesbian character in the Kenyan film Rafiki catapulted the Kenyan High Court to organize a vote regarding the decriminalization of same-sex intercourse in 2019.

Although the vote was unfortunately defeated, it poses an interesting and important question regarding the representation of the LGBT+ community in entertainment media, specifically films: both the quantity as well as the quality of representation are necessary aspects to be considered. It is for this reason that GLAAD has been conducting yearly reports on LGBT+ representation since 2013, with its 2019 report providing mixed results on the community’s success. While 18.2% of all major studio films had some form of LGBT+ representation overall, a 5.4% increase from the previous year, the numbers do not bode too well from a cross-sectional perspective. With a 19% increase, lesbian representation stands at 55% of all-inclusive films, but bisexuality is woefully under-represented at 15%, and not a single major studio film featured any transgender characters at all. Additionally, the portrayal of LGBT+ people of colour also decreased from 57% the year before, to a current 42%: only 7% of all such characters were Latinx.



In order to assess the quality of representation, GLAAD constructed its own version of the Bechdel Test, titled the Vito Russo Test, in honour of its co-founder, which assesses films based on three criteria. The film must contain a character that is identifiably LGBT+, their sexuality must not be their predominant character trait, and they must be tied into the plot in a significant manner, such that their removal would warrant a drastic upheaval. A whopping 65% of all the inclusive films passed the test, but phenomena such as queerbaiting still pose a serious problem in the industry.

Queerbaiting is a marketing technique in which creators hint at, but do not actually depict: same-sex romance or other LGBTQ representation. This is done in order to attract a queer or straight-ally audience with relationships that appeal to them, while also avoiding the alienation of other consumers. Major films like Captain America and Star Wars, as well as television shows such as Riverdale and Sherlock, have come under fire for allegations of such appropriation.

The matter of representation presents polarising results: while there are more lesbian and LGBT+ characters, overall, the B, T, and Q members of the community remain criminally under-represented, as do the people of colour. In the years to come, only inter-sectional growth of the movement will ensure the equal and just representation of humanity in the glaring spotlight of Hollywood.
