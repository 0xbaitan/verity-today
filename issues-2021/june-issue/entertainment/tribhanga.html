 Tribhanga


Anwesh Banerjee

May 1, 2021   |   Entertainment


In Tribhanga, Anuradha Nayantara Apte (hold on to that big name, we’ll return to it), played by Kajol, is a dancer and film star who at one point likens each of the women in her family to a dance pose. Her mother Nayantara (Tanvi Azmi) is abhanga, slightly off-balance, off-kilter. Her daughter Masha (Mithila Palkar) is samabhanga, completely balanced. And she herself is tribhanga, which is the posture resembling an S: the knees go one way, the torso another, the head tilted: she is ‘tedhi, medhi, crazy but sexy’.



But the film’s name is not Abhanga-Samabhanga-Tribhanga; it is simply Tribhanga. I thought about this for a while and it struck me that Anu may liken her family to different bhangas, but in reality, all three women are in the tribhanga zone. Take her mother, whom she refers to as ‘Nayan’ and never ‘aai’. Nayan is a fêted Hindi writer (she’s a language whiz in general, having translated her own novel Kaai into English), who was a fiery feminist in a time being a fiery feminist meant you were a whore. Nayan was married with two children (Anu and her brother Ravi/Robindro), but her lifeblood was writing: she lived to write and wrote to live, and this caused terrible friction with her mother-in-law and eventually even with her well-meaning husband. So she took the children and left, carving a life for herself. She decided that since she was single-handedly raising Anu and Ravi, since their father didn’t feel comfortable keeping in touch, she would give the children her name. Anuradha Joshi was now to be known as Anuradha Nayantara Apte. And while this was an admirable move, it was two generations too early: Anu ends up being laughed at at school for having a different name from her father, for having divorced parents. (I say two generations specifically because I know people my age who carry their divorced mothers’ names with pride.) Later, when Nayan remarries, she is so blinded by love that she doesn’t see the abuse Anu suffers at her new husband’s hands, even though it happens right under her nose.

Anu grows up bitter at what she sees as her mother’s negligence and self-absorption. She insists that Nayan instigated chaos in her and her brother’s lives with her free-spirited lifestyle. She decides she will shield her daughter from what she suffered as a child. So she never marries, has a string of affairs (she believes ‘monogamy is injurious to health’), and never lets her boyfriend stay over. She has a much healthier relationship with Masha than she does with Nayan. And yet, in the film’s most affecting scene, we come to see that like it or not, some of Nayan’s parenting and approach to life has rubbed off on Anu.

And Masha has reacted to the influence of these two fiercely independent women by running in practically the opposite direction: in the desire to give her own children the ‘stable’ and ‘normal’ family neither she nor her mother had, she has married into the kind of large, conservative joint family where the women are expected to cover their heads when men and the elders are in the room. It comes through in the costumes – Anu dresses trendily in flowing shrugs, colourful tops and embroidered jeans, her hair in a messy bun; Masha is nearly always in salwar-kameezes with a bindi to match, and perfectly straightened hair.

The story of these women is told through the framing device of Nayan’s autobiography, which she is writing with the help of a young writer named Milan (Kunaal Roy Kapur). The characters are brought together when she suddenly slips into a coma and Milan rushes her to hospital. These two devices – the narration of one’s life to a biographer/therapist and the illness/death of someone bringing the family together – are very, very been-there-done-that. Writer-director Renuka Shahane does her best to tweak them to mould her story, such as when Anu’s reminiscences about her childhood are interspersed with recordings of Nayan talking about the same time, or Anu, Nayan and Masha’s views on Masha’s marriage – spoken at different times – intercut with one another. But it still feels a bit awkward, especially when Milan conveniently keeps turning up to wring confessions out of everyone. The flashback scenes of Nayan’s struggles in her first husband’s home work better, crackling with tension; this may also be because they flow easily in Marathi. In the present, the characters speak largely in Hindi and quite a bit of English; while it is true that we often grow up and grow out of our mother tongues, speaking to our parents in the language we are educated in, this Marathi-Hindi transition is never fully explored.

And really, it should have been explored because this is a movie that is very conscious of language (it’s very writerly in that sense). Milan is given to speaking in the shuddh-est of shuddh Hindi; he frequently gets on Anu’s nerves for this. When he asks why she uses so many apshabd, she demands why he can’t just say gaali. His English, on the other hand, is a bit weak: Anu upbraids him severely for saying that Nayan wanted to be ‘burnt’ after she died. Meanwhile, in the recordings of Nayan, we hear her speak a beautiful, assured Hindi (and Tanvi Azmi makes the words roll off her tongue much better than Kunaal Roy Kapur, who occasionally gives the impression of a school kid who’s mugged up Hindi lines for annual day): she refers to the chasm that developed in her relationship with her daughter as khaai – which reminded me of the name of her book, Kaai. Even the subtitles have some fun: when Anu dismissively talks about dadajee-padajee, the titles read ‘grandfather-grandfather’.

Shahane’s script, while structurally formulaic, develops the central theme of the cycle of life through its characters. The idea that, like it or not, we become our parents when we become parents is a kind of universally accepted truth that even Philip Larkin wrote a poem about: ‘They fuck you up, your mum and dad,’ he wrote, but acknowledged, ‘But they were fucked up in their turn’. Here, too, Anu has to grapple with the fact that her mother doesn’t stop being her mother just because she stops calling her aai. Masha has to deal with what the examples of her mother and grandmother mean to her. Nayan spends most of the film in a coma, but we see that in her old age, she has had to come to terms with the fact that her blazingly feminist attitude to life may have actually had a negative impact on her children, and particularly Anu.

These are complex women and the actresses really get into them. Both Azmi and Palkar make an impression, one with her zest for life, the other with a kind of muted optimism. But it’s Kajol who gets the most interesting character, the one with two levels of conflict (as a daughter, as a parent), both of which are reflected back to her. Kajol is great when she doesn’t have to speak; but her line readings tend to be … Kajol. She seems to like to play this kind of loud, slightly brash woman, but she is unconvincing in a lot of her exasperated moments. She sounds like an extension of her interviews. But when she’s silent? When there’s nobody around but her mother in a coma? That’s when she lets her face talk, and those scenes are the ones that don’t let you dismiss her performance. It is tempting to imagine, though, what an actress with a felicity for dialogue might have done with Anu. Someone like Vidya Balan, perhaps – who played a very Nayan-esque character in Shakuntala Devi, an independent, self-absorbed, free-willed mother whose daughter comes to resent her.

It’s also tempting to imagine the autobiographical elements that might have gone into this screenplay: Shahane, an actress, is the daughter of theatre critic and bilingual writer Shanta Gokhale, but she has said that she and her mother have a wonderful personal relationship. Her direction here is a trifle shaky. Scenes often overstay their welcome, inching towards completion but dragging themselves out to get there. Occasionally they begin to resemble a soap opera, particularly when Shahane has to stage actual violence. A scene where a pregnant Anu is slapped by her boyfriend whom she then proceeds to whack with an umbrella comes off as embarrassingly amateurish. And it also doesn’t help that Shahane layers some pretty heavy music into charged scenes; several of the confrontations might have played better if left raw and unscored – instead they occasionally tip towards syrupy.

For all this, though, Tribhanga is remarkably efficient, barely stretching to an hour-and-a-half (I almost thought it was too short), making no detours for songs or – god forbid – love tracks. It’s ostensibly about mothers and daughters, but it’s really about being a parent and being a child. It’s also about what it really means to be ahead of one’s time: it’s always said as a compliment, but in actual context it can often turn out to be a very difficult thing. The film never demonises any of its characters, and we are left at the end without the satisfyingly pat ‘happy’ resolution. Sometimes, our personalities are at odds with our lives and that’s just how it is.
