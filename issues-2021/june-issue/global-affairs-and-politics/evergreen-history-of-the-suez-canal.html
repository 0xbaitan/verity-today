 Evergreen History of the Suez Canal


Ananya Singh

May 1, 2021   |   Global Affairs & Politics


It took 10 years and over 1.5 million workers to construct the Suez Canal and it took 1 day and 1 of the largest container ships to block it in 2021. 



The Ever Given is one of the largest golden-class container ships in the world and is operated by the Taiwanese Evergreen Marine Corp. It was bound for the Netherlands when a dust storm hit, leading to heavy winds and poor visibility in the passage from the Red Sea to the Mediterranean. This is supposed to be the primary factor causing the accident on March 23rd. However, there is much skepticism because the ship weighs as much as 220,000 tons when fully loaded and was built to withstand much stronger gusts. 

The ramifications of the blockage in the canal, which is believed to handle about 10 per cent of global maritime commercial traffic, have been huge. By the morning of March 29, hours before the Ever Given was freed, 367 vessels were waiting to pass through the canal. The traffic jam had held up nearly $10 billion in trade every day. Some shipowners had to reroute their vessels around the Cape of Good Hope, at the southern tip of Africa, adding weeks to shipping journeys. The Egyptian government had a vital interest in refloating the Ever Given as they receive billions in revenue from canal tolls.

The canal connects Egypt’s ports on the Mediterranean Sea to the Indian Ocean and enables direct shipping between Europe and Asia, cutting down voyage times by weeks. The idea of the modern Suez Canal picked up steam after Napoleon Bonaparte’s quixotic invasion of Egypt in 1798. He dreamt of constructing a quick passage to India. Decades later, in 1859, a French diplomat- Ferdinand de Lesseps secured financing from the French government and the permission of the Ottoman viceroyalty in Egypt for his Suez Canal Company to begin construction of what would become the Suez Canal. It was a huge undertaking. By one account, more than a million Egyptian peasants were forced into the project and tens of thousands perished, contracting diseases like cholera in circumstances likened to slave labor. In 1869, the canal was launched in a grand ceremony hosted by the Ottoman Khedive Ismail Pasha and six years later, with Ottoman Egypt saddled with debt, he sold off his shares in the Suez Canal Company to the British government. The cultivation of oil fields along the Persian Gulf in the early 20th century only underscored the strategic vitality of the canal for European powers.

Now, we come to what made the Suez Canal so infamous.

The Suez Crisis began on July 26, 1956, when Nasser announced the nationalization of the Suez Canal which had been owned and operated by a British-French enterprise since its construction. Although Nasser offered full economic compensation for the company, the British and French governments, were outraged by the move, as they held long suspicion of Nasser’s opposition to the increase of their political influence in the region. The Egyptian leader, in turn, resented what he saw as European efforts to preserve their colonial domination. This crisis was provoked by an American and British decision not to finance Egypt’s construction of the Aswan High Dam, as they had promised, in response to Egypt’s growing ties with communist Czechoslovakia and the Soviet Union. Nasser reacted to the American and British decision by declaring martial law in the canal zone and seizing control of the Suez Canal Company. He predicted that the tolls collected from ships passing through the canal would pay for the dam’s construction within five years. Britain and France feared that Nasser might close the canal and cut off shipments of petroleum flowing from the Persian Gulf to western Europe. When diplomatic efforts to settle the crisis failed, Britain and France secretly prepared military action (Operation Musketeer) to regain control of the canal and, if possible, to depose Nasser. On October 31st, Britain and France delivered an ultimatum calling on Egypt to open the Suez Canal and accept “temporary occupation by Anglo-French forces” of the Canal area. They bombarded Egyptian targets, and British and French paratroopers and amphibious forces landed on November 5th. On November 6th, 1956, the United Nations called a cease-fire. Both the USA and the USSR demanded Israeli, British and French withdrawal from the area. After lengthy negotiations, and, primarily as a result of American insistence, Israel agreed to withdraw on the following conditions: - Establishment of a United Nations Emergency Force (UNEF) in Sinai to protect Israel’s southern border guaranteed by a group of maritime nations, including the United States. In the end, Nasser emerged victorious while Israel lost the freedom to use the canal and Britain and France lost out further on their influence in the region. Eight years after the 1967 Six Day War, in 1975, Egyptian President Anwar-el-Sadat reopened the Suez Canal for global transport as a gesture of peace. 

Since then, the Suez Canal has remained a critical geo-strategic choke point for  global oil shipping and trade. While the freeing of the Ever Given has been a relief, it also calls into focus the need to have options ready to protect the fragility of global trade.
