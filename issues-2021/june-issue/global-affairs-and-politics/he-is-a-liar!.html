 "He is a Liar! A Mercenary Preaching the Gospel of Democracy" — The Prashant Kishor Story


Dhruv Chandra

June 1, 2021   |   Global Affairs & Politics


What if I told you that a man whose face you won't find on a single poster in Bengal, Karnataka, Gujarat, Punjab or for that matter, anywhere in India–is largely responsible for the governments that have run those states? In fact, even the current ruling national government and Prime Minister Modi owe part of their 2014 landslide general election victory to this man—Prashant Kishor, India’s foremost political analyst and the talk of the town, following Trinamool Congress (AITC/TMC) and DMK’s victories in the West Bengal and Tamil Nadu Legislative Assembly Elections, respectively.



Kishor, having worked as a public health activist and WHO-UN aid mission trainee in West Africa, caught the attention of then Gujarat Chief Minister Narendra Modi in 2011, having written a paper to prove health indices in India's most prosperous states (i.e., Gujarat) were as poor as its most backward states. The duo worked together through 2014, till BJP won the general elections, coming to power with Modi as head—the last time Kishor worked with the BJP or Modi.

The murmur in BJP circles was that Kishor had begun to take too much credit for Modi's victory, and conceded far too little to the organizational efforts of party leader Amit Shah and the BJP hierarchy. Once Modi took office, it soon became apparent to Kishor that he had been cut loose and hung out to dry, off the record. With this, began the friction between Kishor and the BJP – Shah in particular – that characterized the Bengal elections. On the 21st of December 2020 in the weeks leading up to polling, Prashant Kishor, now working for Mamata Banerjee, the incumbent Chief Minister of West Bengal, tweeted:

“For all the hype AMPLIFIED by a section of supportive media, in reality BJP will struggle to CROSS DOUBLE DIGITS in #WestBengal PS: Please save this tweet and if BJP does any better I must quit this space!” (sic)



This tweet, a bold claim, was taken to be a direct response to Amit Shah’s campaign cry that BJP will win over 200 of the 294 constituency seats in the Bengal legislative assembly. Come 2nd of May 2021, with the counting nearing an end and the election results undeniably obvious, BJP managed to win only 77 constituencies — barely half of the required majority (148) required to form the government — while TMC, Kishor’s employers, secured 213 constituencies.

The 5-month-old tweet resurfaced, with media outlets and news channels scurrying to score an interview with the man of the hour, to ask him how he so firmly predicted the election outcome and put his job on the line. Funnily enough, on the first TV-interview Kishor gave on the 2nd of May with NDTV news anchor Sreenivasan Jain, he announced his retirement as an election strategist citing personal reasons. He did, however, explain his thought process behind the tweet: TMC’s success and BJP’s smokescreen of victory.

While he acknowledged that BJP always have been and will continue to be a formidable force in Indian and West Bengal politics, he ascertained that he and his team were confident that TMC would be able to mobilize many more constituencies than the media outlets were willing to concede to them. He credited the win to Mamata Banerjee’s leadership and the faith that the people of Bengal have in her, paired with the lack of course correction and making of amends displayed by BJP. He pointed out how the BJP expected TMC to continue in the same way they had prior to the major loss of 12 seats they experienced in the 2019 Lok Sabha General Elections. According to Kishor this mistake in assuming that TMC were unable to make changes, and take into account the premises under which they were fighting the election, was BJP’s biggest error and what caught them off-guard — presenting a false sense of a lead and inevitable victory.

When asked about the public perception of BJP’s strength created by the 40-50 Modi-Shah rallies that took place and ministers who left TMC to join BJP, Kishor replied by saying that the popularity of a party does not directly translate to votes. While he took cognizance of BJP’s strength as a party, he also recognized the lack of leadership at a regional level in Bengal. The news anchor also brought up instances where the media reported TMC to be “crumbling” under the Modi-Shah effect with party members leaving every day and joining forces with BJP. In reply, citing the numbers, Kishor said that the ministers leaving TMC for BJP were the statistically weaker polling and lower performing candidates — which just strengthened the base on which TMC built its campaign, clearing out the dirt between its cogs. On the other hand, BJP, focusing primarily on their image and public perception, welcomed these ministers with open arms and saffron marigold garlands.

Kishor also went on to give all due credit to his team of over 300 volunteers in the Kolkata as well as Chennai office, who were the ones responsible for doing the research on the ground level – collecting data and understanding what the people and voters wanted in their leaders and governments. At the end of the NDTV interview of Prashant Kishor, the cameraman and news anchor also show a sneak-peek into Kishor’s Kolkata office, with young political analysts and volunteers cheering “Khela Shesh!” (or Game Over! in reference to TMC’s campaign slogan m>“Khela Hobe” or We Will Play!).

To conclude, while the right-wing and BJP IT cell have done their best to label Kishor as a liar and “a mercenary preaching the gospel of democracy”, at the end of the day, it was Prashant Kishor who was able to correctly look at the numbers and formulate a plan which led to TMC winning the elections in an evidently fair democratic process.
