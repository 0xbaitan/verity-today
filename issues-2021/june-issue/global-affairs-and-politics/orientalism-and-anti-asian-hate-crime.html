 Orientalism and anti-Asian Hate Crimes


Dhwani Sharma

June 1, 2021   |   Global Affairs & Politics


Over the last year, we've seen a surge in anti-Asian hate crimes and xenophobia. There has been a 150% increase in anti-Asian hate crimes in cities like New York and Los Angeles. The U.K. police data suggests a rise of 300% in hate crimes towards Chinese citizens. This can be mainly attributed to the spread of the COVID-19, which originated in the Wuhan district of China, and was dubbed “The Chinese Virus” by former U.S. President Donald Trump.



The term "Orientalism" refers to a general patronizing Western attitude towards Middle Eastern, Asian, and North African societies, in which the west portrays these societies as backwards, undeveloped, primitive, uncivilized and lesser than their own.

Orientalism does not exist in a bubble. In fact, it is a tool used by the West and other colonizers to justify colonizing and attacking the East. An example of this is that after the infamous 9/11 attacks, the Bush administration listed the liberation of Muslim women as one of the reasons for its invasion of Afghanistan.

We can clearly see examples of this in the media. There is a sexual pattern of Orientalism in movies: Middle Eastern women are sexualized and seen through the male gaze. For example, in Disney's Aladdin- Jasmine is portrayed in a far more risque and sexualised manner than her white counterparts - the pure and innocent Cinderella or Belle from Beauty and the Beast. The Chinese are also seen as 'dirty' and 'savages' for eating insects, dogs and other meat while it is common and even considered a luxury to eat other carnivorous foods such as duck, deer, cow, pigs etc. in the West.

Think about how the spread of COVID-19 has been reformatted as a Chinese conspiracy by racist conspiracy factions and even world leaders, for that matter. Think about how the media was so eager to say that the virus was caused by the eating bat soup from Wuhan’s “unhygienic” and “filthy” markets even though there is no concrete evidence of this.

The only semblance of evidence for this theory was a video of a woman eating bat soup which went viral when COVID-19 was first identified in late 2019, but according to the BBC, that doesn’t qualify as a directly causal action.

Another thing to think about is how these stigmas were never attached to beef and steak despite the devastation of the mad cow disease. How pork products and European soldiers were not stigmatized during the Spanish flu despite its origins as a bird borne disease that spread to pigs.

When we blame Asians for the spread of the COVID-19 we buy into the myth of the Yellow Peril. The Yellow Peril is an Orientalist ideology that represents the people of East Asia as an existential threat to the west and the rest of the world. The idea of the Yellow Peril has caused acts of violence towards Asian Americans for decades.



In Seattle and Tacoma, after the completion of the railroad system, the Chinese were driven out of neighbourhoods. Once their highly exploited labour was no longer needed, they faced mob violence, upheld by state and federal laws. This fear was rooted in the belief that the Chinese would overrun the local communities, take away American jobs, and leave the American citizens unemployed.

Another example of this: in 1982, a Chinese American man named Vincent Chen was brutally murdered by two white men in Detroit. During this time, the Japanese automobile industry had been growing at a fast rate, forcing the American companies to start downsizing. One of the two men who killed Vincent Chen had been recently fired from an automobile company, while the other was a head supervisor there. They viewed him as a representative and an embodiment of the Japanese Automobile industry even though he was just a drafter. In their eyes they weren't beating an innocent Chinese man, but were striking the Japanese companies that were taking away their jobs. They simply profiled him as the 'Asian other' and attacked him.

Even today, we tend to see Asians as a homogenous group with no nuance or difference. This can be seen with the introduction of the 'model minority' stereotype. We refuse to see them as more than tropes and stereotypes. We project our racist views on them. We must continue to learn, unlearn and grow as people.
