 Seychelles


Saswato Ray

June 1, 2021   |   Global Affairs & Politics


Vaccinations have been going on around the world at very high rates: in countries like the United Kingdom about 26.5% of the total population is fully vaccinated and 53.1% has at least one shot. In places like Spain, a test concert, which was conducted on April 27, ended with no new infections caused at the event itself. Indeed, different parts of the world are pushing back towards the so called ‘pre-pandemic’ normal. However, if I were to ask you amidst the current situation, which is the most vaccinated country in the world?



The answer isn’t the UK or the US or even any country in Europe, it’s a little island country on the Indian Ocean called Seychelles that has more than 60 % of its total population fully vaccinated. Unfortunately, the vaccination efforts were undermined recently, when Seychelles reported a spike in its corona virus cases, and burdened itself with a huge per-capita caseload. Even though the country is having just 100 cases every day, in a country with just 100000 residents- that is quite dangerous. To some extent, Seychelles outbreak is worse than India’s deadly one.

Seychelles, however, isn’t the anti-vaxxer’s paradise. While the condition of Seychelles has brought into question how effective the manufactured vaccines tend to be, it is worth noting that about 65% of the 1068 active cases in the first week of May have had just one dose of vaccine or were completely unvaccinated. But Seychelles’ case has brought into question how much is enough for reaching herd immunity in any community. Yanzhong Huang, a senior global health fellow contends that while countries like Israel and Great Britain have seen significant case-drops since the start of their vaccination campaigns, the condition of Seychelles puts into question the effectiveness of the vaccines that we must take into account as we try to reach herd immunity.

About 60% of the total vaccines registered in Seychelles were produced by the Sinopharm Institute in China and donated to them by the UAE whereas the others were from the Serum Institute of India. The WHO has contended that the Sinopharm vaccines had an overall efficacy of about 78%, whereas US trials have concluded that Astrazeneca vaccines have an efficacy of 79%, far lower than their mRNA counterparts- Pfizer and Moderna. Given the data, few people have also been asked to return for their third shots, but the number is said to be quite low. In general, Rand Corp. estimates that just 50% of the nation’s population has actually reached immunity levels required for protection and that there’s still a long way to go. The Health Department also stated that of all the hospital-admits, merely 20% are fully vaccinated and none of them have developed any serious symptoms.

The minister of foreign affairs, Radegonde said Thursday that only two people in the country are in intensive care.



"The conclusion is that the vaccines are protecting the people. Those who have been vaccinated are not developing any complications," Radegonde said. "We remain confident that the vaccines -- both of them -- have helped the country. Things would have been worse."

It is also worth remembering that the vaccines created in an effort to immunize people against COVID-19 have not been created with the incentive of preventing COVID infections per se, the main motive has been to prevent the cases from getting serious. The fact that a vaccinated patient may get COVID has a very low probability of happening but, as we’ve seen in the news around us, it has happened. However, death post-inoculation is a statistical impossibility. Vox recently published a video titled, ‘Why you can’t compare COVID-19 vaccines?’, explaining the entire science behind the magical numbers of efficacy that have been assigned to the vaccines, explaining why there isn’t enough data to effectively compare the vaccines in general- “The efficacy numbers really just tell you what happened in each vaccine’s trial, not exactly what will happen in the real world.” However, the video concluded that every vaccine in circulation around the world right now was in fact capable of reducing COVID to a basic fever. This was underscored by Jeremy Lim, an associate professor of health at NUS’ Saw Swee Hock School of Public Health who stated that the vaccines may not give sterilizing immunity against the virus.

Reaching herd immunity also depends on various other factors such as the genetics of the population and what kind of vaccine they received. In countries where Pfizer/Moderna vaccines have been circulating, a move back to normalcy will be easier given that both of those vaccines have an efficacy greater than 90%. But Michael Lin contended that getting “any vaccine” was more desirable than being exposed to the 1% death rate due to infection.

Seychelles’ tale can be a cautionary one for the whole world because COVID isn’t probably going anywhere. While countries will try to eradicate it, it is going to be a difficult job given the tracing factors. Many scientists have indicated that we will have to live with COVID and the condition in Seychelles may or may not be replicated in other parts of the world- it is imperative to note that every country, irrespective of what stage of the pandemic they’re in, need to keep track of the variants in COVID mutation from time to time- and keep the public up to date as well. Scientists have also indicated that as many people as possible should try to get vaccinated, and that seems a challenge given, vaccine hesitancy.

As we look at the current situation, it is no doubt, pressing- while the US is gradually suspending mask mandates, and the UK is phasing towards a complete reopening, India has been struggling. It is important that we never forget that this is a war we all must fight together: a single country’s victory will only be short-lived if the others are in turmoil, as this is but a virus with scarce understanding of borders. Seychelles offers us a tale of what a vaccinated could possibly look like, but at present, finding enough data to support a macrocosmic conclusion is beyond our means. All we can do is wait and watch.
