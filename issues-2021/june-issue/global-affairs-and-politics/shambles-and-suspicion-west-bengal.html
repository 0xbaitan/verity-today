 Shambles & Suspicion, West Bengal


Pradyumn Nahata

June 1, 2021   |   Global Affairs & Politics


As India embarks on another day of a dreadful and widely fatal Covid-19 crisis, the dishevelling political atmosphere in the country (West Bengal, to be specific) intensifies. On the 17th of May at 9 AM, 4 TMC leaders were arrested by the Central Bureau of Investigation. 2 of those arrested (Firhad Hakim and Subrata Mukherjee) serve in the Ministry of the WB Government, while the other 2 (Madan Mitra, Sovam Chatterjee) serve/have served in the Legislative Assembly of the State. The arrested, being vital leaders of the party, are predictably being salvaged by Mamata Banerjee- the long-standing pilot of the party.



The cause of arrest is the Narada Bribery Scandal, whereby more than twice those arrested were taped taking bribes from a journalist disguised as a businessman with whistleblower intentions. The tapes were released before the 2016 State Assembly elections involving members who are now part of BJP, the party which governs the country. Although the Central Bureau of Investigation (CBI), is ought to be independent of the Executive, it provides reports to the Centre and even arrests individuals under the supervision of Centre officials.

While West Bengal’s Chief Minister is ineluctably controversial, she is undeniably adamant: a few hours after the arrests, she arrived at the CBI office stationed in Kolkata, protested for hours, and allegedly asked the CBI to arrest her too in a frenzy of rhetoric. After waves of reflex action by Bengal’s governing party, a CBI court granted the leaders bail. However, it was not a cause of relief. On the same night, the Calcutta High Court stayed the bail order, giving the need to keep those arrested in judicial custody till further orders as justification. A division bench comprising Acting Chief Justice Rajesh Bindal and Justice Arijeet Banerjee supported this measure with similar reason. This bench was scheduled to meet in the afternoon of the 20th of May, but the Calcutta High Court postponed this hearing till further notice due to “unavoidable circumstances.”

This, in all transparency, is a cause for alarm, the intensity of its shockwaves depending on how the judiciary responds to the controversy. Not only that but the depth of this conflict is exaggerated by the peculiar time in which it sprang up - especially taking into consideration the fact that this was a scandal that unfolded in 2016. Only 2 weeks before the arrests, the TMC had won the West Bengal State elections yet again, with Mamata taking the pedestal for her 3rd term. It was only the BJP that put on a half-considerable opposition to the continuity of the state party.

It doesn’t end there. A most inciting detail of this whole fracas is that as suspicion spreads, the BJP subtly hides behind a safety curtain. Two members of the BJP, who defected from the TMC and were directly involved in the Narada scandal, have not come under the throes of the CBI. Suvendu Adhikari, who is now the Leader of Opposition in the WBA, escaped arrest when he was proven guilty publicly: both by forensics and witness. The other, Mukul Roy, was not directly involved in this fiasco but directed the bribe to an erstwhile IPS officer. This has not, and will not go unnoticed by the TMC, being a clear-cut indicator of Central involvement and corruption.



This is a huge cornerstone in the recent BJP-TMC conflict and is going to stir controversy as well as violence. “Many are asking what was the urgency to carry out these arrests, especially in the middle of a pandemic. Hakim and Mukherjee are responsible for urban and rural civic affairs, respectively, which are crucial ministries in terms of taking measures to prevent the spread of the coronavirus,” says The Quint. Could the situation be this politically depraved, choosing allegation politics to shift power dynamics amidst India’s biggest health crisis ever? This lack of trust in the politics of the subcontinent is not surprising, it is fully expected-- and necessary. The best we can do is keep ourselves informed and look at the bigger picture because that's not a responsibility our representatives will be taking up in the coming month. Helping out in the crisis by simply staying at home, and on more sophisticated levels, externalizing that help is what is essential. Stay safe.

UPDATE: On Friday, May 28, a five-judge bench of the Calcutta High Court- including but not restricted to Acting Chief Justice Rajesh Bindal- granted interim bail to all 4 incumbent or former leaders of the TMC who were placed under house arrest as per the Court orders. They’ve been ordered to recompense bail bonds of 2 lakh INR each. They will meet the investigating officer through virtual modes on a date which will be specified by the judicial authorities.
