 Sustainable Development In The Contemporary World


Parth Bhatt

May 1, 2021   |   Global Affairs & Politics


Now that 2021 has begun, less than a decade is left to attain the Sustainable Development Goals. The time to think was yesterday, today is the time to act! With the upcoming 10 years having been declared as the ‘Decade of Action’, we are running short of time. A great commitment and actions on the ground are in grave need.



Before talking about Sustainable Development in contemporary world, let’s take a brief glance at its history. Known records of the concept of sustainability date back to the era of ancient Chinese, Greek, and Roman writers with it being an element of their “philosophy of living in harmony with nature and neighbours”. Ancient civilizations in Rome, Egypt, Greece and Mesopotamia faced loss of human life as a result of environmental degradation from loss of soil fertility, mining, river reversals, and deforestation. The concept of sustainability gained momentum between 16th-19th century AD when engineers and politicians realized the issues of human intervention in nature, overexploitation of resources, and overpopulation. In the 17th century, Dutch philosopher Spinoza articulated his ideas of “preserving ourselves in harmony with nature, while tying this capacity to reaching a state of happiness” in the form of ‘Suum esse conservare’. The book Sylvicultura Oeconomica by German mining administrator Von Carlowitz from AD 1713 has mention of the term “sustainability” for forestry practices. English economist Thomas Malthus showed relation between population and future of society in his essay introducing the Malthusian Growth Model in AD 1798, while British naturalist, Alfred Russel Wallance, an exemplary visionary, took this even further and published ‘The Wonderful Century’ in AD 1898, which talks about the greatest successes & failures of the 19th century, including surveys of new technologies like telegraph & railway and disasters such as wars, poverty, overexploitation of the environment, etc. The book excellently articulates “how fundamental is the change they have effected in our life and civilization” and “injury done to posterity”.

The concept of ‘ecodevelopment’ came into the limelight in the 1970s. ‘The Limit of Growth’ came out in 1972 and was commissioned by the Club of Rome for the Project on the Predicament of Mankind. It paid emphasis to how the long running ‘business as usual’ model was unsustainable and alteration in growth trends was needed in order to achieve economic & ecological sustainability. The conclusion was that the followed trajectory of human activity would soon render Earth’s ecological system incapacitated. The concept of sustainable development transpired during the 1972 UN Conference on the Human Environment held at Stockholm, after policymakers urged governments to address poverty, public health, and environmental degradation simultaneously in a more integrated manner, side-lining the long followed "economic growth first and clean up later" principle. The conference focused significantly on human interactions with the environment. As a result, the United Nations Environment Program came into existence. The World Commission on Environment and Development was established in 1983 by the United Nations and was charged with the responsibility to prepare a report consisting of strategies to progress on sustainable development. The report materialized in 1987 as ‘Our Common Future’. It defined sustainable development as “development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to satisfy their own needs."

The Sustainable Development Goals we read about today are a result of a long journey of evolution of concepts. The Conference on Environment and Development, popularly known as the Earth Summit, was held in Rio de Janeiro in 1992, this conference resulted in formulation of what we now know as the Agenda 21, a reference which tangibly defined sustainability in order to determine government & corporate policies to make decisions while keeping sustainability in mind. The member states of the United Nations met at Rio de Janeiro again in 2012, 20 years after the Earth Summit of 1992, for United Nations Conference on Sustainable Development, also known as Rio+20. The motive for the same was to begin the process of formulation of the Sustainable Development Goals, which built upon the 8 Millennium Development Goals which were to be achieved by 2015. This historic moment was the point of convergence of the environmental strand (Stockholm Declaration 1972-Kyoto Protocol 2005) and the social development strand (UN Development Decade 1960s-Millenium Development Goals 2000). After about 3 years, on 25th September 2015, the 193 member states of the United Nations unanimously adopted the Sustainable Development Goals mentioned in paragraph 59 of UN General Assembly Resolution 70/1, or Transforming our world: The 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, which reaffirms all major UN Conferences & Summits. The Agenda identifies 5 areas of critical importance, widely known at the 5 Pillars of Sustainable Development. They are: people, planet, prosperity, peace, and partnership. Achieving the agenda requires contemporaneous progress in all these areas.

From what I’ve learnt during my course at the United Nations System Staff College Knowledge Centre for Sustainable Development in Bonn, Germany, I can say that Sustainable Development is like a Rubik’s cube which can neither be solved by focusing on one side alone, as action on one side affects others, nor by solving sides one by one – we have to work on all sides simultaneously. Sometimes, we even have to undo what’s already done to reach a solution. Innovation, trust, co-creation & collaboration are important, and things may feel worse before improving, requiring the deconstruction of previous thinking patterns and figuring out the right intervention sequences. Prioritizing one side over another will result in noncoherent outcomes.

Achieving sustainable development in a balanced & integrated manner requires new approaches to planning, implementation, follow-up & review, anticipating & assessing specific results against their effects on all five Pillars to foster synergies and avoiding trade-offs & one-sided approaches. There is an incredible amount of knowledge available, but we don’t seem to bring it together. We need to switch from a perspective seeking gradual improvement of a given situation to a perspective allowing disruptive-thinking and enhancing system awareness. The 2030 Agenda is extremely ambitious and encourages innovative & creative ways to tackle the sustainability problems. Breaking the silos and bringing everyone together is the need of the hour. The private sector and youth from all over the world are the key to tremendous progress on the agenda, but they are often overlooked. The amount of potential these sections of the society hold is enormous and including them with the policymakers will greatly enhance inter-sectoral coherence and result in a more efficient policy cycle leading to more sustainable decisions. Countries like Israel lead by example when it comes to nourishing innovation in an extreme environment with scarce resources. Investing in people and uplifting their talent can yield the best return on investment and even complement governments around the world in achieving their goals. Armed with the foresight for transformation, an innovative mindset, backed by the stakeholders, global youth have the ultimate capacity to progress immensely on this ambitious agenda.
