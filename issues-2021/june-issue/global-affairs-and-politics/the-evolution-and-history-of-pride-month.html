 The Evolution & History of Pride Month


Achintya Newatia

June 1, 2021   |   Global Affairs & Politics


Pride month is celebrated every year in June, with the main motive of allowing people from the LGBTQ+ community to truly celebrate their identities. With cities all over the world organising parades, concerts and other festivities, there is always a way to get involved, express your true colours and learn a little bit more about the social history surrounding the queer community.

Pride month essentially pays tribute to those who took part in the Stonewall Riots in June of 1968. The Stonewall Riots were a series of spontaneous demonstrations that took place from 28th June – 3rd July 1969 in New York City. The riots were led by members of the community with one of the prominent names being transwoman Marsha P Johnson. At the time, police raids in gay bars and pubs were a common occurrence. Homosexuality was illegal in all states of the USA except for Illinois. The pubs and bars were run by the mafia who paid corruptible police officers to look the other way and blackmailed the wealthy patrons of the LGBTQ+ community by threatening to ‘out’ them.



The Stonewall Riots started as a response to one such raid carried out by the New York Police Department on the hot summer night of 28th June, 1969. 8 undercover police officers stormed into the Stonewall Inn located in New York City’s Greenwich Village which was at the time, a safe haven for the city’s LGBTQ+ community. People were accustomed to such raids but on that night the members of the community decided to fight back – sparking an uprise bringing about a revolutionary sprit and a new era of resistance.

Set against the backdrop of the ongoing Civil Rights Movement and the Anti-Vietnam War protests, the Stonewall Riots simply fuelled this attitude of speaking up and fighting for justice. Stonewall constructed a historical precedent for future generations to follow: it had begun the process of change with regards to the legislative and societal acknowledgement due to a large community.

In the modern context, pride month started being celebrated only in 2009 when President Bill Clinton decided to dedicate the month of June to ‘The Lesbians and the Gays’ in 1999. This continued till 2009 when President Obama declared June to be Pride Month.



However, the situation as of today is not as chivalrous and unstained as the pioneers of yesteryears imagined it would become. What started out as a commemoration of the sacrifices made during the Stonewall Riots, has evolved into a more business centric corporate ‘event’ in recent times. Every year in June, brands come up with rainbow coloured merchandise to honour the celebration of Pride Month. This, they claim to do in solidarity with the people from the community. As the general support for LGBTQ rights grows, so does the corporate incentive for brands to position themselves in sync with that growing sentiment of support. But in that commercialization lies the disconnect: firms which promote gay pride and publicly condone the LGBTQ+ community may not always be consistent in actually rendering that support genuine. They still profit from exploiting the legacy laid down by the Stonewall Protests and contemporary artists alike. While these brands may claim to use the money for extending out their hand of support and doing their two bits of queer activism, there is no guarantee that the money is actually being used for the betterment of the people in the community.

This really raises the question poking at the true reason for the celebration of Pride. Is it truly a celebration of one’s identity and does it line up with the visionaries of the Stonewall riots or is it just another capitalist, corporate money production scheme by brands who do not seem to care about the existence of the queer community during other months of the year?

While significant progress has been made in the last 50 years with regard to representation of the Queer Community, we are far from achieving the movement started by the people in the Stonewall Riots. So, this pride month, while we stay at home and extend our support to people of the LGBTQ+ community, we must keep in mind the reason for celebrating one’s identity for an entire month, with pride.
