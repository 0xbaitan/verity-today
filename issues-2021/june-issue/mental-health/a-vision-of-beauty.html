 A Vision of Beauty


Niharika Rajsaha

June 1, 2021   |   Mental Health


Filters are possibly the most literal manifestation of humanity’s need to compartmentalise and manipulate. It impacts everyone. It is not an issue exclusive to a group of people or community; it is a worldwide phenomenon that is becoming more of a malady. A filter, by definition, is a porous device for removing impurities or solid particles from a liquid or gas passed through it. The logic used in ordinary mechanical filters also extends to the filters used in beautification apps.



Filters in software serve the purpose of changing the outlook of an image by airbrushing the subject, most often human, to a level of two-dimensional perfection that is impossible to achieve realistically. A filter in fashion and clothing refers to the allotment of labels according to gender and size. The common thread between these different aspects of filtration is that they all filter the physical appearance, altering only the superficial outlook of our faces. Even so, they impact us to our very cores.

The human eye is attracted to brighter and more vivid images due to photoreceptors in the eye that capture light. Refined objects are more impressionable to the mind’s eye. Once we see a beautified version of an object that has been morphed and airbrushed, we take it as the desired representation of the object. Therefore, when we see something in their unadorned self, they appear bleak and almost too bland in our mind. We like to believe that things exist in their beautified forms. When mundane reality disappoints us, we refuse to accept our imperfections. It leads to the overuse of filters and editing facilities. People tend to enjoy the momentary presence of that unrealistic standard of beauty. Therefore, a significant reason for this affinity to digital beautification has its roots in our insecurities and simultaneously feeds the same.

Body dysmorphic disorder (BDD) is a psychological disorder in which a person becomes obsessed with perceived flaws one has in their appearance. It may be so minor that it is barely noticeable by others. However, the impact it has on one’s mind causes them to constantly check their appearance in the mirror and feel ashamed of themselves. It often causes them to avoid social interactions and gatherings due to anxiety and embarrassment. It could branch into countless other disorders, such as depression, social anxiety, and OCD. It is dangerously prevalent as there are about a million cases per year in India alone. It affects 1.7% to 2.9% of the general population, making it more common than schizophrenia and anorexia (1% and 0.21%, respectively). BDD is most common between 14 and 60 years of age. While the cause of BDD cannot be determined with certainty due to varying levels of peer influence and one's preconceived notions, it can be agreed upon that filters erase parts of our faces to make us look ‘perfect’. And when we see those parts in the mirror, we deem them as flaws for being left out of the filtered photograph. As humans, we inadvertently try to perfect ourselves and hide these flaws or imperfections. Before we can even realise it, it has already become an obsession. We scrutinise and tear apart that one feature till it becomes a devil staring back at us every time we face the mirror. We do not realise is that it is not the wrinkle or the scar that is evil but the blur which hides it.

These filters are now dominating the social media scene, as observed in the past decade. The filters have become a hallmark of beauty among many people. It has resulted in people moulding themselves to fit into those frames. A common wildfire trend has recently surfaced. It is referred to as the ‘Instagram Face’. This includes a young face, poreless skin, plump and high cheekbones, catlike eyes, long eyelashes, a small neat nose and full lush lips. What we fail to understand though is that while some might have a natural face that fits into these filters, it is nothing more than a face. And that is the kind of aspiration we subject ourselves to without realising that it chips a part of ourselves away.

We have amplified the need for compartmentalisation to the point where it is blatantly present every time we shop for something as simple as clothes. The millions of existing shopping portals all deploy filters for sizes, cuts, lengths, colours, fabric, and gender. The gender filter consists of two subgroups: male and female. It does not only limit gender to the size and cut of fabric; it also views gender as strictly binary, completely ignoring the spectrum beyond it. However, it does not end there. As seemingly harmless as the allocation of colours and patterns may seem today, it undoubtedly impacts people. I tend to wear darker coloured clothes. Yet, every time I walk into a store, I am led to the pastel blouses under a pink board that reads “Women”. It is not even the fact that being called a woman bothers me. It is the thought that I knew at the back of my mind that if I had ever wandered into the section that catered to men, people would start assuming that I was shopping for a male relative or a friend. And this holds for other genders as well. The labelling of clothes rob people of their identity. It makes them feel like it is wrong to want to look the way they want to. Clothes originally served the function of protection which has evolved into fashion. Thereon, through evolution, it has given rise to inadvertent filters. We need to pull down those boards, remove those filters and find better ways to arrange these objects because, in the end, they are objects and accessories to help us look the way we want. But they have been given the power that can make people feel wrong about themselves. That is the kind of authority no person should have, let alone a few metres of fabric.

I stand by my point that one of the root causes of prejudice is compartmentalisation. Prejudice: it has always been frowned upon, and that is important. One needs to identify the problem before they try to fix it. But we tend to forget the impact which filters have on mental health. It chips away pieces of people until the final result is this watered-down personality that can easily fit into a box. That needs to stop if we want to achieve this vision of a world without filters and labels. To achieve a vision that is accepting, kinder and one that does not force you into boxes or make you feel unwanted, we need to stop giving technology, billboards and catalogues the power to make us feel anything less than the people we truly are.
