 A Vision of Beauty


Niharika Rajsaha

June 1, 2021   |   Mental Health


Filters are possibly the most literal manifestation of humanity’s need to compartmentalize and manipulate. And it impacts everyone. It is not an issue exclusive to a certain group of people or community; it is a worldwide phenomenon that is becoming more of a malady every second. A filter, by definition, is a porous device for removing impurities or solid particles from a liquid or gas passed through it. This, while being the mechanical use of a filter applies the same logic as software. A filter on pictures is software that changes the outlook of the image by airbrushing the subject, most often human, to a level of two-dimensional perfection that cannot be achieved in reality. A filter in fashion and clothing refers to the allotment of labels of gender and size. A common thread between these different aspects of filtration is the fact that they all filter the physical appearance. They are meant to only alter the superficial outlook of our faces and yet they impact us to our very cores.



The human eye is attracted to brighter and more vivid images due to the presence of photoreceptors in it that capture light. Refined objects are more impressionable on the mind’s eye and once we see a beautified version of an object that has been morphed and airbrushed, it places the image as a representation of the object in our minds. Therefore, when we see the real appearance in their true, unadorned self, they appear bleak and almost too three dimensional in our mind. We like to believe that things exist in their beautified forms and when the reality disappoints us, we almost refuse to believe that that is reality. This is what causes us to overuse filters and editing facilities to enjoy the momentary presence of that standard of beauty. Therefore, the major reason for this affinity to digital beautification has its roots in our insecurities and simultaneously feeds the same.

Body dysmorphic disorder is a psychological disorder in which a person becomes obsessed with perceived flaws in one’s appearance. It may be so minor as to be one barely noticeable by others. However, the impact of this on one’s mind leads to them constantly checking their appearance in the mirror and feeling ashamed of one’s own self. This often causes them to avoid social interactions and gatherings due to feeling anxious and embarrassed of themselves. It could branch into or comprise of countless other disorders including eating disorders, depression, social anxiety, and OCD. It is dangerously common with about a million cases per year in India. It affects 1.7% to 2.9% of the general population, making it more common than schizophrenia and anorexia1 (1% and 0.21% respectively). It is most common between 14 and 60 years of age. While the cause of BDD ranges from peer influence and ideals derived from other people on social media, a prominent cause is that filters erase some parts of our faces to make the picture look ‘perfect’. And when we see those parts in the mirror, we deem them as flaws for being left out of the filtered picture. As humans, we inadvertently try to perfect ourselves and these flaws or imperfections and before we realise it, that need becomes an obsession. We scrutinize and tear apart that one feature till it becomes a devil staring us in the face every time we look in the mirror. What we don’t realise is that it’s not the wrinkle or the scar that’s the evil but the blur in place of it.

These filters have evolved from extra features to standards defining of human beauty over the past decade with social media growing into the most fluid form of communication throughout the world. The filters have become aspirations of appearances and people are molding themselves to fit into these frames. A common wildfire trend is what we refer to as ‘Instagram Face’. It includes a young face, pore less skin, plump and high cheekbones, catlike eyes, long eyelashes, a small neat nose and full lush lips2. What we fail to understand though is that while some might have a natural face that fits into these filters, it is one face. And that is a kind of aspiration we subject ourselves to without realizing that it chips a part of our self away, superficially and beneath.

We have amplified the need for compartmentalization to the point where it is blatantly present every time we shop for something as simple as clothes. The millions of shopping portals that we use all have filters for sizes, cuts, lengths, colours, fabrics… and gender. The gender filter consists of two subgroups: male and female. This doesn’t only reduce genders to the status of size and cut of fabric, it also looks at genders as two opposing ideals, completely excluding the wide gender spectrum by focusing on the gender binary. As normal as allocation of colours and patterns may seem today, it has an impact on people. I, personally, have a tendency to wear darker coloured clothes and yet every time I walk into a store, I am led to the pastel blouses under the huge pink board that reads “WOMEN”. And it’s not being called a woman that bothers me; it’s that I knew at the back of my mind, that if I wandered in the section labelled MEN, people would automatically assume that I was shopping for a male relative or a friend. This would and has applied on every other gender. Assigning clothes to personalities rob them of their identity, making them feel like it’s wrong to want to look the way they want to. Clothes originally served the function of protection, which evolved to fashion and in this evolution, an inadvertent filter was formed. We need to pull down those boards, remove those filters and find better ways to arrange these objects because in the end, they’re objects, accessories to help us look the way we want. But they have been given the power that can make people feel wrong about themselves. And that kind of authority is one that no person should have, let alone a few metres of fabric.

I stand by my point that one of the roots of all prejudice is compartmentalisation. And prejudice is often looked at from the angle that we know it is wrong, which is important. One needs to identify the problem before they try to fix it. But we tend to forget that that wrongdoing has an impact on people, whose mental health is affected every time they are put through filters, chipping away at pieces of them until the final result is this stripped down personality that can easily fit into a box. And that needs to stop if we want to achieve this generation’s goal for a new world; one that’s accepting, kinder and doesn’t tell you that if all your qualities don’t fit in a box, you don’t belong. To make the kind of progress we dream of, the world has to stop giving software, billboards and catalogues the power to make us feel like anything less than the full, true people we are.
