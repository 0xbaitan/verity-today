 Oops!... She’s In It Again: Britney Spears’ Conservatorship & Public Response to Mental Health


Mohan Rajagopal

June 1, 2021   |   Mental Health


Pop culture fiends and gossip pages have been rife with news over the past two years surrounding Britney Spears and her controversial conservatorship, which even sparked several documentaries and an online movement entitled #FreeBritney. Although the situation has been exploited by tabloids and the paparazzi for clickbait and easy money, it presents an insight into the longstanding issues of mental health within the larger world, both in jurisdiction and legality, as well as in public opinion.



Before discussing Spears herself, it is necessary to understand what a conservatorship is. According to the Judicial Branch of California, a conservatorship is “a court case where a judge appoints a responsible person or organization (called the ‘conservator’) to care for another adult (called the ‘conservatee’) who cannot care for himself or herself or manage his or her own finances.” Usually, such cases are used in the interest of people suffering from mental illnesses, or who need support in their old age. Such a guardianship was also the subject of the Golden Globe winning ‘I Care A Lot’ (2020), starring Rosamund Pike.

After her initial success, Britney came under great public attack in the 2000s, a phenomenon that seems to be an unfair rite of passage for most female pop stars after they break into mainstream attention. With constant slutshaming and criticism surrounding bad press from her breakup with Justin Timberlake and hypersexualised image in most music videos, things came to a head in 2007, during her divorce with husband Kevin Federline, when she lost custody to both her children. Relentless surveillance by the paparazzi only catalysed an already difficult period of her life, leading to several nervous breakdowns, including the infamous incident when Spears shaved her head bald. In light of these mental health concerns and numerous visits to rehabilitation facilities, Jamie Spears, her father, was granted conservatory rights over her financial assets (as a financial conservator) and over herself as a person (as a personal conservator).

Initially, this seemed to be a wise and respectable move, as Spears was in a genuine need of help. However, the past few years have aroused suspicions as to the effectiveness of a continued guardianship. In 2019, Jamie Spears stepped down from his role as personal conservator due to “health issues”, and Jodi Montgomery was appointed as a temporary replacement. Since then, Britney’s lawyers have issued numerous statements, declaring that she was “afraid of her father”, and that she would like Montgomery to be appointed to this role permanently, and for her father to step down. Unfortunately, this request was turned down by a Los Angeles judge in early 2021, naming the financial firm the Bessemer Trust a co-conservator of her estate instead. Jamie Spears’ conservator powers have also been extended to September 2021. As of now, news has broken that Britney herself has requested to address the court regarding her conservatorship.

While the Spears case is only one of millions around the world, it has done well to shed light on a phenomenon which was rarely heard about by the general public. Both parties have vigorously contested the other’s claims: Jamie maintains that Britney’s unhappiness in the conservatorship are mere “conspiracy theories”, while her lawyers insist that she is unable to speak out herself on account of her father’s suppression. Neither side has been verified yet, but the fact that such an atrocity is even plausible speaks volumes about the mental health infrastructure that is prevalent within the legislations of many nations. Such laws prioritise the guardians over the actual ailing subject, who is unable to voice their own grievances without fear. The framework institutionalised is clearly inadequate, if there is also no adequate system in place to review a judgement that was passed regarding a person’s health more than a decade ago.

The media response to the situation has also been telling, with the paparazzi frenzy echoing that of the 2000s which led to Britney’s initial breakdown. Spears herself has spoken about the numerous documentaries being made about her life, in an Instagram post: "So many documentaries about me this year with other people's takes on my life. These documentaries are so hypocritical… they criticize the media and then do the same thing? Damn…”. It is evident that mental health always has and continues to be trivialised by mainstream media. Urgent reform is required, both in the institutions that have been established to deal with mental illness on a legal basis, as well as the public approach to such sensitive issues that are instead sensationalised in a media circus.
