 Internet Etiquette


Srinjoy Das

June 1, 2021   |   Mental Health


The internet, in recent times, has become the primary source of social interaction. And it is massive. I grew up in a generation that has gained exposure to the web from a young age; hence, interactions over the internet are by no means unfamiliar. On the other hand, most people in particular, older people, prefer traditional face-to-face interactions.



However, both forms of interaction should have one thing in common - etiquette. So, one could assume that human decency would be maintained over the internet since it is was drilled into us by our parents, teachers, and mentors early on in life. Sadly, this is not the case as an embarrassingly large proportion of individuals love to spend their whole day raging about the use of pronouns under someoneâ€™s bio.

I am confident that most of you are familiar with the concept of internet etiquette. However, for the sake of this article, let us discuss what it exactly means.

Etiquette: it is the customary code of polite behaviour in society that also extends to the web. The basic premise of etiquette is to respect personal choices; besides, instructing people to refrain from posting content that is demeaning and harmful to others.

Respect: self-explanatory; unlike what many people would like to opine, it is not an unfamiliar concept. Social etiquette has always been important to people, so should we not expect the same virtually as well? In broad terms, it helps to promote inclusivity which should be agreed upon as a virtue of humanity by all.

Now specifically speaking, internet etiquette consists of maintaining the use of specific devices, namely - trigger warnings, tone indicators, pronouns, etc. The above three will be the prime focus of this article.

Trigger warnings: After certain traumatic events, the brain ingrains sensory stimuli into the memory. Encountering stimuli in a similar but disassociated context causes a person to associate that trigger with their past trauma.

I think it is obvious why trigger warnings should be necessary. More so, with all the mishaps and crises plaguing the world recently. While sharing posts or information about such events, one should be mindful of the negative consequences it might have on others. It is easy to unknowingly expose and subject another person to trauma even without the ill intent. Under such circumstances, trigger warnings help to alert the person of possible triggers in the content they view. Hence, they should not be overlooked or misused.

Pronouns: I am sure everyone reading this knows what pronouns are. However, you might be having this question - why should I put up pronouns on my bio? To clarify, it is a symbol of solidarity with people who do not conform to the gender binary. It also includes individuals who do not identify with the gender assigned at birth, i.e., trans people. It also busts the myth that one's sex or/and choice of gender expression determines their gender identity. In other words, you cannot simply assume a person's gender just because they choose to wear a saree or wear makeup.

I firmly believe that proper use of pronouns is possibly the strangest thing to deny and disrespect. On this note, many online trolls feign superiority to those who put pronouns in their bio. And they never fail to express it through online bullying, frustration, and anger. If you are one of them, do let us know if your skin itches from all the makeup you have put on after being publicly clowned on the internet.

Tone indicators: Tone indicators are signifiers or abbreviations written at the end of statements to clarify the tone and intention of the original poster. They start with a slash followed by letters.

For example, /srs means the intent behind a post is a serious one while /s means it is sarcastic.

Using tone indicators greatly benefits neurodivergent people since people convey, process, and interpret information & emotions differently. It helps people to understand the context of a post and dispels any room for misjudgement or misinterpretation. Even neurotypical people can benefit from the use of tone indicators. However, one should not misuse tone indicators or mock people who use them as it is rude, insensitive, and not to mention - highly ableist.

TL; DR, the past two years have already been hard on most people. The world outside is already too gloomy to be grumpy about inclusivity and progression. Let us not disregard basic etiquette and work together to make the internet a safer place for everyone.
