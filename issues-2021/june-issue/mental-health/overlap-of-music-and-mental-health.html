 Overlap of Music and Mental Health


Dibya Choudhary & Eshan Bannerjie

May 1, 2021   |   Mental Health


Starting with a quote to describe the beauty of music is an insult. Music itself is a quote. The word itself, if understood with enough awareness, brings about a sudden beautiful rush. The beauty of music lies in the intermixing of chaos and calm at the same time, in the serene juxtaposition of glamorously arranged notes, which ensure nothing but at least a minimum arousal of human emotion. Only and only after one is aware of music will one understand that it is no different from our mind. Both take us on a journey of emotional high and low tides. We never know what is coming next. The beauty of each and every note and the beauty of each and every human emotion is something to be glorified, but in this ruthless and mechanical world, only a handful know and experience the true essence of the blend of our mind and music. How often, if one carefully observes, while listening to ‘Moonlight Sonata’ by Beethoven or ‘Fantaisie Impromptu’ by Chopin, does one get electrifying chills. How often when one listens to ‘Money for Nothing’ by Dire Straits or ‘Summer of 69’ by Bryan Adams does one get nostalgic. How often when one listens to ‘In the End’ by Linking Park or ‘Tears in Heaven’ by Eric Clapton does one get really sad. This is nothing but the capacity that music holds to influence our mind. Of course, there is a great deal of biology that is involved, but in everyday life, music largely affects our mental health. That is why a great deal of ‘Music Therapy’ is practiced globally to treat people.



The reason why we tend to cry, or smile, or experience any other emotion, is because we are in constant interaction with our environment, which is a mixture of both good and bad. As the music flows, we tend to view our life or the events shaping it, flowing with the music, and we tend to actively construct scenarios where we see ourselves getting hurt or rising up or we just attach some emotional construct to it. It’s just the journey of one’s own life which one attaches to different genres of music, which tends to make on emotional, happy, sad, etc. Why does classical music give rise to a good deal of arousal in most people who tend to hear it? It is because of the constant arrangement and rearrangement of notes. It is due to the really high and really low notes. One tends to attach a really motivated thought when the notes go higher and faster, and an emotional thought when the notes go lower and slower. Why is it so? It is because this is the very essence of human existence. Human life is made up of faster and motivated things along with slow and emotional situations. Now one might ask what music does to have an effect on mental health. Human existence has a rhythm which is unmatched.

Research shows the advantages of music therapy for various psychological states, including depression, trauma, and schizophrenia (to name a few). Music acts as a medium for processing emotions, trauma, and grief—but music, also can be utilized as a regulating or calming agent for anxiety or for dysregulation. music can be very helpful in the management of pain. One study of fibromyalgia patients found that those who listened to music for just one hour a day experienced a significant reduction in pain compared to those in a control group. At the end of the four-week study period, participants who had listened to music each day experienced significant reductions in feelings of pain and depression. Such results suggest that music therapy might be a crucial tool within the treatment of chronic pain.

All in all, to give this article a justifiable end, I shall remind you, the reader, that your life is the most beautiful melody you will experience. If you are having a tough time, it is just the lyrics that is messed up.
