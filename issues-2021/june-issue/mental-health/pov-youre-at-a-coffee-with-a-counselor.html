 POV: You’re at Coffee with a Counsellor, Except She Spilled Some Tea


Rishita Chatterjee & Akash Nath

June 1, 2021   |   Mental Health


The second wave of the infamous COVID-19 brought with it an equally damaging second wave of deteriorating mental health. It is not surprising therefore that the prolonged psychological distress caused by the virus led to the widespread pervasiveness of depressive symptoms, anxiety, bereavement and anger.



The authors of this article had the chance to interact with a young counsellor, Ms. Deepashree Sonik,who has an Advance Diploma in Counselling Psychology from Jamia Millia Islamia and Masters in Psychology from Kurukshetra University, Kurukshetra. She has also completed her Bachelors from Miranda House, Delhi University.

Miss Sonik is one of the many mental health workers who have started giving out free sessions during these times of anguish, distress and adversity. Since she has been interacting with a lot of people who experienced loss and hardships, interviewing her was ideal as it gave us a professional insight into this wave of mental health.

While the initial intent of the authors was to focus mostly on aspects related to COVID-19, the conversation with Miss Sonik progressed towards covering numerous other dimensions related to Mental Health. The same has been covered under various sections below.

It is not an unknown fact that there has always been a lot of stigma surrounding mental health initiatives, services, and even the very discussion of topics pertaining to it. The authors were intrigued to know whether there were any positive changes in the attitudes of the people in the COVID era owing to the death of close family and friends. Miss Sonik reaffirms that the stigma surrounding therapy or counselling is still very much in existence. She also points out how despite having some knowledge, various misconceptions about therapy lead to people not taking up the very essential first step - approaching and asking.

Mental Health - Stigma vs Acceptance in COVID times?

It is not an unknown fact that there has always been a lot of stigma surrounding mental health initiatives, services, and even the very discussion of topics pertaining to it. The authors were intrigued to know whether there were any positive changes in the attitudes of the people in the COVID era owing to the death of close family and friends. Miss Sonik reaffirms that the stigma surrounding therapy or counselling is still very much in existence. She also points out how despite having some knowledge, various misconceptions about therapy lead to people not taking up the very essential first step - approaching and asking.

The Mental Health Infrastructure

India does not have the ideal mental health infrastructure and has been criticised because of its inherent economic and sociological drawbacks throughout the years. Additionally, reports like “Reconciliations of Caste and Medical Power in Rural Public Health Services” by Sobin George have further contributed to its disrepute. However, Miss Sonik is hopeful about the future. With growing acceptability and reduced stigma among the citizens, public authorities are forging ahead with the requisite modules and programmes to eliminate the pre-existing hurdles in the system. On that optimistic note, she agrees that this is going to be a difficult battle, although, as T. Harv Eker said, "one step in the right direction is better than a hundred years of thinking about it".

“Grief looks different on everyone”

Life and Death have been consistent visitors in every household, however, death always felt like an unwanted guest. The pandemic brought over two hundred thousand unwanted guests to our doorsteps, without giving us a moment to garner the strength we needed to welcome it. Our ways of acknowledging and expressing grief are different, hence there can never be a "single uniform approach" when it comes to grief. The journey of grief is one of the most difficult and emotionally draining journeys one has taken and requires a tremendous amount of patience, self-awareness and determination. As Miss Sonik rightly said, a therapist cannot prescribe a set of activities that can automatically ease the pain, but a therapist can help you articulate emotions, which makes acknowledging your journey with grief easier.

Who therapizes therapists?

While providing therapy to someone, a therapist provides different tools to the person. For example, when someone has anxiety, they are given different grounding exercises which help them to dial down the emotions, bringing their focus to what is happening to them physically instead of clenching to their feelings. Similarly, when Miss Sonik and others like her go through similar feelings, they apply the same techniques. Therapists have to deal with a lot of trauma, a lot of pain. This often takes a toll on their own mental health. “We have to deal with it to help others,” Miss Sonik says. Miss Sonik emphasizes that a therapist should never let any negative emotions from their patients take a toll on their own mental wellbeing. “Personal Vs. Professional Life- That is where the distinction is drawn” That being said, she acknowledges that it is really difficult, even for highly experienced therapists.

We are grateful to Ms Sonik for engaging in such an important and insightful conversation with us. Our interview boils down to two important points, sensitize and acknowledge. The pandemic did not come with a manual for us to follow on how to deal with it, and in fact, we have been hard on ourselves in most cases, making it all the more difficult to articulate and acknowledge our emotions. Investing and prioritising our mental health is crucial, particularly in these times. Seeking help has never been more important.
