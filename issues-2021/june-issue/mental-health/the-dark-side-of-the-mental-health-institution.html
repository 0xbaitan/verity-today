 The Dark Side of The Mental Health Institution


Rishita Chatterjee & Srinjoy Das

May 1, 2021   |   Mental Health


We have spoken about the different components of mental health/illnesses, in order to create awareness and stimulate readers to engage more with the said issue. However, there is indeed one facet of mental health, we have not shed much light on. Like most aspects of our life, mental health too has been incredibly capitalised over the years, therefore undoubtedly inhabiting a very big yet regrettably flawed infrastructure.



Now, if you are one of the 1.5 billion Instagram users in all likelihood you must have come across at least one post preaching about “acknowledging mental illness and seeking professional help”. However, “seeking professional help” does come at a cost, and this cost has both sociological and economic impact on every single one of us. Delving deeper, we come across the two folds of the same problem, the first and obvious one being - stigma. It is to no surprise that mental health therapy in India exists in a highly stigmatised space, and opting for therapy usually prompts people to use labels like “crazy” or suggesting alternative solutions like “doing yoga” or the classic “just stop using your phone”. And this is stacked upon another very crucial problem, the cost of therapy and the commitment it takes to see the gradual results making access to therapy not only more difficult but also very expensive.

India being a country where 20% of its population (approximately 650 million people), suffer from some kind of mental illness, there are no major health insurance policies that provide mental health coverage. In 2017, the government passed The Mental Healthcare Act, which mandated insurance companies to cover all kinds of mental illnesses. After the act was passed the Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India (IRDAI) made it obligatory for all insurance companies to include mental illnesses in their offerings. And these decisions were backed by a 2018 survey conducted by the National Health Survey, which concluded that over 150 million citizens were in dire need of immediate intervention for mental health disorders and most of these needs remained unattended. Irrespective of all these obligations none of the insurance companies in India cover mental illnesses at all. Now, the obvious question is - why are there no government policies to rationalise therapy costs? and the most “disappointed but not surprised” answer to this is corruption. Before opting for medicines like antidepressants and sedatives it is crucial to choose a non-drug alternative like mental health therapy.

However, our government does not support this methodology simply because they do not make money out of it. Antidepressants and sedatives are sold at high margins by the biggest pharmaceutical companies, making them one of the most profitable drugs. However, non-drug alternatives like therapy would hamper their revenue. Reports are stating that most government-funded psychiatric wards do not have basic facilities to provide therapy. And even after these horrifying reports, the government continues to support this holy grail of capitalism at the cost of making their citizens more vulnerable to substance abuse, mental illness and various other disorders.

Irrespective of the prevailing economic hardships, the different layers of discrimination based on sexuality, caste, gender, and religion has indefinitely made the mental health infrastructure further marginalised. A recent study by Sobin George titled “Reconciliations of Caste and Medical Power in Rural Public Health Services” published in 2019 illustrates caste discrimination in healthcare services in India. According to Gorge, discrimination essentially occurs on two levels- inclusivity and treatment, where there is insidious almost normalised discrimination. A large number of mental healthcare providers in India come from non-SC/ST backgrounds insinuating the lack of representation or inclusion in the system. And concerning discrimination in terms of medical interactions, respondents have reported experiencing differential treatment from higher-caste health workers. Including limited interactions, avoidance and inadequate time for consultancy, resulting in constant and several visits to attain the right diagnosis.

The mental infrastructure is very closely intertwined with human well being, especially in the ongoing pandemic where millions are left vulnerable and more susceptible to mental illnesses. Therefore, it is only through the correct policy framework, implementing programmes that take a strong intersectional and a community-centric approach is the bare minimum the government can work on to fix this broken infrastructure. Acknowledging and acting upon your privilege to provide adequate resources to people coming from marginalised socio-economic backgrounds can go a long way as well.
