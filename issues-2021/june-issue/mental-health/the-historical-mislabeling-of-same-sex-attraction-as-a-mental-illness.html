 The Historical Mislabelling of Same-Sex Attraction as a Mental Illness


Mohan Rajagopal

June 1, 2021   |   Mental Health


With the rise of LGBTQ+ activism and more and more people having the courage to come out, the queer community is no longer relegated only to the socio-political aspect of life, but has also become a subject of great scientific interest. The exact cause of same-sex attraction is one that has baffled researchers for decades now, especially due to the weaponisation of pseudo-scientific claims by medical and religious institutions used to frame homosexuality as a choice or an illness. Although the modern world has come a long way, traces of such misconceptions still exist in many cultures, which can be traced back to the late twelfth century, and rooted in religious, legal, and medical underpinnings.

Before the High Middle Ages, same-sex relationships were largely ignored and allowed to continue by the Church in Europe. However, writings by Thomas Aquinas and other religious and secular leaders in the latter half of the twelfth century led to hostility towards homosexuality, which was soon institutionalised within the society. This religious contempt eventually gave birth to legal sanctions, especially in Europe and the Americas, where any form of deviant sexual behaviour (such as sodomy or pre-marital intercourse) was liable to be punished with stiff criminal penalties. By the end of the nineteenth century, medicine had joined the fray with religion and law in trying to establish its jurisdiction over sexuality. Homosexuality was hence considered a pathological phenomenon, or a mental disease. Although incorrect, this was taken to be a progressive move at the time, as ailing individuals would be less ostracised and punished than sinners and criminals.



Even within psychiatry, however, homosexuality was not universally viewed as a pathology. The most famous opposing theories were those put forward by Sigmund Freud and Sandor Ferenczi. Freud believed all human beings were innately bisexual, and that they become heterosexual or homosexual as a result of their experiences with parents and others (Freud, 1905). Nevertheless, Freud agreed that a homosexual orientation should not be viewed as a form of pathology. On the other hand, Ferenczi opined that a gay man was one who was confused about his sexual identity and felt himself to be "a woman with the wish to be loved by a man". He believed that it to be a mental illness, the cure of which might be possible in the future with an advancement in psychoanalytic techniques.

The inter-sectional class angle of such action taken against homosexuality should also be noted. For example, in twentieth century England, it was the working-class men that were most likely to end up in prison if caught engaging in same-sex relations. Meanwhile, the more privileged middle-class men were offered conversion therapy as a “softer” option to jail-time. It was within this context of class, privilege and sexuality, coupled with religious undertones, that conversion therapy was conceptualised, one of the most famous instances of homophobic suppression.

Conversion therapy is the pseudoscientific practice of trying to change an individual's sexual orientation from homosexual or bisexual to heterosexual using psychological, physical, or spiritual interventions. There is no reliable evidence that sexual orientation can be changed, and medical institutions warn that conversion therapy practices are ineffective and potentially harmful. Methods resorted to include behavioural modifications through electric shocks and nausea-inducing drugs, as well as ex-gay ministry and lobotomisation. Fortunately, with the declassification of homosexuality as a mental illness by the American Psychiatric Association in 1973, instances of conversion therapy have reduced, with several nations taking legislative action against it. However, it continues to be legal in certain countries, including India. The law which criminalised homosexuality in India was a colonial imposition, brought by the British empire. Homophobic narratives and those which try to medicalise same-sex attraction can also be traced back to colonial ideology. Yet some powerful post-colonial institutions and individuals adopted these narratives and incorporated them into an anti-LGBTQ+ agenda, which continues among some practitioners today.

Scientific causes of homosexuality are still to be confirmed, yet scientists are certain that it is most definitely not a mental illness or a pathological disease, nor is it a choice made by an individual. Such narratives are furthered by institutions in an attempt to stigmatise the queer community, and this taboo has hampered the study of sexual orientations, as research into the topic has only begun recently, due to the taboo surrounding it. Nevertheless, new findings are being made every so often to suggest that sexuality is a complex behaviour, not determined by a single factor, but precluded by a variety.
