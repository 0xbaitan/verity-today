 Knights in Barely Legal Armour


Pragya Roy Barman

June 1, 2021   |   Social Change


India experienced a second wave of the pandemic starting from April, where COVID cases started to rise to almost 4 million per day. Everyone was caught off-guard when the papers started reporting these incredible statistics and reporting on horrific accounts of death all around. Yet there was no call for a national lockdown, and it came upon the citizens to take matters into their own hands in terms of ensuring their safety. The steps taken included: wearing masks, using sanitisers, staying home as much as possible, and sharing resources for people requiring help in their need for plasma, oxygen cylinders etc.



While the government was busy campaigning for their elections, the youth took their place at the forefront of the battle against Covid. For every rally they conducted, someone shared a link to a life-saving resource. For every speech they ordained with promises that never seem to come through, another organised a covid relief programme. Through the use of social media, and the public outreach that it gives us from the safety of our homes, the youth decided to take matters into their own hands and actively work towards saving lives. Be it a need for plasma, oxygen cylinders, or any information related to Covid, they have been very vocal about sharing and spreading the word. Twitter was flooded with links to self-compiled documents and excel sheets that listed resource availability. Teenagers were calling hospitals and sources left and right for verification. They formed a chain of communication, delegated responsibilities and amplified any leads and links that could help. Discord servers for volunteers willing to put time and effort started popping up. They had city-wise, state-wise and resource-wise divisions for easier access and more efficient communication. Google links had an organising committee that spent half their waking moment updating new information and revising the exhausted links. Instagram became a forum for amplification as well. People started tagging young influencers for sharing their requests, instead of looking to the government. Every time another story or post came up, there was a flurry of replies with recently verified links. People put the effort into compiling a guided list of posts and accounts that were actively working towards sharing covid resources. The technologically savvy youth started working on better and more efficient forums of aid. There was a software created that would search Twitter hashtags based on key requirements and location to provide the best responses. Someone created an entire app based on meal plans and delivery systems. Another worked on a software that would update automatically as per the nearest medical resources.

Furthermore, with a country divided with polarised perspectives, there has been a lot of misinformation flooding the internet about this pandemic. The youth has done a great job at debunking these myths and trying their best to curb the spread of misinformation to the general public. Their continuous efforts as the situation of the pandemic continues to worsen throughout the country is a beacon of hope for many common people.

But, while they are doing their best to care about the people of the country, who cares for them? It is not just a retweet button or another share option that they are pressing, it is looking at continuous casualties, sifting through the multitudes of hopelessness to come across that one verified information that they give away to a random stranger, never knowing if it is enough. They have to call and get bashed by a dozen hospital staff to get that one correct information about ICU beds that this unknown account asked for because their parents are in a desperate condition. They have to keep the multiple Whatsapp groups on mute because it never stops pinging, people never stop dying. They are we - me, my friends, us, you, your friends. We feel selfish to say we are being affected because we know what the truly infected look like. We think it is wrong for us to take a break from these platforms because what if we could help one more person, reduce one more statistic. We assume we are less than the hurt because we have no documentation to prove that we are collapsing from the inside too.
