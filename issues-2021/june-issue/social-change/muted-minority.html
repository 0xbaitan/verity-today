 Muted Minority


Pragya Roy Barman

May 1, 2021   |   Social Change


India is witnessing a surge in violent crimes against women and especially, women from minority communities. From physical assault to verbal harassment to sexual abuse, the statistics keep on rising and there is little done to battle them down, apart from censoring the female population of India.



All of us girls have grown up with comments about how our pride has never risen from between our legs, or how our entire persona can be deduced by our dress code, and everything in between. And this is a childhood narration from the central hub of a metropolitan city, so what about our rural counterparts?

As per the last central survey of India, the female literacy of rural India had increased to 56.8% in comparison to the male literacy rate of 72.3%. This number is after the numerous initiatives of the government that incentivise education with money, so children, often the girls, are registered with proxy attendance to only avail the money promised without ever showing their face in the institute. And the gap, thus produced between the two genders, does not only symbolise who can write their name and who cannot, but also represents who understands their rights and who does not. For how do you speak up against a crime if you do not know whether there is a law to protect you from it or not?

The National Crime Records Bureau of India stated that for every one crime recorded against a woman from a minority community, there are over thirty that go undocumented, and not just from the victim’s side. If a woman does overcome the fear of being ostracised and stigmatised by the incident and goes to file a report, she is met with harassment at every stage. Often the police refuse to accept sexual harassment FIRs, particularly if they are filed against upper-caste or well-known men of the community. They either send the woman away with false hopes only to discard the allegation or threaten her themselves. So how do you fight for the law, when the law enforcers are against you as well?

In the Hathras case of 2020, a Dalit woman was brutally raped by a group of upper-caste men. The police refused to accept the report and even burnt the body in the middle of the night against her family’s wishes. There were mobs of people rallying in support of the rapists. This incident took the nation by storm and numerous people came out to raise their voices against this cruelty. Not even a week later, in Balrampur, a 22-year old woman was raped by two men, to which the police let out a press statement that the victim had apparently tried to force one of the rapists to marry her as if this claim could justify the deed. In the same week two more rape reports, an eight-year-old and a fourteen-year-old surfaced in the same state. A state that has a chief minister like Yogi Adityanath who has been known to make statements like “women need protection, not independence”, and yet he still remains in power.

And this is not the only failure of the people responsible for the workings of our country. In January 2021, the Bombay High Court ruled that groping without skin-to-skin contact cannot be categorised under sexual assault. Yet, women every day talk about the daily harassment they face on public transports, a daily commute of the working class. How many steps can we take to shift away from a straying hand till the court finally decides our safety is not in the comforts of clothes. The Supreme Court threatened a rapist to marry his victim or he would lose his government job. Yet, people claim sexual assault complaints ruins a man’s life.

We act surprised every time a new rape case graces the national media as if the ever-rising unsolved cases with the accused roaming free are not ample evidence against the clear partiality of our society. We stop going to the streets or holding candle vigils as soon as the media drops the case because we are used to it. We are used to being abused, harassed, violated.

How loud can you be when faced with deaf ears?
