 Synonymised Reality


Niharika Rajsaha

June 1, 2021   |   Social Change


After generations of tabooing every aspect regarding sexuality, Gen Z conversations are ones where people have no qualms with talking about sexuality. We’ve realised our sexuality is not something to be put in traditional boxes. It’s an important part of who we are, a part that’s ours to keep. And the process of generalizing us as people has been a centuries-long insult to that part of ourselves.



As a teenager in a world where an open conversation is a real concept, I’ve found myself in the heart of many conversations regarding my dating life and my usual answers are “I’m still figuring myself out” and “I don’t really know” and even in all of their ambiguity, they felt like the easiest words to describe how I felt. I’ve given this a bit of thought although I mostly believe that I’ll figure it out when I do. Sometimes it’s unclear to the point where I don’t understand whether it’s that I’m feeling something complex or just simply overthinking the absence of it. But I know that I am not the only one who feels this way. I realised that other than not being able to find the best word to define my sexuality, I don’t have issues with the lack of a label. This is something that has had a lot of misconception attached to it. It has been misconstrued as being confused or afraid of admitting our sexualities to ourselves. That’s the thing. We are not confused. We identify with this sexuality; we aren’t confused that this is what we feel. Not defining exactly who we are attracted to does not make us people who still need to figure themselves out. It means that we have figured ourselves out as people who don’t or can’t define who we are or aren’t attracted to. And I am not afraid to admit that to myself.

It has been called out as not a ‘real’ sexuality. I figure that the ambiguity confuses some, but it is real. It is something that real people feel. Pomosexuality is defined as one which does not fit into any labels for a particular kind of attraction. They may feel as though there isn’t a term in vocabulary apt to define their feelings or may choose to not define them or both. Pomosexuality is a sexual orientation in itself but has often been considered as an umbrella term that branches into four more specific sexualities: Comsexual, Novisexual, Cryptosexual and Omniacepomo. Comsexuality is a sexuality that refers to those who feel attraction but aren’t able to or choose not to define their sexuality any more than that. Novisexuality is a sexuality that refers to people who identify their feelings of attraction or lack of them to be too complex to specifically define. Cryptosexuality refers to a person who cannot discern their sexual orientation in specific words. There is no regularity or pattern in when or towards whom they do or do not feel attraction. Omniacepomo refers to a person who is under the asexual umbrella but doesn’t/can’t define where under it or preference of gender of partner. Omniacepomo consists of three words constituting it: Omni stands for omnisexuality and pansexuality, indicating that the person is attracted to all genders; ace depicts that they are in the asexual spectrum and pomo is the abbreviation of pomosexuality to indicate that they can’t/don’t label their sexuality further.

A question that arises is why we don’t choose to identify with a ‘definite’ sexual orientation. It’s because sexuality is not a choice and by choosing, one would be insulting that particular sexuality and pomosexuality by reducing it to a choice. It would not be fair to us either because we would be making ourselves feel out of our own self by identifying as someone we aren’t. Even the choice that one makes to not define their sexual orientation is one that is made from a place of feeling comfortable with their own identity. Hence, although sometimes we can choose the words to define our identity, we don’t choose the feelings that help us make the decision not to.

This misconception that pomosexuality is synonymous with being confused about one’s sexuality or comes from a place of fear of self-acceptance has often brought about consolation. It is, however, misleading and offensive to tell someone “It’s okay, it’ll all pan out eventually” or “It’ll get better” because it isn’t something to get better from. It is not a missing piece or an incomplete void that needs to be filled. The fact that we choose not to/can’t define the attraction we feel as it comes is us figuring out yet another piece of our lives. That, in itself, is that part and it is one we cannot run away from. It cannot be replaced with words like experimenting or confused because it is not synonymous.
