 The Other R: Rights Representation


Priyanka Banerjee

June 1, 2021   |   Social Change


People often assume that queer spaces and queer representation are free of the crippling effects of sexism, it is deemed to only exist in the sphere of heterosexual relationships. However, when we take a closer look at the representation of the LGBTQ+ community in popular media, the signs of sexism are glaringly obvious. From encouraging hostility towards the queer community to portraying inclusive homosexual relationships on-screen, there is no doubt that the world has come a long way in terms of queer representation. However, most advertisements and movies focus on displaying relationships solely between men. Portrayals of romantic or sexual relationships between women are still extremely rare and, when there is any representation at all, it is overly sexualised for the gratification of male viewers and does not authentically represent women of the LGBTQ+ community. How many movies or television shows that are iconic for their positive representation of queer communities only focus on the ‘G’ in LGBTQ+? Why is it harder for us to recall advertisements featuring lesbians or bisexual people than it is to think of ones that feature gay men? How many women who are part of the LGBTQ+ community can say that their sexualities have been accurately represented by popular media even today?



Gay men have historically been featured more prominently by advertisers than women who are a part of the LGBTQ+ community. In the 1990s, when brands and advertisers first became aware of the gay market, it was considered more attractive to advertise gay male couples as men were not associated with traditional images of domesticity. To this day, it is still very rare for us to see female couples featured in advertisements, especially those that haven’t been oversexualized to satisfy male viewers. And when lesbians are featured in advertisements, it is stereotypically shown as a huge tragedy for all the men involved. For example, an advertisement by XXL All Sports United, depicts a woman walking through an airport with men lusting after and make desperate attempts to impress her. In the end, when she reveals her sexual identity, the men are devastated because they lost out on a beautiful woman.

In Hollywood too, it has been refreshing to see the increase in queer representation in an industry that has a history of homophobia. But from ‘Brokeback Mountain’ to ‘Call Me By Your Name’, the most iconic queer movies of our time have focused on gay men and male couples. Queer women are massively underrepresented in Hollywood and movies like ‘Portrait of a Lady on Fire’ and ‘Carol’ that do represent queer women are considered to be art and indie films that unlike movies like ‘Love, Simon’, have still not found their way into mainstream media. According to GLAAD, 83% of LGBTQ+ representation in media consists of gay men while only 35% consists of lesbians. Here too, representation of queer women is stereotypical, and most lesbian characters fit the Lipstick Lesbian trope of being traditionally attractive and are hypersexualized. More often than not, lesbian characters repeatedly experiment with heterosexual relationships on-screen, making lesbianism seem like a flippant lifestyle choice that can be taken up and dropped whenever one desires. Even more innovative television shows, like ‘The L Word’ hypersexualise lesbians. It is not to say that lesbians are not sexual beings but that their representation cannot be skewed to cater to heterosexual male fantasies.

The lack of strong female lesbian characters in movies and television shows also leads to a dearth of femslash fanfictions on most popular fanfiction sites. Femslash can be defined as a subgenre of slash fan fiction that focuses on romantic and/or sexual relationships between female fictional characters. On most popular fanfiction sites, there are at least more than twice as many fanfictions about relationships between men than relationships between women. For example, records from a popular online fanfiction database, Archive of Our Own (Ao3), show that, as of 2019, 41.9% of total fanfictions are about male homosexual couples, 17.6% about heterosexual couples and only 5.8% are about queer females. This is due to the lack of a ‘shippable’ dynamic between female characters and because the media often accords more value and complexity to relationships between men than relationships between women. Women characters also often exist as love interests of male characters, leading to compulsory heterosexuality and squashing the chances of femslash ships becoming canon.

Even in an art form as liberating as drag, intended specifically for queer acceptance, we see a lack of representation. Drag is a gender-bending art form in which a person dresses in clothing and make-up meant to exaggerate a specific gender identity, usually of a sex that is not the same as the one they are assigned at birth. Historically, drag kings were usually female performance artists who wore ‘masculine’ clothing and personified male gender stereotypes, while the opposite applied for drag queens. However, in recent years, with people accepting gender as a spectrum rather than two sets of opposing ideals, there have been trans men, cisgender men and non-binary people performing as drag kings as well. Not only does women’s underrepresentation in front of and behind the camera play a huge role in the absence of drag kings from popular culture, but women assuming male characteristics also denies patriarchy what it sees as traditional gender roles, an act that is often considered intimidating and even terrifying.

Drag kings no longer necessarily exude masculinity by conforming to gendered clothing and have come a long way from dressing up in bland jeans and t-shirts. Today we have genderless drag kings, more effeminate drag kings, and kings who play with a variety of make-up and costumes, giving drag kings as well as the art form more depth. But given that large drag audiences often do not even know who a drag king is and that the word ‘drag’ is still associated with drag queens and extreme femininity, there is still a long way to go.

What is common when it comes to queer representation in advertising, Hollywood, fanfiction and even drag is that people seem to think that representing just one sexuality and just one gender is enough. Lesbianism has been trivialised by skewed representation in the media. This needs to change. Queer women need to be represented on popular television with just as much value and depth accorded to female relationships as male relationships. While the battle for our rights has come a long way, the fight for representation still needs to be fought.
