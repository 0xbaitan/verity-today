 Into Neuralink's State-Of-The-Art Cyborg Brain Technology


Tiya Gupta

June 1, 2021   |   Technology


As of 2020, Neuralink has its headquarters in the Mission District of San Francisco, sharing the former Pioneer Trunk Factory building with OpenAI, another company that Musk had co-founded. Even though Musk didn't hold an executive position, he majorly owned Neuralink as of September 2018.

A tweet in August of 2020 has confirmed reports that Musk is the incumbent CEO of Neuralink now. The company's trademark was purchased in January 2017.



The technology used in the future involves the placement of a small module outside the highest point where it can wirelessly receive information from electrode threads. These threads are thin and flexible so that they can be embedded inside our brains with ease.

Musk insists that it will be a "kind of a Fitbit in your skull with tiny wires". It has a robotic apparatus that embeds the threads to prevent the damage of blood vessels in the body.

Currently, the electrodes are unable to record the firing off an individual neuron owing to its large size, so as to record alone the firing of a gaggle of neurons. The representatives of Neuralink believe they can fix the problem by employing a refined algorithm. However, it is expensive and doesn't produce completely accurate results.



In 2018, Gizmodo reported that Neuralink "keeps its work highly confidential," despite public records showing it attempted to open an animal testing facility in San Francisco. After which, he began research at the University of California, Davis. In 2019, in a live demonstration at the California Academy of Sciences, the team showcased a prototype to the public. It is a system that includes an ultra-thin probe inserted into the brain with a neurosurgical robot that will perform operations. In addition, a high-density electronic system that can process information from neurons is also present. It is based on technology developed by the University of California, San Francisco and the University of California, Berkeley.

The Probe

The probe is composed primarily of polyimide (a biocompatible material) with a thin gold or platinum conductor. This probe is then inserted into the brain through an automated process performed by a surgical robot. Each consists of a wide area containing electrodes capable of locating electrical signals in the brain and a sensory area where the wires interact with electronic systems that allow the amplification and acquisition of brain signals. Each probe contains 48 or 96 wires, and each wire comprises 32 independent electrodes, thus forming a system with up to 3072 electrodes per array.

The Robot

Neuralink stated that they developed a robot that can quickly insert many flexible probes into the brain, thereby avoiding the tissue damage and life issues associated with larger and more rigid probes. The robot has an insertion head with a 40 Âµm diameter needle, made of tungsten rhenium, designed to adhere to the insertion ring. It is used to transport and insert a single probe and penetrate Meninges and brain tissue. The robot can plug in up to 6 cables (192 electrodes) per minute.

The Electronics

Elon Musk on Neuralink Neuralink has developed an application-specific integrated circuit (ASIC) to create a 1,536 channel recording system. The system consists of 256 individually programmable amplifiers ("analog pixels"), an on-chip analogue-digital converter ("ADC") and a peripheral control circuit to serialize the digital information obtained.



Their goal is to convert the information taken from neurons into understandable binary codes to comprehend the function of the brain and the ability to stimulate these neurons. At present, the electrode is too great in size to record the discharge of a single neuron. It can only record the neuronal discharge of a group of neurons. A Neuralink representative believes that they can mitigate this issue with an improved algorithm. However, its computational cost is high and may not produce precise results.

According to Musk, in July 2020, Neuralink received innovative device designation from the FDA, allowing limited human testing following FDA medical device guidelines.

As of 2017, some neuroprosthetics can interpret brain signals and allow people with disabilities to regulate their prosthetic limbs (arms and legs both).
