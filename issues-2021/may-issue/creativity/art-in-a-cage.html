 Art in a Cage


Ishani Dutta

May 1, 2021   |   Creativity


“The object of art is not to reproduce reality, but to create a reality of the same intensity.” The reality that surrounds us today is compelling in so many ways. A pandemic rages around us, the world is on fire, and little to no action is being taken, thus fuelling internal agony, frustration, anger, pain, anxiety, making helplessness a mascot of the people. It is said that the darkest of circumstances fuel the most beautiful artistic creations. It holds a degree of truth, and during this lockdown, we have all turned to art, in some way or the other. It reminds me of the Renaissance, the Dark Ages overturned by the creation and force of art. This feels like a contemporary Dark Age, but that hasn’t put a block on any form of art. As artists, we often feel pressure to create works that outperform our previous accomplishments. It’s the same for all fields, but the worst phase in an artist’s career is that ‘mental block’ or in my case as a writer, ‘writer's block’. This lockdown has allowed and encouraged me to write things from multiple perspectives, take on fictional scenarios and work them into completed fabrics of art. There is no doubt that my creative power has flourished during this time, but it has also witnessed the lowest of lows. I often couldn’t write anything for months.



Did I stop writing because of writer’s block? No. I kept going, even if it didn't feel great, the key is to always write. Finishing something is sometimes better than perfecting it. It might not feel great at the time but it will soon. During this lockdown I wrote almost 35 poems and about 10 songs. Some of them stand out more than the others. I could only write after having a eureka moment. This lockdown compelled me to work on my abilities and write, even if it doesn’t seem perfect, it is something worth reading and feeling inspired by. Art can be created anytime, anywhere. It can be driven by anything, it’s what we choose to and how we drive ourselves to create that matters. If we treat art as a job, something we must do, not because we enjoy it, nothing we create will ever give us the unconscious smile and accelerated heartbeat when we complete it. The art that we show to our loved ones, and when they ask what inspired it, how deep you delve into your mind as to what drew you to create. The shine in your eyes, as you explain, the expression, almost a race against time to get every single thought out onto a canvas, or a notebook, a sketchbook, or anything. How attempting to recall a thought seems more difficult, than trying to recall Archimedes principle during your exams. The dedication when you wake up at 4 in the morning after having a dream, knowing in your heart it can be translated into something more tangible and meaningful. Or even writing a poem while listening to your favourite song because you feel particularly inspired. Or when you remember the last time someone broke your heart, and you repressed your feelings, and you decide to unleash them into a fury of thoughts and strokes and words. When you decide to write a letter to your old best friend who you haven’t seen in a while, talk about all the things you wanted to do, but now can’t. When you write a song to your old crush about how much you used to love them, and even though you knew that things wouldn’t work out, you still held out a childlike hope that it would someday. The dawning of adulthood on your mind, the anxiety that follows, and the responsibilities you must be proficient in by this time, art is the universal mode of expression, resolution, and acceptance, for in art, no one is ever a stranger, no one is ever unwelcome. The power of art lies in the minds of every person that walks the earth.

The biggest word of advice I can give to anyone struggling to create something meaningful, just don’t ever give up. Just because something doesn’t seem perfect, doesn’t mean it is not capable of receiving love and fascination, prompting conversations we haven’t had before, but should.
