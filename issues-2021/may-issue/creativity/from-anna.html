 From Anna


Priyanka Banerjee

May 1, 2021   |   Creativity





Clarence Elementary School,
102, Downing Street,
Kent - 533425.

2nd March, 1917.

Clarence House,
14, Harley Street,
London - 523412

My dearest Caroline,

 I am so very sorry for not having replied to your last letter until now. Everything has been so busy here, but I had no intention of worrying you. I received your letter a week ago and yes, I am quite well, as I hope you are. At least as well as one can be in a war-stricken country.

 I am so saddened and enraged to hear that the publishers have threatened to suspend your column in the newspaper! Word of your campaign for equal pay for female factory workers has even reached our small village here; just the other day, I passed by a group of women in the town square who were discussing one of your pamphlets.Women are finally starting to see things for what they really are, and I think that’s what all these big newspaper men are afraid of. Why ever else would fighting for half the population’s rights be deemed ‘controversial’?

 The war is only becoming worse than ever now. There is hardly any time for us to eat and sleep. At least three dozen soldiers are being brought in every day and so many of them are already dead. We barely have enough equipment and staff to help everyone in this small school that they have turned into a hospital. There are only about a dozen nurses here, including myself, and we are having to make beds out of tables and chairs for all the wounded coming in. And do not even get me started on the food shortages.

 Back here at the hospital, no one can think about anything but the war but anyone trying to dismiss women’s rights with frivolous excuses about how the war is bigger and more important is simply trying to delay and deny the change that is sweeping through our nation right now. In fact, I would do a lot to think about anything other than the war and death and politics right now, if only for a few moments. And almost all of us here believe that it is more important for stories and articles like yours to be published now because, like you said, with all the women who are losing loved ones and family and income in such times, they need help and they need their rights now more than ever. I can only hope that more women will rise to be leaders like you in this fight.

 As for everything you told me about President Wilson’s decision in your last letter, I am so glad that the chances of our victory and of the war ending sooner than either of us anticipated are so much higher now. Catherine, the girl here at the hospital I told you about in my last letter, her husband in the army tells her that their lieutenant informed their platoon that the United States would be entering the Great War soon. I am sure that will tip the balance in our favour and that we will finally be able to defeat the Germans and perhaps we will even see each other sooner than we thought! Of course, I am too scared to get my hopes up right now. The wounded and the dead are still arriving in astonishing numbers and Dr. Philips says that none of this will be getting much better for a while, so it is hard to believe that this war might actually be over soon. I wish you were here, for your presence and your strength would have made all of this so much easier. I know there is nothing you can do, I really do know that, but I still miss you so very much, more than I can put down in words, and the only thought that keeps me going through the tragedy of it all is the thought of seeing you again when this war is finally over.

 As for my brother, neither my mother nor I have heard from him in months. Mother is worried sick, she writes all the time and I hardly have enough time to respond to all her letters in between tending to one wounded soldier and the next. The girls here tell me that it is very likely that he just is not receiving our letters, it seems that that happens a lot with soldiers at the front, but I cannot help but feel my heart fill with terror every time I think of him and wonder what I would do if he were to suddenly show up here one day with all the other dead and wounded soldiers.

 But enough about me. How have you been? I hope you did not take what those ghastly publishers said to heart. I know you have never been one to worry about what others think of you, but in these times, I know it is hard to keep our spirits up. The women here love your writing, they wait to read your column every week, and if the publishers cannot see how good you are, I feel sorry for them. As for me, I wait with bated breath every Wednesday for the newspaper because reading it feels like hearing your voice, and I feel like I can almost see your eyes light up as they always do when you are talking about something you are passionate about. It feels like having a part of you with me, if only for a short while. Your column is so good and it’s inspiring so many people, love.

 I am afraid I have to end this here now, my break is almost over, but know that I miss you and I love you very much and I cannot wait to see you again. I can only hope that we will meet each very soon. Until the next letter, goodbye my dearest.

All my love.
Yours,
A.
