 Spring in Japanese Art & Literature


Sreemita Mukherjee

May 1, 2021   |   Creativity


From the earliest of times, the Japanese have celebrated the beauty of seasons and have thoroughly recognized the poignancy of their inevitable evanescence. These celebrations were mainly in the form of rituals and festivals that adorn the Japanese calendar quite endlessly—from the welcoming of spring at the lunar New Year to the offerings made to the harvest moon. Immense sensitivity to seasonal changes has always been a major part of Japanese culture. Poetry proved to be the earliest model of this representation, and in response, painters and sculptors formed images that could be ultimately traced down to seasonal inspiration.

This attention to seasonal change stems partly from the fact that Shinto—Japan’s native belief system—paid great importance and heed to the topic. Most Japanese books from the time explain that because Japan was an agricultural country, it depended deeply on climate and of course, seasons. Thus, it became a common Japanese myth that instead of trying to ‘fight’ nature, the Japanese lived in harmony with it. The literature thus born out of this notion was called fūdo.



Man’yōshū 万葉集 (Collection of Ten Thousand Leaves) edited in the eighth century, is the earliest evidence of poetry that has significant exploration of the four seasons. Books eight and ten of the Man’yōshū consisted of 31 syllable poetry (waka) arranged by seasonal topic in seasonal order. Kokinshū 古今集 (Collection of Japanese Poems Old and New) which was edited in the tenth century, further refined the seasonal arrangement found in the Man’yōshū.

Kokinshū 古今集, in particular, had two books that extensively consisted of poems about spring. In total, there were 134 spring poems in the Kokinshū. The seasonal motifs that were usually associated with spring were mainly—plum blossoms, warbler, cherry blossoms, yellow kerria (yamabuki), and wisteria. Each season, as represented through the Kokinshū was also said to progress in three gradual phases. In the case of spring, there would first come the full bloom of plum blossoms signifying the start of spring, then it would move on to cherry blossoms, and lastly to wisteria and yellow kerria that would signify late spring.

Although it is clear that poets and artists favored spring and gave it stark importance hinting that perhaps spring was long, Japan’s climate said otherwise. Spring was exceedingly short lasting for a month or so, before giving way to a long and indispensable summer. In literature, it was exactly the opposite. Summer was reduced to a short and inconsequential season, while spring was given undivided attention. The love for spring poetry was heightened to the point wherein, winter poetry most often represented winter as a sign of how close the spring season is.

By the thirteenth century, seasonal motifs became codified and structured, and almost all poets and artists incorporated these motifs to specify seasons. Seasonal motifs were not only restricted to flowers now, the use of birds as seasonal motifs, were also introduced. For Spring, these birds were specifically— bush warbler (uguisu), pheasant, and skylark (Hibari). These motifs were extremely influential and became a part of Japanese culture. Many, many festivals take place in the spring. For example, the Hina-matsuri (ひな祭) is celebrated in late spring.

The Edo period proved to be one of the more peaceful and economically stable eras, wherein the arts flourished. The use of seasonal greetings and seasonal phrases were especially popularised. These seasonal phrases or kigo 季語 became an essential part of the seventeen-syllable haiku poems. During the same time, Satoyama (literally meaning farm under the mountain), which explored the life outside of the cities in the form of art and literature, heavily flourished. Their seasonal motifs were different. The coming of spring in Satoyama was distinguished by the presence of chestnuts and barley, sparrows, and swallows.

In modern Japanese literature, one will often find the sentiment of loss being represented when it comes to the portrayal of spring, as most authors believe that recent technological advancements have hindered the beauty of nature. However, there also exists a perspective, in which Japanese authors try to incorporate the ancient sense of 'harmony with nature to the Western humanistic view of humankind. In modern times, the use of kigo in literature has severely declined and most seasonal motifs have changed as the Japanese have migrated to the Gregorian calendar.

What persists, is the impulse, the influence of all these poems, stories, and art. Spring in Japan is still celebrated widely, albeit not in the same way it was a couple of hundred years ago. The words to portray the awe and wonder one feels as they gaze upon the fleeting beauty of a cherry blossom can be found in plenty, many of which are still being written. The art is made to capture a single scenery; a single moment of this painstakingly short season will never be enough. With each passing year, there is an influx of new seasonal motifs, new paintings, new poems—and as long as there exists an eye to witness Spring, in all its wonderful glory, these artists and their thoughts will keep producing material worth dissecting and studying; for a long, long time.

春されば  When spring arrives
のべにまづさく  And fields begin to bloom
見れどあかぬ花  If you see a flower
まひなしに  That’s just a sprout
ただなのるべき  Mere whispers are best
花のななれや  Until it’s in full bloom.
