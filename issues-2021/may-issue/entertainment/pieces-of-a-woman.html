 Pieces Of A Woman


Anwesh Banerjee

May 1, 2021   |   Entertainment


There is a strange, lovely quality about this film. It’s delicate, aware of sound and touch and smell and growth and life; sometimes, it’s so delicate, it’s inert: characters stare, run, take trains, drive cars and walk a lot as Howard Shore’s beautiful score swells and ebbs on the soundtrack, and nothing much happens. It’s about the inertia of grief, a kind of grief that many of us are lucky enough never to experience, a grief that numbs and dumbs and tears frayed relationships.



This is a very visual movie; dialogue is frequent, but so are silences. We see close-ups of characters’ faces, but also of their necks, lips, shoulders. There are long, long takes, in which the camera glides around, choosing whom to observe and not always picking the one we, the audience, might, preferring to linger on a wordless face and not on the person speaking. The film’s most significant scene, its pièce de résistance, is a literally breath-taking twenty-three-minute one-take in which Martha Weiss (Vanessa Kirby in a deeply committed performance), surrounded by her partner Sean (Shia LaBeouf, more on whom later) and midwife Eva (Molly Parker), gives birth at home. Martha begins to experience contractions and from that minute, we follow her as she collapses onto the sofa, writhes on the floor, is transferred to a full bathtub, and then makes her way to her bed, as Sean and Eva hover around. The mechanics of staging this huge scene are tough to imagine, but the three performers – especially Kirby – pull it off admirably. It isn’t an easy one: there are close-ups of Martha’s swollen belly, we see her bite Sean’s thigh in pain, we see the baby begin to emerge, we are witness to Martha’s boundless distress. I will never know what that pain is like although Kirby has not delivered a child herself, but it is an extremely convincing sequence. The consequences of this painful delivery effect and inform the rest of the film.

It’s also visual in the sense of recurring motifs, most significantly a bridge that Sean is working on building (he’s a construction worker). We meet the characters once a month from September to March, and each time the date appears on the screen there’s a shot of the progress on the bridge’s construction. Sean also brings up resonance, the scientific concept involving vibrations and rhythm. What this bridge metaphor means is never made explicit (almost nothing in this film is), but I got the sense that the building of the bridge is a reverse reflection of Martha and Sean’s relationship: as one progresses steadily to wholeness, the other fractures. And the other motif that we see is apples. Apples embody life and growth (especially in a biblical sense), and the smell of a newborn is compared to the smell of apples. The silent resilience and renewal of the fruit and its seeds is something, perhaps, that Martha both finds solace in and aspires to.



But the most enduring theme, of course, is grief. Sean and Martha grieve differently, cope differently, and sometimes mirror one another. Martha speaks very little. Sean comes off as something of a dick: he even refers to himself as boorish. And it’s impossible now not to look at this character in light of FKA Twigs’ lawsuit accusing LaBeouf of a ‘relentlessly’ abusive relationship. The two of them were in a relationship in which LaBeouf subjected her to assault and violence, sexual, verbal, mental. In the film, Sean at one-point forces himself on Martha. She is initially resistant, but eventually compliant; his is a raw sexual energy as he insists, she touch his penis and as he tries to yank her pants off. She asks that he give her a second to take them off herself, but he stalks away. It’s an unsettling scene and the filmmakers make the slightly problematic decision to stay with Sean, weeping in the open, after this scene. Is his bad behaviour being passed off as grief? Again, it’s not made explicit, but I was left both confused and uncomfortable. Martin Scorsese, who is one of the film’s executive producers, said the film examined a “moral conflict with all its nuances, drawn out with care and compassion but without judgement”. Director Kornél Mundruczó and writer Kata Wéber (the film opens with both their names under the joint credit “a film by”) don’t judge their characters, but I don’t think we can help ourselves as viewers.

And life, as ever, goes on. When you experience insurmountable grief, do you ever find happiness again? And how do you help a grieving person? I don’t know, and nor does anyone in this movie. Pieces of a Woman doesn’t tell, it shows – sometimes viscerally and sometimes so quietly that we almost don’t notice. And as it slips by, culminating in a dreamy closing scene where the apple metaphor reaches its apotheosis, it gives us a little window into the life of a woman. How we interpret what we see through the window is left for us to decide.
