 Tenet - Nolan's Self-Serving Masterpieces


Pradyumn Nahata

May 1, 2021   |   Entertainment


2020 has not exactly been the perfect year for the film industry. Empty cinema halls, jobs lost, premieres cancelled, and deals made by executive producers with Hulu- hell, it may have been one of the worst years. That is essentially why Christopher Nolan’s (director of Inception, Dunkirk, The Dark Knight Series, etc.) brand new production Tenet- which has been released in Indian cinemas like a black sheep in an array of postponed releases, catches the spotlight and as explained in this article- may remain with it. Also, just a heads-up, for the sake of Indian formality, I say we assume Tenet has gone hot in India only because the theatres haven’t seen many other movies come in and also due to the fact that Nolan is one of the most popularly esteemed directors in the circuit. Not at all because Dimple Kapadia, a Bollywood icon has a major role in the film, and this would make the walls of our recently empty theatres resound with restless whistles. Totally not. Got it? Cool, let’s start!

As the name clearly fails to suggest, TeNeT is an action-thriller blockbuster, infiltrating the Sci-fi Genre very explicitly- your classic Nolan combo. It explores a turn in the career of a secret intelligence agent (played by John David Washington (BlackKKlansman))(working for the CIA) , who after an encounter with a group of terrorists in an Opera House, takes a suicide pill after being caught and wakes up on a government-owned ship. Disclaimer at this point- CIA’s suicide pills are apparently worse than their inability of letting leftist Presidents live. A superior tells Washington’s character that he has been chosen for a special directive, the directive being saving the present from the future. Fancy. The protagonist accepts his fate, and goes on a rummage to deconstruct the agenda of this threat for himself. This leads him to Bombay (Nolan very completely, iconically, symbolically, originally achieves Indian culture by showing us the rainy streets of Bombay filled with busy vendors selling homogenous puchkas :D) where he is approached by a man who introduces himself as Washington’s ally, called Neil (p.b. Robert Pattinson.) After that, the movie sees it’s ascending action roll off with special effects dispersed on a frantic overdose as Washington progresses on his mission and finds Kat, the female lead (played by Elizabeth Debicki), and wife of hunky Russian arms producer, Andrei Sator (who as we find out in a few, is much more than a shabby gun manufacturer), our antagonist.



That’s where I end with the plot description, having laid down the base of it for you if you haven’t seen it. To examine Tenet, we have to first understand that this is a coming-of-age movie which is consolidated in two genres- Sci-Fi and Action. Therefore, we will analyse —

a) The action as it takes place on screen, how it is complemented by cinematography and special effects,

b) The development of the plot points and incorporation of the sci-fi elements into that development, and it’s subsequent conveyance on screen.

If Tenet is truly a movie which will describe Nolan for the succeeding ages (though we really do prefer other productions of his to take up that role, it’s really tough to judge) and establish itself as one of the many great films in the sci-fi genre, it’ll be because of it’s graphics, the fact that it was shot on almost only IMAX Cameras, and the visual effects Nolan easily plays around with as if they’re his trademark puppets and he, a seasoned puppeteer. To give you a better understanding of how Tenet does at visual effects, you’ll have to know the scientific basis the plot builds itself upon.

So basically- the future is super-pissed with the present because of the intensive deterioration of climate conditions artificially brought about by us, and in the process has declared war upon us. This is done by inverting a technology called “inversion’ by which objects and people go backward in time by reversing their entropy, and using our antagonist as a middleman- a facilitator. Through this the sociopaths in the future are manufacturing lethal bullets (and other stuff), inverting them, and in the process, flooding us with pretty dangerous tangible material with reversed entropy- or whatever they call it. Not only that, there are literally several inversion machines of the same technology in the present world by going through which you can invert your entropy.

In the movie, many characters go through these machines. A lot. And boy, does Nolan have fun with it. You basically have a totally new concept besides time travel, and based on this concept you reverse whatever takes off a scene you have, both in terms of the order of events and the position of objects. This allows Nolan to direct an encounter of Washington’s character with himself, cars turning over on themselves, the sun going from the west to east instead of the other way around, etc. In Tenet’s last sequence, since time moves in different directions for non-inverted and inverted persons, it’s a mania on the screen. Audiences see the same building implode and explode simultaneously simply because the implosion happens in universe A while the explosion happens in universe B. The executive team has greatly capitalized on the opportunity the science, whether credible or incredible, presents the directors with- and has gone all in on making Tenet a modelling of how cinematic technology has progressed. Here, Tenet has my go-ahead as a Nolan classic, though it moulds itself more as a spy thriller in the initial portions.



However, that’s not all. The plot in terms of its direction is essentially where the problem is. Remember, the plot is not simply the content which fills the movie and gives reason to the action- but the very arrangement of that content and in the tone it leads the viewers to the content. For me, the plot of Tenet was really disjointed. For the first 1 and a half hours of the movie or so, it is quite basically impossible to understand what is going on- and why it’s happening. Yes, it is understandable that Nolan directs complex movies and confusion is just a conventional give-and-take concept, but the confusion which normally arises out of a Nolan movie is usually —

i) at the end of it

ii) is more curiosity than confusion

In this case, you’re staring at the screen watching all the fascinating takes with the visual effects doing their part, but at the same time, there is no real interest to carry you away to home-base because knowledge generates interest- and all the knowledge of the movie’s proceedings come in it’s last quarter- with too much responsibility given to the climax and the post-climactic scenes of the film to delineate why everything that has taken place, has taken place.

While movies like Inception and even Interstellar do have an enormity of concentration of content in climactic scenes, they usually have a fair amount of distribution of exposition through character interactions, scripted dialogue, and the informative exploration of scientific bases. In Tenet, you have nothing like that until the very last moments, with all the characters engrossed in ignorant action and the movie barely giving space to the viewers for them to register proceedings. That is, for me, the main reason why Tenet does not and cannot get as much clout as other Nolan movies- besides it’s untimely release in the middle of pandemic panic.

This does not at all discredit the genius of the content Nolan has transformed from thought to Tenet, and frankly, the last quarter of the movie is quite a pleasure. If we all had his perspective, and his knowledge of the plot beforehand, the movie would be an instant turn-on. But we don’t, and only a few including Nolan do. That is the primary factor behind the title of this article: Tenet — Nolan’s Self-Serving Masterpiece

Despite my amateuristic criticisms, Tenet is an indicator of not only how much Nolan has seasoned his talents of creation, but also how much the film industry has achieved and how easily it tackles concepts of physics unfathomable to the human eye. While being a production pretentious viewers would applaud at the intermission and call “genius” while not understanding the half of it, Tenet is a production you cannot help but grin at and appreciate when the credits roll.
