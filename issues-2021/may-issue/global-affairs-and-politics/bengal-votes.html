 Bengal Votes


Priyanka Banerjee

May 1, 2021   |   Global Affairs & Politics


“Indian democracy’s greatest strength is that we have always put nation ahead of politics.” — Atal Bihari Vajpayee



The 2021 West Bengal State Assembly Elections might put that claim to the test. The elections, which took place across 294 constituencies in eight phases from 27th March to 29th April, has been one of the most aggressively-contested state elections in our country’s recent history, and possibly one with the most far-reaching consequences. The main political parties involved are the Trinamool Congress (TMC), which has been the ruling party in Bengal for the last ten years, the Left Front, which governed Bengal for thirty-four years before the TMC came into power, and the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP), which currently controls the Central Government of India. In the parties’ ongoing contest to govern West Bengal, the welfare and interests of the citizens have been forgone in favour of gaining power and control over the state. A case in point would be the several rallies packed with thousands of supporters that have been held by each of the parties in the middle of a pandemic that is only becoming deadlier by the day.

The Indian Constitution guarantees the universal adult franchise as a fundamental right to every Indian citizen above the age of 18. Yet, five people were killed at a polling booth in Sitalkuchi on 10th April, 2021. Reports of repeated bombings in front of people’s houses in villages, death threats to voters and confiscation of Voter ID cards are all examples of how political parties are trying to discourage civilians from voting by inducing violence and fear in an attempt to prevent the balance from tipping in favour of the opposition.

The sanctity of the electoral process and the freedom to exercise one’s right to vote are not the only aspects that have been compromised in this election. Even the style of campaigning has reached a new low. Political campaigning now involves using divisive policies to polarize the electorate and disrupt the communal harmony in the state. In fact, this election marks one of the rare instances in West Bengal’s history where communal issues have been the focus of a state election. Attempts to appease certain communities, encourage religious fanaticism, and make blatantly sexist remarks at rallies full of thousands of people, are all new to the ethos of the Bengal elections. The political parties have resorted to catcalling and blatant ridicule of their opposition in an attempt to promote their party, with ministers making a mockery of one another in their political speeches, effectively premising the entire election on slander and insult.

Election campaigns are supposed to be based on election manifestos where the contesting parties outline their plans for the state and promises to bring benefits to the people if they are elected to power. The political leaders are expected to explain this agenda to the voters in order to secure votes in their favour. However, in the current scenario, concrete agendas and plans for the people’s welfare have been replaced by empty promises that hardly sound convincing. Instead, parties have focused on spending their resources on buying the support of candidates and voters.

The West Bengal elections hold immense national and historical significance for the BJP right now. If the BJP, a relatively new contender in Bengal, succeeds in defeating the TMC, it would not only bring another state under their control but also make them seem invincible, crushing the spirits of all opposition-run states in India. On the other hand, if the TMC were to successfully resist the BJP and win this election, it would lift the morale of other opposition ruled state governments, which is important because democracies should be premised on strong opposition parties, different state governments and different opinions.

The 2021 Bengal election is like no other because this election will determine what will happen to the state of West Bengal over the course of the next five years. Since BJP and TMC’s policies and goals for the state are fundamentally opposed to one another, it has led to extreme polarisation between the supporters of these parties in Bengal. In Mamata Banerjee’s opinion, these elections are more important than any in Bengal’s history as they are a fight for the state’s ‘somman’ (respect), for Bengal’s pride, history and heritage.
