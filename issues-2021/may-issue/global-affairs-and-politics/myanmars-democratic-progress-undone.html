 Myanmar’s Democratic Progress Undone


Achintya Newatia

May 1, 2021   |   Global Affairs & Politics


Most people should have heard about the absolutely terrible situation in Myanmar, where the military forces, known as the “Tatmadaw”, led by Senior General Min Aung Hlaing, staged a coup d’état on February 1, 2021, against the government led by Aung San Suu Kyi, seizing control of the country and proclaiming a state of emergency for the next year. Since the uprising, the people belonging to all sections of society, be it doctors, teachers, monks, social activists, etc. have risen up in protests against the forceful seize of the country by the military, but the military have fiercely and violently suppressed the public protests that demand the release of their leader Aung San Suu Kyi, who has been held in detention by the Tatmadaw since the beginning of the coup. Not only have the military taken control over the country but have also arrested several political leaders and have levelled charges such as import of illegal communications equipment against Aung San Suu Kyi. But before we delve into the implications of the coup, let us get some history out of the way to give you a better understanding of the situation.

Myanmar, (then Burma), gained independence from British rule in 1948 to become a democratic republic. But in 1962, the political regime changed as the military forces staged a coup to replace the democratic system as they scrapped the Constitution and established a ‘junta’ i.e a military government, which is practically a dictatorship. The next 50 years, which I would personally call Myanmar’s dark phase, saw the military close the country to the international community and crack down hard on regional rebellions. It also soon became clear that the military had a vendetta against the ethnic minorities, which were gravely oppressed and deprived of basic human rights. However, during the late 1980s and 1990s, there was a meteoric rise in popularity of Aung San Suu Kyi and her party, the National League for Democracy (NLD), which aimed to liberate the people and bring about reforms. She rose to prominence among the 1988 public protests against the junta caused by problems like corruption, faulty economic policies and food shortages. As one might have expected, the military violently suppressed the protests, placed Aung San Suu Kyi in detention and continued to hold political and economic power over the country.



Then came in 2010, what is known as “Myanmar’s Experiment with Democracy”. Under the pressure of numerous sanctions by the international community and international condemnation for their violent killing of Buddhist monks during the 2007 Saffron Revolution, the military decided to hold multi party, free and fair democratic elections in 2010. Also, the military’s refusal of foreign humanitarian aid during the cyclone Nargis 2008 and other humanitarian issues like violent killing of ethnic minorities forced the international community to push for change.

However, the aim to elect a true people’s government failed as the 2010 election was characterized by military interference as the military enacted election laws which disqualified Aung San Suu Kyi from running for President and there was proof of polling station irregularities, with the UN and many other nations dismissing the election results as fraudulent. The military-backed USDP (Union Solidarity and Development Party) was proclaimed the election winner although the NLD had boycotted the election. Thus, this election could arguably be considered a ploy by the military to legitimize its power under the new Constitution they brought into force in 2008. In the 2015 elections, however, Aung San Suu Kyi’s party won in a landslide and she assumed the position of State Counsellor and a role as the leader of the people. The subsequent years until 2020 saw some change and progress under her government although her powers were fairly limited considering the provisions in the 2008 Constitution which gave the military some exclusive political powers

The reason given by the Myanmar military is largely a faulty claim that the 2020 Myanmar elections, won by Aung San Suu Kyi’s NLD, were rigged and they had uncovered signs of “terrible fraud”, although no substantial proof was presented backing their claim. Yeah, this reminds me of Donald Trump as well. The Myanmar military said that it was acting in the interests of the people of Myanmar and would hold free and fair elections post the 1-year emergency period. Ironic, isn’t it? The military is promoting people’s interests by killing hundreds of innocents peacefully protesting.

For me, this coup, as many reports have stated, might be a desperate attempt by the military to hold onto their political and economic power over the country as Aung San Suu Kyi’s popularity continued increasing with her pushing forward constitutional reforms and social reform plans which would have benefitted ethnic minorities. Another theory, though it might seem a bit far-fetched, is that General Min Aung Hlaing acted in his own personal interests with the aim to salvage his political career and maintain his power. However, the internal deliberations and discussions of the military have been highly secretive and thus, whatever might be the military’s real reasons behind the façade of “holding free fair elections”, isn’t yet known. The reason may also be, as what was stated by an Independent analyst on Myanmar, Larry Jagan, “The game plan appears to be silencing Aung San Suuu Kyi and preventing her from having any political future.” Also, to defend the decision to stage a coup, the military-owned Myawaddy TV station cited an apparent provision in the 2008 Constitution which allows it to declare a national emergency.

In most nations, the military is but an armed force serving the country and acting on the commands of the government, but there is a starkly contrasting scenario in this regard in Myanmar. In essence, the military in Myanmar has taken the form of a dominant political party acting at the behest of an authoritative controlling figure. As per the 2008 Constitutional provisions, the military have been accorded 25% parliamentary seats irrespective of the election results and they hold important portfolios in the Cabinet such as defense and immigration. The military have used their parliamentary seats to their great advantage, as they have been successful in blocking many significant reforms, both constitutional and social. The military also has two well-established conglomerates and therefore their influence extends to the economy as well. Thus, the military’s extensive powers have allowed them to act only in their individual self-interests and retain control of the country. The only thing to ponder over is whether it is justifiable that the military itself functions as a ruling political party in a country, especially when they have committed numerous human right violations and the general public themselves do not consent to their governance. Yes, there have been instances of junta governments being established in the past but they usually emerged only for a few years during a period of crisis for the country and did not rule for decades.

The military have made moves to violently suppress the pro-democracy peaceful protests with civilian casualties exceeding 600 and thousands being injured. They have also banned public gatherings, periodically suspended internet, social media and telephone services, and have also banned all domestic and international flights. Unfortunately, the military attacks only keep escalating on the resilient public, who still protest in large numbers against the coup. However, now, the front liners in the protests, as stated in the New York Times, have transformed into a “guerilla force” with them trying to defend the people against the military crackdowns and protesting against them with a basic form of warfare by attempting to sabotage the military facilities. The few pro-democracy politicians, that haven’t still been arrested or detained and the human rights activists are rallying the people against the military and urging them to raise their voices against the state of lawlessness that the military seems to be representing right now.

The United Nations, the United States, the European Union and various nations, within weeks of the coup, condemned the military’s actions and demanded that they release Aung San Suu Kyi immediately and honor the results of 2020 election. They have also pressed hard on the agenda of human rights, considering that the military has often displayed a kind of “genocidal nature” against ethnic minority groups like the Rohingya Muslims, the Shan and the Kokang. A formal United Nations Joint Statement condemning the military’s actions and threatening further actions that are within the ambit of the UN Charter was vetoed by China and Russia, who have friendly ties with Myanmar and consider them an important strategic partner in Asia. China and Russia vetoed the resolution on the grounds that its tone would rather worsen the situation than do any good.

However, a Presidential Statement has been adopted by all the 15 nations of the Security Council, although it has been drafted in a milder language and does not condemn or threaten the military perse. The Presidential Statement is a step below a formal resolution but it still holds significance as it does become a part of the official records of UNSC. The Presidential Statement, although weaker than the initial proposed move, called for the release of State Counsellor Aung San Suu Kyi and President Win Myint, stated that it supports Myanmar’s democratic transition and stressed on the need to uphold democratic institutions, refrain from violence and fully respect human rights. Any other action in this stage by any country or the UN would hold immense international implications as any action even subtly threatening the military would be harshly met by countries like China and Russia. But, at the end of the day, something must be done and I am backing the United States, the UK and the European Union to push forward on their stances and hopefully improve the situation in Myanmar.
