 The Belarusian Protests


Ananya Singh

May 1, 2021   |   Social Change


Adding on to yet another unsurprising edition of blatant police brutality being pursued by governments, we have the recent Belarus protests. On 9th August 2020, Belarus was set to have its presidential elections with two key candidates- Aliaksandr Lukashenko or as he is fondly known, Europe's last dictator, who has been reigning for over 26 years and Svetlana Tikhanovskaya. Lukashenko has won every presidential election since 1994, with all but the first being labelled by international monitors as neither free nor fair. Svetlana Tikhanovskaya was a stark contrast to Lukashenko. A stay-at-home mother, she felt compelled to challenge Lukashenko's rule when the established opposition figures, including her husband Sergei Tikhanovsky, a blogger, were jailed or forced into exile ahead of the vote. She called for change after years of economic stagnation and repressive rule and gained popular support. However, Lukashenko was declared the winner in the election and credited with 80% of the vote.



This unleashed an unprecedented wave of peaceful protests in Belarus against the electoral results that were marred by widespread fraud. To stifle the protests, the Belarusian authorities engaged in a widespread, brutal crackdown on the peaceful protesters, detaining thousands of people and subjecting hundreds to torture. Security officials and police violently dispersed the protests using excessive force and rubber bullets, stun grenades, tear gas etc. By mid-November, police had detained more than 25,000 people holding them in degrading conditions. It is alleged that the police held detained the protesters for days in overcrowded cells, often denying them food, water, medical assistance, and access to the toilet. Former detainees have reported beatings, prolonged stress positions, electric shocks, and in at least one case, rape. The Belarusian Association of Journalists documented 336 detentions and 60 incidents of violence against journalists in the months after the presidential vote. The authorities opened hundreds of politically motivated criminal cases against political opposition figures, protesters, and their supporters, civic activists, and human rights defenders. At least 121 are behind bars awaiting trial, according to Belarusian human rights group Viasna. Despite this intimidation, tens of thousands continued to demonstrate peacefully for fair elections and justice for the abuses. For now, Lukashenko has prevailed, relying on the support of Russian President Vladimir Putin.


More than six months of protests have left Belarus’ economy on its knees, and even a $1.5 billion bailout from Russia last September has not succeeded in stabilizing the country. The European Union has not recognised the election outcome, calling it unfree and unfair, and imposed sanctions on Belarusian officials involved in the post-election crackdown, as have the United States and the United Kingdom. They condemned the violence, detentions, and internet shutdowns. In November, an expert report based on an investigation by the Organization for Security and Co-operation (OSCE) in Europe found “massive and systemic” human rights violations and called on Belarus to hold new elections. In September, the United Nations Human Rights Council (UNHRC) got involved as well.

In a show of outrageous high handedness, Tikhonovskaya was forced out of the country by the government. However, if Lukashenko believed that banishing Tikhanovskaya would end her threat to his rule, he was wrong. Tikhanovskaya has been negotiating with international leaders from exile. One key strategy, forged in regular talks with U.S. and E.U. officials, is to push for far tougher economic sanctions on Lukashenko’s government and international recognition for Tikhanovskaya to lay claim to the governance. The task of knitting together a viable political opposition from outside the country is quite daunting but Svetlana Tikhanovskaya is not one to be discouraged easily. She is calling on Belarusians to take to the streets this week and revive the mass protests that swept the Eastern European country last fall. It seems only a matter of a short time before the last dictator of Europe has to kneel before the real authority- the people.
