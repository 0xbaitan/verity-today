 Ugly, Ugly Moneyball: A Financial Perspective on the European Super League


Karnav Popat

May 1, 2021   |   Sports




The European Super League is a travesty. It really is. To lean into a reddit stereotype, its the Americanization of a profoundly unamerican sport. What the ESL proposes is not just a break away from the long-standing region-based structure of football, but also a model that is fundamentally incompatible with a sport rooted in the joy and tears of the working-class lifelong fan.

The premise of the ESL is this: most of the value of region-based leagues come from a few clubs (the Manchester Uniteds and the Real Madrids). As a consequence, these clubs have decided that if they’re creating most of the revenue, they’re entitled to most of the revenue. So far, so good.



The only problem is the way this revenue is actually created. Revenue comes from fans, and fans flock to football for the emotion, not the commercialization. Football is completely unlike, say, tennis or golf, which are fundamentally sports of beauty and grace, sports which are popular for cultural reasons. Football is an emotion sport. Yes, there’ll always be the Twitter accounts praising Leo Messi for that incredible bit of skill and yes, most people value aesthetic as much as results, but the average football fan does not tune in every weekend because the players are just so smooth. There’s a reason literally half the world follows football, and that’s because it appeals to something every primal, very unevolved in us.

From hooliganism to pub culture, this primalism is evident in every single aspect of The Beautiful Game. It’s in the screams of delight when the ball thwacks against the net, the flamboyant celebrations, the violent confrontations and boiled-over tensions that mark the very best matches. And most importantly, it’s in that feeling you get when your club wins. It’s the reason fans say “we” instead of “they”. Football is part of its fans’ identities. Whether it’s in the devotion that fans show for decades, or the humble beginnings that characterize so many of the best players, football is nothing if not the connection it has with its fans.

And football is dying.



Gunnersaurus was sacked. Wimbledon turned into a franchise. The humble beginnings are mostly forgotten, reserved for an occasional mention at an award ceremony. Over the past decade, football has been dominated by a tiny number of clubs: Manchester City and United, Barcelona and Real Madrid, Paris St Germain, Bayern Munich, Juventus. Half are owned by billionaires, and the other half spend as if they are, too. It’s even worse at the not-quite-dominating-but-good-enough-to-try level. Kroenke Sports Enterprises. Suning Holdings Group. Red Bull GmbH. These are hardly names that evoke what you’d call the spirit of football. The cynic might say that football is already dead, bankrupt morally if not financially.

And what’s even worse is the fact that the ESL is stupid. It doesn’t even make logical sense. It’s one thing to be an evil, money-grubbing villain, it’s quite another to be genuinely bad at your job. In the long-term, it’s going to wreck every club in the ESL, wreck football as a sport (or atleast curb it from achieving its potential as a truly global phenomenon), and probably stunt a generation’s worth of players along the way. The only ones to benefit will be the billionaire owners… and something tells me that’s not unintentional.

You see, the ESL’s premise is awfully flawed. Its defenders have tried to use trickle-down economics (of all things) to justify it, the idea being that since the ESL will give 15 clubs loads and loads of money, and they’ll have to spend it buying players from the smaller clubs for loads and loads of money, the smaller clubs will also benefit.

This is stupid. This is like saying we should donate all our money to Jeff Bezos, because he’ll have to spend it one day, so we’ll obviously get rich when he buys things from us.

What will actually happen is what’s been happening at Old Trafford for the past decade. The owners (and they are such a bunch, got everything from genocide to financial fraud on their resumes) will drain the broadcasting money from the club’s pockets. With no impetus to achieve, since they’ll be guaranteed a spot in the moneybags league anyway, they’ll completely ignore the need to invest in their squads, ultimately hurting the quality of the game.

But that’s just from the perspective of the big clubs. It’s much, much worse from the perspective of the small clubs. You see, no matter what the neo-Reaganists tell you, trickle-down economics doesn’t work. In the short-term, clubs excluded from the ESL will be immediately up against it, especially when their domestic leagues start getting devalued in favour of the mega-matches. But even if they manage to survive the lean years soon to come, over the long-term, their extinction is practically guaranteed.

Because even if most of the ESL money goes on to line billionaire pockets, the sheer magnitude of these clubs’ windfalls means that their infrastructure will soon balloon far, far beyond the capabilities of any other club. With infrastructure comes training facilities, globe-spanning scouting teams, lots and lots of visits to Dr. Cugat. It’s the defining feature of capitalism: the more money you have, the more money you can make.

That right there wrecks any possibility that an ESL-having model of football could possibly be sustainable in the long-term. The City Football Group already has a network of clubs across the world which they can use to snap up footballing talent. With the ESL, such strategies will magnify tenfold, allowing these 15 clubs to grab all the talent right from the parks and the academies across the world, eliminating the “feeder-clubs” altogether and wrecking the extremely fragile ecosystem of football, the lower tiers of which depend heavily on producing and selling quality footballers to pay their expenses.

But the most severe problem of all, the one which will inevitably affect even the ESL clubs, is a problem of appeal. The magic of football, the romance of it, comes from anticipation. Football is defined by its derbies and its rivalries because it hits just the right note of rarity. It’s the same reason the World Cup is such a big deal: it doesn’t roll around every three months, you have to wait for it. And this isn’t just something people say: If you want proof, ask the fans of Barcelona and Real Madrid who were around for that famous four-Clasico month. On paper, that stretch should have been among the greatest times in history to be a football fan: The two best teams, featuring the two best players under the two best managers, were playing four times in three weeks.

But it wasn’t. There was nothing special about those matches, because there was no anticipation, how could there be? Even football’s unremembering fans remember that the period was more exhausting than exciting.

If the ESL gives us twenty “big” matches a year, one after the other, it will not (despite the opinions of dear Andrea and Flo) make the fans happy. It will make the fans disinterested. And in the grander scheme of things, that is suicide. The justification for the ESL seems to be that the younger generation wants it, and that Asia and America are ripe for a megafranchise. They are wrong. As a member of the exact demographic they are targeting, I assure you that they are incredibly wrong. Football’s romance, the essence of its appeal, is in the big matches. And when every match is a big match, none of them are. They will kill the entire brand value of football.

But enough theorizing. It’s time for a look at the underlying numbers, and what better example to use but Manchester United, the Biggest Club In The World?

Mind, however, that since this will be happening to every club in the league, the effects will not so much be evident through league position, but in the market. A natural consequence of there being one high-profile league with extreme amounts of money to throw around should be that all the top players are attracted to it (ala the EPL in the 2000s and 2010s). Unless UEFA and FIFA go ahead with their threats to ban players from the World Cup, this will probably happen.

Unfortunately for every other club in the entire world, this creates a closed economy. If the ESL clubs can produce the majority of the talented players (because they’ll have the money to outbid every other academy and scouting institution), and they’re also the biggest market for talented players, what we’re looking at is a league-wide version of Chelsea’s loan system, where all the talent is hoarded by a small group of clubs which deprive smaller clubs of vital revenue while also stagnating the development of most of its youth products for the sake of conserving value. Nobody wins, except the billionaires.

But back to the finances. Per present reports, it seems that US banking firm JP Morgan will provide initial financing for the ESL in the form of 6 billion dollars worth of grants to the clubs involved. 3.5 billion of this is essentially a bribe, given as “infrastructural” and COVID-recovery grants. The prize money and other related add-ons come to almost triple the current UCL, all of which is to say that these clubs will be swimming in money. Surely if the top institutions have the money to spend on football, football will improve?

Of course not. It’s important to remember that the ESL is a closed league. Manchester United’s poor showing in the Prem under the Glazers has already told us that owners value the steady stream of broadcasting and bribe money far, far higher than the meagre additions of performance-related grants. It seems that Manchester United’s long-standing plan of being just good enough to bank on the UCL without having to spend enough to perform well in it, will be the dominating strategy.

Inevitably, therefore, football will stagnate. Instead of driving player compensation higher and higher as is happening in the present bubble, player compensation will remain at the same level while all excess revenue is drained off into the owner’s accounts. A few owners with non-traditional goals (sportswashing) will probably end up serial winners — not because they’re better, but because the other clubs won’t want to spend the money to compete.

Additionally, this ESL deal? It’s for 23 years. 23 years, right off the bat. That number makes no sense from a sporting sense — every sporting competition, including the UCL, typically changes format practically every decade. But it does make sense financially. These owners see their clubs as long-term investments, not football clubs. 23 years is the amount of time they want to siphon off all the club’s money — Peter Lim style — and then retire to the Bahamas (while the rest of us drown from all the climate change, probably).

The big comparison that the ESL owners have seemingly taken inspiration from in doing this is the American leagues (of course). And while it’s true that the NFL and NBA are plenty succesful, it is for different reasons entirely. American sports, remember, are cultural sports. More than that, they are productions. Remember that episode of How I Met Your Mother which featured the Superbowl?



“And here’s the funny part, as unforgettable as that Super Bowl was, here it is 23 years later and I don’t remember who won… Hell, I don’t even remember who played. What I do remember is that we drank beer, we ate wings and we watched the Super Bowl together.”

That’s what American football is about. But the most memorable moments of (real) football revolve around, you guessed it, football. No Liverpool fan would forget about Istanbul, not even 23 years later. Hell, England fans still get teary-eyed about England 4–2 Germany, and that was 1966. Football is a sport about emotion, about the memorable moments, not about the ads and the half-time shows and the hot dogs. To say otherwise is a profound misunderstanding of everything that makes the sport the world’s most popular pastime.

All the Super League will do will be to get rid of that unique charm and replace it with generic American pre-packaged entertainment. And why not, you might ask? After all, it works. American football is incredibly profitable.

Yes it is. But it is not the world’s most popular sport, not by a long distance. And perhaps most importantly for these people, it has nowhere near the kind of reach football has achieved, precisely because it lacks that special emotional appeal. It is completely rational to expect that by the end of the ESL’s 23 year term, football will have shrunk, not grown, financially. People will still buy their tickets and they’ll still wear the shirts, but not enough of them, not nearly enough to justify this incredible 6 billion dollar deal. In their haste to exploit football’s finances in the short-term, all these money-grubbers will do is sabotage it over the long term, leaving it a husk of itself.

And that’s the crux of it, isn’t it? The Glazers don’t care about popularity or quality or integrity. They care about money, and they want it within their lifetimes. Notice the disconnect between this:



and this:



This is case of differing priorities, and it is one that will catch “legacy” football fans in the crossfire.

In Machester United’s case, here’s what I expect to happen (and stop me if this is even a little bit out of character for the Glazers). They’ll join the ESL for the two-decade carnival, and in their first year they’ll finish upper-mid-table, probably make maybe half a billion Euros. Great returns. They’ll grab a nice and flashy player on a Neymar-level deal, someone exciting, someone to keep the kids tuned in. Maybe Sancho, or Haaland. But they won’t buy a decent right-back, because why would they? Nobody buys tickets because of Ashley Young.

In the next year, they’ll do about as well, maybe make the playoffs, grab another half billion Euros. Now it’s dividend time though, time to gather up the financial sheets and figure out how much the profit was for last year. The Glazers will probably pocket 300 million. Loans and interest, what can you do? The team needs a goalkeeper but none of the options have resale value so it’s not worth it, just chuck Henderson in.

The next year will be a little disappointing, maybe they’ll only finish mid-table. Time to sack the manager, get a statement signing. Ooh, that Musiala guy’s selling a lot of jerseys, isn’t he? Dividend’s a little higher this year, 400 million. The fans are muttering about “trophies”? Whatever, trophies don’t win you sponsorship deals. Next year the goal is to cut costs while still staying in the half-billion range.

The next year.. you get the point. That is not football. That is a business. If you drain the excitement of the CL, and you kill all the underdogs, and you stop any risk of promotion or relegation, why oh why would anyone possible pay to watch you? This is nothing but a last gasp attempt by failing clubs to protect their fat cheques.

Here’s something for the Glazers to chew on. This is an infographic showing the world’s most valuable clubs for 2019:



And this is an infographic showing the world’s most valuable clubs for 2020:



Notice those red arrows next to United? That means bad. That means that this model of using clubs as cash cows is unsustainable, and it leads to stagnation of the sport, and it is everything that a club should not be doing. Instead, they’re doing everything they can to get the financial muscle to keep doing it. This is bullshit.

In the end, of course, barring a rare moment of competence from Boris Johnson or from UEFA, it is going to happen. Manchester United, just like the rest of the stodgy old guard, will laugh all the way to the bank while the “legacy” supporters are left with nothing but bittersweet memories. Oh, well. Atleast we’ll be able to enjoy Uber Eats Presents the European Super League: Shanghai Tour, Sponsored by the CCP, Partnered with Nestle and Amazon, Special Sponsor Adolf Hitler! /s
