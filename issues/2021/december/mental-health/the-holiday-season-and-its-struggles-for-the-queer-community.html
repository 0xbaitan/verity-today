The Holiday Season and Its Struggles for the Queer Community

    • Mental Health   31/12/2021   Ash Mir

    Being loved for who one is plays a big role in one’s wellbeing, and queer individuals are no exception. For many of them, love from their biological families comes with an asterisk— a list of terms and conditions— that forces them to leave an intrinsic part of their identity at the door when visiting home for the holidays. In addition to feeling battered by the emergence of a new variant, the pressure of facing invasive questions, snide remarks, and general ignorance can be a lot to deal with.

    The holiday season should be a time for celebration, joy, and love, but the queer community is often forced to suppress themselves and align in accordance with the status quo, leaving their minds clouded with dejection and sorrow. Many might still choose to spend time with their loved ones or may not have the option to not visit their home. While every person’s relationship with themselves and their family is different, it is helpful to keep certain things in mind when engaging in conversations at home.

    Coming out during this time can be a bad idea; you never know how your family might react, and if you receive a negative response to your identity, you will not have a person or a place to escape to for comfort and reassurance. It might be best to emotionally prepare yourself to remain in the closet a little while longer. If you are already out, remember to set boundaries. You are not obligated to make everyone around you feel comfortable; you can politely, but firmly, request avoiding certain topics if you think it would be better for your emotional health. If tensions arise and arguments spiral out of control, separate yourself from the situation and seek comfort in an activity you like - whether that be a walk in the park or a call with your best friend. Going home and facing your family takes a lot of courage. Putting aside prior rifts and misunderstandings can feel compromising and humiliating, but you should be proud of yourself for opening up. More than being ‘tolerated’ or ‘accepted’, you deserve to be loved and celebrated.

    For those who don’t return home, redefining family traditions can be a good way to kickstart the holidays. If you feel uncomfortable taking part in religious celebrations, find something meaningful to you and celebrate that instead. Sharing these experiences with your found family and creating your own holiday rituals may help ease the disconnect you feel during this season. It is also important to remain hopeful during this time. With adequate space, extensive conversations, and mutual understanding, relationships can still be healed or recreated. If they can’t be mended, that’s alright too, because you’ll always have your community— a family who loves and cares for you— supporting you through your hardships.

    After being isolated during a pandemic for months on end, celebrating with your ‘quarantine pod’ and connecting with your newfound friends virtually can be a healthy reminder of all the relationships you have built over the past year. Spending time with yourself can be cathartic as well. Take out a few hours out of your schedule to rekindle old hobbies or learn something new altogether. At the end of the day, you should reclaim holiday activities and make them your own; do whatever makes you happy and comfortable.

    Getting through the holiday season is definitely not easy, and redefining the holiday spirit isn’t for everyone. Having the freedom to celebrate this month the way you want is a privilege in itself. If this period of a few weeks is something you wish to completely block out, do so. Prioritise yourself and take care of your needs. Offer to yourself the gift of kindness and compassion. For those returning home, stay safe. Finding yourself has been a long journey - don’t let that go. Living as yourself is also an act of bravery and resistance. Your visibility is vital, and it is your right. Have fun during this holiday season and spread love within your community. Happy holidays!
