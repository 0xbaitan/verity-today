Why Get A Tattoo

    • Mental Health   31/12/2021   Geetanjali Roy

    Standing at the foot of the staircase leading up to a tattoo parlour, I still remember exactly how I felt when deliberating whether or not to take the plunge and get my first tattoo. Anxious. Liberated. Both amalgamating into a pit of churning excitement in my stomach, giving me the impetus to climb the steps, push open the glass door and tell the artist, “I’m here for a tattoo.”

    It was a charged decision, on many levels. I was permanently modifying my body, running the perpetual risk of one day growing out of the imagery I chose to put on it at eighteen. But on a more personal note, I was redirecting a dangerous impulse to self-harm, an ongoing battle to which my scarred left forearm bears graphic testament. It felt— in that moment— like the reclamation of a sense of agency I had lost long ago to an addictive escapist desire.

    A connective link between body modification and mental health, specifically the manifestation of mental illness as self-destructive urges, has been documented in both academia and social platforms. Academia largely portrays this connection quite negatively— claiming tattoos, coloured hair and piercings are emblematic of self-destructive tendencies and probably concurrent with drug addiction, alcoholism or gang membership. On the other hand, social platforms, which are a documentation of the current generational moment, reflect a vastly different ground reality.

    Project Semicolon (Project;) is a USAmerican grassroots mental health organisation providing information and support to encourage recovery and advocate for mental health equity and de-stigmatisation. The semicolon’s symbolic significance is captured in their tagline, ‘Your story isn’t over.’ From its inception in 2013, Project Semicolon has gained global acclaim for the work it does and its deeply resonant imagery which has made its way onto the bodies of many individuals struggling with mental health issues or striving to support loved ones going through them. #projectsemicolon on Instagram has 95,493 posts as of writing this, a majority of them pictures of tattoos of stylised semicolons. Over nine years, it has gone beyond a single organisation or nation to become a global movement of solidarity and community-building.

    Body modification is mostly a relatively harmless— sometimes permanent, sometimes temporary— coping mechanism simultaneously serving as a form of authentic self-expression. Radical change in appearance can redirect self-destructive urges, or fulfil a need to reinvent oneself after struggling with mental health issues or trauma, or project a more self-affirming image. Certain neuro-divergences and mental illnesses like depression, anxiety, ADHD, PTSD cause a sense of emotional impermanence surrounding self-confidence, love, identity, community, etc. Tattoos and piercings often serve as permanent reminders of the same, becoming a source of self-validation leading to emotional independence.

    The body is often the receptacle of physical and emotional violence we enact against ourselves to lash out at internal or external battles. This violence is born out of deep emotional volatility and instability, to the point where our agency over the enactment of it is often lost. Self-harm is considered an addiction in the same vein as drugs and alcoholism. In my case, and in the case of many others, body modification that we choose, and give personal weight and meaning to, is a way to reclaim control over how we experience our body moving forward. It is a way to acknowledge the wars we have had to fight, give them their due, and move forward on the winding path to recovery.
