The Farm Laws Repeal

    • The Affairs   31/11/2021   Anikait Chakraborty

    On November 19th, 2021, Prime Minister Modi announced the repeal of the three disputable farm laws in a televised address to the country, as a response to the large scale uprisings it had sparked by bringing thousands of revolting farmers from the adjoining areas of Punjab and Haryana onto the outskirts of the national capital, since last year. The Farmers (Empowerment and Protection) Agreement on Price Assurance and Farm Services Act, 2020, the Farmers' Produce Trade and Commerce (Promotion and Facilitation) Act, 2020, and the Essential Commodities (Amendment) Act, 2020 were all repealed by the Farm Laws Repeal Bill, which was introduced in the Lok Sabha on November 29, 2021. Forging a stable structure for contract farming (agriculture carried out in terms of an agreement between the farmer and the buyer), imposing stock limits under unprecedented situations and enabling barrier-free trade of agricultural produce in and out of various states, were some of its salient purposes. PM Modi had previously stated that the farm reforms will be essential in bringing about a revolutionary change in the agriculture sector by empowering tens of millions of farmers. However, things did not come out exactly as intended. The reforms had been objected to since farmers throughout the country feared that the creation of private markets (mandis) and state-run Agriculture Produce Market Committees (APMC) would force all agriculture enterprises to private markets, resulting in a paradigm shift away from government markets. The inherent belief being that the laws were pro- big business. Some grew intensely apprehensive that their subsidies would be discontinued, given that the Bill claims that electricity subsidies would be handed to farmers in cash, which according to the farmers is practically impossible given that the majority of small and marginal farms cannot pay their power bills first and then apply for subsidies. Congress Leader Rahul Gandhi seized the opportunity to claim that Prime Minister Modi accepted that he has made a mistake on the legislation of the farm laws given that he apologised to the farmers and the nation at large. To this, he also added that it is imperative for the families of the estimated 700 farmers (who succumbed to the year long protest owing to extreme conditions of heat and cold, and not to forget, the pandemic) to be compensated.

    In practical terms, the repeal exposes the discord of the BJP’s actions against the interests of the rural agricultural communities, considering that this is the second rollback of the NDA( the first being the Land Acquisition Reforms). It is evident that the farm reforms were overturned with the motive of the upcoming assembly elections in Punjab and Uttar Pradesh in mind. The fact that the BJP has eventually backtracked demonstrates that it is cautious of being seen as pro-big business. Moreover, the fact that it took them a year to realise the incompatibility of their ideologies with the needs and interests of the farming communities indicates a sluggish response mechanism within the party.
