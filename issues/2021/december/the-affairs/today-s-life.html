Today’s life

    • The Affairs   31/11/2021   Chhavi

    Life can be compared to a long game of temple run in which man is a fearless treasure hunter attempting to escape with gold , and life is an infinite run with obstacles and traps encountered along the route, including traversing cliffs, forests, and even mines. Not to mention the most thrilling aspect of the game: the evil monkey monsters hunting you, which for us can be long working hours, but they are no longer simply a matter of adult life; they now begin while a child is in his/her teenage years. Many times in this competitive society, the word "sukoon" is absent, causing sadness even in young children.

    After being lost for hours, selecting depression is the most accurate choice from the infinite pool of mental health problems. Depression has been defined in various ways throughout history, and many people still have no idea what it means. Many researchers have attempted to understand depression over the centuries, but based on their findings, we can conclude that depression evolves over time, much like covid, and that its effectiveness varies from person to person. The problem with measuring depression is that researchers use questionnaires, and we can't expect a depressed person to provide accurate information about their condition, so there's still a disconnect between statistics and reality. concluding depression as complex is the best way to explain it. For example, according to statistics, 41 percent of people in their depression stage never felt low energy while 51 percent did. This is the same as sleep problems, loss of interest, appetite change, depressed mood, difficulty concentrating, low self-esteem, psychomotor agitation, and suicidal ideation. As a result, we currently lack a parameter to measure depression. On the other hand, most people in this century are forced to work long hours in order to maintain or improve their living standards. According to statistics, as a country's GDP rises, the number of hours worked decreases, implying that growth and working hours are negatively correlated. In the category of long working hours and low GDP, we have Cambodia, Myanmar, China, Bangladesh, and Mexico, all of which have more than 2200 annual working hours per person. We can also say that, thanks to technological innovation, people are able to work less in more productive countries.

    We can say that long work hours can cause depression, but we can also say that working less hours can cause depression. People who work more than 80 hours are more likely to develop a high risk of depression, according to some research, but what about those who work less than 80 hours? Are they not depressed? Psychologically, if a person only works 15 hours per week, will this provide him/her with all of the happiness? Perhaps, but on the other hand, if a person works fewer hours, he will be concerned about how he will pay his bills, maintain his living standards, and save. According to historical data, countries with higher GDP are more depressed, and GDP has a negative relationship with working hours. As a result, we cannot conclude that long working hours cause depression. And, as previously stated, depression is a complex phenomenon for which we have no way of knowing who is affected. People of all income levels, male or female, developed or undeveloped, are all same on the path to depression.
