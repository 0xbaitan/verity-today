Music, Society, and a Legend-Adele

    • The Arts   31/12/2021   Ishani Datta

    Took a trip down memory lane, the other day. It took me about an hour to realise my ‘memory lane’ consisted purely of Adele and Taylor Swift. I’m not going to lie, I do listen to Taylor more, but when it comes to Adele, I save her songs for days when I feel as though I need to be heard and listened to. And when Adele pulled off a total power move, returning with her album, or more of an emotional spectrum of a masterpiece, ‘30’, let’s just say, it was time to descend into a rabbit hole of emotions.

    ‘Easy On Me’, her single from the album, where do I even start? The emotion she sings of is very specific, and it’s something we’ve all felt at some point. It’s an intrinsic cautiousness of being in a relationship, hoping and begging it would end well, or rather, not end. Adele’s voice delves deep into that fear and insecurity, and it’s easy to feel the pain she felt, and this ballad delivered in every aspect of the word. Another song that got me in the feels was ‘Strangers by Nature’, a rather short song, but not short in the emotional rollercoaster that Adele has so famously and beautifully mastered. She sings of seeing things differently, a little more, perhaps she sees the flaws and the cracks that she didn’t before. She feels a kind of isolation when says that no one knows what it’s like, and it’s a common feeling that all of us relate to. Safe to say, this song was enough for me to slump my shoulders, stare off into the distance, and hold a pillow for comfort. If you ever need to have a long cry, Adele slides into the chat.

    I’ve alway idolised the creativity of the passion that Adele channels in her music, and it sets her apart from the rest of the industry, an industry that already places women on pedestals to always be perfect, and never be ‘old’. Something that really sets me off is the insane body image standards society places on women in the entertainment industry, and the incessant trolling and body-shaming all women experience, for not having the ‘ideal’ body, and Adele was no stranger to such cruelty. After the release of her album ‘25’, Adele went off the radar. She was always ridiculed for being ‘heavier’ than her female peers. When she returned this past year, after losing 45kgs, she was completely glorified by social media, as everyone took to commenting on her new look. Society clearly reserves a special place for ‘overweight’ people, who go on to lose weight and have a ‘glow-up’, and that’s how Adele was treated as pictures of her new figure circulated the internet.

    Adele has opened up prior about unhealthy binging habits, preventing her from having a healthy, productive life. Once she lost weight, common words being circulated were ‘stunning’, ‘glowing’, ‘gorgeous’, and whatnot. We’re living in an age where weight loss is idolised, to the point where society changes their views of you, and treats you vastly different to how you used to be treated. It makes me sick that a person’s value is reduced to their physical structure, and nothing could be more dehumanising than that. I recall there being an article titled ‘She may have lost weight, but her voice is still the same.’, and believe me when I say there is nothing more disrespectful than reducing a person’s value to their body. What does an individual’s physical features have to do with their vocal abilities? There is also this romanticised notion when losing a lot of weight hurriedly, as if it makes someone more desirable, more attractive. It’s a disgusting notion, but it perpetuates deeply today.

    We, as a society, are failing the more we base a person’s value on their body, and it sends a horrible message to the younger generation, kids who are seeing and believing the idea that being ‘thin’ has greater value than being anything but. The first step is to stop glorifying an individual’s weight loss journey, because it means the glorification of fat-phobia, which is so widespread even today. Instead of glorifying an individual’s weight loss journey, we should glorify their qualities as a human being instead.
