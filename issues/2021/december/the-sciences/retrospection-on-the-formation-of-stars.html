Retrospection on the formation of stars

    • The Sciences   31/12/2021   Wrijurekh Mukherjee

    The universe is massive, its bigger than anything we can measure, made up of things smaller than anything we can measure. We can only perceive 5 percent of the universe; this five percent has been termed the observable universe. The rest of it cannot be observed using our current technology and thus remains unknown. But we can learn a lot about the universe as a whole just by observing the five percent or the observable universe. Stars, for example, give us a great detail of information about the early stages of the universe.

    Stars are some of the oldest objects that can be observed, they are essentially giant balls of flaming hot plasma. It mainly consists of hydrogen and helium and these gasses form the basis of the reactions that occur inside the suns core. The helium present inside the star is turned into helium which releases a tremendous amount of energy. These reactions happening inside the sun cause it to heat up to millions of degrees. however, once the sun runs out of helium it loses the delicate balance between its gravity pulling it inward and the reaction force pushing it outward, causing the star to collapse on itself. Then the star either keeps fusing elements until it reaches iron or it turns into a black hole. But this entire process takes billions of years and to fully understand this, scientists had to look back billions of years into the past, when the first stars began to form in the dawn of the universe. The universe was initially made up of giant, superheated gasses that formed when independent sub-atomic particles grouped together. These gasses were initially too hot to stabilize due to the big bang, but slowly cooled down. This cooling down caused the gasses to slowly gather together under the influence of gravity to form stars. The stars then died in a supernova explosion and made even more complicated elements with more sub-atomic particles, which in turn turned into stars and exploded again to form even more complicated elements. Stars then clumped together to form galaxies and formed the observable universe.

    The sun is also star, it formed in a similar way and will die out in a similar way. The sun forms the basis of life on earth and also teaches us a lot about its bigger counterparts. We can expect all stars to die out one day and the universe will become a dark, frigid place like it was before the big bang. The immense light and activity in the universe will be recorded as a phase in the history of the cosmic universe that lasted billions of years. But nobody will be here to witness the last star die out in a magnificent and colourful explosion.
