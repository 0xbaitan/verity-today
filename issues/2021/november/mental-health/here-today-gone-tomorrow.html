Here Today, Gone Tomorrow

    • Mental Health   31/11/2021   Anayaa Agarwal

    If you could erase your worst memories, would you? We all have experiences that we want to forget, the experiences that remind us of the worst times of our lives where we felt our lowest and every time we think about those memories, we get the sense of panic, sadness, hopelessness or simply a blank feeling.

    So, if you could erase them, would you regardless of the ethics of it?

    Some say the good does not outweigh the bad. That as happy as you get, some part of you will always remember that horrible time or that bad memory and with all the happiness in your life, somewhere or the other you remember that memory.

    Dealing with trauma is like strengthening a muscle. If you’ve done your bicep curls, the next time you have to lift a heavy box you can do it more easily.

    Our mind and body need time to rebuild and rejuvenate since A scientific process known as Loftus, similar to several other processes that can help erase memories mentions how the concept of erasing memory affects the brain. If the memory-dampening pill erases something then would that particular thing’s value be erased? Memories can’t completely be deleted or gone forever, instead, they get buried in a box and thrown into the deepest corners of our brain, the corners that our subconscious can’t reach and ultimately causing us to forget them. The bad instances that we remember can’t be “fixed” but they can be edited and changed to fix our own narrative over time. As we grow older, a part is added to them or removed from them subconsciously or consciously. or sometimes it’s because of other people’s recollection of them or just because we simply can’t remember that part. Memories are a dangerous thing; we want to have them to remember the same feeling years later but at the same time we don’t want them because of how they make us feel.

    They are like the tangled branches of an old lilac tree, they overlap, intertwine, connect and diverge and leave us with feelings we want or don’t.

    Is it possible that by deleting a particularly bad memory, one can find the “true” meaning of happiness?

    But if we erase our memories, our experiences are erased too. They make us who we are. We are built through our experiences which come from our memories, worst or the best. What does it mean to be “happy”

    By erasing a memory, is the “true” meaning of happiness and satisfaction with life guaranteed? While we won’t be thinking about that particular hard memory every time we are about to do something but what about the factor of the memory that makes us who we are, the factor that tells us to put our left foot forward first or right? Sometimes, letting a bad memory stay but replacing it with a happier memory and surrounding ourselves with things that make us happy can help.

    Our experiences, bad or good make us who we are. They affect the way we act. Each and every single one of our memories shapes us and form our mentality, our personality and in the end who we are as a person. So, erasing them would be erasing a part of us, as bad the memories are.

    In the end, we are the sum of our memories.
