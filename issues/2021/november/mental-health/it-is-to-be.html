It is to be

    • Mental Health   31/11/2021   Niharika Rajsaha

    “They were meant to be successful; it was written in the stars.” It most likely was not. It was probably written on exam sheets and a lot of those had triple-digit scores which was probably the result of years of perseverance to finally reach the pearly gates to success. In fact, there was probably no help whatsoever from cosmic forces that helped them achieve their goal. So much so that said cosmic forces were probably working against them, in the form of student loans, cynicism, anxiety and the historically ubiquitous self-doubt.

    Paulo Coehlo says that even when we are facing adversity, the universe conspires to help us reach our destiny- whatever that may be. As much as I bask in the genius in his folklore, a single thought persisted throughout my journey of reading The Alchemist. It is only towards the end that we realise that his destiny was to persevere and the reward was waiting for him. Destiny only exists in retrospect.

    Putting myself in his shoes, I could barely see myself letting go of my livelihood with a sure hand of success, let alone trust the words of a stranger I had met ten minutes ago just so that I could achieve a destiny I knew nothing of. Or even if it existed. What if Santiago had just chosen to stay at the crystal merchant’s shop a little longer so that he could make a little more money? He would have never reached the caravans. The war in the desert would have reduced the oasis to ruble. No other caravan would have traversed that land for months. He would never have met the Alchemist or reached the pyramid or found out that the treasure was back home all along and that his real destiny was learning all he had during the journey. It would have made for just another story about yet another person who never got to fulfil their destiny.

    The general cynicism and anxiety that courses through the veins of humanity will more times than not, keep us rooted in our comfort zones. Necessity would keep the best of people from risking it all for something that could be. And the belief that we are all destined for one thing comes with an enormous wave of reasonable doubt that stains everything we do. Every action, every decision will be watermarked with self-doubt.

    The term "fatalism" can refer to the view according to which human beings are powerless to do anything other than what they actually do, closely intertwined with the belief that humans have no power to influence the future or indeed the outcome of their own actions. We like to believe that we have the power to decide what our future is. We fight for free will, for the ability to make decisions without the hindrance of what other people opine. Humanity’s long fight for that basic right gets nullified by the belief that we have one purpose and one destiny alone and that that should be what we work towards. It takes you down a spiral of hopelessness and anxiety and eventually, intensifies to the point where it affects the functioning of our day and our response to the world around us. It is an added anxiety. Every time a child is told at an early age that they are destined to pursue one career without even considering the changes they might go through over the course of eighteen years adds another barrier between them and what they truly want. Eventually, they see what is put in front of them as the predetermined choice and they go with it because ‘that is what you were always meant to be’.

    According to the National Library of Medicine, fatalism is correlated with depression and the weakening of locus of control. Locus of Control refers to an individual's perception of the underlying main causes of events in their life. A group of participants were recruited who completed self-evaluation scales measuring depression symptoms, classic fatalism, active fatalism, coping skills, and locus of control. It was found that while classic fatalism was significantly and positively associated with depression and negative coping, active fatalism was positively correlated with positive coping skills, and negatively correlated with depression and external locus of control. Finally, the study found that active fatalism impacted a gradient in both depression and anxiety symptoms, regardless of whether it is influenced by classic fatalism.

    Active fatalism is the belief in a predetermined future, combined with the belief that one must do their part to bring this predestined future into reality. The helplessness towards one’s own life becomes an active participant in our emotions instead of just being a unitary belief or something that people say to comfort themselves when the people they want to end up together in a movie are going through a rough patch. It leads to a sense of resignation from one’s life as soon as a certain action or decision yields a negative result. The general opinion is that that is where the breadcrumb trail disappears and their destiny is lost. Or the belief gets twisted into them believing that their destiny was to fail and whatever adversity happened was going to happen regardless of whatever choice they made. That is a sadness that forms around you like a night terror- something you never see a way out of. You start walking on eggshells around yourself because the slightest thing could set it off. The slightest chink could take your one purpose away from you, a purpose you didn’t decide to be yours.

    Nothing that happens is star crossed before it happens. It happens, following which it is etched into the stars. We have too much to offer to the world to let it be painted over with the numbness of a purpose we did not decide. Romeo and Juliet weren’t destined to die, they chose to. They could have just as easily done one thing differently and they would have lived, maybe not together but alive. Louisa May Alcott defied everyone’s cries about Jo and Laurie being destined to be together because she alone saw the potential Jo had as a writer and explorer. As someone who would pen down the rich life she had. As someone who would give children all over the world a timeless faith in love and friendship.

    We need to know to accept that when something good happens, we have worked for it. It was not placed on a silver platter. But as humans, we also have to accept responsibility for the mistakes we make and fix them instead of submitting to a hopeless reverie because the fault, dear Brutus is not in the stars, but in ourselves.
