Britney Spears: No Longer a Slave 4 U

    • Mental Health   31/11/2021   Mohan Rajagopal

    This article is a follow-up to one published in the June issue of Verity Today. Reading it first would provide greater context to this one: Oops!... She’s In It Again: Britney Spears’ Conservatorship & Public Response to Mental Health

    My last article on the Britney Spears conservatorship case ended on a rather sombre note: Spears’ request to remove her father, Jamie Spears, from his role as the conservator for her estate had been denied and the conservatorship had been extended to at least September 2021. Moreover, Britney herself had requested to address the court regarding her guardianship for the first time in the 13-year-long case. Fortunately, the situation has moved rapidly in the past few months.

    The most groundbreaking revelation in the case was Spears’ direct address to the court in the latter half of June 2021, over a phone call. With an impassioned speech, she begged the court to end her father’s conservatorship with shocking allegations that he had drugged her, compelled her to work, and refused to let her remove her birth control device during the course of the guardianship. “I’ve been traumatised, and I just want my life back,” she cried to the Los Angeles judge.

    The media and internet were quick to rush to Spears’ defence once again, this time with renewed vigour. In the past, Britney’s stance on the conservatorship had remained murky: on her Instagram posts, she had stated that she was perfectly fine with the situation, but many suspected that the posts were being made by Jamie to feign normalcy. Now, the whole world had definitive proof that Spears was vehemently against her estate and body being controlled by her father: “I’m not happy. I can’t sleep. I’m so angry it’s insane.”

    In July of this month, a new lawyer was appointed in Britney’s defence, Matthew Rosengart. Immediately, his approach to the case was completely different from the indolence of the previous lawyers. He attacked the case with vigour and urgency, demanding the immediate suspension of Jamie Spears in light of his abuse of power and financial misbehaviour with Britney’s funds.

    September saw the justice that the whole world had been waiting for with bated breath. In the latest hearing on 30th September, after hearing both sides of the case, Judge Brenda Penny suspended Jamie Spears from the conservatorship. In her closing statement, she declared, “The current situation is untenable. It reflects a toxic environment which requires the suspension of James Spears.” Most importantly, a court hearing has been set for 12 November 2021, where the conservatorship is expected to be permanently terminated, once and for all. As per Rosengart and Spears’ wishes, an accountant by the name of John Zabel has been temporarily appointed temporary conservator over her finances, until the upcoming court date.

    Since then, Britney has taken to social media to express her joy and liberation, stating that she was on “Cloud 9”. Unfortunately, she has also spoken up about her fears regarding life post-conservatorship, especially due to the paparazzi attention she is receiving once again. Notably, it is the media circus that initially led to her breakdown which consolidated the conservatorship in 2008. In a length Instagram post, she wrote, “I’ll just be honest and say I’ve waited so long to be free from the situation I’m in … and now that it’s here I’m scared to do anything because I’m afraid I’ll make a mistake,”

    Regardless, the suspension of the conservatorship is undoubtedly a positive step towards improving the public perception of mental health. With several documentaries and news outlets picking up the story, the entire world knows much more about such guardianships necessitated by mental breakdowns, and more importantly, the legal loopholes that can be exploited in these cases. For now, it is only a matter of time before these laws are reworked to prevent the conservator from abusing their power and to prioritise the interests of the subject. Mental health is ubiquitous, and redefining its implications in institutions of law, politics, and the economy is essential in destigmatising and providing critical care to all individuals.
