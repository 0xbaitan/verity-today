Faiz Ahmad Faiz: The Poet of Our Times

    • Social Change   31/11/2021   Priyanka Banerjee

    Bol ki lab āzād haiñ tere
    Bol zabāñ ab tak terī hai
    Terā sutvāñ jism hai terā
    Bol ki jaañ ab tak terī hai

    Speak, for your lips are free:
    Speak, your tongue is still yours,
    Your upright body is yours—
    Speak, your life is still yours.

    — ‘Bol’, Faiz Ahmad Faiz

    The above lines have been taken from the famous poem ‘Bol’, written by Urdu poet Faiz Ahmad Faiz. In keeping with Faiz’s image as a poet of the revolution, the poem urges people to speak out against tyranny and injustice with whatever they have, a universal message of freedom of expression which goes far beyond the boundaries of India and Pakistan.

    Faiz is one of the most popular modern poets of the 20th century whose work is widely read in both India and Pakistan even today. That is because almost everything Faiz wrote about his own times is still relevant in India's current political climate. Poems that were written pre-Partition, at the peak of India's independence movement, or post-Partition, as a new country was taking shape, remain relevant seventy-four years after Independence. Poems about standing up against tyranny, speaking out against oppression, and raising our voices against brutality, resonate with us because the seeds of intolerance, fear and communal violence, that the makers of independent India wanted to expunge from the country, still breed and scour our land even today.

    Faiz Ahmad Faiz was born in Sialkot, a region of Punjab that sits along the fault line of Partition, in 1911, in British India. He went on to study at Government College and Oriental College and was trained in the classical Persian poets Rumi and Hafiz. Faiz is very popularly known as a Marxist and a Leftist poet and won the Lenin Peace Prize in 1962. In the 1930s, he became one of the protagonists of the anti-fascist, pro-socialist Progressive Writers’ Movement and a few years later, he became one of the founders of the Progressive Writers’ Association. He served in the British Army during the Second World War but soon resigned from the army and after India and Pakistan gained independence in 1947, Faiz moved to Lahore and became the editor of the Leftist English daily, Pakistan Times. After India’s hasty partition by the British in 1947, Faiz documented the country in the throes of uncertainty, communal tension and horrific violence. He was arrested in 1951 for the Rawalpindi conspiracy case and in 1955 on charges of conspiring to overthrow the government. Today, he has achieved an archetypal legacy, not just for his widely acclaimed love poems but also as a revolutionary poet for his poems about the protest against oppression.

    Vo din ki jis kā va.ada hai
    Jo lauh-e-azal meñ likhkhā hai
    Jab zulm-o-sitam ke koh-e-girāñ
    Ruuī kī tarah uḌ jā.eñge

    — ‘Hum Dekhenge’, Faiz Ahmad Faiz

    When one thinks about Faiz’s most relevant poems today, the first one to be thought of is ‘Hum Dekhenge’. The poem was initially written in 1979 as a protest against the rising Islamisation of Pakistan under General Zia-ul-Haq, the sixth President of Pakistan but recently, the poem has become very popular in India as it was used during the protests against the Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) and the National Register of Citizens (NRC). When the song was sung during such a protest at the Indian Institute of Technology (IIT), Kanpur, a professor raised objections about the poem being ‘anti-Hindu’. Ironically, the very paragraph that the professor raised objection to was the same one that was banned by the extremely right-wing Zia regime for quoting the Quran. The poem is one of Faiz’s most popular revolutionary poems today, in both India and Pakistan, and continues to call out extremism in both countries.

    Nisār maiñ tirī galiyoñ ke ai vatan ki jahāñ
    Chalī hai rasm ki koī na sar uThā ke chale
    Jo koī chāhne vaalā tavāf ko nikle
    Nazar churā ke chale jism o jaañ bachā ke chale
    Hai ahl-e-dil ke liye ab ye nazm-e-bast-o-kushād
    Ki sañg-o-ḳhisht muqayyad haiñ aur sag āzād

    Bury me, oh my country, under your pavements,
    Where no man now dare walk with head held high,
    Where your true lovers bringing you their homage
    Must go in furtive fear of life or limb:
    For new-style law and order are in use,
    Good men learn, — ‘Stones locked up, and dogs turned loose’.

    The above lines of the first stanza of ‘Nisar Main Teri Galiyon Ke’ talks about the true lovers of the country, the real patriots and activists who have to bow their heads in fear of imprisonment and brutality when power falls in the hands of those whose brutal propaganda becomes the law and order of the country. Although this is another of Faiz’s poems written against the Zia regime of Pakistan, we can see reflections of such politics in our society today. Unreasonable brutality towards those who raise their voice for democracy, activists scared and silenced with terms like ‘anti-nationalist’, and new legal systems introduced that nurture intolerance and communalism. The poem talks about a right-wing government that silenced and threatened and frightened the masses to keep themselves in power.

    Bol ye thoḌā vaqt bahut hai
    Jism o zabāñ kī maut se pahle
    Bol ki sach zinda hai ab tak
    Bol jo kuchh kahnā hai kah le

    Speak, this little time is plenty
    Before the death of body and tongue:
    Speak, the truth is still alive—
    Speak, say whatever is to be said.

    In the overall oeuvre of progressive poetry, Faiz Ahmad Faiz’s contribution has often been considered paramount and ‘Bol’ is one of his many poems which must be credited for this. The poem adheres to Faiz’s politics and Marxist beliefs and was initially written to address the labourers and the downtrodden in society. But the poem not only has a universal appeal, its lines also remain relevant even today. It encourages people to stand up and speak out against injustice and totalitarianism and will, therefore, remain relevant in every age and society that attempts to stifle and silence the voices of its people.

    During times of darkness and suppression, works of the revolutionary poet not only speak of our discontents and soothe our disquiet, but also awaken our complacency, bring us together and move us to action against authoritarianism and tyranny. Faiz Ahmad Faiz has been globally acclaimed as one of the 20th century’s most progressive and relevant poets and his work continues to respond to contemporary moments of crisis. His work not only challenged structures of authority and power and the failure of governments to respond to the situation of the oppressed and the downtrodden, but it also introduced to the masses the concept of revolutionary poetry and popularised it, so much so that his poems are till date used as slogans of protest against intolerance, brutality and discrimination. His work continues to inspire generations to rise up and take action against failing structures of authority, a movement as necessary in India today as it was more than seventy-four years ago.
