NO to Child Marriage, not Marriage Registration

    • The Affairs   31/11/2021   Dipali Banka

    On 17th September, 2021 the Rajasthan Assembly passed The Rajasthan Compulsory Resgistration of Marriage (amendment) Bill, 2021 which asked for a mandatory registration of marriage with a slight change in the age of the bride. The opposition Bhartiya Janta Party claims that the bill provides for legalisation of child marriage in the state.

    The bill faced various criticism from non-profit organisations, activists as an infringement of child protections laws of the country, and a Public Interest Litigation has been filled with the Supreme court challenging the constitutionality of the amendments.

    The Leaflet said “The ad nauseam with respect to the Rajasthan Compulsory Registration of Marriages (Amendment) Bill, 2021 (the “Bill”), which was passed by the State Assembly on September 17, 2021, is much ado about nothing.” The Leaflet is an online news portal based in India, an independent platform for progressive, legal and political opinion.

    Let us first compare the previous act and the present amended bill, below:

    Rajasthan Compulsory Resgistration of Marriage Act 2009, Section 8:

    “The parties, or in case the parties have not completed the age of twenty-one years, the parents or as the case may be, guardian of the parties, shall be responsible to submit the memorandum within a period of thirty days from the date of solemnization of the marriage to the Registrar within whose jurisdiction the marriage is solemnized or both or any of the parties resides. (2) A memorandum, which is not submitted within the time limit specified in sub-section (1), may be submitted at any time on payment of penalty as may be prescribed.”

    Rajasthan Compulsory Resgistration of Marriage Bill, 2021, Section 8:

    ”The parties to the marriage, or in case the bridegroom has not completed the age of twenty one years and/or bride has not completed the age of eighteen years, the parents or, as the case may be, guardian of the parties shall be responsible to submit the memorandum, in such manner, as may be prescribed, within a period of thirty days from the date of solemnization of the marriage to the Registrar within whose jurisdiction the marriage is solemnized, or the parties to the marriage or either of them are residing for at least thirty days before the date of submission of the memorandum.”

    The comparison shows that the 2009 Act and amended Bill of 2021, both provided for the registration of marriage including child marriage. What the state assembly amended was nothing but a technical move to bring the age line of a child in tune with the Central Act section 2(a), which prescribes an age below 18 for a female and 21 for a male one. the amended bill also adds a new subsection which would allow the widows to avail beneficial schemes, (1-A) states that in case of death of one party, the widow or widower or their heir in case of both parties are deceased can register the marriage 30 days after the death. The amended bill also in addition allowed the block MRO, i.e., Marriage Resgistration Officers to register marriage to enhance the transparency and improved results.

    The articles published by various media houses fail to provide a simple stance to its readers and simplify the terms used in the amended bill. The Wirein’s article of 19th September had a headline which stated “Rajasthan’s Amended Bill to Register Marriages Includes ‘Registration of Child Marriage’”, another headline of Livemint in its article published on 18th September said “Rajasthan Govt amends Marriage Act, allows registration of child marriage.”. these headlines serve as a disturbing distraction from original factual information.

    It essential to understand that child marriage in India is not void, illegal but voidable at the option of the contracted party who was a child at the time of the marriage, the documentation i.e. the marriage registration here merely provides documented evidence of marriage which helps in accessing benefits under the Central Act. While discussion relating to the legality of child marriage and turning it into void ab initio, the parliament has failed to make any progress. Under the Central Act 2006 Section 3, the party can file a petition to annul the marriage within 2 years of attaining the majority age. Since there are no rights and obligations after annulment, Section 4 provides safety and stability in terms of finance until remarriage.

    Another important find is that registration of child marriage does not make it legal, or as a matter-of-fact registration of any marriage doesn’t make it legal. It only serves as medium to collect requisite data with the State for explicit safeguards which in detailed is explained by the Supreme Court’s judgement in the case of Smt. Seema versus Ashwani Kumar in 2006. More over when a child marriage is registered, the Child Marriage Prohibition officers can be alerted. This registration will also add to prosecuting groups and individuals who have indulged or preached the same.

    This controversy which is particularly caused because the opposing group was unaware of the factual information has also shed some light on the actual issue we should be concerned about i.e., child marriage which only aggravated during the global pandemic The Amended bill was withdrawn by the state according to the NDTV reports; however, the deep-rooted tradition of child marriage is stays intact as the Central Law and its implementation fail to provide us with a solution to completely irradicate it. The practise of child marriage not only one of the primary causes of poverty and illiteracy in rural areas but also impacts the mental health of the minor.
