Dilemma between Labour and Sustainability

    • The Affairs   31/11/2021   Chhavi

    In the fashion industry, there are two types of women: those who make what you wear and those who wear it. That, however, is not the end of the adventure. In fact, there isn't even a speck of it. We discuss inequality, the rich and poor, the gender gap, and other topics, but we rarely discuss the same gender gap. Consider the difference between a woman who is struggling to make ends meet, working in dangerous factories, facing sexual harassment, and who knows what else, and a woman who works in the office, receiving fair pay, respect, and a life. The issue isn't so much their way of life as it is what they did differently to acquire it, and how doing differently created them an image by which we may gauge a country's growth. Most people believe that if a country's women are well dressed or wearing expensive clothes that these women in the same industry can afford, then the country is flourishing, but the reality is found in the factories.

    These fashion brands hire labour mostly females from developing countries (such as China, Bangladesh, and India), and as we all know, working conditions for females from these countries in factories are a do or die situation for them. Apart from that, women working in offices are also dissatisfied because they work at lower levels for two reasons: First, homophily, a sociological notion that is widely found in network situations. , in which a person tends to associate with others who are similar to themselves; as all of the high positions in the industry are occupied by males, they talk to other males and it's not about talking, it's about getting comfortable; for example, company parties, where most females prefer not to drink and most of the time they can't stay late, which distinguishes them from the males on the higher level or from lower levels which make their chances of socializing with the other males less ; Second, gender preconceptions such as males being good at agentic skills while women are good at communal skills; hence, they are only dealing with consumers in the hierarchy of responsibilities most of the times .

    Apart from the labour routine, these brands move from one location to another in search of cheap labour, as evidenced by the pandemic, in which all lower-level workers are more likely to lose their jobs than higher-level workers; they proudly tell their customers that they are paying their workers fair wages, but this is not the case; they are paying them minimum wages, which keeps them trapped in poverty. For example, these minimal wage jobs would provide them with food or perhaps a reasonable life, but this will compel them to remain poor. They are getting increasingly poor economically, and they are trapped in the cycle for the rest of their lives.

    Apart from labour and wages, the fashion industry's biggest issue is that it is producing environmental difficulties; it is responsible for 20% of global garbage, 10% of carbon emissions, and much more, and it is not going to get any better unless we find a solution. The emergence of digital fashion is one option that stands out among a slew of others. Why is it vital to discuss digital fashion? It's significant because it sheds light on customer psychology, their market behaviour, and how they select the best option based on their preferences. Most of the clients, mostly females, want to buy designer garments to post on social media, but most of them cannot afford to do so. It also generates less pollution, but the question is if it is worth it. Will it have an impact on the lives of others who work at lower levels? Will it make the fashion sector more competitive? What kind of fashion do you prefer? Will digital fashion become a method for people to flaunt themselves, and what are the psychological implications of this?

    These long-term problems are necessary to consider and address, but it is true that there is a negative correlation between digital fashion and labour in the fashion industry to some level. Not right now, but perhaps in 2050, when society becomes more materialistic and garments go out of fashion every month, it will have an impact on labour, but commenting on how will take some time.
