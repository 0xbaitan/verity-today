Linking the Oil Curse and Latin America's Economic History

    • The Affairs   31/11/2021   Manoli Arora

    The 'oil curse' refers to the situation of developing countries with oil-rich economies experiencing economic decline. Countries that are rich in petroleum are seen to have less democracy, less economic stability, and more frequent internal conflicts than countries without oil. The Oil Curse shows that oil wealth typically creates less economic growth than it should, produces jobs for men but not women and creates more problems in poor states than in rich ones.

    Countries suffering from the ‘oil curse’ are managed inefficiently. All onus for economic development shifts on the oil reserves the countries possess leading to decline in other sectors. Similar is the case of many countries in Latin America - they have abundant natural resources but stunted economic growth. Oil curse is the foe, but reasons that fuel it are different for different countries.

    It is possible to identify a number of characteristics, such as a lack of competent management, instability, a singular concentration on the oil business, corruption, and authoritarianism, that are common in Latin American economic history and contribute to the creation of the "oil curse." The economic phenomena known as the "oil curse" hampers developing countries from achieving their full potential.

    Venezuela's oil sector is state-owned, and other businesses and markets have not flourished as a result of the country's concentration on oil earnings. The focus on such wealth as oil caused economic balances to alter, and this equilibrium is still not achieved today.

    The Mexican economy is extremely diverse, and oil is simply one of its many major industries. However, oil is one of the country's major exports. High oil prices are beneficial to Mexico's economy, but they are not vital.

    Nonetheless, the Mexican economy is also suffering from the 'oil curse' that has plagued the country in recent years. The issue is an inability to efficiently manage the industry while paying close attention to the development of other industries.

    While some researchers like Haber and Manaldo impose that there is no direct relation between such aspects of the countries’ economic history as the role of authoritarianism in the economic development of the Latin American countries, others believe that the entire economic history of Latin American countries is an example of ineffective government management, with a focus on authoritarianism and its associated corruption, economic repression, and catastrophic management, as well as excessive borrowings and debts that are not paid for decades while contributing to economic decline.

    The general economic deterioration is noted if the governments of Latin American countries rely heavily on the expansion of the oil industry. However, it is conceivable to argue that the 'oil curse' phenomena is largely due to the poor techniques adopted by developing-country governments to manage the new economic sector and stabilise the economy.

    Lack of democracy and economic stability, sole focus on the oil industry and a lack of competitiveness in other industries, concentration on increasing oil prices, which leads to inflation, ineffective management, and widespread corruption are all characteristics of the development of Latin American oil-rich countries.
