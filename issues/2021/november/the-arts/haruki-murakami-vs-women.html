Haruki Murakami vs Women

    • The Arts   31/11/2021   Anoushka Dasgupta

    Haruki Murakami has managed to earn a huge name for himself in the world of English Literature. The Japanese writer is known for his eccentric tales which weave reality with fantasy, and create a form of escapism that is unique to his work. However, one of the most controversial aspects of his work is his depiction of women and the role they play in his stories, which has made him the subject of feminist critique over the years. While the author himself provides negligible context to most of his writings, it is interesting to see how the 21st century feminist movement throws light on his work.

    Murakami’s world of fiction is one in which the male protagonists are unravelling their own dilemmas in a different dimension, while the women are left to face reality. If you’ve read enough of his work, it is easy to glean a pattern emerging in these male protagonists- they are aloof, disconnected from the world and seeking to recover something they have lost. Their journey of recovery often blurs the lines between reality and fantasy, and ends with a spiritual awakening at the end. They are eccentric but likeable, and are often supplemented by other male characters whose mystery and charm makes them very attractive on paper.

    Female characters, on the other hand, see a vastly different narrative. Generally, supporting women are sculpted to fit one of two roles- the self-sacrificing caregiver or the courtesan. Murakami's work contains a whole spectrum of different women- some who exude a confident charm like their male counterparts, some who are quiet and passive and some who exist for sexual gratification of the protagonist only. Murakami has often said that these sexual activities in his work don’t exist just for the sake of existing. There is always an ulterior motive- something philosophical, which exists as a stepping stone for the protagonist to either unveil his character or move further to recovering what he has lost. Hence, his women characters aren’t meant to be vessels of sexual gratification, but rather key components to develop the plot.

    But this begs the question- is the existence of these characters still justifiable? When you pick up certain characters, it often becomes difficult to understand their motives or gauge the morality of their existence. Take for example 13-year-old Mariye Akigawa from Killing Commendatore. Her first encounter with the middle-aged protagonist resulted in her discussing her breasts (or lack thereof) in great detail. Clearly, this young teenager saw this 40 something year old man as a sexual object. However, in a 2017 interview with Mieko Kawakami, Murakami said that the very fact that she brought up the subject of her breasts meant that she regarded him as anything but a sexual object, which made their conversation completely introspective. Mariye can be contrasted to characters like May Kasahara from The Wind Up Bird Chronicle, who exhibited a kind of profundity very unusual for someone her age. Some of her dialogues were surprisingly intelligent, and she is, perhaps, one of Murakami's most layered characters.

    Coming back to Killing Commendatore, the character of Menshiki was one of Murakami's typical supporting characters- mysterious, suave and always calculated. His personality was reflected in Mariye's aunt, Shoko Akigawa, who was mainly described by the clothes she wore (a very common Murakami technique of describing women). Very little was revealed about her throughout the course of the story, which made her all the more intriguing and placed her on the same pedestal as that of male supporting characters like Menshiki. Another example of powerful female characters would be in his novel After Dark, where a majority of the characters were women (including the protagonist, Mari Asai). Characters like Kaoru, and those of the workers working at the Love Hotel were also multidimensional and complex, and displayed real human emotions that made them stand out. They were flawed and relatable, and it was through them that the story progressed seamlessly. Here, it is also worth mentioning Norwegian Wood's Naoko and Midori, who each stood out in their own way and propelled the protagonist to make his decisions in the story.

    Some other popular women characters from Murakami's novels fit into the category of the self-sacrificing oracle- the ones who are rooted in reality and end up shedding their blood while the male protagonist is lost in the subconscious. Some of Murakami's most iconic full-length novels follow this sequence, like how in The Wind Up Bird Chronicle, Toru Okada's wife Kumiko was the one who killed Noboru Wataya by pulling his life support plug. Similarly, in 1Q84, Aomame was the one who killed the Leader and had to face its consequences. Almost none of the male protagonists have had to adopt this role, and although Murakami has repeatedly said that the characters he creates transcend gender, this common trope is much too overused for it to not be frustrating.

    Lastly, one can't really discuss Murakami's women characters without bringing up his short story Sleep. Written entirely from a woman’s perspective, this story was so fantastic that on its first English publication, flocks of American women wrote to Murakami saying how much they resonated with the character.

    Based on a woman who is unable to sleep, the story went beyond just "bored-housewife syndrome" and captured the universally resonant emotion of loneliness. It is these characters that truly capture the human experience in an expertly subtle way, and make the reader feel a genuine sense of empathy and understanding- something that the author does very well.

    All in all, it has become evident that Murakami's depiction of women is too diverse to fit into a general category. They all possess a sense of individualism typical of his characters, and while they may be questionable at times, there is no doubt that they are too well written to be classified as black or white. Though it is impossible to gauge Murakami's position on the feminist ladder, there is no doubt that his works will be celebrated for decades to come. Perhaps the Nobel next time?
