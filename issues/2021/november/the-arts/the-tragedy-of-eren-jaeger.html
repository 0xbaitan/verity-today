Freedom, Freedom, Forgive me — The tragedy of Eren Jaeger

    • The Arts   31/11/2021   Sreemita Mukherjee

    The year is 845. Humanity is said to have perished due to the tyranny of gigantic man-eating humanoids referred to as Titans. What remains of the human race, is protected from these monsters behind three humongous walls namely, Maria, Rose, and Sina. The walls protect humanity from its otherwise cruel fate, they block out the harsh reality and let humans live prosperously for however long they can manage. The walls haven’t been breached in 100 years though. The walls provide safety, people of the walls cheer, humanity shall prevail, they hope.

    Eren Jaeger, at the mere age of 9 however, vehemently disagrees.

    The walls may provide safety but at the cost of freedom. For nine-year-old Eren, who is hot-tempered and rigid, who is righteous and is curious, who loves his mother and wants nothing more than to taste the salty water of the sea and marvel at the beauty of the world; for Eren Jaeger, the world is a strict black and white, and the walls provide him safety but they take away the one thing more essential to him than his mother’s hugs and his friend, Armin’s philosophical quips--the walls take away his freedom and freedom, Eren believes, is something every human should have.

    Attack on Titan, created by Hajima Isayama, creates a world wherein death runs rampant. Loss and the shattering realization that this all accounts for nothing are found at every corner. Within the walls of Rose, defeat, and mourning for the loss of wall Maria and its people continues; Humanity stands halfway between eradication and survival, and between the screaming and fighting, stands a 15-year-old Eren, with a strong resolve, and a vow to eradicate the race of Titans that stole his town, mother and dreams.

    It is only unfortunate that after an enthusiastic exchange with his fellow soldiers whilst on duty, Eren finds himself facing the very Colossal Titan that kickstarted the attack on his hometown of Shiganshina. Eren finds himself being hauled left and right by Armin in an effort to protect them from the stream of Titans entering wall Rose. Eren watches helplessly as his fellow soldiers die the most horrendous deaths, whilst the remaining soldiers scramble about trying to decide whether they’re helpful or helpless. It is then that Eren realizes just how powerless he is. So, when Eren’s hand finally slips from Armin’s and he goes straight down the throat of a Titan, death seems bittersweet, but almost deserved. In hindsight, Eren would’ve died knowing he lived a somewhat happy life.

    However, Eren doesn’t die then, he only discovers his Titan abilities. He doesn’t die anytime soon either but he comes close to it a couple of times, and each time he realizes that the peace of knowing he lived a happy life becomes more and more unattainable.

    Death becomes an unwanted friend of Eren and his friends in all its all-consuming glory. For Eren, watching the people he loves most, die is worse than his own run-ins with death. What he doesn’t know at fifteen though, is that, soon enough, death will be his hope in the future--his hope for salvation from his cruel world.

    Eren watches as his entire squad gets trampled by Titans, he watches his three comrades turn out to be Titans themselves, he watches people get killed one by one, all of their deaths being solely for their will to protect him. His relationship with death gets more and more convoluted until he realizes with a startle, that he is the cause of all this destruction and almost paradoxically, the people around him chant his importance to the survival of humanity. At fifteen, Eren trusts the people around him more than he trusts himself and so, he lets their voices drown out the incessant need to embrace the destruction.

    Season three of Attack on Titan starts with an exploration of the political scenario of the walls. This time, the enemy isn’t the Titans, it’s humans. The main cast grapples with the reality of having to resort to such morally ambiguous deeds, as they slowly transition from cut-throat soldiers to hesitating assassins. The blood on their hands still haunts them but it haunts no one more than Eren who has convinced himself that his death is the only way forward for humanity. More importantly, that he can almost accept death at this point in his life.

    There is something haunting about seeing Eren’s descent from a kid who was ready to charge headfirst into battle just a few months ago, welcoming death despite his hesitance. He doesn’t want to fight anymore, but unfortunately for him, this is just the beginning of the destruction, he will be the sole cause of.

    Part two of Season three takes us back to the town that started it all. Nostalgia is denied entry because a brutal fight has to be fought against the people who turned Shiganshina into the ghost town it is today. Eren puts aside all his emotional baggage to fight, only for a total of nine people to make it out of the fight. The mission had been to get to Eren’s father’s basement, with the key, Eren has had ever since the beginning, and when Eren gets to his house he’s not returned to in six years, they find a book that changes the course of not only Eren’s life but also of the entire human population. The people of the walls now have a name, they’re Eldians.

    Eldians have a more complicated history than being mere survivors of humanity. Eldians can turn into Titans but except for the nine Titans derived from their founder, Ymir, all of them turn into mindless giant humanoids, with no consciousness except for the need to eat humans. Eldians were once the oppressors but are now the oppressed, especially Eldians on the island of Paradise, unaware of their ancestors’ deeds and left in the dark with a warped version of their apocalyptic past, left to die without any redemption. Eren used to think that freedom lied outside the walls, only for his perception of the world to be turned on its head, and suddenly, freedom was nowhere to be found. There were only wars to be fought and revenge to be taken.

    So Eren finally turns towards destruction. For the first time in his life, he embraces his role in the world fully, and lets go of everyone and everything he loves because if he cannot achieve freedom in life, he can achieve it in death. Eren becomes ruthless. He kills to get what he wants, he’s ready to sacrifice the entire world for the protection of his homeland, and when the people around him are forced to see him in the light he has seen himself in for so long, Eren knows, for once, he’s successfully completed his mission. Eren accepts death, and destruction, and all the things he never wanted to be.

    Eren Jaeger dies at age nineteen, after having lived a life of sheer chaos and unrest. Eren dies at age nineteen, and he is finally free from this cruel, cruel world.
