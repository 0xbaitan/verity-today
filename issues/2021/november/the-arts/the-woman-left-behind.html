The Woman Left Behind

    • The Arts   31/11/2021   Priyanka Banerjee

    I was about seven years old when, one day, I found a painting in a big box of old things my mother said she had brought from her mother’s house when she got married many years ago. The painting was old, its wooden frame had chipped at the edges. It was almost as tall as me and much wider, but of the many books and portraits and photographs in the box, this was the one that caught my eye. The painting showed a man standing under a blossoming tree with a flute to his lips and a peacock feather stuck in his curly hair. He wore a yellow dhoti, had a garland around his neck, and was adorned with a great many jewels. But the strange thing about this man was that his skin was the darkest shade of blue, making him look almost ethereal with all the shining gold and pearls on him. Next to him stood the most beautiful woman I had ever seen. Her skin was fair, almost milky white, and her hair was tied in a braid that was decorated with flowers. She was adorned with fewer jewels than the man next to her, but with more flowers, making her look simpler, kinder, and more human. She wore a long white skirt and a light pink cloth was tied around her upper body. I noticed that the man and the woman were standing very close to each other, their eyes locked in a loving gaze. Her hands rested on the man’s shoulders, her body partially leaning on his, and she looked at him like he had designed all of Creation just for her. Little did I know then that I wasn’t far off the mark.

    The woman intrigued me for reasons I can’t explain even now, nearly twenty years later. With great difficulty, I picked up the large, heavy painting and somehow managed to carry it to my mother, who was sewing a blouse in the next room. I sat down next to her to ask her about it, hoping the painting would catch her attention more than the fact that I had found it while sneaking around in her cupboard.

    “Mother,” I said, timidly. She stopped her sewing to look up at me and I turned the painting to her. “Who are these people?”

    My mother smiled at the painting and then at me. “That is Krishna, a form of Vishnu, one of the gods we worship. His portrait is there in your father’s room among the other gods. Haven’t you noticed it before?”

    I shook my head to say no. “And, who is the woman?”

    “That is Radha, Krishna’s eternal consort.”

    “What does a consort mean? Does it mean a Goddess? If she isn’t a Goddess, why is she in the painting? Can humans be next to gods?”

    She laughed at my curiosity and ruffled my hair. “A consort means someone you love very much, like Krishna loves Radha. She is considered to be a Goddess by some people and they worship both of them. Some think she is just a human being Krishna loved whose devotion towards him made her memory immortal in our minds. Either way, she appears in the painting because Krishna loves her and wants her by his side.”

    “Well, how did Krishna meet her? Are they married, like you and Father?”

    At that question, her face fell a little, as though she was feeling another’s sadness. “No, they are not married. When Krishna met her, he was much younger than he seems to be in this painting. Perhaps three or four years older than you. But Radha was seventeen or eighteen and already married to another man. So Krishna could never marry her.”

    I was puzzled. I did not know that a woman could love another man when she had already been married. If she did not love her husband, why did she marry him then? “Tell me their story.”

    “You want to know Krishna’s story?”

    “Yes. And Radha’s too.”

    “Well, Krishna was born to Devaki and Vasudeva. Devaki was the cousin sister of Kansa, the prince of a place called Mathura and he hated almost everyone but his sister. But on her wedding day, there was a prophecy that her and Vasudeva’s eighth son would kill Kansa because he was an evil man who scared the people. The thought of his death scared—”

    I stopped her here. “Mother, this is about neither Krishna nor Radha! Tell me stories about them, about their love. It must not have been easy for Radha, being married to a different man.”

    “Okay then. Once upon a time, in a village called Vrindavan…” And my mother went on to narrate a story that I now know as well as my own name.

    The story was full of demons and young boys, gopis and beautiful dances, cows and stealing butter, miracles and a great many interesting things. The story was about Krishna’s childhood and triumphs. It was a hero’s story, and a great one too. But that day, nearly twenty years ago, it wasn’t he who had caught my attention in the painting. I listened to my mother with rapt attention but I didn’t understand one thing: if Radha was barely there in the story at all, why was she important enough to be in the painting?

    My mind was full of questions. I waited for my mother to finish the part about Krishna leaving Vrindavan, and then asked her, “Mother, did Krishna ever come back?”

    “No, he did not. He knew he was meant for bigger things in life. He had a more important destiny to fulfil, which he could only do if he left Vrindavan.”

    “But then, what happened to Radha? He just left her? How could he leave her if he loved her? He may have gone on to do greater things, but what of her? What happens to the woman left behind?”

    My mother stopped and looked at me with a puzzled expression on her face, as though I had just asked her a question she had never thought about before. But that expression disappeared just as suddenly as it had come and she smiled fondly at me again. “Sometimes, we have to leave behind the ones we love for the greater good. Radha never raised questions about why Krishna left her because she believed in his love. We shouldn’t question their love either. Now, put the painting back where you found it and go outside and play. Let me get back to my stitching.”

    I was bursting with a hundred more questions. What happened to her after he left? What is her story? Did she ever see him again? How was she not hurt by this? He may have gone on to fulfil greater destinies, but did he leave his consort behind in a little village in an unhappy marriage with no bigger destiny for her? I wanted to ask my mother all these things but I could sense that she wasn’t going to answer any more of my questions today so I got up and did as she had asked.

    But the question lingered in my mind. I spent hours imagining elaborate scenarios about Radha’s life, her heartbreak, and her sorrow after Krishna left her. Ever since then, I’ve read a great many books, I’ve asked my father, my grandparents, my friends, I’ve even consulted scholars and looked it up on the internet, but I haven’t found the answer to my question even today. What happens to the woman left behind?
