How CRISPR is changing the future of human genetic modification

    • The Sciences   31/11/2021   Tiya Gupta

    Genome editing or gene modification gives scientists the ability to change the DNA of a living organism. These technologies allow genetic material to be added, removed, or modified at a specific location in the genome. Many approaches have been used for genome editing. The most effective and up-to-date is known as CRISPR-Cas9, short for “Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats and CRISPR Associated Protein 9”. The CRISPR-Cas9 system has generated much enthusiasm in the scientific community because it is faster, cheaper, more accurate and more efficient than other existing genome editing methods. CRISPR-Cas9 was adapted from a natural genome editing system in bacteria. As the founder says, “bacteria take DNA fragments from invading viruses and use them to create DNA segments which are known as CRISPR arrays.” These arrays allow bacteria to "remember" viruses (or closely related ones). If the viruses attack again, the bacteria produce RNA segments from the CRISPR arrays to attack the DNA of the viruses. The bacteria then use Cas9 or a similar enzyme to cut the DNA, which in turn inactivates the virus.

    Jennifer Doudna is a household name in the world of CRISPR, and she is considered responsible for the invention of CRISPR. Dr Doudna was one of the first scientists to suggest that this microbial immunity mechanism could be used for programmable genome editing. This tool has the potential to change the genetic makeup of any and all organisms and solve an infinite number of problems. Dr Doudna's discovery changed the world of genetic modification, as the techniques used before the development of CRISPR were time-consuming, tedious, and not as convenient. In 2020, Dr Doudna and Dr Charpentier won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for discovering CRISPR. Some bacteria have a built-in gene-editing system similar to the CRISPR-Cas9 system that they use to respond to invading pathogens like a virus, similar to an immune system. With CRISPR, bacteria cut parts of the virus' DNA and retain some of it so that they can recognize the virus in the next attack and defend the body against it. Scientists adapted this system so that it can be used in other animal cells, including mice and humans.

    The CRISPR-Cas9 system consists of two key molecules that introduce a change (mutation) In DNA. There is an enzyme, called Cas9. These act as "molecular scissors" that can cut the two strands of DNA at a specific point in the genome so that DNA fragments can then be added or removed. The second is a piece of RNA called guide RNA (gRNA). It consists of a small piece of pre-made RNA sequence (about 20 bases long) that sits within a longer RNA scaffold. The frame part binds to DNA and the premade sequence "leads" Cas9 to the right side of the genome. This ensures that the Cas9 enzyme cuts the DNA in the correct place in the genome. Guide RNA was developed to find and bind to a specific sequence in DNA. The main RNA have bases that are complementary to those of the target DNA sequence in the genome. This means that the guide RNA, at least in theory, only binds to the target sequence and not to other regions of the genome. Cas9 follows the lead RNA to the same place in the DNA sequence and cuts through both strands of DNA. The cell here recognizes that the DNA is damaged and begins the process to repair it. Scientists use the DNA repair machinery to make changes to one or more genes in the genome of a cell of interest.

    Usually, the guide RNA consists of a specific sequence of 20 bases. These are complementary to the target sequence in the gene to be edited, however not all 20 bases have to match for the guide RNA to bind. The problem with this is that a sequence with several complementary bases such as 19-20 bases can exist in a completely different place in the genome. This means that the guide RNA can possibly bind there instead of with the target sequence. The Cas9 enzyme then cuts in the wrong place and ends up introducing a mutation in the wrong place. While this mutation may not matter at all for the individual, it could affect a critical gene or some other important part of the genome.

    CRISPR-Cas9 has great potential as a tool to treat a number of diseases with a genetic component, such as cancer, hepatitis B, and even high cholesterol. Many of the proposed uses involve editing the genomes of somatic (non-reproductive) structures, but there has been much interest and discussion about the potential for germline manipulation of cells (reproductive). Since all changes made in germline cells are passed down from generation to generation, it has important ethical implications. Germline gene editing is currently illegal in the UK and most other countries. On the other hand, the use of CRISPR-Cas9 and other gene-editing technologies on living cells is unparalleled as of now. In fact, they have been used to treat human diseases in a small number of rare and/or life-threatening cases.

    It is likely that many years will pass before CRISPR-Cas9 is used routinely in humans. Much research is still focused on its use in animal models or isolated human cells with the goal of eventually using the technology for the routine treatment of diseases in humans. There is much work focused on eliminating "off-target" effects where the CRISPR-Cas9 system cuts a different gene than the one that should be edited.

    Scientists are eager to find a way to ensure that CRISPR-Cas9 will adhere and cut accurately. This can be achieved in two ways: a) by designing better and more specific guide RNAs using our knowledge of the genome's DNA sequence and the "off-target" behaviour of different versions of the Cas9-gRNA complex, b) the use of a Cas9 enzyme that only cuts a single strand of the target DNA instead of the double-strand. This means that two Cas9 enzymes and two guide RNAs must be in the same place for the cut to take place. This will reduce the likelihood of the cut being made in the wrong place.

    It is not a perfect system. Occasionally cutting in the wrong place and not working as expected causes scientists to scratch their heads. Well, there are few research solutions that can help CRISPR identify more than 90% of all genetic diseases. There are certain problems with CRISPR, but there are solutions to these as well. It can be used for cloning, which would increase the dangers and likelihood of identity theft. Since we can edit any gene, this could encourage people to edit their genes as desirable (for bigger muscles or faster metabolism, for instance), which could increase competition between people with similar desirable genes and lead to a tougher society in which anyone with unwanted genes would be discriminated against.

    CRISPR is still in its early stages but the scientists of the world anxiously hold their breaths in hopes that this newfound technology will be able to accomplish what no human has before: changing an organism from the inside out.
