Radio bursts from outer space

    • The Sciences   31/11/2021   Ansh Garodia

    Fast Radio Bursts, famously known as FRBs, were first spotted in 2001. Since then, scientists have discovered up to 1000 FRBs! Most of us see this word in the news daily but don’t know what it means; that’s fine because neither do scientists. There is much ambiguity about what FRBs are and how they work. Even with improved technology and expansive knowledge, FRBs remain a mystery. What we do know is that Fast Radio Bursts are radio waves, which is part of the electromagnetic spectrum.

    As humans, we cannot see radio waves as it is not visible light. An FRB is a fast and sudden burst of energy. It is said that a single burst can provide more energy than the sun in a day even though they are extremely short-lived. However, let me remind you that we are not completely oblivious to their nature. Over the past few years, scientists have deduced that FRBs originate from magnetars due to their incredibly strong magnetic field.

    FRB 2020012E was discovered as recently as January 2020. It was spotted using the Canada hydrogen intensity mapping experiment telescope, which has spotted several FRBs over the years. The telescope has exponentially increased the number of FRBs discovered from 30 to over 1000 within three years. This particular FRB was discovered by using a network of radio telescopes. To everyone’s surprise, the FRB originated from a globular cluster in the M81 Galactic system, 40 times closer than any other FRB ever discovered. Globular clusters are spherical collections of stars that are billions of years old, sometimes even as old as galaxies. Usually, FRBs originate from magnetars. Magnetars are neutron stars that have an extremely powerful magnetic field and thus they can power the emission of high energy electromagnetic radiation like x-rays, gamma rays and FRBs. However, scientists are now questioning the birth of such an FRB. As globular clusters do not contain magnetars, perplexed scientists could not fathom how an FRB could be born inside one.

    Investigation and experiments to find a plausible explanation are still ongoing. However, scientists do have a few conjectures. Northwestern University believes there are other ways magnetars can form within globular clusters, but to prove that, more information must be recorded. While some believe the density of a globular cluster affects its composition. others say that it might be possible to generate something similar to a Fast Radio Burst while maintaining the absence of magnetars. Theories related to black holes producing FRBs have also come to light.
