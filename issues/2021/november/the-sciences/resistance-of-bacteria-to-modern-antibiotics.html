Resistance of bacteria to modern antibiotics

    • The Sciences   31/11/2021   Wrijurekh Mukherjee

    Evolution is a process by which all living organisms mutate themselves in order to survive. Over the course of many generations, creatures start developing features that help them resist external threats to their survival. Flightless birds are a good example of this behaviour. Almost all flightless birds had an ancestor that was capable of flight. However, they lost this ability to focus on acquiring a different one that would be of use to them. Penguins, for example, have flippers instead of wings, as swimming is more advantageous than flying in the frigid environment of Antarctica. The same goes for humans: we have evolved over millions of years to get to where are today. Evolution has helped us survive for so long and is a major factor in the existence of all conscious life. However, this factor is slowly driving us into a dangerous situation.

    Evolution is not limited to complex, multicellular organisms only. It also takes place with the tiniest organisms known as bacteria. While some bacteria are harmless, others exhibit aggressive and parasitic behaviour. They invade our bodies and use us as a means of survival. To tackle this threat, our bodies developed the immune system, which is a highly complicated network of proteins, cells and other microscopic substances that help eradicate harmful bacteria. However, bacteria have evolved to enter our body despite the presence of the immune system, either by going unnoticed or by invading the immune system itself. Thus began the era of antibiotics. These are very specific toxins targeted at specific types of bacteria and it keeps killing them to the point where their population is no longer a threat. This convenient medicine went on to become a worldwide necessity and revolutionised the healthcare industry.

    However, studies observed a strange phenomenon occurring in recent times. Many bacteria have started to mutate themselves in order to attain immunity from the toxins released by antibiotics. Evolution occurred yet again and caused these bacteria to become resistant to the substance that kills them. Scientists said that this was because of how accessible the antibiotics were. They were so convenient and easy to take that people took them more and more, becoming a daily intake for a lot of people worldwide. This caused the bacteria to become heavily resistant to the antibiotic, and also made the bacteria stronger. This means that developing an antibiotic for it will be even harder and much more complicated. Even if these new antibiotics are developed, they too will be overused after a point, and this cycle will continue until the bacteria become so deadly that even a common cold could kill a person and a minor virus could wipe out humanity.

    Such is the nature of antibiotics. Using it more leads to a healthy life in the short term but will eventually ruin us. This biological crisis has been termed the “antibiotic resistance crisis” and is one of the most challenging situations biologists have to face. It elaborates how the tiniest creatures pose the greatest threat and why they keep growing and evolving beyond our control.
