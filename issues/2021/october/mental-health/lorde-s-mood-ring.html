Lorde’s ‘Mood Ring’ and the Appropriation of Wellness Culture

  • Mental Health   16/10/2021   Mohan Rajagopal

After a torturous wait of more than four years, Kiwi artist Lorde finally put out her third studio album, ‘Solar Power’, a follow-up to the sultry, Grammy-nominated ‘Melodrama’ from 2017. Revamped with a whole new sound and aesthetic, Solar Power explores the healing qualities of the Sun and Lorde’s intimacy with nature following her recovery from the adolescent angst and heartbreak of her previous album. As such, the record tends to take on quite a spiritual presence, delving into different practices of wellness culture, although not necessarily framing these in an entirely positive light. The ambiguity towards such applications is best seen in her latest single, ‘Mood Ring’, which also happens to be my personal favourite from the album.

In an interview with Genius, Lorde said that the character in the song is not her; it is a satirical take on a person “seeking wellness, enlightenment, or even utopia”. As such, the song explored various tropes of spirituality and well-being, which more often than not have been appropriated by Western societies and white-washed. Mood Ring, hence, is a commentary on the modern person’s need to have coping mechanisms to deal with the turmoil of everyday life. Usually, the people partaking in such mechanisms do not have much knowledge about the origins of these practices, or even why they do what they do. Sometimes, it serves as an instrument of socialisation to fit in with others, and other times, it’s simply a well-intentioned (albeit ignorant) attempt at drowning out the noise of life. Lorde has gone on to say that the themes of the song have been inspired by the New Age wellness movement of the ’60s, which according to her, is a similar trajectory that the world seems to be heading in now, in terms of spiritual practices.

The protagonist of the song is a “vapid young woman” who seems to be completely unaware of all the mechanisms she takes part in. This theme of blind participation runs throughout the song and is at the heart of satirical criticism. She instructs her fellow women to “begin sun salutations” and meditations, and although she offers to “get high” and let loose, there is a sense of neuroticism in her agreeing to do it “only if the wind blows just right”. Even while putting on a show of relaxation and flexibility, the woman is high-strung and anxious, refusing to calm down unless the conditions are conducive for it.

The second verse continues in a similar vein when the woman complains about not being able to find what’s wrong with her mood. Despite all her attempts at incorporating meditations and spirituality, she continues to consume “celebrity news” and other information on the Internet. Lorde has been rather vocal this era about the toxicity of online platforms and social media, and the inclusion of this tidbit pokes fun at the woman who is willing to take vitamins and cleanse crystals for her mental health but is unwilling to let go of the Internet, a major cause for anxiety and depression. Later on, she says, “Let’s fly somewhere Eastern, they’ll have what I need”, and it is here that the full context of appropriation is uncovered. Lorde has made it clear that the song does not wish to criticise the protagonist herself, or any other real-life individuals who may partake in similar practices. Instead, it is the wellness industry as a whole that must be blamed for constantly stealing elements from other cultures and white-washing them into a Westernised ideal. The global wellness industry is now worth a whopping 4.2 trillion USD, and this number is only on the rise over the years. Nations in the East are looked to as havens of wellness and spirituality, but instead of appreciating them for such aspects, people have simply been appropriating their rituals without any knowledge about the historical background or intent.

Shaman Durek Verrett, a spiritual guide, has commented on this phenomenon:

“Every person in wellness is taking from the cultures of indigenous people, whether it’s India, Africa, or Indonesia. But the Western world takes these practices and glorifies them into a narcissistic version. You don’t see the Indian, African, Asian or Latino person represented at all.”

The satire and ridicule come to a climax in the choruses of the song, where the ignorance of the protagonist is clearly highlighted:

“I can't feel a thing, I keep looking at my mood ring
Tell me how I'm feeling
All the sad girls sing, We'll keep dancing 'til the mood rings
Tell us how we're feeling”

In a slump, the woman is unable to identify her own emotions and instead looks towards a useless plastic ring to tell her how she feels. This introduces an idea that is at the crux of a lot of spirituality; even if we know something to be illogical and incorrect, we look for support from it due to feelings of anxiety and insecurity. It’s for the same reason that several people believe in superstitions or often check their astrological horoscopes. The protagonist in the song is so out of touch with herself that she is forced to seek help from an instrument that she knows to be a scam, and that is the perfect summary for wellness culture today. Spirituality is well and good, but only as long as you know what you’re doing, and why you’re doing it.
