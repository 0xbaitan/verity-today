The Comprehensible Opinion

  • Mental Health   16/10/2021   Niharika Rajsaha

Grammy-winning ‘Folklore’, was regarded the most influential album of 2020 with the romance of a lonely witch’s life in a cabin in the woods proving a respite for a world hit too hard with a harsh unimaginable reality. Released on July 24th, 2020, Taylor Swift’s eighth studio album is still evoking a worldwide sense of mellow nostalgia and the smell of trees a year later. ‘August’ caressed us with the warm winds and ocean spray of a seaside summer love and ‘Exile’ knocked that wind right out of your stomach leaving you empty and confused about a love that could have been. The appeal of Folklore is not contained in the ingenious songwriting or the compatibility of the music with the theme but in the reality of the themes addressed. They filled the loneliness and the absence of these ubiquitous circumstances while echoing the saturation of all those feelings that the aforementioned circumstances bring.

A song where the subject is singularly pointed out is ‘the last great American dynasty’, inspired by the socialite outcast Rebekah Harkness. The song draws references between Rebekah and Taylor Swift herself, the most tangible link being that she now lives in Holiday House- Rebekah Harkness’ mansion in the 1950s, central to the most lavish gatherings up until her death in 1982. However, once you look past the entertainment that is posed in painting her neighbour’s dog ‘key lime green’, the themes of prejudiced perception shine through. The chorus refers to the public’s notion of her being ‘the maddest woman this town has ever seen’, highlighting their disapproval of her majestic individuality.

Rebekah Harkness, the second daughter of prominent stockbroker Allen Tarwater and Rebekah Tarwater, was born in 1915, in St. Louis, Missouri. She attended finishing school at Fermata School for Girls in Aiken, South Carolina, with peers from distinguished families like the Roosevelts and the Auchinclosses. However, she was fairly unaffected by the prestige held in the people in her surroundings and set out to accomplish her goal as recorded in her journal, to ‘do everything bad’. This was an excerpt from the journal of a teenager ridden with angst, anger, and sadness after facing every form of neglect from her parents. She was raised by a nanny who was hired just because of her professional precedence as an employee in a psychiatric institution under the belief that she would be able to ‘cure’ Rebekah’s wild ways. Rebekah would labour endlessly at mastering the art of ballet, eventually falling in love with the art but failing to accomplish her goal to impress her emotionally immovable parents.

Merriam-Webster’s dictionary defines hysteria as “a psychoneurosis marked by emotional excitability and disturbances of the psychogenic, sensory, vasomotor, and visceral functions”. It is a purely medical term that has its roots in one of the most infuriating philosophies, the doctrines of which have found their way into today’s colloquy and the world’s perception of all Rebekah's. The term hysteria is derived from the Greek word ‘hystera’ which directly translates to ‘uterus’. The inner workings of the uterus were theorized by an array of male philosophers including the famed Plato, Hippocrates, and Aristotle, all of whom believed that the womb was something of an ‘animal alive inside an animal’, causing damage to the fragile female psyche. Known to be the seat for the female mind’s evil, madness, and weakness, it was an imminent threat to the egocentric patriarchal societal structure. Hysterike pnix, meaning hysterical suffocation, was a disease that was said to cause erratic behaviour in women, causing them to rebel and emote strange and vivid outbursts. The medical evidence put forth by Argonauto Melampus to support this theory was that virgins of the Greek city of Argo were insane for refusing to partake in sexual activity and that ‘madness’ festered in their uteruses. Emotions were said to saturate and rot inside the uterus causing emotional outbursts and the only ‘cure’ to this was constant pregnancy to ‘keep the uterus busy’. This was a theory supported by the Greek physician Galen and the French physician Auguste Fabre who believed that all women carried the ‘rotting seeds of hysteria’ within them.

This theory put forth that a woman who was expressive beyond her faithfulness to any man in her life was ridden with madness and that, unfortunately and repulsively, has seeped its way into today and not as an abstract essence of that theory. A woman, today, in 2021, who so much as shakes her fist while making a point is at a very common risk of being called ‘crazy’ or ‘overdramatic’. A very universally relatable case in point can be found in every heterosexual teen romance featuring an American high school. While two boys wearing letterman jackets getting into a fistfight over a girl is considered cute and protective, two girls getting into a similar fight is a preview to the “walking through the hallway with everyone whispering about the ‘catfight’' montage. This article is not to condone violence, it is to say that the ability to cause it has nothing to do with an empty uterus. Rebekah did not clean her pool with Dom Perignon because she wasn’t pregnant. Whatever her reason may be, she was answerable to no one for spending her fortune and should not have been reprimanded for being a woman living on her terms in luxury. Taylor Swift draws comparisons to herself and the socialite outcast in terms of her public image being tainted from the fight against Scooter Brawn and her old record label, for having relationships that were targeted to quantify her integrity down to writing music as a teenager navigating her way into adulthood under everyone’s microscope. Her ability to defend herself with a steady hand was echoed by the louder screams of those calling her angry, dramatic, and sensitive as though they were the wrong responses to the horrors she faced.

“And you’ll poke that bear till her claws come out/ And you find something to wrap your noose around/ And there’s nothing like a mad woman” - Hysteria used to be a medical concept that is not a part of Western medicine. It was exclusive to women because barring witchcraft, it was the only explanation they could think of for a woman being opinionated and knowledgeable of the fact that she was hers and no one else’s. Today, science has come a way long enough to have drawn a clear line between a condition affecting mental health and the simple access to one’s ability to express opinions. The only thing left to do is open our eyes and see which side of it we’re standing on.
