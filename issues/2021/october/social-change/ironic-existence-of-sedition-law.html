The Ironic Existence of the Sedition Law

  • Social Change   16/10/2021   Karen Kaur

The question often arises, "Why is India using the same law that the British used to curb the voices of our freedom fighters, to suppress criticism or to eliminate national threat?” To answer this question, we must go over the provisions of the law, its origin, use/misuse, interpretation and precedents.

Section 124 A. of the Indian Penal Code states the Sedition Law of India, and says, "Whoever by word, either spoken or written, or representation, by signs, visible or otherwise, brings an attempts to bring into hatred or contempt, or exciter on attempts to excite disaffection towards the Government established by law in India, shall be punished with imprisonment for life, to which fine may be added, or with imprisonment to which may extend to three fine may be added, or with fine."

Sedition is a cognizable offence, which means, a police officer has the authority to make an arrest without a warrant, and to start an investigation with or without the permission of the court. Such offences are grave because they are non-compoundable offences, that is, the accused is not allowed to be freed with some settlement. It is the "state" that files the case, so the question of settlement does not arise. Under sedition, the granting of bail cannot be claimed as a matter of right;it is a matter of discretion.

A very important factor in the incorporation of Section 124A in the IPC is the suggestion of James Fitzwilliam Stephen. He provided five specific heads to the crime of sedition:

(1) To excite disaffection against the King, Government or Constitution, or against Parliament or the Administration of Justice.

(2) To promote, by unlawful means, any alteration in church or the state.

(3) To incite a disturbance of the peace.

(4) To raise discontent among the King's subjects.

(5) To excite class hatred.

Stephen's version is a much broader definition than that provided in section 124A. The latter limits sedition to exciting disaffection towards the Government established by law, which is only a part of the first item on Stephen’s list.

Any law is not only limited to its provisions, but also its application and interpretation. Therefore, it is important to understand past case judgments around Section 124A. The Allahabad High Court declared the sedition law void, and the Punjab High Court struck down the law, but in 1962, a Supreme Court judgment brought back the law, justifying that it only applies if there is "incitement to violence."

In Tara Singh Gopi Chand vs. the State, the judgment said that there can be no dispute that Section 124 A is a restriction on the freedom of speech and expression which is guaranteed to all citizens by Clause (1) of Article 19 of the Constitution. The question is whether the section is redeemed by Clause (2). India is a sovereign democratic state; governments may resign and be caused to step down without the foundations of the state being impaired. A law of sedition established during the period of foreign rule has become inappropriate by the very nature of the change which has come about. The only conclusion must be that Section 124A of the Penal Code has become void in contravening the right to freedom of speech and expression guaranteed by Article 19 of the Constitution.

The right to spread disaffection against the Government or any other person is included in the right to freedom of speech and expression guaranteed by the constitution; if Article 19 (1) (a) did not exist, one could not say that an Indian has the right to spread disaffection against the government.

While the courts of India think that the law should only be limited to incitement of violence , the use of the law by law enforcement agencies shows otherwise. From 2016 to 2019, the number of cases filed against Section 124A of the Indian Penal Code increased by 160 % according to the data provided by the National Crime Record Bureau.

As per the NCRB report, among the cases under trial, twenty-one closed due to insufficient evidence and six were found to be dismissed as Civil cases. Out of 96 cases under sedition charges in 2019, only two men have actually been convicted. The Supreme Court has asked law enforcement agencies on numerous occasions to not misuse IPC Section 124 A to curb freedom of speech and expression, and has directed the state to follow guidelines provided in the Kedarnath vs. State of Bihar trial.

The irony here is that while the Indian government is misusing the section of 124A IPC which was brought in by the British to curb dissent by their ancestors in the colonial era, the United Kingdom itself has abolished the law. In 1977, the Law Commission said that the law is redundant, and that it is not necessary to have any offence of sedition. The law commission recommended the common law offence if sedition in England and Wales be abolished. Sedition was abolished in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland by Section 73 of the Coroners and Justice Act 2009, and in Scotland by Section 51 of Criminal Justice and Licensing Act of 2010.

India is the world's largest democracy, and like any other, it is necessary to have criticism and dialogue in order to keep the spirit of democracy alive. The question we addressed was if Section 124A of the Indian Penal falls under the ambit of Article 19 (b) of the Indian Constitution, and my conclusion is that observing the current trend of the use of the sedition law, we have to notice the scope it leaves in its interpretation for misuse and curbing of dissent beyond "reasonable restrictions" of Article 19 (a). We must acknowledge that the crimes of seditious libel, words, and such can be dealt with under general provisions addressing hate speech.

Even though the law leaves the door open for the government to curb dissent, in the current scenario it will be unrealistic to imagine the scrapping of Section 124 of the Indian Penal Code. Therefore, a more feasible solution would be as follows:

• Mens Rea be incorporated in the Act, which is the person's intention to commit a crime, or the knowledge that one's actions or lack of action would cause a crime to be committed as per the section.

• The scope of this section should be widened, incorporating the Constitution of India, Legislatures and Administration of Justice (Judiciary).

• Bridging the odd gap between imprisonment for life, imprisonment which may extend to three years, or fines, by fixing the maximum imprisonment for sedition at seven years of rigorous imprisonment.
