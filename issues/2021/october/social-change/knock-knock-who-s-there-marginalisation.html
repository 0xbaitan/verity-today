Knock Knock, Who’s There? Marginalisation.

    • Social Change   16/10/2021   Pragya Roy Barman

    Cornered away from the rest of India lie the seven states of Arunachal Pradesh, Assam, Manipur, Meghalaya, Mizoram, Nagaland, and Tripura, collectively known as the north-east region of India. They have, for years, hosted the largest diversity of the indigenous population in India, along with some of Asia’s most radicalised militant societies. However, as my introduction suggests, Northeast India is not only geographically isolated but also socially and politically underreported, leaving the people of these states voiceless and vulnerable. Often, they are the butt of insensitive, racist and derogatory remarks that enforce the marginalisation they have been subjected to for all these years.

    A lot of this unrest stems from the fact that the Northeast region was not part of pre-colonial India, and only came to be integrated in the year 1826 with the help of the Treaty of Yandaboo. Owing to the fact that it was initially seen as a buffer zone between India and China, or Burma, military enforcement was deployed and large militarised infrastructure was built. This sidelined the existing tribal communities and their political and social ideologies in order to cater to the nation’s interest, thus ensuing a never-ending conflict.

    To retain control over these lands, the government enacted the Armed Force Special Powers Act 1958 or AFSPA which gave authority to the armed forces to arrest, search, or conduct encounters without a warrant, an act which is in effect over certain areas to date and continue to be imposed by the government whenever they feel threatened. This causes the common people to feel like they are losing their autonomy in their own homes, giving birth to infamous armed rebel groups and militant societies, while violence continues to be a part of their everyday lives.

    In Meghalaya, one of the first militant organisations was Hynniewtrep Achik Liberation Council (HALC), formed in the 1980s with the objective of countering the influx of ‘dkhars’ or outsiders in their territories. They later split into two separate societies - Hynniewtrep National Liberation Council or HNLC, which represented the Khasi and the Jaintia communities, and Achik National Volunteers Council or ANVC, which stood for the Garo community. The former was headed by Cherishterfiels Thangkhiew as the founding general secretary and was governed by the Khasi political ideology that demanded independence from India.

    Thangkhiew died in a police operation on August 18th, 2021, which sparked violence in the state, resulting in the deployment of central armed forces, a curfew, and even the suspension of internet services. The Meghalaya police claim that he had become active in the last six months of his life after years of dormancy, and that they hold proof of his contributions in the low-intensity blasts that occurred in East Jaintia Hills in July, and in Shillong in early August. Additionally, they allegedly received a tip that Thangkhiew was planning another blast and decided to act on it before any further violence could take place.

    The police define the incidents as such - Thangkhiew tried to escape the police by attacking them with a knife, and in defence the police had to shoot him, causing his death. On the other hand, the local residents believe that this was a fabrication, and his own family accuse the police of murder. Demands for his justice have taken the city by storm with more than hundreds of people forming a funeral procession, while other groups put up banners of a “black flag day”. However, not all that followed Thangkhiew’s death was peaceful, as the city of Shillong became wrecked by vandalism, arson, and pelting.

    While this might portray public support for the cause of HNCL, what is happening goes much deeper. The citizens are exhausted from being the sacrificial lambs for the gain of the state government. Their plights have been ignored during the lockdown, while illegal resource extraction seems to be carrying on. This, along with the “encounter” as they believe it happened, turned the militant leader into a vigilante, and what the people demand justice for is much greater than one death - they want to be free from being sidelined as puppets in their own land, and they want justice for the extortion that they are facing. They want representation. They want acceptance. And above all, they want to own their land, and not live in fear of the next authoritarian development they will be subjected to.
