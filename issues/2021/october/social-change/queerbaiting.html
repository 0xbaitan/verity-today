Queerbaiting — A Sin Or A Sign?

  • Social Change   16/10/2021   Priyanka Banerjee

Queerbaiting is a marketing technique used by artists or creative editors to hint at the assumed queerness of a particular character. It is aimed at attracting the queer audience but does not actually depict any positive LGBTQ+ representation. It is a tactic used by artists to appeal to the masses and coerce the LGBTQ+ community into consuming any product by feigning inclusivity and solidarity.

To a large number of people, the very existence of queerbaiting is more a positive sign of progress than a problem. The fact that it is even an issue with the queer audience can apparently be taken as a sign of improvement because “producers would not be accused of queerbaiting in the first place if LGBTQ+ representation had not progressed” and “ten to fifteen years ago, even this representation would have been a victory for queer people.” However, the difference between then and now is that the queer audience is no longer desperate for unreal hints at one’s supposed, possible queerness. Queerbaiting cannot be considered any representation at all, only a capitalist marketing ploy which appropriates queer culture for popularity. When we talk about queerbaiting on television, the problem is not a character being confused about their sexuality or not being prepared to ‘come out’. The problem occurs when a character’s almost-queerness becomes the makers’ calculated strategy to misleadingly attract the queer audience without actually satisfying any of their expectations.

Storylines that have been accused of queerbaiting include Betty and Veronica’s relationship from the popular teen drama Riverdale, where the audience expected some queer development in the story after they kissed, and not for them to go back to their happy heterosexual relationships. Dumbledore and Grindelwald’s relationship from the Harry Potter series has often been cited as an example of queerness since this development regarding Dumbledore’s sexuality was addressed by the author, J.K. Rowling, much later and it didn’t create any representation or even any changes in the actual story. The popular ship Destiel from the television series Supernatural is also considered to be a well-known instance of queerbaiting where the interactions between Dean Winchester and Castiel are consistently suggestive of sexual attraction and hint at a same-sex relationship, but fail to deliver any real representation.

What many people might wonder is: how is queerbaiting really harmful? Even if it doesn’t confirm a character’s queerness, it shows something, and that’s better than nothing, right? Well, not really. Imagine a closeted teenager having feelings they are unsure about, which they have been taught aren’t natural. To a person in this scenario who is looking for something to validate the reality and normalcy of their feelings, watching a relationship on-screen that reflects their feelings but isn’t actually queer only makes them believe that their feelings aren’t real and valid either. The relief this person would feel for even a moment is invalidated the moment the character on-screen becomes heterosexual and cisgender as a marketing ploy, and not a reflection of a real queer person. Imagine someone out and proud, reading something because it’s been advertised to trick that person into believing this includes real representation. This person reads about a character they believe is a part of their own community and finds comfort in the fact that someone has taken care to represent them, only to find said character settling in a happy heterosexual relationship with no further mention made of this possible queerness. This furthers their feeling of being alone and makes this person, along with thousands of other queer people, realise that this character was not queer at all, only part of a gratuitous storyline that tricks us into feeling invested. A little queer experimentation, done and dusted, and that’s really all the representation offered.

Not only does queerbaiting negatively impact the queer massses who are trying to find reflections of themselves and their community in media, it also makes it seem to the rest of the world that queerness cannot really be portrayed correctly in most media, even in 2021. Queer representation has almost always been inadequate and inaccurate in popular culture. In such a case, queerbaiting only furthers the idea that queerness is a ‘phase’ or a ‘special case’. By calling queerbaiting ‘queer representation’, we put them in the same group as media that is actually real enough to address queerness satisfactorily. This tactic furthers the idea that heteronormativity is more ‘acceptable’ and more profitable which becomes a hindrance to media actually attempting to normalise queerness.

People fought for the right to identify with their sexuality and continue to do so. Even the more privileged of us in the community are aware of living in a society that continues to work against us and to deprive us of very basic human rights. Queerness is the ground reality of several people and to sacrifice that representation to earn higher profits does a huge disservice to the queer people and the queer community who had to work a lot harder than most to achieve the bare minimum of human rights.
