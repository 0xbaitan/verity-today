Reviewing the Efficacy of ‘Nightlight Data’

  • The Affairs   16/10/2021

Many economists are using ‘nightlight data’ to measure the economic activities of different countries. The data provides a numerical measure of the brightness of the earth in the night captured by satellites. This data allows different countries with weak statistics to estimate various social and economic indicators at different intervals. For instance, in India the difference between 2012 and 2016, India glowed up and the GDP from 5.456% to 8.256%. Some say it is the best substitute but the main drawback of luminosity is that it captured everything which gives it the tag of ‘’just a rough approximation” of economic activities. Like shadow economy, also called underground, informal or parallel economy- it needs electricity as a physical input for illegal activities and for production of legal goods and services whose generated income is unreported. luminosity doesn’t cover agricultural activities which make it a much rougher estimate as 45% of the world population relies on agriculture for its livelihood. Many argue it reveals urbanization, sectorial distribution and human well-being measurement.

Apart from the economic growth, the difference between South Korea and North Korea tells a different story. One country is shining and the other is just sitting in dark as a result of democracy and de-facto absolute monarchy. Not just the growth, but also lay bare about the government spending. All resources spent on army strength, weapon system darkens the country whereas investment in capital, education light it up. Government influence matters a lot as it determines what sectors of the economy show progress. However, claiming that all undemocratic countries are poor is irrelevant. The absolute monarchic counties like Qatar, Oman, Vatican City making a lot of money. So, what is the benchmark for calling a country dark? Certainly not the political rule.

Meanwhile, countries like South Sudan are in gloom because of the lack of literacy, healthcare, food security. Burundi narrates its story as a continuous cycle of violence and war that had been a major determinant for them. Whereas Somalia is rooted in civil conflict same as Syria with limited resources, natural disasters and a lack of an active central government. War, violence and terrorism serve the economy with a negative effect by creating direct economic destruction, uncertainty in the market, affecting tourism and most important affecting the human resource of the country. Still, it is not the benchmark but it can be a reason.

In conclusion, the clear light up world map tells different stories for different countries but nightlight is not the magic fix for the statistics. The figure from 2012 and 2016 clearly indicates that urbanization for many countries still leaves many suffering from poverty. As the gap between rich and poor is visible but, there is a lot more to the story which is far away from reach looking from outer space- hidden under the books of government or in the eyes of the sufferers. To summarise, the night light is simply a visual representation and we cannot judge a book by its cover.
