Intricate Italian Politics:
An overview during the Renaissance

  • The Affairs   16/10/2021

Renaissance ushered in the utopian concept of humanism: focusing on the roots of artistic antiquities and the revival of classical forms of philosophy, literature and art. While the hotly contested debate on its origins has still not been put to rest, it is generally believed to have begun in Italy during the 14th century. The problem that concerned historians was with the clustering of so many creative geniuses, as seen in Greece and Rome. The Humanist, Leonardo Bruni affirmed politics to be the root of this problem. According to him, the decline of the Roman Republic also meant the collapse of Roman culture. Italy, during this period, was categorised into a number of city-states and kingdoms and if it weren't for these subdivisions, the Renaissance wouldn't have taken place.

During the Middle Ages, most peasants were serfs- individuals tied by law to the land they worked. By the late 1400s, however, serfdom was declining throughout Europe and peasants were freer to move about and to rent farms for themselves. Thousands of peasants migrated to urban areas in search of jobs, and cities and towns swelled in size. As populations grew, the demand for food skyrocketed. Meanwhile, the new freedom of peasants meant that landowners had to pay more for their labour. These developments made goods more expensive and caused market inflation across Europe. The combination of rising prices and an increase in the number of people needing goods and services encouraged merchants to expand their businesses.

The trade carried out by the prosperous city-states facilitated the transmission of culture, goods and services and philosophies from the East. Florence became the centre for finance and Luxury goods (such as spices, dyes, and silks) bought in the Levant, were imported to Italy only to be resold throughout the rest of Europe. The rising merchant classes gained complete autonomy of the city-states. This led to increased security, as those who were inherently wealthier risked a chance of having their lands confiscated.

The most important social change brought by new economic growth was the rise of a new merchant class. They were an exclusive business elite, making up about 2% of residents in the great Italian trading cities. Many became enormously wealthy, built palaces, funded popes and princes. Italian Politics, at around this time, was manoeuvred by the House of Medici who had absolute control over Florence. They were instrumental in the development of the Renaissance, by encouraging the growth of art and humanism. They sponsored and commissioned artists to make fine pieces of art which allowed them the liberty to work without the constraints of funds. The House of Medici, along with other families of Italy, such as the Visconti, the Este of Ferrara, and the Gonzaga of Mantua, fostered the birth of the Italian Renaissance, an event historically credited for opening up new areas of commerce, art and polity.
