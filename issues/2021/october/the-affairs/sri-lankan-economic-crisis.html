Sri Lankan Economic Crisis

  • The Affairs   16/10/2021

A Casualty of the Covid-19 Pandemic, the Sri Lankan Economy Crumbles as the country Grapples with a ‘Food Emergency’. With empty shelves in supermarkets and pharmacies, it is safe to say that Sri Lanka has a crisis on its already heavy shoulders. On Monday, September 6, the Sri Lankan Parliament ratified the national emergency announced by President Gotabaya Rajapaksa on August 30. The nation’s economic crisis is multi-faceted, including a tangle of internal and external causes that vary in intensity and are interwoven.

During the pandemic, the Sri Lankan government, like many other developing countries, had to choose between protecting the jobs of its citizens and saving their lives. To deal with the significant spikes in cases, many regions of the nation have been under intermittent curfews since March 2020. As hospitals struggled to cope with the more aggressive Delta strain, a countrywide lockdown was re-imposed on August 20. Even though such restrictions are credited with limiting the virus' spread, they have undeniably resulted in a loss of livelihood for many Sri Lankans, particularly those who work in the informal sector.

The restriction on imports imposed by the Sri Lankan government has had the most significant direct impact on ordinary citizens, resulting in shortages in supermarkets and pharmacies. Over the last year, import prohibitions, which Sri Lankans have been closely and unwillingly acquainted with, are simply a symptom of a far broader macroeconomic crisis. The ongoing Covid-19 pandemic has worsened an existing economic crisis and produced a countrywide shortage of vital supplies, prompting the military to be deployed into the streets to prevent civilians from stockpiling food as of September 16.

Food essentials such as rice, milk, and cereals have tripled in price in some parts of the country due to high inflation. Sri Lanka had one of Asia's fastest-growing economies before the Covid-19 epidemic, but that growth was based on borrowing money from other countries. Sri Lanka owes debtors, mainly China, about £24 billion in 2021.

Another factor contributing to the nation’s collapsing economy is the state of the Tourism sector, which accounts for more than ten per cent of the country's GDP, which flourished pre-pandemic and acted as a significant source of foreign income and provided employment. What is important to note is that even though the country re-opened to tourists in January this year, the restrictions and stringent rules, although necessary, have caused the tourism sector to barely resume its operations.

Money transfers sent home by Sri Lankans working overseas, notably in the Middle East, have also been affected by employment losses caused by the pandemic, particularly among blue-collar employees. According to agricultural experts, the prohibition on chemical fertilisers imposed in March, when President Rajapaksa stated that the country will grow exclusively organic food from now on, making it the first country to do so, may exacerbate the scarcity. The decision was made to save foreign money on fertiliser imports. Still, it is believed that the abrupt shift in the middle of a crop, without sufficient soil preparation, may harm vegetable and rice harvests.

Hence, on August 30, to forestall a bailout from the International Monetary Fund, the Sri Lankan Government officially announced an economic emergency and halted the import of over 600 essential products, including grains, starches, and fruits. This decision, however, appears to have only further worsened the situation and caused inflation to increase significantly.

The United Nations has warned that the Covid-19 epidemic would cause food shortages in many impoverished nations, which is a reason for alarm. Food shortages have already occurred in cities across China and India during lockdowns. Supply lines have been affected in several African countries, including Sudan, where costs of necessities have increased by 50%.
