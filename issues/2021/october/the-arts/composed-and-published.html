““As by the dead we love to sit, become so wondrous dear-”

Emily sat by the window of her childhood home, The Homestead, scribbling away about a life that she did not know, one that she could not achieve from travelling the seas or picket fences, both of which she had access to. She looked out the window at the flowers and trees she so dearly learnt about in botany reminiscing over Sarah, Eliza, Emeline, Harriet and Susan, whom she would cherish in her art like pressed flowers in old pages of lines highlighted and margins scrawled. Looking into her life through that very window, she never lived in this world in the first place. Her world had one gateway, one road and one destination- death and the air smelled of strife and love. She was so consumed with the beauty and the realism in the concept of mortality that she wrote her life into it and around it.

Today, a hundred and thirty-five years after greeting the friend she longed to know most about, we forget that Emily Dickinson, before an established poet, was a person. She was a daughter, a sister, a friend and an adversary to the mundane cycle of life and death. She cared for those in her vicinity and she gathered the knowledge and kindled the passion that set fire to all that she thought humanity felt. More than that, she destroyed what she thought she was to feel. Her link to Death was so strong that despite the fact that she wrote almost 1800 poems in her long lifetime, she only ever published ten titled ones. The rest of the plethora of her poems were published by her sister Lavinia ‘Vinnie’ Dickinson after Emily’s death to be represented by the dates of composition and publication.

These poems were the different people inside her mind- a dominating female protagonist, a person in disdain of herself for not being lovesick with a person but with the feared Carriage, a memory and nostalgia, Nobody and a Funeral. She wrote these poems as her own self. She used analogies and words and names that only she would understand because they were not meant for everyone to relate to. They were meant to draw a picture in her head, a picture of the life beyond death, the life before death and the line of death between existence and the lack thereof. These pieces not only articulate how deeply she thought of everything, it was evidence that she was far from the general character of morbidity that has been pinned on her, owing to the most superficial opinion of Death- her protagonist.

She loved deeply and she knew the importance of life due to having lived the wonders of it, no matter how confusing and painful they may have been. In a poem she composed in 1858, which was later published in 1914, she writes about a sister she has in her house, referring to Lavinia and “One, a hedge away.” She goes on to describe how she sang a different tune and her hum was still built into their hearts the same way the star she chose for her was etched into the sky. The subject of this poem was Susan Gilbert, her sister-in-law who had become her closest and most fiercely loved friend. She expressed that her friends were more worthy of love and adoration than she herself and that her life belonged to solidarity and solidarity to her. She revels in the gift of recollection and memory and emphasizes on the ‘word that bears a sword’- forgot over and over again because to her, recollecting had to be forgetting, missing had to be merry and mourning had to be gay because only then would the fingers that were writing and collecting her life be worth all the answers she was yet to receive.

She walked lightly, trying not to let the weight of the world on her shoulders crush the earth beneath her feet. She sat by death, letting her sorrow wash past her onto that ground because death is a silent friend, one who will listen and never look at you like they see only the horror in your mind. They have seen everything everywhere and have never been received with open arms and kind eyes. They look into the terror in the trse, accept the permanence of the past and try to reassure beauty in the indefinite. She found comfort in the idea that Death might be a wandering soul, looking for friends, leading others down their paths only to be sent away with Their head hung low. She looked at the world through Their eyes and saw that the colours she saw herself were not too different. She wrote the circle of her life and the lives of the flowers and bees around her in those colours in hopes that when Death finally knocked on her window, that very window at The Homestead, she would give those words to her sad and lonely friend, smile kindly and let herself be helped onto the Carriage that hold but ourselves and Immortality.
