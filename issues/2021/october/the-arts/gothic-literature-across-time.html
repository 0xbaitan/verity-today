Gothic Literature: An Overview

  • The Arts   16/10/2021   Anoushka Dasgupta

From Frankenstein to The Twilight Saga, ‘Gothic’ as a genre spans across decades of twisted tales and supernatural occurrences, embedded in the intricacies of the human mind. Immortal protagonists are woven into stories of fantasy and tragedy, and have evolved from ‘damsels in distress’ to headstrong teenagers. In short, Gothic literature employs increasingly fascinating themes to explore the dark side of human existence, and has transformed substantially since its origin.

The word ‘Gothic’, which stems from Germanic tribes of the same name associated with barbaric and primitive connotations, is often used in the context of architecture - conjuring images of sharp arches, billowing buttresses, crumbling cathedrals and cryptic vaults. However, in Literature, the word ‘Gothic’ symbolises an illicit world of the supernatural, often coupled with grotesque, gory images and a romanticised-yet-horrific atmosphere. Gothic literature essentially uses fear and paranoia as the primary mood, with either male villains or femme fatales as the main antagonists. It also often uses supernatural elements like ghosts, spirits, witches or even a manipulated human psyche to emphasise on its protagonist's (mostly pale and thin virgin women) helplessness. Madness and desperation coupled with a certain degree of peculiarity is characteristic of this genre, which includes fictional places and ironically, Gothic style castles as the primary setting.

Although several commonplace myths and folklore can be attributed to having typical Gothic characteristics, the formal origin of this genre is credited to Horatio Walpole’s 18th Century novel The Castle of Otranto (1764), which displays a host of Gothic characteristics; from the setting (a haunted mansion) to an ancestral curse - evoking simultaneous feelings of awe and terror. William Beckford’s Vathek (1786) is another novel which goes a step further by displaying more spine chilling scenes like eternal damnation and the sacrificing of children. The Mysteries of Udolpho (1794) and The Italian (1797), both written by Ann Radcliffe, as well as The Monk by Matthew Lewis explore similar themes that grow more sinister and macabre with every page- a characteristic trait of 18th Century Gothic literature.

Some of the most well known pieces of literature of the 19th Century contain distinct gothic undertones as well. Take Dracula, for example, which includes not only a decrepit, haunting mansion, but a supernatural element which is filtered in through its eerie owner. Written by Abraham Stroker in 1897, the fictional setting of Transylvania, the usage of the art of seduction to lure victims and the unexplained mysterious events gives rise to one of the most classic pieces of Gothic literature in history.

The work of the Bronte sisters, too, can be observed as containing typical Gothic elements, especially Emily Bronte’s iconic novel Wuthering Heights, which gave us one of the longest standing self-destructive Byronic heroes - in the figure of the tormented Heathcliff. Published in 1847, it deals with the protagonist's inner turmoil of living in a patriarchal society and also outlines haunting descriptions of sickening cruelty. Charles Dickens, despite his much later Victorian politics and known for his bitter commentary on a society post industrial revolution also displays common Gothic characteristics in his book Oliver Twist (1837-38), which focused on the urban tale of an impoverished young boy and employed a more subtle manifestation of the genre through the protagonist’s suffering.

Two other popular names from the genre include The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde (written by Robert Louis Stevenson in 1886) and The Picture of Dorian Gray (written by Oscar Wilde in 1891). While the former is a thriller for the ages and an unmatched exploration of the split personality syndrome, the latter is a spine-chilling reworking of the Faustian legend with deep and abiding queer undertones. The same can be said for Mary Shelley’s Frankenstein (1818) which has thrived through millions of adaptations and features an outlandish monster born of strange experiments and is a definite bridge between the darker side of the Romantic imagination through the lens of the Gothic.

The 20th Century saw a departure from traditional Gothic characteristics. Novels now revolved around an omnipresent air of mystery and suspense, foregrounded with a tragic romance. The best example of this would be Daphne du Morier’s Rebecca (1938) which showed the psychological torment of the protagonist when she was trying to live up to the name of her current lover’s dead wife, Rebecca. With romance as one of the main themes, the trademark ghastliness of the human mind in Gothic literature was also highlighted in this book which also gave a modern spin to the trope of the mad woman in the attic from the other standout Gothic romance Jane Eyre by the eldest of the Bronte sisters.

Some other classic Gothic works of this era include The Phantom of the Opera (1910) by Gaston Leroux, The Shining (1977) by Stephen King and Flowers in the Attic (1979) by V.C Andrews. All of these books contain a nail biting and murky atmosphere drenched in suspense and clouded with a sense of evil.

When it comes to the 21st Century, Gothic undertones have become increasingly popular. Take, for instance, The Book Thief (2005) by Marcus Zusak- it has a distinctly mysterious element throughout it which is expounded by the protagonist’s misery. Despite being the story of a child it has a fascinatingly critical obsession with Death which gets expounded in the novel’s employment of the same figure as the primary narrator. Even in stories like The The Blind Assassin (2000) by Margaret Atwood, one is exposed to a seemingly normal tale until the layers are stripped and an undeniable, dark repugnance is revealed. Some other popular examples of 21st Century Gothic literature include No Country for Old Men (2005) by Cormac McCarthy, The Lovely Bones (2002) by Alice Sebold and The Twilight Saga (2005 to 2020) by Stephanie Mayer. All of these examples have become increasingly significant in pop culture, especially The Twilight Saga, which just goes to show how immortal the Gothic genre has become.

What makes Gothic Literature in general so relevant today is its depiction of the twisted nature of the human mind and its eerie manifestations in the real world. Dark tales of werewolves and vampires are embedded in the folklore of many cultures, and it's through these age-old stories that Gothic Literature has been renewed time and time again. Even today, ‘Gothic’ as a genre has moulded itself to tell new stories through a different lens, and will no doubt continue to evolve and transport readers into a dark world of fantasy for ages to come.
