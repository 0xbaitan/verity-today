Interrogating Queerness in Cinema

    • The Arts   16/10/2021   Dipali Banka

    Edgar Dale said we remember 20% of what we hear and 50% of what we hear and see. We have heard how difficult it is for a member of the queer spectrum to disclose their sexual preference or, as most prefer calling it, coming out in front of the society. This very society who has made life unacceptable and filthy for anyone who is not a binary conforming, cis-gendered heterosexual, is the reason why a member of the spectrum finds themselves in the closet hiding, and even when they are not hiding and are brave, assertive and fierce about themselves, loaded with self-confidence -there comes a point where you just cannot ignore the violence of bigoted natures of people around you - because at the end of the day, we’re all humans with breaking points.

    Considering the impact of the entertainment sector in creating awareness, here is looking at a few titles from recent years, which outline the trials of non-conforming people, lying on the margins of society in the cinema of our times. All four of these films have established commons grounds to display the struggle and distress of a queer individual in different situations which could be avoided if only heteronomaticity was no longer the go-to norm.

    Shubh Mangal Zyada Saavdhan (2020)

    Shubh Mangal Zyada Saavdhan is 2020 romantic-comedy bollywood movie, about a gay couple Karthik and Aman who face a long road of struggles as they stand up against Aman’s family. The movie focuses on the struggles and complications anyone from the spectrum has to face in a non-urban, middle-class brown family. Aman’s family and relatives refer to their sexual orientation as a ‘disease’, however this does not force or intimidate Karthik and he stands strong with the love of his life. This is not the first movie about homosexuality in the Indian mainstream, but it is the first to deal the issue headlong with a leading actor. Its recipe for success? Biting comedy which underlying a bag of very solid and relevant emotions.

    Love, Simon (2018)

    Love, Simon is 2019 romantic-comedy American movie starring Nick Robinson as Simon Spier who is a closeted gay teenager, with a very close family and amazingly supportive friends. He starts communicating with another closeted gay ‘Blue’ via emails, after the latter makes a confession on student-run website. As the two begin a dream high-school romance online, tragedy strikes as Simon gets outed by a fellow classmate and his idyllic world of lies comes crashing down. The movie brings out the distress in the life of an ordinary high schooler due to his sexual orientation, by observing details which are minute and yet mundane, and struggles which are ordinary yet disturbingly palpable.

    Four More Shots Please! (2019)

    Four More Shots Please! is an Amazon Prime series with two seasons directed by Nupur Asthana and Anu Menon. The series revolves around four best friends, among which one identifies as bisexual. Everything goes as per plan, as Umang who works as a fitness trainer begins a torrentus love-affair with one of her clients, until the tabloids leak their relationship, throwing Umang in a very unhealthy and distressful situation. In a series that looks at urban female friendships, the struggles faced by the character of Umang are endearing in their honesty and reality - as an economically and sexually independant queer woman.

    The Half of It (2020)

    The Half of It, a Netflix movie written and directed by Alice Wu is an American comedy-drama which also received the Founders Award for Best Narrative Feature at Tribeca Film Festival. The story is about a young high schooler Ellie Chu, who earns extra money by ghost writing for her fellow classmates and is approached by a footballer- Paul, to write a love letter to Aster, his girl crush at their school. This begins a soulful correspondence between Elli, now posing as Paul, and Aster - taking the former on a journey of sexual awakening. the film succeeds not only as a queer teenage drama, but also for foregorudned and brough to life Asian characters in a narrative mostly hijakced by white American and British characters.
