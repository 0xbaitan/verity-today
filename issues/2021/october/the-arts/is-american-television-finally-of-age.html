Is American Television Finally Coming of Age?

  • The Arts   16/10/2021   Priyanka Banerjee

“And that’s life. And it’s beautiful and it’s messy. It’s a beautiful mess but you can’t cling to what was. You have to look at what might be.” -Meredith Grey, Grey’s Anatomy

On 27th March, 2005, the first episode of the first season of Grey’s Anatomy aired in the United States and no one could have imagined that in 2021, over 15 million people in the world would be eagerly waiting for its eighteenth season. This single longest-running primetime medical drama has only gained immense popularity over the last sixteen years, a fact that owes to how well the characters and storylines have evolved with television. The evolution of television over nearly the last two decades can be observed by looking at how this one show that continues to be one of the most popular and loved shows airing on television even today, has changed drastically over time. The demands of viewers of shows as popular as Grey’s Anatomy have changed considerably since 2005 and continue to change rapidly. From introducing the world to the ‘dark and twisty’ intern, Meredith Grey (played by Ellen Pompeo), as she opened up to McDreamy (played by Patrick Dempsey) with the good hair and a secret wife, to showing us issues regarding politics, gender, race and sexuality being addressed realistically on-screen, Grey’s has come a long way, fulfilling popular demands, breaking up our favourite couples, and raising a hue and cry for a variety of socio-political issues.

The show started sixteen years ago as your everyday medical drama with a white blonde protagonist in love with her boss, Derek Shepherd. It included a predominantly white, cisgender and heterosexual class with a heterosexual romance at the heart of the narrative. However, even though the main content of the show was suited to a medical drama over a decade old, its casting was still more diverse than most primetime shows in 2005. James Pickens Jr., who played the Chief of Surgery, Richard Webber, on the show, said that he had never seen an African-American in such a role before, while the network executives had intended Cristina Yang’s character to be played by a white woman; however, it was made legendary by the Asian-American actor, Sandra Oh. But in spite of including African-Americans and Asian-Americans in its cast, the show was practically colour-blind and hardly addressed any issues regarding race or other socially relevant issues. Nonetheless, its huge commercial success and immense popularity even when it first aired goes to show that its content and casting met the demands of the audience of the time.

As the show progressed, its storylines started creating more space for diversity in characters and accurate representation. That the show received little to no backlash for further diversifying its cast and characters and continued to remain one of the most popular shows of all time goes to show how the demands of the viewers were changing over the years. Sara Ramirez’s Calliope Torres became the longest-running queer character in the history of US television and the character’s bisexuality also marked one of the earliest series regular queer rioles on primetime television. Ramirez’s character addressed the conflict that often arises with coming out in a very religious Catholic family as Callie’s family cuts her off and abandons her soon after she reveals her sexuality. With the arrival of Arizona Robbins (played by Jessica Capshaw), television’s longest-running lesbian character, in season five of the series, we also get to see a rare case of a happy queer person on television, filled with warmth and sunshine, and not necessarily going through the stereotypical sexuality crisis. Her arrival also birthed one of the show’s most popular ships, Calzona, which went on to become the longest-running same-sex relationship on network television.

In recent seasons, the show has not only added more diverse characters to its main cast but it has also used its platform to voice the truth about social issues. It has dealt with the American epidemic of drug addiction through the character of Amelia Shepherd, a woman who, despite undeniable talent, is belittled, never taken seriously by her seemingly very accomplished family and constantly made to question herself and her skill. It addressed racism by showing the terror with which people of colour live in the US everyday, particularly highlighting the lack of proper access to healthcare for people of colour in its most recent season which dealt with the Covid-19 crisis and its disproportionate impact on people of colour. The show also addressed the issue of racism and police brutality in season 14 through the story of a young African-American boy who was shot fatally by a police officer for no justifiable reason. In season 18, the show also commented on the murders of George Floyd and Breonna Taylor and its aftermath in terms of the Black Lives Matter (BLM) movement. It focuses on sexism and sexual assault in season 15, particularly during the incredibly powerful episode of the story of a woman who was raped in a bar and was too scared to ask for help in fear that she will not get any justice and that her marriage will fall apart as a result. The issue of domestic violence is addressed in the same season through Jo Wilson’s character and her past relationship with a famous but abusive husband whom she had to change her identity to escape from. It also talked about the state of the healthcare system during the COVID-19 pandemic in its last season and the toll the pandemic has taken, not only on doctors all around the world but also on the general population.

The first batch of interns, which comprised the main cast of the show, were 80% white and entirely cisgender and heterosexual people. Now, the latest batch of interns includes Grey’s first homosexual man, Levi Schmitt; a transgender person, Casey Parker; a Muslim woman, Dahlia Qadri; and Reza Khan, a vascular surgeon in Pakistan who was forced to repeat his surgical internship after migrating to the US to practice medicine. The Meredith Grey who started off as a doe-eyed intern having an illicit affair has now become one of the most empowered women and one of the best surgeons on the show on her own merit and skill. Moreover, she has evolved into a spokesperson and activist fighting for radical change both within and outside the healthcare system. Going back to watch an episode from season one now shows a distinct change in the content of the show, the casting, the topics at the focus of the show and so on, a change which reflects how the demands of the current generation are very different from what was popular a decade ago. Grey’s Anatomy proves that television series can be popular while simultaneously being politically correct and remaining relevant to its present time, serving an audience which is finally demanding on-screen representation of marginalised communities and addressal of the most basic human rights issues. While the show has always adhered to the demands of the audience to remain one of the most popular television series of all time, it has also always been ahead of its times, not holding on to what was but choosing to look, and fight for, what might be.
