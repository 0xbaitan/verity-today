Kurosawa’s Ran (1984): The Eternal Folly of Men (Or God?)

  • The Arts   16/10/2021   Pradymn Nahata

“It is not titles that honour men,
but men that honour titles”
Niccolò Machiavelli

When I was torrenting Akira Kurosawa’s 1984 war film Ran, the movie seemed like an enigmatic feast for the cinephile: the still shots painted it as a majestic masterwork. Now, after I watched it, it was still very much in the proximity of a masterwork, but there was a seismic shift in my perception of the film. All the characters were massively fallible: they were either too proud, too stupid or too devious. Kurosawa had taken characters who honoured themselves to the fullest, and had put them all into a mess characterized by suffering, karma and blood.

The film is set in the ‘Sengoku’ period in the 16th century, and revolves around ‘Great Lord’ Hidetora Ichimonji and his three sons: Jiro, Taro and Saburo. After a fine hunt on a pleasant day, as his sons and his consigliere’s feast together, Hidetora announces that he will abdicate his throne in favour of his eldest offspring Taro. Jiro accepts this without much thought but Saburo objects - and is banished from the kingdom. While Taro is assumed to be a righteous successor and Jiro a loyal brother, both quickly undergo sleights of heart and betray their father in a desperate civil war to conquer the kingdom. Then follows a war sequence that has gone down in the history of cinema as an iconic blend of colour, conquest and chaos.

As the Great Lord observes heartless bloodshed around himself and is debilitated by the abandonment of his trusted sons, he loses all mental capacity and is soon rendered senile. He wanders through the ridges and plains of an expansive countryside, against the storm, followed by his entertainer and his commander, shouting ‘Where am I?’ and displaying pathological shock at meeting the sons and daughters of those he slaughtered when young. He becomes a true mad-man, a mere puppet of a protagonist. Kurosawa, through the futile movements and sheer shock of the bereaved Great Lord, explicitly reminds the viewers how truly vain and fragile life is, or can be. We all identify ourselves with titles and accomplishments, but they are truly impertinent to what happens when the waves of the stormy sea crash against the abstract rocks of wind we dig into floors of sand - to project ourselves as those worthy of worship and honour. If mountains and cities have become naught, what is a pristine king?

Jiro eventually kills Taro and is romanced by the elder’s wife (Lady Kaede, who has a meticulous plan of her own) while already in ‘possession’ of a lady (Lady Sue). A significant background detail to these marriages would be that The ‘Great Lord’ had killed both Lady Sue and Lady Kaede’s royal families, binding them, in the process, to his prodigal sons. These women are indisputably more intrinsic to the turns and toil of this movie than the obvious picks. The viewers are kept glued to the screen more because of the villainous and intriguing character of Lady Kaede and the conflicted interplay between her and Shuri (Jiro’s right-hand man) than Taro and Jiro’s adolescent antiques. She is the heart of the movie’s most engaging dynamics.

The more spiritually relevant sub-plot, however, is that of Lady Sue and her blinded brother Tsurumaru. Their parents were slain by Hidetora when they were just children, and they have since become silent, burning victims. Lady Sue embraces the stoicism of Buddha and finds in herself the ability to forgive Hidetora and build peace through her devotion, but for Tsurumaru, it is not as simple as that. In fact, the conflict between unmitigated suffering and the existence of God (or in this case, Buddha’s truths) is Ran’s key question.

But Kurosawa understands that an oblivious affirmation of stoic faith should not be so easy.

On a small diversion from the escape Tsurumaru embarked on with his sister, Lady Sue is beheaded on the orders of Lady Kaede. Her religious devotion eventually culminated in the void, and Kurosawa mercilessly visualizes this in a silent shot of Lady Sue lying beheaded in a situated heap of flowers alongside her dead maid. The last shot of the movie zoom cuts to Tsurumaru, standing on a cliff’s neck, blind, waiting for his dead sister. In trying to walk, he loses hold of the scroll of the Amida Buddha his sister gave him as a symbol of strength, and the camera cuts to the scroll, lying, covered by shadows.

Thus, Ran raises a spiritual quandary long bemoaned in the history of man: Is it possible to be good in a world with greedy, war-waging men? Hell, is it even desirable to be good if the greedy men are eventually going to execute the good? Hidetora kills, and stands victor to an empire. He steps down gently but is metaphorically stabbed without his sons having any concern for familial, or even humane, morals. Life is deliberately related to loss in this frenzy bloodshed of sin and swords.

The questions above make up a deeply conflicted area for the film: Kurosawa treats both his spiritually sound and intrinsically immoral characters equally woefully. And, in my opinion, with this he consciously or unconsciously clears all bouts of anti-religious commentary apparent in the film. It is a glorious affirmation of the Buddha: this world is a world of suffering and all pleasure is temporal. We all will die someday, and our molecules will arrange themselves into rocks and pillars.

What are our choices? Be spiteful and tyrannical as Jiro, and face defeat. Lose everything and blur your conscience like the senile Hidetora, and face a losing negotiation with fate. But you can also welcome the suffering of consciousness and embrace your cosmic shortsightedness like Lady Sue, and die peacefully, for death will come to all sentience.

Ran stinks of the mess of mankind (narrated along the lines of a Homeric fall, being inspired by Shakespeare’s King Lear): Kurosawa’s characters look upto the clouding sky in search for God, and only in death do they find him. It teaches an endearing lesson, it is nearly impossible to forgive someone who has killed your loved ones, humiliated and beaten you, or stolen your cheerio (and live in devotion to the Buddha). It is a noble ideal. This is not easy. This is not new. That is the point.
