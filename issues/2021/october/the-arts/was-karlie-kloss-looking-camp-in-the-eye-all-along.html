Was Karlie Kloss Looking Camp Right in the Eye All Along?

  • The Arts   16/10/2021   Mohan Rajagopal

The Internet is often a beautiful place, simply because of all the timeless moments of irreverence and innocent indignities that it allows for. Some of my favourite aspects of pop culture are fashion, celebrities, viral memes, and absolute flops that the Twitterati have a field day with. In a twist of fate, each of these came about in a glorious medley of entertainment: Karlie Kloss at the 2019 Met Gala.

Anyone who knows anything about the entertainment industry will immediately know what I am talking about when they hear the buzzwords “Karlie Kloss” and “Met Gala” in conjunction. For the uninitiated, a crash course: the Met Gala is considered the most exclusive social event in the world. Hosted by Vogue, celebrities of a certain stature are invited to attend the party at the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York, dressed according to a theme fixed for each year. Other than fashion weeks in London, Paris, and Milan, the Met Gala is fashion’s biggest night. In 2019, the theme for the event was “Camp: Notes on Fashion”, referencing the aesthetic of Camp first conceptualised by American essayist Susan Sontag. In brief, Camp is a stylistic sensibility that deals with exaggeration, irony, and theatricality. It’s big, bold, and overstated, and simplicity is not a concept that exists in its vocabulary. As such, the 2019 Gala was extremely looked forward to, as much of the fashion world was expected to turn up in elaborate eleganza.

Cut to the day of the event: Karlie Kloss tweets a dramatic, suspense-filled teaser of her costume for the party, captioned “looking camp right in the eye” (a phrase which, by now, has become a Twitter-favourite). In light of the tweet, there were great expectations for her, until she actually arrived at the event… in the most basic, frankly ugly looking, short gold dress with big black poofy sleeves, serving underwhelming B Barry Benson realness. Needless to say, it was immediately trashed on by the entire internet and was the most talked-about outfit at the event for all the wrong reasons.

Unfortunately, owing to the COVID-19 pandemic, that was the last Met Gala for a while, as 2020 did not see the event happening. The 2021 Gala, hence, is one of the most anticipated occasions of the year, to be held in September. Already, great hype has been built around the party, with fake seating arrangements and invitation lists floating around online, but Karlie Kloss’s colossal failure refuses to leave my head. Exacerbated by my recent perusal of Sontag’s original essay on Camp, a hypothesis gradually formed over the past few weeks: yes, it may have been hated on, and yes, it was a hideous dress, but was Karlie Kloss actually Camp all along?

In order to properly analyse the concept of Camp and which stylistic choices apply to it, I shall be using Sontag’s essay as my Holy Grail, during the course of which she outlines the different properties and components of Camp. A major topic covered by the essayist in her piece is that of the “Camp vision”. Essentially, Camp, or any other aesthetic for that matter, is defined not only by the creator or the wearer but the beholder as well. It is the audience’s vision that largely dictates whether or not a piece of clothing is Camp, and hence, it is not in the context of the intentions of the wearer (Kloss) that we must analyse the outfit, but in the opinions and reception of the viewer (the press and the Internet).

There is one principle that is central to my argument, which is at the very core of Camp itself:

“The ultimate Camp statement: it’s good because it’s awful.”

With this principle in mind, the Camp-ness of Kloss’ outfit is only too outfit. In failing entirely at meeting expectations and in failing at meeting the traditional qualities of exaggerated and theatrical Camp, Kloss’ outfit is so awful that, paradoxically, it becomes Camp. It is the very lack of Camp that makes it Camp, and that is the complexity of aesthetics and style in the postmodern era. Additionally, Sontag states that “the pure examples of Camp are unintentional; they are dead serious”. The seriousness with which Kloss took her outfit is easily seen in the dramatic buildup to her reveal; she did not expect to become the butt of a viral joke. Pure, naive Camp is never deliberate but takes itself as seriously as possible. “Intending to be Campy is always harmful”, says Sontag, but that is where the perspective of the viewer, as the holder of the Camp vision, comes in. It cannot be denied that most of the audience never intended Kloss’ outfit to be Camp- the years of memes and jokes are testament to that fact. But once again, perhaps that is exactly why it is Camp after all. The innocence attached with dismissing something as not being Camp inundated it with the primary qualities of being Camp in the first place. To even get a little carried away, some might say that the ultimate Camp statement is to simply not be Camp.

An extract from the essay reads, “There is a sense in which it is correct to say: ‘It’s too good to be Camp.’” Unfortunately, for Karlie, the opposite is only too true; the dress is unshapely and made of an unappealing fabric, and the puffy sleeves only detract even more from the costume. Camp is the “love of the ‘off’, of things-being-what-they-are-not”. One could easily argue that in refusing to hold up to great expectations of extravaganza, her outfit easily meets all these conditions.

Can Karlie be redeemed then, and can her dress be called Camp after all? Not quite yet, because another primary feature of the sensibility is that of aesthetic quality, a much more subjective parameter than the more objective features discussed before. Most people (including me) agree that the outfit barely had any stylistic advantages, with an odd cut ending far above the knee and the crinkly folds that looked like cheap gift wrap. In my opinion, though, it does have a certain Campiness to it, in terms of how awful it looks.

The verdict? It’s Camp. That, however, does not take away from the hilarity of the fact that Karlie Kloss completely failed at what she originally tried to do. In retrospect, though, was she any worse than the multitudes of men who turn up every year in the bare minimum, wearing a bland tuxedo that is invariably blue or black and pale in comparison to the real effort put into the women’s outfits? Absolutely not.
