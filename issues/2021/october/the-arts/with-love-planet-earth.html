With Love, Planet Earth

  • The Arts   16/10/2021   Sreemita Mukherjee

Humanity has major trouble accepting the sheer vastness of the universe. In our fleeting lives, we remain so obsessed with ourselves and our admirations and discriminations, seldom do we truly understand how small and insignificant we are in the grand scale of things. This insignificance doesn’t have to be a grave injustice though, I say as I try to convince myself that failing Physics will not affect my life in any way. It is sometimes when that insignificance, desperation, and loneliness truly, truly settles itself into our bones for moments, minutes, hours, even entire lifetimes at a time, do we get gratuitous attempts at being remembered beyond our extinction. The Golden Records attached to the Voyager Spacecrafts I and II are perhaps the greatest examples of such.

In 1977, NASA launched Voyager I and II Spacecrafts with various, ambitious objectives. Voyager II’s objective was to fly by Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune, and it has done its job immensely well. Voyager I on the other hand was made to focus only on Jupiter and Saturn, helping in the discovery of five new moons of Saturn, two Jovian moons, and rings around both Jupiter and Saturn. Voyager I became the first human spacecraft to venture into interstellar space, and it’s estimated that transmissions from either spacecraft will cease to continue after 2030. That is to say, our Voyagers are never coming home.

What they carry though, as they venture further and further away from us, straying away from the warmth of the sun, into an empty void that stretches on and on, is far more crucial to our faith, and everything we have ever achieved--they carry the Golden records, which if found, would introduce humanity in a way we have been craving for so long.

The Golden records are constructed of gold-plated copper and are 30 cm in diameter. Protecting the cover is Uranium-238, which has a durability of 4.468 billion years. Thus, the records will not endure any destruction for about 4 billion years. But will humanity last as long for extraterrestrials to obtain the record, decipher it, and be greeted by a civilization that lost, and fought, and loved so hard, that it almost destroyed itself? Will we even exist to receive the confirmation after centuries of wonder and curiosity that we’re not the only ones living and thinking?

The chance isn’t even narrow, it’s near impossible. We thus conclude that long after humanity goes extinct, whether it be with a scream or a whisper, the only proof of our existence will remain trapped in the form of waves, on a disc, attached to two lonely Voyagers who drift further away from their home, waiting, hoping, that someone discovers the veiled evidence of humanity and it’s wonderful, terrifying existence.

How do you sum up life on earth though?

The solution is to remind ourselves what we truly are, what matters and what does not, what defines us collectively rather than our intricate journals filled with a being that will probably not make a mark in the history of anything, but they still live, thrive and die. We’re nothing but pawns of a game that decides its own destiny, but ambition, hope, and stubbornness are also inherently human qualities.

The record is a culmination of audio, visual, and brainwaves detailing life on Earth. First comes the audio. It contains greetings in 55 languages from all over the world, all of them either wishing for peace in the universe, describing who we are and what planet we inhabit. In some cases, we wish ‘welcome home’ to whoever is listening. Maybe we expect them to be alone, maybe it’s dedicated to our very own Voyagers, maybe it’s our way of introducing what a ‘home’ is to them. The next section is composed of sounds. Sounds of thunder, lightning, and storms. Of birds, whales, and wind. Of laughter, footsteps, heartbeats, and dogs. Then there is music. Folk and rock, Beethoven and Bach. Songs sung at weddings, and songs that scream loneliness. There is music from various different languages, and it all culminates to show that we’re all singing in our own distinct voices, languages, and situations, but we’re singing nonetheless.

Next, there are images. Images detailing our solar system, metric systems, cells, and human anatomy. There are families, children, mothers, fathers. Leaves falling in autumn, valleys, rivers, and many more pictures that boast the beauty of our home. Then come the more mundane things. Black and white athletes running, traffic in India caused almost single handedly by auto-rickshaws, an astronaut in space, a woman smiling at the camera whilst eating in a supermarket, a picture detailing eating and drinking, children crowded around a humongous globe. These are obscure pictures that we find whilst looking through a photo album and laugh recalling the memories of a time long left behind. Maybe that is us at our greatest.

The most fascinating part of the Golden records is perhaps the hour-long recording of the brainwaves of Ann Duryan. Ann Duryan thought of many things. She thought of our history and glory but she also thought of our misery and violence, to ultimately present how paradoxical, good and bad our civilization is. But right at the end, she thought of love. She managed to conceal our greatest achievement in that disc that will carry humanity’s memory.

It’s to say that we lived, we died, we discovered, we destroyed, we wondered and found and lost and cried, we fell, we stood, but throughout it all, there was always love, love, and love.
