Midnight Blues Caused By Blue Light

    • The Sciences   16/10/2021   Tiya Gupta

    Sleep. Can't live without it, and for some reason, can't get it. But why? Well, it has all got to do with your pre-bedtime ritual. Do you, by any chance, use your phone before bed? Maybe your laptop? Well, the blue light they emit, the one that allows you to see your screen in high resolution, actually has several negative effects on your brain and therefore, your sleep quality.

    But what is Blue light?

    Blue light is one of the several coloured lights displayed in a visible light spectrum. The other colours in the spectrum include red, purple, and yellow, amongst others. All the colours in this spectrum are emitted at different wavelengths and energy levels; blue light has shorter wavelengths and higher energy than other colours. Some research shows a link between eye damage and short-wave blue light with wavelengths between 415 and 455 nanometers.

    Blue light tends to trick our brains into thinking it is still daytime. As a result, our brains are hardwired to stay awake and try to be productive. This happens because as our brain thinks it’s daytime, the sleep hormone, melatonin, isn't released. Melatonin secretion begins a few hours before bedtime, and that is why a set bedtime and a proper night routine are highly encouraged, such that the release of melatonin is on time every night and there isn't any obstruction to the secretion. The darkness of the night and a relaxing routine help increase the melatonin hormone, but the blue light only reduces it, if not stopping it altogether.

    Teenagers are more sensitive to the effects of blue light than adults. Therefore, you are more likely to move around after the usual bedtime. Coupled with other things that make it difficult for us to fall asleep, such as the biological clock that makes us feel awake at night, more homework and distractions, and early school hours, teens usually do not get the amount of sleep they require.

    Researchers at Harvard University conducted an experiment comparing the effects of exposure to blue light for 6.5 hours with exposure to green light of a comparable brightness. Blue light inhibits melatonin for about twice as long as green light, and the circadian rhythm changes twice as long as green light (3 hours vs. 1.5 hours). In another blue light study, researchers at the University of Toronto compared melatonin levels in people exposed to bright indoor light and wearing anti-blue light glasses with people exposed to dim light but not wearing glasses. The fact that the hormone levels in the two groups are roughly the same supports the hypothesis that blue light is an effective melatonin inhibitor. It also shows that if shift workers and night owls wear glasses that block blue light, they could protect themselves. Cheap sunglasses with orange lenses block blue light, but they also block other colours, so they are not suitable for indoor use at night.

    Projection time (especially at night) is related to lack of sleep. The blue light emitted by electronic devices can interfere with our circadian rhythms or sleep cycles. In one study, exposure to blue light for just 2 hours at night reduced or stopped the release of the sleep hormone melatonin. Turning off the power to the digital device at least 3 hours before bed may help.

    Furthermore, exposure to blue light can even increase your risk of certain cancers. One study found that people who work night shifts have an increased risk of breast, prostate and colorectal cancer.

    What about children?

    A child's eyes are unable to filter blue light as well as older individuals. Too much information gleaned from the device screen can increase the child’s chances of obesity, myopia, and poor concentration. At night, it can cause the body to release melatonin more slowly than adults. To protect their eyes, the child's screen display time should be limited. They should be asked to put away all electronic equipment, including portable gaming devices, at least half an hour before going to bed.

    Why do you get sad right before falling asleep?

    In animal studies, nighttime exposure to blue light is associated with symptoms of depression. But exposure to blue light during the day can have the opposite effect. It has been used to treat seasonal affective disorder or SAD. This is a type of depression related to seasonal changes. Studies have shown that exposure to blue light for 20 minutes in the morning can help relieve SAD symptoms.

    How can you tell if you’re a victim of blue light and sleep deprivation?

    The most obvious tell-tale sign is if you tend to stay awake and toss and turn for hours in bed, resulting in sleepless nights. This leads to a lack of a fixed sleep schedule and you wake up feeling groggy and miserable. Few other signs are blurry vision before falling asleep, dry and irritated eyes, headaches, and worst of all, damaged retinas; this disease is known as phototoxicity.

    How can I reduce it and sleep well?

    Now, all this might sound scary, but there is an easy and efficient way to reduce the effects or cut it out entirely. The age old advice: stop using electronics two hours before bed! Before you shake your head and move on to another article, think about it. What would you rather have? A good night’s sleep so you can wake up refreshed with a smile on your face to the birds chirping (very cinematic), or checking your social media and waking up feeling sick and tired and wishing you were still asleep?

    If you absolutely have to use electronics and simply can't live without it, then using blue light blocking glasses and dimming your device might help. Try reducing the time you use it for, if you can't cut it out altogether. Use dim red lights during the night, as red light is less likely to change your circadian rhythm and suppress the hormone melatonin. Perhaps, by calling a friend instead of texting them, or by reading a paperback book instead of watching a youtube video tonight, you’ll wake up feeling a little better tomorrow.
