New Quantum Stopwatch With Ultrafast “Time Lens” Can Improve Imaging Technologies

    • The Sciences   16/10/2021   Wrijurekh Mukherjee

    Timing intervals is generally a simple task. We simply measure the time it takes for a certain action or task to be completed in any unit we see fit. While it doesn’t take much to measure the time for simple actions, like running a lap around a playground or swimming across a pool, it gets troublesome when we record things on a macro or micro scale. For example, this is the case with the time taken for two galaxies to collide or for a photon to strike our eyes after getting reflected off a surface. In the micro scale there are errors of nanoseconds or picoseconds, and while these may seem trivial, they can make a huge difference in calculations. This is problematic because it is very difficult to measure time accurately in the quantum realm. However, the invention of a recent quantum stopwatch has drastically helped make calculations more accurate. It makes use of an ultrafast time lens to record even the tiniest of intervals more accurately than ever before. It can also be used to improve imaging technologies.

    Time-correlated single photon counting (TCSPC) makes use of technologies such as fluorescence lifetime microscopy and time-of-flight 3D imaging, and can be precise enough to time the detection of photons emitted from an excited sample just a few picoseconds apart. When we are able to measure time accurately in the femtosecond range, the imaging gets even more advanced. A team of researchers at University of Colorado, Boulder made a stopwatch that can do just that. They did this by enhancing the abilities of the TCSPC by equipping a single photon detector with a time lens. The TCSPC counts photons as they hit the single photon detector. However, it cannot differentiate between photons that hit less than a few picoseconds apart. The newly developed “time lens” uses loops of silica fibres to magnify the gap between the photons before hitting the detector, which allows it to accurately count photons just a few hundred femtometres apart. When this technique was applied to 3D imaging, it showed much clearer results. This could lead to huge improvements in imaging technologies from mapping forests and mountains to diagnosing diseases like Alzheimer’s and cancer.

    This timer is able to count photons more accurately not by being more precise but by separating the photons that are about to strike the timer. It does what Li (one of the lead researchers in this development) refers to as magnifying time. Photons are pushed apart from each other to increase the gap between them by several femtoseconds. This distance is measured in time and thus the stopwatch is magnifying time. The stopwatch does this by using silica loops that are known as “time lens”. To understand how this time warp works, imagine a number of identical rocks falling from the Empire State Building at terminal velocity. They are so close that the recorder can’t differentiate between them. The time lens lets the rocks pass through a material (silica loops, in case of photons) that separates all of them just enough for the recorder to be able to identify them. Therefore, it magnifies the time taken by the rocks to hit the recorder. The best thing about it is that it works with even the cheapest TSPC monitors.

    Scientists have just recently developed this, and thus there is still a long way to go before it can be fully implemented. Nevertheless, this is a great discovery in the field of science that can potentially revolutionise the way imaging works, which would help lots of studies in various fields around the world.
