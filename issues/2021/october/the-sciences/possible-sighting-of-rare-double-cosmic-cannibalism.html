Possible Sighting of Rare Double Cosmic Cannibalism

    • The Sciences   16/10/2021   Reevu Majumdar

    Space consists entirely of phenomena we can’t even begin to comprehend, ranging from dark energy, to dark matter, black holes, and their properties. With every scientific discovery, however, we either get a step closer or further from discovering the truth. This is illustrated in a recent discovery by the astronomers at Caltech, studying data from the Very Large Array, also known as the VLA, a radio telescope consisting of 27 enormous dishes in the New Mexican desert near Socorro.

    What in the World Happened?

    Scientists have managed to capture stable proof of a cosmic act of cannibalism: a star consuming another object that might have been either a neutron star or a black hole. The end result led to the object eating at the core of the star, causing an explosion and leaving behind a black hole. The first hints of this gruesomely cosmic activity was observed by the VLA in late 2017, but the event could have occurred well over 500 million years ago, judging by how far the light was coming from.

    The first signs of such an event occurred through a magnificent burst of radio energy, akin to bright light from a supernova, appearing in a dwarf-star forming galaxy roughly 500 million light-years away. Such a phenomenon could not be overlooked, and a group of astronomers at Caltech made follow-up observations of the galaxy, using both the observatory at VLA, as well as the W.M. Keck Observatory in Hawaii.

    The telescopes at the W.M. Keck Observatory in Hawaii were especially useful as they operate in the same optical light as our eyes. Hence, the Keck telescope was successfully able to capture a luminous outflow of material in multiple directions, at 3.2 million kilometers per hour from the central location, suggesting that an energetic explosion had occurred there in the past.

    Piecing the Information Together

    Through thorough research and careful examination of archival data, the team of astronomers from Caltech found evidence of an extremely bright X-ray source in the same place as the radio, dated back to 2014 from the Monitor of All Sky X-ray Image (MAXI) telescope, a Japanese instrument that sits on the International Space Station.

    Using the information they collected, astronomer Dillon Dong and his colleagues were able to create a picture about what might have happened all those years ago. About 500 million years ago, a binary pair of stars were born orbiting each other; one died in a spectacular supernova and became either a neutron star or a black hole. The dead star managed to enter the outer layers of its larger stellar sibling as gravity brought the two objects closer together.

    For centuries, the compact object spiraled inside the still-living star, eventually reaching and devouring its partner's core. The larger star shed massive amounts of gas and dust during this time, forming a material shell around the duo.

    Gravitational forces and complex magnetic interactions from the dead star's munching caused enormous jets of energy to be launched into the living star's core, as well as causing the larger star to explode, as seen in an X-ray flash in 2014. Debris from the detonation smashed into the surrounding shell at incredible speeds, generating optical and radio light.

    Now, such an event has been conceptualised by astronomers, and was given the complex term, “A merge-triggered core collapse supernova”. However, this marks the first direct observation and recorded sighting of the phenomenon.

    The Future of this Observation

    An astrophysicist at Princeton University by the name of Adam Burrows complimented the astronomers on their ability to piece together the information obtained from the 2017 observation with the 2014 observation. He elaborated that these findings could help constrain the timing of a process known as common envelope evolution, wherein one star becomes immersed within another.

    In cosmic time, such stages in a star's life are relatively brief, making them difficult to observe and simulate. Most of the time, the engulfing partner dies before its core is consumed, resulting in two compact objects orbiting each other, such as white dwarfs, neutron stars, or black holes.

    Now that such a phenomenon has been observed, the team of astronomers from Caltech believe that knowing how to find these multiple lines of evidence will enable them to find more common examples of the phenomenon.
